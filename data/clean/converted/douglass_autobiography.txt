preface

august 1841, hit up an anti-slavery convention in nantucket. met frederick douglass there. fresh out of the south, curious about abolitionists. heard some things while he was still in chains, so he came by, living in new bedford then.

lucky break, really. for him, for everyone still in chains, for the movement. douglass has done a lot for freedom. made friends by sharing his struggles, remembering those still bound. people across the country learned from him, cried, got angry at the injustice from his words.

his first speech at the convention, unforgettable. crowd was shocked, cheering nonstop. never felt such anger toward slavery before. saw him there, just another fugitive slave, but with intellect, eloquence. created almost like an angel, yet a slave, a runaway, not daring to trust even one white person to have his back. he had potential, just needed some education. but by law, by people, just property.

A buddy from New Bedford got Mr. Douglass to speak at the convention. He stepped up with some hesitation, like anyone would in a new spot, especially with a mind as sharp as his. He started by pointing out his lack of formal education, reminding folks that slavery doesn't nurture the brain or heart. Then he shared his story as a slave, dropping powerful thoughts and reflections along the way. When he finished, the room was buzzing with hope and admiration. I stood up and said that not even Patrick Henry, with all his revolutionary talk, matched what we'd just heard from this hunted fugitive. I still think that. I reminded everyone of the danger surrounding this young man, even in the North, even in Massachusetts. I asked if they'd let him be taken back into slavery, law or no law. The crowd yelled "NO!" loud enough to shake the South. Then I asked if they'd protect him as a brother, and they shouted "YES!" with such force you could feel it echo across the land.

I knew right then, if Douglass could dedicate himself to the anti-slavery cause, he'd give it a huge push and challenge northern prejudice. I tried to encourage him, to give him the courage to take on such an unusual and risky role. I wasn't alone; Mr. John A. Collins, from the Massachusetts Anti-Slavery Society, backed me up. At first, Douglass was hesitant, doubting he could handle the task. The path was untraveled, and he feared doing more harm than good. After some thinking, though, he agreed to give it a shot. Since then, he's been a lecturing agent for the American or Massachusetts Anti-Slavery Society. He's worked tirelessly, and his success in fighting prejudice and stirring public thought has blown past our highest hopes. He's gentle and humble yet strong in character. As a speaker, he's got pathos, wit, a knack for comparison, imitation, strong reasoning, and fluent language. He's got that mix of mind and heart you need to enlighten and win others over. May his strength hold, and may he keep growing in grace and knowledge, serving humanity's cause, wherever he is.

crazy how one of the strongest voices for slave rights is Frederick Douglass, a fugitive slave himself. then there's Charles Lenox Remond, speaking for free colored folks in the US, getting applause from both sides of the Atlantic. makes you wonder why some still cling to ideas of racial inferiority when time and opportunity are the real game changers.

now, think about this: could any other group handle the horrors of slavery and come out as strong? the system tried to break them, dim their minds, and erase their humanity. but look at how they've resisted this monstrous oppression for centuries. Daniel O’Connell talked about this too—he shared a story about an American sailor enslaved in Africa for three years. when he got out, he was a shell of himself, couldn't even speak his native tongue, just some mix of Arabic and English. shows that slavery brutalizes everyone it touches, white or black.

douglass wrote his own story, his own way. didn't hire anyone to do it for him. all him. considering the hell he went through as a slave and how little chance he's had to learn since breaking free, it's a testament to his strength. if you read it without tearing up or getting mad about slavery, you're just not human. it's true, no exaggeration here. his story's not unique, just a slice of slave life in maryland. not the worst, but still horrible. treated like an animal with no help, no hope. longed for freedom, thought and felt deep under the whip. faced dangers trying to escape. yet he's here, against all odds, in a nation of enemies.

the narrative's full of powerful moments, but the one that hits hardest is him dreaming of freedom by the chesapeake bay, watching those ships sail free. that bit's packed with thought and emotion — a direct hit against the crime of owning another person. slavery crushes the human spirit, makes people less than animals, and lifts slave traders above all. why should it last a second longer? it's just pure evil, and its existence shows a lack of fear in god or care for man. may it end forever.

folks just don't get it. slavery's got layers of cruelty they can't wrap their heads around. they hear about it and just wave it off as overblown stories. they know slaves are property but can't connect that to the real pain and injustice. you tell them about the whippings, the branding, the lack of basic human rights, and they act all shocked, like it's made up. but come on. it's the natural fallout of treating people like things. you need all that brutality to keep the system running — whips, chains, bloodhounds, all of it. marriage rights? gone. so all sorts of abuse runs wild. think folks'll get fair trials? nah. absolute power means absolute abuse. and yeah, there're skeptics who just don't want to see it. they hate the truth, want to protect slavery, look down on anyone with a different skin. they'll try to tear apart what douglass says, but he names names, places, dates. prove him wrong if you can.

douglass tells it like it is. two brutal stories — one where a slave got shot for fishing on the wrong land, another where a guy got his head blown off trying to escape a beating. nothing happened to their killers. nothing. march 17, 1845, baltimore american talks about a similar case: young matthews shot a slave for not following orders. no arrest, no trial. he just hid out at his dad's place, no one cared. remember, a slave or any black person can't testify against a white man. that's the law. no protection for them. cruelty runs unchecked. can you even imagine a worse society?

religion and southern slave masters—pretty wild mix. douglass paints a picture that ain't pretty. slaveholders claiming christianity while being man-stealers? that's a joke. no way you can balance that scale.

so, where do you stand? with the slaveholders or the oppressed? if you're with the former, you're against God and humanity. if you're with the latter, are you ready to fight? break the chains, let freedom ring. no compromise, no union with slaveholders.

boston, may 1, 1845
WM. LLOYD GARRISON

BOSTON, april 22, 1845

dear friend,

remember that old fable about the lion complaining he'd be better represented if lions wrote history? well, now they are. we've had enough of slave masters' tales. the reality of slavery is ugly, and some folks don't even need proof beyond the obvious.

back in 1838, people waited for results from the West Indies before joining abolitionists. the results came, but not many converts did. you gotta judge emancipation on more than just sugar production. hate slavery for its real cruelty, not just its surface.

your story shows how even the most ignored kids start sensing their rights early. experience teaches hard. you knew the slave's pain wasn't just hunger or whips, but the soul-crushing reality.

what makes your insights stand out is that you're from where slavery supposedly looks 'best.' so let's see it at its 'best' and imagine the darkness deeper south, where the Mississippi flows.

WENDELL PHILLIPS

we’ve known you a long time, and there's full trust in your honesty. anyone who's heard you speak feels it, and anyone reading your book will know they're getting the real story. you’ve shown the truth, not just complaints. sure, some kindness breaks through the harsh system now and then. you've been here long enough to compare how things are up north to the dark times down south.

reading your life, nobody can say we’ve chosen rare stories of cruelty. the hard times you faced aren't one-offs, they’re built into the system.

i'll read your book and worry for you. remember when you started telling me your real name and i stopped you? i wanted to stay ignorant. until you shared your memoirs. i wasn’t sure if i should thank you, knowing it’s still risky to say your name in massachusetts. folks in 1776 signed with a noose around their necks. you publish your freedom with danger around you. there isn’t a spot under the constitution where a fugitive can say, “i am safe.” no northern law protects you. in your shoes, i’d toss the manuscript into the fire.

you might be able to tell your story safely, loved as you are. but that’s because of your work and those who defy the law to protect the oppressed. maybe one day the humble can speak freely about the cruelties they’ve endured.

it’s sad that the hearts welcoming your story are against the law. keep going, friend, till the free spirit becomes law; till new england stands as a refuge for the oppressed. we won't just hide the outcast, but welcome them loudly enough to reach every corner of the south.

god speed the day.
till then,
yours,
wendell phillips

frederick douglass

frederick douglass, born in slavery as frederick augustus washington bailey, near easton in talbot county, maryland. didn't know his exact birth year, but it was 1817 or 1818. as a kid, sent to baltimore to be a house servant. there, his master's wife helped him learn to read and write. in 1838, he escaped to new york city and married anna murray, a free woman he met in baltimore. changed his name to frederick douglass. in 1841, he spoke at a massachusetts anti-slavery society convention in nantucket, impressing them enough to hire him as an agent. his oratory was so compelling, people doubted his past as a slave, so he wrote _narrative of the life of frederick douglass_. during the civil war, he helped recruit men for the 54th and 55th massachusetts regiments and pushed for emancipation. after the war, he worked to protect freemen's rights. held roles like secretary of the santo domingo commission, marshall and recorder of deeds for d.c., and u.s. minister to haiti. wrote more autobiographies, _my bondage and my freedom_ and _life and times of frederick douglass_, published in 1855 and 1881. he passed in 1895.

chapter I

born in tuckahoe, near hillsborough, about twelve miles from easton, talbot county, maryland. no real knowledge of my age, no records to check. most slaves don't know their ages much like horses don't. masters prefer it that way. never met a slave who knew his birthday. best we get is guessing by the seasons. not knowing my age bothered me as a kid. white kids knew theirs. i wasn't allowed to ask my master about it. he found it improper. only clue i got was hearing my master say in 1835 that i was about seventeen. so now im between twenty-seven and twenty-eight.

my mom was harriet bailey, daughter of isaac and betsey bailey, both dark-skinned. mom was darker than her parents.

my dad was a white man. everyone said so. some thought my master was my father. can't confirm, never had the means to know. mom and i were split when i was a baby, before she was even a mother to me. common in maryland where i escaped, separating kids from moms early. often before a kid hits twelve months, the mom's sent away, kid left with an old woman. maybe it's to kill the bond between them. that's what happens.

i only saw my mom a few times, maybe four or five, and always at night. she worked for a guy named mr. stewart, about twelve miles away. she'd walk all that way after working in the fields just to see me. she had to leave before sunrise, or she'd get whipped unless she had special permission, which was rare. i never saw her in daylight.

she'd lie with me till i fell asleep, but she was gone when i woke up. we hardly talked. she passed when i was seven, on one of my master's farms near lee's mill. i wasn't there when she got sick or died. when she died, it felt like losing a stranger because we had so little time together.

she never told me who my father was. there were whispers it was my master, but who knows. what matters is that slaveholders made it law that kids follow their mother's status. it's a system for their own gain, making their desires profitable.

i know cases where slaves have it worse because their master is also their father. the mistress always finds fault, never pleased unless she sees them punished. these slaves often get sold to keep the mistress happy. selling your own kids sounds cruel, but sometimes it's the kinder choice. otherwise, the father has to watch one son whip his brother, and if he shows any sympathy, it just makes things worse for everyone.

every year, more and more folks are born into slavery in the south. one southern politician even said this population growth would end slavery. maybe that’ll happen, maybe not, but it’s clear the folks in slavery look different than the ones brought over from africa originally. if nothing else, their existence challenges the idea that slavery’s justified by some curse on ham’s descendants. lots of us, like me, got white fathers, often our own masters, and if only ham’s line is supposed to be enslaved, then southern slavery’s losing its so-called scriptural backing.

ive had two masters. first was captain anthony. don’t remember his first name. folks just called him captain cause he sailed on the chesapeake bay. not a big-time slaveholder, only had a couple farms and about thirty slaves, overseen by a man named plummer. plummer was a real piece of work, always drunk, swearing, and violent. carried a cowskin and cudgel everywhere. he’d beat the women horribly, even my master would get mad sometimes but only when it got real bad. master wasn’t kind himself, took pleasure in whipping slaves. i’d wake up to my aunt’s screams, tied up and whipped till her back was a mess of blood. louder she screamed, harder he whipped. he’d stop only when tired. i saw this first as a kid, and it’s burned into my memory. it was my entry into the nightmare of slavery.

aunt hester got whipped after going out one night. master told her not to go out or be seen with this young guy, lloyd’s ned. why he was so protective? anyone’s guess. she was stunning, better looking than most women around, black or white.

aunt hester stepped out against orders, ended up with lloyd's ned. that was the real problem. if the man whipping her had any morals himself, maybe you'd think he was protecting her. but anyone who knew him knew better. he dragged her into the kitchen, stripped her down from neck to waist. called her a d——d b—-h, made her cross her hands, tied them up tight, and hooked her to a joist. she was on her toes, arms stretched. told her he'd teach her a lesson for disobeying. rolled up his sleeves and started with the cowskin. blood hit the floor quick. i hid in a closet, scared i'd be next. hadn't seen anything like it before. lived with grandma, away from this kind of horror.

CHAPTER II

my master's family: two sons, andrew and richard, one daughter, lucretia, and her husband, captain thomas auld. all under one roof at colonel edward lloyd’s home plantation. my master was the overseer, basically boss of the other overseers. spent two childhood years there. saw the bloody events in chapter one there, too. first taste of slavery's ugliness. the plantation's up north of easton, talbot county, near miles river. grew tobacco, corn, wheat—tons of it. kept a sloop, sally lloyd, running goods to baltimore. captain auld ran the sloop, crewed by colonel’s slaves: peter, isaac, rich, jake. the other slaves looked up to them, getting to see baltimore was a big deal.

Colonel Lloyd ran the show with 300-400 slaves at his main plantation, plus a whole bunch more on nearby farms he owned. Closest ones were Wye Town and New Design. Noah Willis ran Wye Town, and Mr. Townsend was in charge of New Design. All these places, over twenty, took their cues from the managers on the main plantation. It was the HQ. Any overseer disputes got sorted there. If a slave stepped out of line, acted up, or tried to escape, they'd get whipped, packed onto a sloop, and sent to Baltimore to be sold, making an example for the rest.

Monthly food and yearly clothing came from the main place too. Slaves got eight pounds of pork or fish and a bushel of cornmeal each month. Yearly clothes? Two linen shirts, a pair of trousers, a jacket, winter trousers, stockings, and shoes — all for under seven bucks. Kids' rations went to their moms or caretakers. Kids too young for fieldwork didn’t get shoes or proper clothes, just two linen shirts a year. When those wore out, they'd be naked till the next handout. You'd see kids, seven to ten, almost naked all year round.

Beds weren't a thing unless you count a coarse blanket. Only the men and women got those. But honestly, beds weren’t the biggest problem. Lack of sleep was worse. After fieldwork, they had washing, mending, cooking, with hardly any tools. Most of their night got eaten up getting ready for the next day. When done, they’d crash on the cold, damp floor, blankets barely covering them, until the driver’s horn got them up. No dragging feet allowed. Everyone had to be in the field on time. Miss the horn? Mr. Severe, the overseer, waited with a hickory stick and a cowskin whip for anyone not up — no excuses, no mercy.

mr. severe, the name fit. cruel man, through and through. caught him whipping a woman once, blood running for half an hour. her kids were right there, crying, begging for him to stop. didn’t faze him—seemed to enjoy it. his words were just as harsh. every sentence, another curse. field was his stage, cruelty and profanity the act. from morning till night, he was a nightmare, cursing and slashing. didn't last long though. died not long after i got to colonel lloyd’s. went out cursing, same as he lived. slaves saw it as a mercy.

mr. hopkins took over. different guy altogether. still whipped, but didn’t get a kick out of it. quieter, less cruel. slaves called him a good overseer.

colonel lloyd’s home plantation looked like a small village. all the work for the farms happened there. shoemaking, blacksmithing, cartwrighting, coopering, weaving, grain-grinding—slaves did it all. place felt like business, not like the other farms. lots of houses too. slaves called it the great house farm. getting picked to run errands there was like winning an election. meant overseers trusted you. better than being in the fields, under the whip. being chosen was a big deal. slaves tried hard to please overseers, like politicians trying to win votes. same behaviors you see in politics, you’d find there.

going to the great house farm for supplies was a big thing. they’d head out singing, filling the woods with their voices. joy and sadness mixed into those songs. didn’t care about time or tune—just sang what came to mind. always something about the great house farm in their songs. especially when leaving. they’d sing loud, full of feeling:

“i am going away to the great house farm!
o, yea! o, yea! o!”

they sang in chorus, words that might sound like nonsense to others, but meant everything to them. sometimes i think hearing those songs would hit you with the truth of slavery more than any book.

when i was a slave, i didn’t get the full weight of those rough, jumbled songs. being in it, i couldn’t see or hear like an outsider might. those songs told a story of pain i couldn’t grasp back then; loud, long, deep—it was like their souls were crying out. each note was a stand against slavery, a prayer for freedom. hearing them always dragged me down, filled me with a sadness i couldn’t shake. sometimes i’d find myself in tears just listening. even thinking about those songs now hits me hard, and writing this, i feel it again. those songs first showed me what slavery really did to us. that truth never leaves me. they still haunt me, fueling my hatred for slavery and my empathy for those still trapped. if someone really wants to get the soul-crushing reality of slavery, they should go to Colonel Lloyd’s plantation, stand in the pine woods on allowance-day, and just listen to the sounds that seep into their soul—if they aren’t moved, their heart's made of stone.

coming north, i was shocked to hear folks say slaves sang out of happiness. couldn’t be more wrong. slaves sing most when they’re hurting. those songs are their hearts’ pain, and singing is like crying—it’s a release. that’s how it was for me. i’d sing to drown out sorrow, not to show joy. crying and singing for joy were rare in slavery. saying a castaway sings out of happiness makes as much sense as saying a slave does; both sing from the same deep place.

Colonel Lloyd ran this big garden, always needing four guys and Mr. M’Durmond, the main gardener. It was the big draw around here—folks traveled from Baltimore, Easton, Annapolis to check it out. The place was packed with fruit, everything from apples to oranges. But man, keeping folks out of it was a hassle. The fruit just called to the kids and older slaves, and most couldn’t resist. Seemed like every summer day, someone got whipped for swiping fruit. The colonel had to get creative to keep 'em out. His best trick? Tarred up the fence. If you got caught with tar on you, that was proof you tried getting in, and you'd get whipped by the chief gardener. Worked like a charm—tar became as scary as a beating. They figured out you can’t mess with tar without getting caught.

He also had some fancy horse gear. His stables and carriage house looked like a big city livery joint. Top-notch horses, three slick coaches, a few gigs, plus some stylish dearborns and barouches.

colonel lloyd had two slaves running his stables—old barney and young barney, father and son. taking care of the horses was all they did, but it wasn't simple. lloyd was picky about his horses, and any slip-up meant harsh punishment. no excuses. if he even thought something was off, those two were in trouble. they never knew when they were safe. sometimes they'd get whipped for nothing, other times they'd dodge it when they actually messed up. it all depended on how the horses looked and what kind of mood lloyd was in when he needed them. if a horse wasn't fast or proud enough, it was on barney and his son. you'd hear lloyd's complaints out loud by the stable-door—horse wasn't brushed right, fed wrong, too much hay, not enough grain, or vice versa. even if old barney left something to his son, it was a problem. they couldn't say a word back. lloyd wouldn't have it. he'd make old barney kneel and take a beating—saw it myself. lloyd's sons and sons-in-law at the farm enjoyed their right to whip anyone, from barney to the coach-driver. i've watched them leave marks on a man's back from a distance with a whip.

lloyd's wealth was something else—like something out of the bible. ten to fifteen house servants, around a thousand slaves. he owned so many he couldn't recognize them all, and not all knew him. story goes he once asked a slave on the road who his owner was. "colonel lloyd," the man said. lloyd asked if the colonel treated him well. "no, sir," the slave replied. worked too hard? "yes, sir." enough food? “yes, sir, enough, such as it is.”

The colonel figured out where the slave was supposed to be and just kept riding. The guy went back to his work, not knowing he'd been talking to his master the whole time. Didn’t think about it again until a couple weeks later. That’s when the overseer told him he was gonna be sold off to a Georgia trader, just for speaking his mind about his master. Chained up, handcuffed, and ripped away from his family without any warning. That’s what you get for speaking the truth when asked some straightforward questions.

This is why when slaves get asked about how things are or what their master’s like, they usually say they’re happy and their masters are good. Slaveholders even send spies to see what the slaves really think. So, you learn quick—keeping quiet is smart. They’d rather keep the truth to themselves than face the fallout. If they talk about their masters, it’s usually positive, especially to someone new. Even I, back then, when asked if I had a kind master, always said yes. Not because it was true, but because kindness was relative, based on what was around us. Slaves are like anyone else, got prejudices and think their own situation’s better. Some even argue their masters are better, even when they’re not. On our plantation, Colonel Lloyd’s slaves would argue with Jacob Jepson’s slaves over who had the better master—Lloyd’s for being richer, Jepson’s for being tougher. Fights settled it. Whoever won, won the argument. Being a slave was one thing, but being a poor man’s slave was seen as worse.

Now, Mr. Hopkins didn’t last long as overseer. Not sure why, but maybe he wasn’t tough enough for Colonel Lloyd. Replaced by Mr. Austin Gore, who had all the right qualities for a top overseer. Gore had worked for Lloyd before on an out-farm and proved he was up for the main job at the Great House Farm.

mr. gore was the kind of guy who fit right into the system. cold and ruthless. he turned anything a slave did into an excuse for punishment. no arguing back, no explaining. if he said you were guilty, that was it. punishment followed accusation, always.

he demanded respect, but he'd bow to the master without a second thought. wanted to be top overseer and he'd do whatever it took. cunning and cruel, didn't care about right or wrong. he scared the hell out of everyone. his voice cut through like a knife.

never cracked a joke, never smiled. just orders and the whip. he saw whipping as his duty, didn't hesitate. always did what he said he'd do. never flinched, never wavered.

one time, gore tried to whip a slave named demby. demby ran into a creek, up to his shoulders, wouldn't come out. gore called him three times, said he'd shoot if he didn't come out. after the third call, gore just shot him. no hesitation. demby's body sunk, blood mixed with water.

ok so here's the deal. horror spread through everyone on the plantation except mr. gore. he was cool when asked why he went extreme. colonel lloyd and my old master wanted answers. gore said demby was unmanageable, setting a bad example. if left unchecked, it could lead to chaos among the slaves. his defense? convincing enough. he stayed as overseer, got famous for it. no court looked into his crime. slaves saw it, but couldn’t testify or sue. so, gore went on living respected, like he didn’t have blood on his hands.

now, killing a slave in talbot county, maryland? not seen as a crime. thomas lanman of st. michael’s killed two slaves, bragged about it. called himself a benefactor for it, said others should follow.

and then mrs. hicks—her husband lived close by—murdered my wife’s young cousin. brutal. she broke the girl’s nose and breastbone with a stick. the girl died after a few hours. her crime? falling asleep while minding a baby. mrs. hicks beat her when she didn’t hear the baby cry. there was some talk in the community, but not enough for justice. warrant was out, but never served. she walked free, no court, no punishment.

ill mention another bloody act from my time at colonel lloyd’s plantation, around when gore killed demby.

colonel lloyd’s slaves used their nights and sundays fishing for oysters to make up for their meager food. one old guy wandered too far, ended up on mr. beal bondly’s land. bondly got pissed, grabbed his musket, and shot him dead.

next day, bondly shows up at colonel lloyd’s place. maybe to settle up, maybe to explain himself. either way, it got swept under the rug fast. folks hardly talked about it. around there, even kids said it was worth half a cent to kill a “nigger” and another half to bury one.

chapter v

my time on lloyd’s plantation wasn’t much different than other slave kids. too young for field work, so i had lots of free time. my chores were rounding up cows, keeping chickens out of the garden, tidying the yard, and running errands for mrs. lucretia auld. otherwise, i helped master daniel lloyd find his birds after he shot them. he liked me and looked out for me. wouldn’t let older boys mess with me and shared his cakes.

didn’t get whipped much, but hunger and cold were rough. summer or winter, i wore just a coarse linen shirt. no shoes, no jacket, nothing. no bed either. on freezing nights, i’d sneak a corn sack, crawl inside, and sleep on the damp clay floor. my feet got so cracked from frost, you could lay a pen in the cuts.

food was irregular, just boiled cornmeal mush. dumped in a trough, and we’d eat like pigs—oyster shells, shingles, bare hands. no spoons. fastest eater got the most; strongest got the best spot. few left full.

i was about seven or eight when i left the plantation. leaving was pure joy. i’ll never forget hearing i’d go to baltimore to live with mr. hugh auld. found out three days before leaving, and those were some of my happiest days. spent them scrubbing off plantation dirt, getting ready to go.

mrs. lucretia had this thing about being clean. she said i had to scrub off all the dead skin before heading to baltimore. folks there don't mess around with dirt, she said. she promised me a pair of trousers, but only if i got clean first. the idea of owning trousers was big. almost big enough to make me scrub down to the bone. so, i scrubbed, actually looking forward to it for once.

leaving home wasn't hard. it never felt like home. my mom was gone, grandma lived far away, and my siblings and i were more like strangers. i was ready to find something better. even if baltimore had its own hardships, i figured they couldn't be worse than what i already knew. cousin tom's stories made baltimore sound like a dream compared to where i was. i left with no regrets, just hope.

we set sail for baltimore on a saturday. that's all i knew back then, just the day of the week. as we sailed off, i took one last look at colonel lloyd's plantation, then turned forward. i was all about the future, not the past.

we stopped briefly at annapolis, the first big town i'd seen. didn't get off the boat, but even from there, it seemed more impressive than anything back home. way more interesting than the great house farm.

got to baltimore early sunday, landed at smith’s wharf, close to bowley’s wharf. had a bunch of sheep on the sloop. helped drive them to mr. curtis’ slaughterhouse on louden slater’s hill. rich, one of the crew, took me to my new place on alliciana street, near mr. gardner’s shipyard at fells point.

mr. and mrs. auld were home, met me at the door with their son thomas. i was there to look after him. what hit me was mrs. auld’s face, bright and kind. never seen anything like it before. felt like a light switched on in my life. was introduced as freddy to little thomas, and got told to take care of him. started my new role with a hopeful outlook.

leaving colonel lloyd’s plantation was huge for me. had i not moved to baltimore, i might still be stuck in slavery, not sitting here free, writing this. going to baltimore was the start of my freedom. always saw it as a sign of good fortune guiding me. picked from many slave kids, first and only choice, which i found remarkable.

some might call me superstitious or full of myself, but i believe divine intervention played a role in my favor. id rather stay true to myself and face ridicule than lie to myself. always felt slavery wouldnt hold me forever. even in my darkest times, the hope and faith stuck with me, like angels guiding me through. i thank god for that spirit.

when i first met my new mistress, she seemed like the kindest person. she'd never had a slave before me and had worked for her own living as a weaver. she hadn't been touched by the ugliness of slavery yet. honestly, her kindness floored me. didn't know how to act around her since she was so different from other white women id known. typical slave behavior didn’t work with her. she didn't like the servility, seemed bothered by it. looking her in the eye wasn't a problem, and everyone felt better around her. her smiles and voice were gentle.

but it didn’t last. the power got to her. slowly, slavery changed her. her eyes turned angry, her voice harsh, and her face lost its kindness.

moved in with mr. and mrs. auld. she started teaching me the alphabet right away. once i got that down, she helped me spell some short words. then mr. auld caught wind of it. told her to stop. said it was unlawful to teach a slave to read. he warned her, "give a nigger an inch, he’ll take an ell." thought learning would ruin a slave. said if i learned to read, i’d be unmanageable, worthless to him. said it would make me discontented.

those words hit hard. woke something up inside me. suddenly, a lot of things made sense. i saw the power in reading, and it felt like a revelation. understood then how a white man kept a black man down. it was like finding a key. knowing that made losing mrs. auld's help sting, but what mr. auld said drove me. i knew learning to read was my way out. his fears confirmed what i needed to do.

his strong opposition, in a way, taught me more than she did. i wanted exactly what he feared. his warnings just fueled my determination. learned a lot from both of them. irony, huh?

didn't take long in Baltimore to notice a big difference in how slaves were treated compared to the country. city slaves are almost like freemen when you put them next to plantation slaves. better fed, better clothed, and they get some privileges country slaves wouldn't even dream of. there's a bit of shame in the city that keeps the worst cruelty in check. slaveholders don't want their neighbors to hear the screams or think of them as cruel. especially don't want to be known for not feeding slaves enough. so most city slaveholders make sure their slaves get enough to eat. but not all. across from us on Philpot Street, Mr. Thomas Hamilton had two slaves, Henrietta and Mary. Henrietta was about twenty-two, Mary about fourteen. they were the most beat-up and starved folks I'd ever seen. if you saw them, you'd have to be colder than stone not to feel something. Mary's head, neck, and shoulders were cut to ribbons. I'd feel her head, and it was covered in sores from the whip. never saw her master hit her, but Mrs. Hamilton, yeah. i was at their house a lot, and she'd sit there with a cowskin whip. barely an hour went by without her using it. she'd tell them to "move faster" and whip them, drawing blood. "take that" she'd say, and "if you dont move faster, i'll move you!" they were half-starved too. Mary fought pigs for scraps in the street. she'd been kicked and cut so much, people called her "pecked" more than her own name.

CHAPTER VII

spent about seven years with Master Hugh's family. during that time, taught myself to read and write with some tricks. no regular teacher. my mistress started teaching me but stopped after her husband told her to. gotta give her some credit though; she didn't turn cold right away. took her time to get used to having that kind of power over me.

she started off kind, really. treated me like another human, which was rare. didn't see me as property at first. eventually, owning slaves changed her. when i arrived, she was warm-hearted, always helping folks out. slavery wore her down, turned that kindness into something harsh. stopped teaching me, took on her husband's ways. got even more intense than him. nothing set her off like me with a newspaper. she'd come at me, rip it away, scared of what i might learn. figured out pretty quick that education and slavery didn't mix.

after that, she watched me close. if i was alone too long, she'd assume i had a book and drag me out to check. but she'd already given me the start when she taught me the alphabet. from there, i took it further, no stopping it.

my strategy was pretty straightforward. made friends with the white kids on the street. turned them into my teachers. their help, bit by bit, place to place, eventually got me reading. whenever i ran errands, i'd take a book, hustle through the task, and sneak in a quick lesson. always carried bread with me. had plenty at home, unlike some of the white kids around. id swap it for knowledge. almost want to name a couple of those boys to show my gratitude, but better not. might put them in a tough spot. teaching a slave to read wasnt exactly smiled upon then. they lived on philpot street, near durgin and bailey’s shipyard. we'd talk about slavery. id tell them i wished for freedom like theyd have at twenty-one. "you'll be free then, but im stuck for life." those words hit them hard. theyd feel for me, hoping something would break me free.

i was about twelve, and the weight of being a "slave for life" was getting heavy. around then, i found “the columbian orator.” read it whenever i could. one part stuck out—a dialogue between a master and a runaway slave. the slave, after being caught again, tore down every argument the master had for slavery. he said some sharp, powerful stuff. so much so, the master ended up freeing him.

in the same book, i hit one of sheridan's speeches on catholic emancipation. those words were something else for me. i read them over and over, couldn't get enough. they echoed thoughts i'd had, ones that flickered and faded 'cause i couldn't find the words. the big takeaway? truth's power even against a slaveholder's conscience. sheridan's words hit hard, calling out slavery and standing up for human rights. reading them gave me the voice i needed to argue against slavery, but with that came a new, sharper pain. the more i read, the more i hated my enslavers, saw them as thieves who'd robbed us from africa and chained us here. loathed them for their cruelty. as i dove deeper into this, the discontentment master hugh warned would come with learning to read hit me, and it hurt. reading seemed like a curse, showing me my misery but no way out. i envied those who didn't know, wished i could be anything but human just to stop thinking. couldn't escape it, everything around me was a reminder. the idea of freedom was awake in me, never to sleep again. it was in every sound, every sight, always there to remind me of my place. it was everywhere, in every breath of wind, every storm.

felt a lot of weight back then. like i was stuck and couldn’t see any way out. thought about ending it, but the hope of freedom kept me going. got curious about slavery talk. that word “abolition” kept popping up. took me a while to figure it out. dictionary wasn’t much help – just said “act of abolishing.” but abolish what? didn’t dare ask anyone. seemed like something they didn’t want us to know.

waited it out, found a paper talking about ending slavery in D.C. and between states. finally clicked what abolition was about. whenever i heard it, i listened close. slowly, things started making sense. one day, helped some Irish guys unload stone. one asked if i was a slave for life. said i was, and he got real quiet. told me i should run north, find friends, be free. acted like i didn’t get it, worried they’d turn me in. white folks had done that before. but their words stuck.

decided to escape someday. too young then, and i needed to learn how to write. maybe write my own pass. held onto that hope, kept my head down, learned what i could.

saw how the ship carpenters in Durgin and Bailey’s yard would mark timber. They'd hew it, get it ready, and then mark it with letters to show which part of the ship it was for. "L" for larboard, "S" for starboard. Forward got an "F", aft got an "A". Simple code. I picked up on those letters quick. Started copying them, and soon enough, I could write them. I'd challenge boys who knew how to write, say I could match them. They'd doubt, so I'd show 'em my letters. Chalk on fences and walls became my practice ground. Moved on to copying Italics from Webster’s Spelling Book, kept at it till I knew them without looking. Master Thomas went to school, filled copy-books, and left them lying around. While the mistress was out, I'd fill the blanks in those books, copying his hand till mine looked the same. Took years, but that's how I learned to write.

on to Chapter 8... so i moved to Baltimore and Richard, my old master’s youngest, died not long after. three and a half years later, Captain Anthony died too. Left no will, so they needed to divide his estate between Lucretia and Andrew. I got called in to be valued like the rest of the property. Felt a deep hatred for slavery rise up again. Realized how low my situation was. Leaving Baltimore, my heart was heavy, full of dread. Boarded the Wild Cat with Captain Rowe, and after a day’s sail, I was near my birthplace. Hadn’t been back in almost five years. Still remembered it well, even though I was just a kid when I left to live with my old master on Colonel Lloyd’s plantation. Must've been around ten or eleven then.

at the valuation, they lined us up like livestock. men, women, horses, pigs, all in a row. young and old, married or not, got the same rough look-over. seeing it like this, you get a clear picture of how slavery messes up everyone involved.

after that came the division. can't really capture the mix of excitement and dread we felt. our lives were about to be split up, and we had no say, just like the animals. a word from the white men could rip apart friends and family. worst fear? ending up with Master Andrew. he was a drunk, mean as they come, already wasted much of his father's stuff. falling into his hands was like being sold to the Georgia traders. pure horror.

i was more anxious than most. i'd tasted kindness in Baltimore; they hadn't. they were used to beatings, their skin tough from it. mine wasn't. in Baltimore, i got few whippings and had decent folks around. the thought of leaving them for Master Andrew, who just recently beat my little brother so bad blood poured from his nose and ears, was terrifying. he even said he'd do the same to me when he owned me.

by some luck, i ended up with Mrs. Lucretia and went back to Baltimore, back to Master Hugh's family. they were as happy to see me as they were sad to see me go. it was like escaping a lion's mouth. i was only away from Baltimore for a month, but it felt like six.

came back to baltimore and not long after, lucretia died. left her husband and their kid, amanda. not much later, master andrew passed too. now everything from my old master, including us slaves, was in stranger's hands. strangers who didn't earn it. not one of us got freedom. all stayed slaves, from the young to the old. what really nailed the evil of slavery for me was how they treated my grandmother. she was loyal to my old master all her life. she built his wealth, filled his land with slaves, became a great-grandmother serving him. rocked him as a baby, cared for him as a kid, served him till he died, even closed his eyes at the end. yet still, she was left a slave. a slave to strangers, watching her family split up like cattle, not even able to say a word about it.

and the worst part? she outlived my master and his kids, but now they saw her as worthless. old, in pain, couldn't do much. so they dumped her in the woods, gave her a little shack with a mud chimney, and left her there to survive alone. like they were just waiting for her to die. if she's still alive, she's alone, mourning her lost family. all sold off to the rice swamps, where nothing good waits.

her home empty. kids who used to sing and dance are gone. she stumbles in her old age for a drink. instead of her children's voices, she gets the dove's moans or the owl's screams at night. everything's dark. death close by. when she needed family most, she's alone in that hut with a few dying embers. no kids or grandkids to comfort her or bury her. won't a just god see this?

couple years after Mrs. Lucretia passed, Master Thomas got hitched again. Rowena Hamilton was her name, Mr. William Hamilton's eldest daughter. They moved to St. Michael's. Not long after, a spat broke out between him and Master Hugh, so to stick it to his brother, he pulled me over to St. Michael's. Another rough split for me, but not as bad as the one when they divided the property. Master Hugh and his wife weren't who they used to be; brandy and slavery did a number on them. Honestly, didn't feel like I was losing much there. But leaving the Baltimore boys? That stung. Learned a lot from them, and it hurt to go. No chance of coming back either. Master Thomas said he'd never let me return. That rift with his brother? He thought it was unfixable.

Kicked myself for not trying to run away sooner; way easier from the city than the country.

Caught a ride on the sloop Amanda with Captain Edward Dodson, heading to St. Michael’s. Watched how the steamboats headed to Philly. Instead of going down, they veered up the bay at North Point, north-east. Figured that was crucial info. Got the itch to run off again. Just needed the right moment. When it showed, I’d be gone.

im at a point where i can put dates to things. left Baltimore, moved in with Master Thomas Auld at St. Michael's, March 1832. been over seven years since i was with him at Colonel Lloyd's place. we were basically strangers now. new master for me, new slave for him. neither of us knew the other's temper yet. didn't take long to figure it out though. got to know him and his wife real quick. they were a pair, both mean and cruel. first time in over seven years i felt hunger like that. hadn't been that hungry since Colonel Lloyd's. back then, i never had enough, but with Master Hugh, i always had good food. Master Thomas? mean man. not giving enough food, even slaveholders see that as real low. rule is, food can be coarse, just make sure there's enough. in Maryland, that's mostly how it goes, with exceptions. Master Thomas? didn't give us coarse or fine. four of us in the kitchen—my sister Eliza, aunt Priscilla, Henny, and me. less than a half-bushel of cornmeal a week, barely any meat or veggies. not enough to live on. we had to rely on neighbors, begging or stealing, whichever worked. many times we were starving while food rotted away nearby, and our so-called pious masters would pray every morning for blessings.

Captain Auld was a piece of work. Mean through and through, and didn't even bother hiding it. He wasn't born into this slaveholding mess, just lucked into it through marriage. Adopted slaveholders like him? The worst.

He tried to act tough, like Napoleon one minute, then got all lost and clueless the next. Wanted to be a lion, but his ears gave him away. His attempts at nobility just highlighted his meanness. He copied the big slaveholders, but was a lousy imitator. Tried to deceive but lacked the skill, so he just ended up inconsistent and looked down on, even by us.

We didn’t call him "master" much, just "Captain Auld," if at all. His wife wanted us to use "master," but he couldn't make us. In 1832, he got religion at a camp meeting. I thought maybe he'd free us or at least be kinder. Wrong on both counts. If anything, he got worse. Used his newfound piety to back up his cruelty.

Turned his place into a preacher hangout. While we starved, he fed them well. Preachers loved staying there. Mr. Cookman was different though. He seemed genuine, like he actually cared. We thought he was trying to help free slaves, and when he visited, he made sure we joined in prayers. Felt like he saw us, which was rare.

living in St. Michael's with my master, we had this young guy, Mr. Wilson, who tried to start a Sabbath school. he wanted to teach slaves to read the New Testament. we managed just three meetings before Mr. West and Mr. Fairbanks, both class-leaders, showed up with sticks and other stuff to break it all up. they shut us down fast. pious town, St. Michael's.

my master claimed religion to back his cruelty. one time, he tied up a lame young woman and whipped her with a heavy cowskin on her bare shoulders, making her bleed. he'd justify it by quoting Scripture—“He that knoweth his master’s will, and doeth it not, shall be beaten with many stripes.”

he'd leave her tied up for four or five hours. sometimes he'd whip her before breakfast, leave for his store, come back at dinner, and whip her again, cutting into the same wounds. "Henny" was almost helpless, burned badly as a child. couldn't use her hands, only carry heavy loads. she was an expense to him, and he resented her. he tried giving her away to his sister, but she didn't want her either. eventually, he just "set her adrift." this was a man recently converted, clinging to the mother while casting out her helpless child. master thomas was one of those slaveholders claiming to care for their slaves, really just using it to justify keeping them.

my master and i had our share of clashes. he thought city life messed me up, said i was no good for anything decent. i let his horse wander off to his father-in-law's farm five miles from st. michael’s. i'd have to fetch it back, but there was good food there. master william hamilton, his father-in-law, always fed his slaves well. never left hungry, even if i was supposed to rush back. master thomas got fed up after nine months and constant whippings that did nothing. decided to send me to be "broken" by edward covey. covey was known for breaking in young slaves. he rented his farm and the help to work it. his reputation meant he could get his farm worked cheaper. some slaveholders thought it was worth losing a slave for a year to get them trained by him. he was also a religious man, a methodist class leader, which boosted his rep. i knew all this, heard it from a guy who lived there. still, i was glad to go, at least i’d get enough to eat. hunger's a big deal when you're starving.

left Master Thomas’s place for Mr. Covey's on Jan 1, 1833. first time as a field hand. i felt more out of place than a country kid in a big city. just a week in, Covey whipped me hard, cutting my back and drawing blood. here's how it went down: Covey sent me early one freezing January morning to fetch wood with a team of unbroken oxen. he told me which was the in-hand and the off-hand ox, tied a rope around the in-hand one's horns, and handed me the other end. "hold on if they bolt," he said. never driven oxen before, so i was clumsy. managed to reach the woods edge, but then the oxen spooked. they bolted, dragging the cart into trees and over stumps. thought my head would smash into a tree. they finally flipped the cart into a thicket. no idea how i survived. there i was, alone, in strange woods, cart busted, oxen tangled, no help. after a long struggle, got the cart upright, oxen free, and yoked again. headed to where i'd chopped wood the day before, loaded the cart heavy to calm the oxen. started back home, already half the day gone. made it out of the woods and felt safer. stopped to open the gate; oxen bolted again, smashed the gate, nearly crushed me. twice in one day, dodged death by inches. told Covey what happened. he sent me back to the woods, followed me, then stopped the cart, "teach you not to waste time," he said. cut three big switches, told me to strip. i stood there, clothes on. he yelled again, i didn't budge. then he tore my clothes off and whipped me till the switches broke, leaving marks that lasted. first of many beatings for stuff like this.

lived with Covey for a year. first six months, it was rare to go a week without getting whipped. back was sore most of the time. he’d blame my clumsiness. we worked right to the edge of what we could handle. up before dawn, horses fed, and off to the fields with hoes and plows. Covey gave us enough food, but barely time to eat it—less than five minutes for meals. started work at first light and didn’t stop till the sun was gone. during fodder season, sometimes till midnight.

Covey was out there too. he'd spend afternoons in bed, then come out fresh in the evening, pushing us with words or the whip. he was one of the few slaveholders who worked alongside us. he knew exactly what we could do, no fooling him. work ran smooth whether he was there or not 'cause he had this knack for surprising us. never came out in the open if he could sneak up instead. called him “the snake.” in the cornfield, he’d crawl on all fours, then suddenly pop up and shout, “Ha, ha! Come on, dash on!” never safe to pause, he’d just appear out of nowhere. seemed like he was always around. under every tree, behind every stump. he’d pretend to ride off to St. Michael’s, then half an hour later, there he was, hidden in a fence corner, watching. sometimes he’d act like he was leaving for a trip, turn his back, pretend to head to the house, then sneak back to spy on us till sunset.

covey had a knack for fooling folks. his whole life seemed built on pulling the wool over everyone's eyes. he twisted any bit of learning or religion he had to fit his deceitful ways. thought he could even con god. he'd do a quick prayer in the morning, a long one at night, and somehow, some folks still bought his act. family devotions kicked off with singing, but he couldn't carry a tune, so he'd have me start the hymn. sometimes i'd sing, sometimes not. when i didn't, it threw him off. he'd try to sing on his own, but it was a mess. yet oddly, he'd pray even harder. poor man fooled himself into thinking he was a true believer, even while forcing his slave woman into adultery.

covey was starting out, didn't have much, so he bought one slave — caroline, from thomas lowe. she was strong, about twenty, already had a kid. just what he wanted. then he hired a married man to live with them for a year, locking him up with caroline every night. by year's end, she had twins. covey loved it, saw it as boosting his wealth. he and his wife treated caroline well during her confinement, like nothing was too good for her.

those first six months with covey were the worst. worked us hard in every weather, day or night. i was tough to handle at first, but he broke me down. body, soul, spirit — all crushed. that spark of life was snuffed out. sunday was the only break, mostly spent in a daze under some tree. sometimes i'd get a burst of hope, but it vanished quick. thought about ending it all, even taking covey with me, but fear and a sliver of hope stopped me. now, those days feel more like a nightmare than something real.

our house was close to the chesapeake bay. wide open water, always dotted with sails from everywhere. those ships, looking so free, felt like ghosts to me. they'd remind me of my own chains. many a quiet summer sunday, id stand on those high banks, watching sail after sail drift towards the ocean. it hit hard every time. id speak out loud, just me and the universe, to the ships:

"you’re off and free; im stuck here, a slave. you glide on the breeze; i trudge under the whip. you’re freedom’s messengers; im bound in iron. oh, to be free! to be on your decks, under your wings! but here i am, water between us. go on, go on. if only i could go too! swim or fly! why was i born a man turned into a beast? the ship’s gone now, swallowed by the distance. im left in the hottest hell of endless slavery. god save me! make me free! is there a god? why am i a slave? i'll run. i cant take it. caught or free, i'll try. better to die running than standing still. only a hundred miles north, and im free! try it? yes! god helping me, i will. i wont die a slave. i'll use this bay to find my freedom. steamboats head northeast from north point. i'll do the same. when i reach the bay’s end, i'll ditch the canoe and walk through delaware to pennsylvania. there, no pass needed. i’ll travel undisturbed. first chance i get, im gone. till then, i'll endure. im not the only slave. why fret? i can bear it like the rest. im just a boy; all boys answer to someone. maybe this misery will make freedom sweeter. better days are coming."

thats how i thought, swinging from madness to making peace with my fate.

i've mentioned before that the first six months at mr. covey's were rougher than the last. what changed there was a big turning point for me. you've seen how a man becomes a slave; now you'll see how a slave becomes a man. august, 1833, hottest day of the month. me, bill smith, william hughes, and a slave named eli were fanning wheat. hughes cleared it, eli turned, smith fed, i carried. simple work, more about muscle than brains, but tough if you've never done it. around three, i broke down. head pounding, dizzy, shaking all over. knew i couldn't stop, so i pushed on, hauling grain until i couldn't stand. when i finally fell, felt like i'd been pinned by a mountain. fan stopped, and everyone had their own job to handle. no one could pick up another's slack without dropping their own.

covey was about a hundred yards from the yard where we were working. when he heard the fan stop, he came right over. asked what was up. bill said i was sick and no one was bringing wheat to the fan. id crawled off under the fence trying to escape the sun. covey asked where i was. one of the guys pointed him my way. he came over, looked at me, asked what was wrong. i told him, barely had the strength to speak. kicked me hard in the side, told me to get up. tried, fell again. kicked again, told me to rise. got up, tried to get the tub for the fan, staggered, fell. while i was down, covey grabbed a hickory slat, hit me on the head. big wound, blood everywhere. told me to get up again. i didnt move. decided id let him do whatever. after that blow, my head felt better. covey left. first time i thought to go to my master, complain, ask for protection. but id have to walk seven miles, and i was weak from the sickness and the beating. waited for a chance, ran for the woods when covey wasnt looking. he saw me, yelled threats, but i didnt stop. took the woods to avoid being caught, stayed near the road to not get lost. didnt get far, collapsed. blood still coming from my head, thought id bleed out. but the blood matted my hair, stopped it. after about forty-five minutes, got up, continued through bogs and briers, barefoot and bareheaded, tearing my feet. took five hours to cover seven miles. made it to masters store, covered in blood. told him everything, he seemed affected but still sided with covey. said i deserved it, asked what i wanted. said i needed a new home, id die if i stayed. master thomas laughed at the idea covey would kill me, said covey was a good man. wouldnt take me away, id have to go back. gave me salts, let me stay the night, but ordered me back to coveys in the morning. no supper or breakfast. reached coveys around nine. covey came running with a cowskin, but i got to the cornfield first and hid. he searched a long time, angry, but gave up. later, met up with sandy jenkins, another slave. he took me to his free wifes place, gave advice. told me to get a root to carry on my right side, said it would protect me. didnt believe it, but took the root anyway. sunday morning went back, covey passed by without a word. thought maybe the root worked. monday morning, tested it. called to work on the horses. covey tried to tie me with a rope. i fought back, grabbed his throat, he was shocked. he called for hughes, hughes tried to tie me, kicked him hard. hughes backed off, covey lost his nerve. asked if id keep resisting. told him yes. he tried to drag me to a stick to hit me, i pulled him down. bill showed up, covey asked for help, bill refused. we fought for two hours. covey finally let me go, said if i hadnt resisted, he wouldnt have whipped me so much. truth is, he didnt whip me at all. after that, he never touched me again. he said he didnt want to get hold of me anymore. thought to myself, he better not, it wont end well for him.

this fight with mr. covey changed everything for me as a slave. it lit the last sparks of freedom inside, reminded me of my own strength. brought back my lost confidence and pushed me to focus on freedom again. beating him was worth anything that came after, even death. only those who've fought back against slavery know that deep satisfaction. felt something new, like rising from the grave of slavery to the sky of freedom. fear left, defiance took over. decided then, no matter how long id be a slave in form, id never be one in spirit again. made it clear—any white man wanting to whip me better be ready to kill me too.

after that, never really whipped again, stayed a slave for four more years though. had some fights, but never beaten.

for a long time, wondered why mr. covey didnt have me whipped publicly for hitting a white man. only thing that makes sense now—not fully satisfying—is he had a rep as a top overseer and slave-breaker. losing face by sending a sixteen-year-old to the whipping-post would've hurt him. to keep his standing, he let me go unpunished.

my time with mr. edward covey ended on christmas day, 1833. between christmas and new year's, we got holidays; no work except feeding and caring for the stock. this was our time, a gift from the masters, and we could use it as we liked. those with families far away often spent all six days with them. others stayed busy making things like corn-brooms or horse-collars, while some hunted. most spent the time playing games, running races, fiddling, dancing, and drinking whisky, which the masters preferred. a slave working during holidays was almost shamed for it, seen as rejecting the master's favor. it was a disgrace not to drink at christmas, and you were lazy if you hadn't saved enough for whisky during the year.

holidays for slaves aren't just days off. they're a tool. slaveholders use them to keep folks from rising up. if they stopped these holidays, there'd be rebellion for sure. the holidays let off steam. without them, desperation would hit a peak, and slaveholders would be in trouble if they tried to stop it. they'd face something worse than an earthquake.

these holidays are part of the big lie of slavery. it's called kindness, but it's selfishness. they give time off, not because they don’t want the work, but because it's dangerous not to. slaveholders want slaves to spend the days in a way that makes them glad it's over. they push them into dissipation so they hate freedom. they’ll even bet on who can drink the most. it tricks slaves into thinking freedom's no better than slavery. many of us ended up preferring the fields to the so-called freedom filled with rum.

this trick is a piece of the wider system of slavery's deceit. it's about making freedom look bad. like when a slave loves molasses, steals some, and then the master forces him to eat so much he gets sick. same with food. run out and ask for more, and you get too much, forced to eat until sick. then punished for being hard to please. seen it over and over. plenty more examples, but you get the idea. it's common practice.

started living with mr. william freeland on january 1, 1834. place was about three miles from st. michael's. freeland was a whole different deal from mr. covey. not rich, but an educated southern gentleman. covey was a trained slave-driver, no respect for anything. freeland had his faults, typical slaveholder stuff like being passionate and fretful, but he wasn't into the degrading vices covey had. freeland was open and honest, you always knew where you stood. covey was a sneaky deceiver, only the sharp ones could catch his tricks.

one big plus with freeland was he didn't pretend to be religious. i think southern religion just covered up the worst crimes and gave a pass to awful things. if i ever got enslaved again, being under a religious master would be the worst. religious slaveholders are the meanest, most cowardly of the bunch. i lived in a community full of them. near freeland was rev. daniel weeden, and nearby was rev. rigby hopkins, both part of the reformed methodist church. weeden owned a woman slave whose name i forgot. her back was kept raw from his lash. his rule was to whip a slave occasionally, just to show who's boss. that was his belief and how he acted.

mr. hopkins was worse than mr. weeden. he bragged about managing slaves. his thing was whipping slaves before they did anything wrong. every monday, he'd whip one or more to scare the rest. he'd find any excuse, no matter how small. a look, a word, or an accident—any of these was enough. if a slave looked unhappy, he'd say they had the devil in them. if they spoke too loud, they were getting uppity. didn't take off their hat? no respect, whip for that. tried to defend themselves? impudence. suggested a better way? presumptuous. broke a tool? careless. hopkins always found a reason to use the lash. no slave wanted to live with him. yet he acted like the most religious man around, praying and preaching all the time.

now, about mr. freeland. he was different. gave us enough to eat and time to eat it. worked us hard but only sunrise to sunset. plenty of work, but good tools. big farm, but enough hands to make it manageable. compared to mr. covey, mr. freeland treated me well.

mr. freeland owned two slaves, henry and john harris. he hired the rest of us—me, sandy jenkins, and handy caldwell. sandy, he was the one who gave me roots to keep covey from whipping me. he believed they worked. superstition was common, and every slave death was seen as the result of trickery.

when i got to Henry and John, they were sharp guys. didn't take long for them to catch the reading bug. that itch spread quick to the others. they dug up some old spelling books, insisted i run a Sabbath school. so i did. spent my Sundays teaching my fellow slaves to read. none of them knew a single letter at first. word got out to nearby farms, more folks came to learn. we had to keep it quiet though. couldn't let the masters at St. Michael’s find out we were reading instead of fighting or drinking. they'd prefer that, keep us down. makes my blood boil thinking about how Wright Fairbanks and Garrison West, these so-called Christians, stormed in with sticks and stones to break us up. can't believe they call themselves followers of Jesus.

held the school at a free black man's house. not naming him here, don't want trouble for him, even ten years on. had over forty eager learners, most grown men and women. those Sundays were a joy, some of the best days of my life. teaching them was a blessing. leaving them each Sabbath was tough. thinking of them still trapped in slavery now hits me hard. makes me question if a righteous God runs this show. these folks didn't come to learn because it was trendy; they risked getting whipped for it. their minds were starved, locked in darkness by their masters. i taught because it meant doing something for my people. kept it up almost a year with Mr. Freeland; taught at home three nights a week in winter too. happy to say, some learned to read, and one got free thanks to what we did.

year went by easy. felt half as long as the one before. got through it without a single hit. credit to Mr. Freeland, best boss ive had till i became my own. but gotta say, it was also thanks to the crew around me. noble folks, with hearts and guts. we were tight. loved them more than anything since. folks say slaves cant love or trust each other, but man, id never loved or trusted anyone more than those at Freeland’s. we'd die for each other. never made a move without talking it through. we were one, not just 'cause of the mess we were in, but 'cause we were cut from the same cloth.

question whether we were right to even think about freedom. But man, the thought of staying slaves forever... that was worse. I knew I couldn't just sit and accept it. Started talking to Henry, then John, and others, getting their take. Had to be careful, though. Couldn't just blurt it out. But they got it. They were ready to make a move if we had a good plan.

We met, hashed out ideas, faced our fears. Sometimes it felt like too much. We’d think about giving up, but then we’d get back on track. The odds were stacked against us. Every plan had risks. If we made it, freedom wasn't even guaranteed. Canada was just a name, and New York was still a risk. Getting caught meant worse punishments.

We imagined all the dangers — guards at every turn, starvation, drowning, bloodhounds, and more. But the real nightmare was living as slaves. Freedom, even with all its risks, was a call we couldn’t ignore. Sometimes it seemed bleak, but even with the fear, we had to try. It was the only way we'd ever have a chance at something better.

got to weigh risks when you're planning something big. it's like patrick henry's choice, but for us, liberty was shaky and death was almost certain if we messed up. i'd rather die than stay chained.

sandy backed out but kept cheering us on. we had henry harris, john harris, my uncle henry bailey, charles roberts, and me. charles was family too, tied to my master's old man, mr. william hamilton.

the plan? grab mr. hamilton's big canoe, set off saturday night before easter, and paddle up chesapeake bay. it's 70-80 miles to the bay's head, then ditch the canoe and follow the north star past maryland. water route meant less suspicion, like we were just fishing. land route? anyone white could stop and check us.

week before, i wrote passes for each of us:

“This is to certify that I, the undersigned, have given the bearer, my servant, full liberty to go to Baltimore, and spend the Easter holidays. Written with mine own hand, &c., 1835.

“WILLIAM HAMILTON, “Near St. Michael’s, in Talbot county, Maryland.”

not heading to baltimore, but it covered us on the bay.

as the day got closer, nerves kicked in. life or death. i was busy clearing doubts, boosting courage, saying once we start, we're halfway there. we've talked enough. time to act. if not now, never. fold arms and accept being slaves? no way. last meet, we swore we'd go for freedom. middle of the week, nerves high, but we hid it well. kept working like normal, hearts racing.

saturday morning finally hit, and man, i was ready. anxious too, carrying the weight of it all since i was leading this whole thing. friday night? no sleep. out in the field early, spreading manure, when it hit me like a ton of bricks. turned to sandy and said, “we're betrayed.” he felt it too, just then. didn’t need more words. i just knew.

we went up for breakfast. wasn’t hungry, just going through the motions. saw four white men on horseback, two black guys walking behind. tied to the gate-post. didn’t know what was up yet. then mr. hamilton showed up, all fired up, asked for master william. he was at the barn. hamilton tore off that way, brought william back. now we got three constables in the mix, all serious.

they called me over, said some gentlemen wanted to see me. soon as i stepped outside, they grabbed me, tied my hands. wouldn’t tell me why at first. finally said i was in a “scrape” and needed to be examined. if they were wrong, they said no harm would come.

then they went for john, tied him easy. but henry? stood his ground. “i won’t!” he said. constables pulled pistols, ready to fire. henry dared them, “shoot me, shoot me!” then, quick as a flash, knocked their pistols away. they piled on him, beat him, and finally got him tied.

during the scuffle, somehow got my pass into the fire without anyone noticing. we were all tied up, ready to head to easton jail. then betsy freeland, william's mom, showed up with biscuits for henry and john. she looks at me and goes off, calling me all kinds of devil, blaming me for putting the runaway idea in their heads. i didn’t say a word, and they rushed us off to st. michael’s. before the scuffle, hamilton wanted to search for passes, but got busy tying henry. they forgot or thought it was too risky to search, so no proof of our plan yet.

halfway to st. michael’s, henry asks what to do with his pass. i tell him to eat it with his biscuit and deny everything. we all agree, “own nothing.” our trust in each other held strong, ready to face whatever, together. we’d travel fifteen miles to easton jail, ready for anything, mostly worried about being separated. at st. michael’s, they questioned us about running away. we denied it, more to see their evidence than to avoid being sold. we figured out who snitched but kept quiet. off to easton jail we went.

barely twenty minutes in jail, and slave traders swarmed us, eyeing us like treasure. never seen such a crew of devils. they taunted us, asking if we’d like them as masters. we stayed silent, let them guess. they cursed us, claiming they could break us fast if they had us.

in jail, we ended up way more comfy than we figured. food? not much, not good. but the room was clean, and we could watch the street out the window. beats a dark cell by a mile. things were going alright with the jail itself. once holidays wrapped up, Hamilton and Freeland showed up out of nowhere, took Charles, the two Henrys, and John home, leaving me behind. felt like a final goodbye. that separation hurt worse than anything. guessed they thought i was the ringleader, so they took the others and left me to get sold. noble Henry almost didn't want to leave, like he felt at home in the prison. still, we figured we'd be split up if sold, so he went home.

left alone now, staring at stone walls. days ago, full of hope, dreaming of freedom. now? buried in gloom, total despair. freedom seemed gone. stayed like that about a week, then Captain Auld, my owner, showed up, shocker, took me out. planned to send me to Alabama with some guy he knew. but plans changed, sent me back to Baltimore instead, to live with his brother Hugh and learn a trade.

so, after three years and a month, back to Baltimore. master sent me away cause folks were out for me, worried i might get killed.

a few weeks after i hit baltimore, master hugh had me working for mr. william gardner, a big-time ship-builder on fell's point. i was supposed to learn calking there, but it turned out to be the worst spot for it. gardner was building two large man-of-war brigs for the mexican government that spring. they had to be launched by july, or he'd lose a ton of money, so when i showed up, it was chaos. no time for learning. everyone had to stick to what they knew. my orders from gardner: do whatever the carpenters told me. meant i was at the mercy of about seventy-five guys. their orders were law. it was brutal. needed a dozen hands, getting pulled in every direction. voices everywhere—"fred, help cant this timber," "fred, carry that timber," "fred, bring the roller," "fred, get water," "fred, saw this timber," "fred, grab the crowbar," "fred, hold this," "fred, hit the blacksmith's for a punch," "fred, fetch a chisel," "fred, light a fire under the steam-box," "hey, turn the grindstone," "move and bowse this timber," "come on, heat up the pitch," and on and on. three voices at once sometimes. "come here! go there! stay put! move and i'll take your head off!"

spent eight months at this place, and probably would've stayed longer if not for a brutal fight with four white apprentices. almost lost my left eye and got pretty messed up. here's how it went: when i started, black and white ship-carpenters worked side by side. everyone seemed fine with it. the black carpenters, many of them free, were doing well. then suddenly, the white guys decided they wouldn't work with free black carpenters anymore. they were afraid we'd take over the trade and leave them jobless. so they pressured Mr. Gardner to fire the black workers.

didn't directly affect me at first, but my fellow apprentices soon acted like it was beneath them to work with me. they started picking on me, calling us names, saying we should be wiped out. encouraged by journeymen, they made my life hell, pushing me around and even hitting me sometimes. i'd made a vow after fighting Mr. Covey to always fight back, so i did, and as long as i could keep them separate, i held my own. but eventually, they ganged up, armed with sticks, stones, and handspikes. one got me with a brick in front, others flanked me, and the guy behind nailed me with a handspike, knocking me out cold. when i was down, they piled on, beating me. i waited, gathered strength, then surged up to my hands and knees. right then, one kicked me in the eye, felt like it burst. seeing my eye like that, they backed off. i grabbed a handspike and chased them, but then the carpenters intervened. figured it wasn't worth it; i was outnumbered.

this happened in front of fifty white ship-carpenters. nobody helped—some even shouted to kill me. lynch law was the rule there; hitting a white man meant death. i managed to escape without more hits, but just barely.

headed straight home and laid it all out to Master Hugh. Now, he wasn't a religious guy, but compared to his brother Thomas, he was a saint. Heard me out fully on what went down. My old mistress, heart softened up again, was moved seeing my eye swollen shut and my face a mess. She sat with me, cleaned me up, and bandaged my head with care, even put a slice of fresh beef on my eye. Almost made the pain worth it to see that kindness again.

Master Hugh was furious, cursing up a storm about those who did it. Once I was a bit better, he took me to Esquire Watson's on Bond Street to see what could be done. Watson asked about witnesses. Hugh said it happened in Gardner's shipyard, midday, loads of men around. But Watson said without a white witness, nothing could be done. My word wasn't enough. If I'd been killed with a thousand black witnesses, still wouldn't be enough to arrest anyone. Hugh admitted it was a terrible situation. No white man would stand up for me against their own. Even those who might sympathize wouldn't risk it. Back then, showing humanity to a black person got you labeled an abolitionist, and that was dangerous. The bloody-minded folks around just said, "Damn the abolitionists!" "Damn the niggers!" Nothing happened, and nothing would have happened if I'd been killed. That's how it was, and still is, in Baltimore.

hugh couldn't find a way to fix it, so he kept me, and his wife patched me up till i was healthy again. then he brought me into the shipyard where he was foreman under mr. walter price. started calking right away. picked up the mallet and irons fast. within a year from leaving gardner's, i was pulling in top wages for calkers. made me pretty valuable to hugh. six to seven bucks a week, sometimes nine. it was a buck fifty a day. once i knew how to calk, i found my own gigs, made my contracts, collected my pay. life got smoother. more comfortable. when calking jobs were scarce, i'd do nothing. and then those old thoughts of freedom would sneak back in. at gardner's, i was too busy to think about much beyond survival. but when life got better, my hunger for freedom grew. realized to keep a slave content, you had to make him thoughtless. cloud his mind, kill his reason. make him see no wrong in slavery. only then does he stop being a man.

so, i was making a buck fifty a day. my work, my contracts, my money. but every saturday, i'd have to hand it all over to hugh. why? not cause he earned it, not cause he helped me earn it, not cause i owed him, not cause he had any right. just cause he had the power to take it. same right as a pirate on the seas.

gonna talk about how i planned and finally pulled off my escape from slavery. but im not sharing every detail. here's why: if i spill everything, others could end up in real trouble. plus, more details mean slaveholders might tighten up, and that could slam a door shut for someone else trying to get free. wish i could lay it all out for you, it would make the story more interesting, but keeping those doors open is more important. i'll take any blame thrown my way if it means leaving a path open for a brother in chains.

never liked how some folks handle the underground railroad all loud and public. they mean well, risking their necks and all, but shouting about it helps the masters more than the slaves. it just makes slaveholders more alert and better at capturing runaways. gotta think about those still trapped down south. help those heading north but don't mess it up for the ones still trying to break free. keep the slaveholder clueless, let him fumble in the dark. don't light the path for him to catch our brothers. now, on to my escape — the parts only i can be blamed for.

early 1838, couldn't shake the restlessness. handing over my wages every week felt wrong, like feeding a thief. he'd count the money, eyes sharp, always asking, "is this all?" never satisfied till he had every cent. sometimes he'd toss me six cents for making six dollars, thinking it was some kind of encouragement. just pissed me off more. made me think he knew i deserved it all. giving me a few cents was his way of feeling like a noble thief. hated it. i was always looking for a way out. figured if i could hire my time, i might save enough to escape. spring 1838, master thomas came to baltimore for his spring goods. i asked him to let me hire my time. flat-out refused. said it was just another escape plan. told me i couldn't go anywhere he wouldn't find me. said if i ran, he'd hunt me down. told me to be content and obedient. said i'd be happy if i didn't plan for the future, if i just relied on him. tried to kill my thoughts, make me accept slavery. but i kept thinking. couldn't stop. thought about the injustice, about escaping.

about two months in, i asked master hugh if i could hire my own time. he didnt know id already asked master thomas and got turned down. hugh was gonna refuse too, but he thought about it and finally said ok. deal was: i get to manage all my time, make my own work deals, find my own jobs, but i gotta pay him three bucks every week, cover my own tools, food, and clothes. board cost me two and a half a week, so with the rest, i was out about six bucks total every week. rain or shine, work or no work, i had to have that money or lose my deal. all upside for him — no hassle, just money. for me? slave life plus freeman worries. still, better than before, so i kept at it. worked day and night, managed to cover costs, even saved a bit. kept it going till august. then hugh cuts me off cause i missed a payment after going to a camp meeting. i thought he'd be fine waiting, but he flipped out, said i was gonna run. brought my stuff back, didnt work that week out of spite. he wanted my usual wages saturday, i had nothing. he was ready to fight. i stayed quiet but ready. he said he'd find work for me now. figured i'd make my escape plan, set it for september third. had three weeks. grabbed a job at mr. butler's shipyard monday, before hugh could set anything up. brought him eight or nine bucks at week's end. he was happy, asked why i didnt do that before. little did he know. worked steady to throw him off my plan. second week, paid him again, he gave me twenty-five cents, told me to use it well. i said i would.

things were rough on the inside, but outside looked fine. when the time came to start, man, it was tough. had real friends in baltimore, ones that meant the world. leaving them felt like tearing a part of myself away. i think a lot more would break free from slavery if it wasn't for those ties. the thought of leaving them was the hardest thing. love for them nearly broke my resolve. then there was the fear of failing again, remembering that brutal defeat. if this attempt went wrong, i'd be stuck forever, facing the worst punishment and locked down tight. couldn't shake the dread of what failing would mean. slavery's misery and freedom's blessing were all i could think about. it was life or death. but i held on. september 3, 1838, i broke free and made it to new york without a hitch. can't share the how or the path—got reasons for keeping that quiet.

people always ask how it felt when i first got to a free state. never found the right words to nail it. it was like the wildest rush ever. imagine a sailor saved from pirates by a big ship. when i hit new york, i wrote a friend saying it was like escaping a pit of lions. but that thrill faded fast. loneliness hit hard. i could still be dragged back to slavery. i was in a crowd but totally alone, no home, no friends. couldn't trust anyone—fear of getting snatched by kidnappers. my motto: "trust no man." saw every white man as a threat, and even among my own, i was wary. it was hell—a fugitive in a land where slaveholders hunted freely. no home, no money, scared out of my mind. surrounded by plenty but starving, surrounded by people but feeling like prey. until you're there, it's hard to get it.

thankfully, i wasn't stuck long. david ruggles pulled me out. his kindness saved me, and i owe him big time. he's blind now, needs the kind of help he once gave. he found me in new york, took me to his place. he was deep in the darg case then, always helping other fugitives. surrounded by danger but always one step ahead.

right after i got to mr. ruggles' place, he asked where i wanted to go. staying in new york wasn't safe. told him i was a calker and needed work. thought about canada, but he said new bedford would be better for work. anna, my soon-to-be wife, came soon after i wrote her about getting to new york. wasn't easy—no home, no money—but i let her know i made it.

mr. ruggles brought in rev. j.w.c. pennington to marry us. in front of ruggles, mrs. michaels, and a few others, we got hitched. here's the certificate:

“this may certify, that i joined together in holy matrimony frederick johnson and anna murray, as man and wife, in the presence of mr. david ruggles and mrs. michaels.

“james w. c. pennington new york, sept. 15, 1838”

she was free. changed my name to johnson from bailey.

after getting the certificate and a five-dollar bill from mr. ruggles, we packed up and headed for the steamboat to newport, aiming for new bedford. ruggles gave me a letter for mr. shaw in newport, said if money got tight to stop there for help. but when we hit newport, we were too eager to reach safety to stop. took seats on the stage to new bedford, promised to pay when we arrived. two guys from new bedford, joseph ricketson and william c. taber, understood our situation and were real helpful.

felt good to have friends like that. in new bedford, we went to mr. nathan johnson's place. him and mrs. johnson were great, really looked out for us. when the stage driver wouldn't give back our baggage without payment, mr. johnson covered it. they lived up to the name abolitionists.

arriving in new bedford was a trip. first morning there, sitting at breakfast, started thinking about what name id go by. mom named me frederick augustus washington bailey, but id already dropped the middle names back in maryland. left baltimore as stanley, switched to frederick johnson in new york, thought id stick with that. but new bedford was crawling with johnsons. so, i let mr. johnson suggest a name, but said frederick stays. he just finished "lady of the lake" and said douglass. so frederick douglass it is, and that's the name i keep.

new bedford wasn't what i expected. thought the north was rough compared to the southern slaveholders' luxury. expected poor, hard-living folks since they didn't own slaves. figured wealth and refinement needed slaves. so i thought. but the place was buzzing with wealth—ships everywhere, granite warehouses packed with goods. people working quietly, focused, like they cared about their jobs, no yelling or cursing like in baltimore. wandered the town, saw churches, houses, gardens—wealth and taste i'd never seen in slaveholding maryland. eye-opener, for sure.

everything looked spotless and fresh. didn't see any run-down houses or folks barely scraping by. no kids running around half-dressed or women with no shoes like back in Hillsborough, Easton, St. Michael's, and Baltimore. folks here seemed stronger, healthier, happier than in Maryland. for once, seeing wealth didn't come with the sting of poverty. but what really caught me was the state of the colored folks. lots of them, like me, had made it here, escaping those hunting us down. met folks who hadn't been free for seven years living better than most slaveholders in Maryland. take my friend Mr. Nathan Johnson. man was a lifesaver — fed me when i was starving, took me in when i had nowhere to go. his house was tidier, meals better, read more papers, understood the world more than nine out of ten slaveholders in Talbot County. still, he was a working man. hands rough from labor, and same with Mrs. Johnson. the spirit here was something else, a real commitment to keep each other safe from the blood-thirsty kidnappers. heard a story soon after i got here. a fugitive was threatened by another colored man to be given up. they called a meeting — “Business of importance!” — and decided to deal with him. an old religious gentleman led the meeting, suggested taking the guy out and dealing with him for good. folks charged at him, but some stopped it, and the betrayer got away. hasn't been seen since. no more threats like that, and if there are, bet they'd handle it for sure.

got a job three days after i showed up, loading oil onto a sloop. dirty work, tough too, but man i was all in, heart and hands. finally, my own boss. no more Master Hugh taking my pay. the work felt... different. i was doing it for me, for my new wife. a new start. after that, tried to get a calking gig, but the white guys wouldn't work with me. too much prejudice. so, ditched the calking gear and grabbed whatever work i could. Mr. Johnson helped out, let me use his wood-horse and saw. i sawed wood, shoveled coal, swept chimneys, rolled oil casks. three years in New Bedford doing that before folks knew me in the anti-slavery circles.

four months in, a guy asked if i wanted the "Liberator" paper. i did, but just escaped slavery, couldn't pay then. eventually subscribed. read it every week. can't really describe how it hit me, like fuel for the soul. it was all fire and truth, calling out slaveholders, exposing slavery, and it lit me up.

didn't take long reading the "Liberator" to catch the anti-slavery vibe. jumped in, did what i could. happiest at those meetings, even if i didn't speak much. others said it better. but at an anti-slavery convention in Nantucket, August 1841, Mr. William C. Coffin pushed me to speak. felt like a big ask, but i did. started slow, nervous, but then it came, and i got through it. been speaking up for my brothers since. how well, i'll let others say.

been thinking about this. reading back, i might sound like im against all religion. im not. what i said targets the slaveholding religion here, not Christianity itself. there's a massive gap between the two. one’s pure and peaceful, the other's corrupt and hypocritical. i love the Christianity of Christ, but i hate the version here. it’s a fraud, a lie dressed in holy robes.

look around: ministers who steal men, missionaries who whip women. the same hands that beat you preach on Sundays. the guy who takes my wages wants to guide me to salvation. they sell people to build churches and call it God’s work. everything's twisted. church bells and auction bells ring together, praising while breaking. religion and robbery are allies here. devils in angel costumes.

“just God, these are your priests?
men who preach and kidnap?
who bolt the doors of liberty on the poor?”

what's really going on here? folks claiming to follow Christ but chaining people down. call it what it is, a bunch of hypocrites. they act like they’re doing good, but they're just keeping up appearances. all for the show, none for the soul. they’re like those old scribes and pharisees, more worried about looking pious than doing right.

this so-called Christianity in America is a mess. they’ll judge a sheep-stealer but turn a blind eye to a man-stealer, then call me an infidel for pointing it out. they're all about the rituals and none about the real stuff like mercy and justice. they send missions across the world but ignore the suffering next door. what kind of faith is that?

so, let’s be clear. when i talk about the religion of this land, i'm talking about those so-called Christian churches that are okay with slavery. that's the religion i’m calling out.

i'll wrap this up by sharing a raw look at southern religion back in the day. this was penned by a northern methodist preacher who got a firsthand view of how things really went down with slaveholding. it's not exaggerated, just the truth. "shall i not visit for these things? saith the lord. shall not my soul be avenged on such a nation as this?"

a parody

"come listen up, saints and sinners, let me tell
how holy men whip jack and nell,
buy women, sell kids, all while preaching hell,
and sing about heavenly union.

"they'll bleat and baa like goats,
choke on black sheep, then strain at motes,
wear fine coats, grab throats,
all for heavenly union.

"they'll church you for a dram,
damn you over a lamb;
but robbing tony, doll, and sam
of rights and bread, that’s heavenly union.

"they talk christ's reward,
bind his image with cord,
lash the lord’s own, sell them aboard,
handcuffed to heavenly union.

"they read, sing songs, pray long,
teach the right, do the wrong,
welcome the throng
with heavenly union words.

"wonder they can sing,
praise the lord, then swing,
whip, sting, cling
to slaves in guilty union.

"they raise crops, cheat, lie,
lay treasures high
by making the whip fly,
hoping for heavenly union.

"they crack skulls, preach loud,
seize wool, pull proud
for heavenly union.

"a man-thief living on meat,
won’t help the needy,
but loves heavenly union.

"'love not the world,' he said,
winked, shook his head;
seized tom, dick, and ned,
cut their bread, still loved union.

"another preacher spoke
of one with a heart broke;
tied nanny to oak,
prayed for heavenly union.

"two others, iron jaws,
waved stealing paws;
their kids, all gewgaws,
stint backs, maws, keep union.

"all good from jack they take,
entertain flirts, rakes,
dress snakes, cram cakes;
that's union."

hoping this little book shines a light on the slave system and speeds up freedom for my brothers in chains. i trust truth, love, justice to help my efforts. i pledge again to the cause.

frederick douglass.

lynn, mass., april 28, 1845. the end.
