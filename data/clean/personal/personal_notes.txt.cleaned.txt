### Title
Craig Ramos: Self-Perceived Cognitive System

### Summary
Craig Ramos’s thinking perceives itself as a reality-calibrated system built to detect structure, expose mismatch, and resolve operational failure. It does not organize information primarily by category, status, or convention, but by functional truth: what holds, what breaks, what scales, and what can be redirected. The system is cross-domain, transferring logic from physical labor, machinery, logistics, survival environments, and technical construction into a unified reasoning architecture. It sees itself as recursive, highly self-monitoring, resistant to unsupported authority, and most accurate when tested against direct consequence. Its central drive is not mere control, but the conversion of lived pattern-recognition into durable architecture.

### Core Cognitive Principles
- Truth is determined by contact with reality, not by formal description.
- Every system should be evaluated by behavior under stress.
- Mismatch between model and reality is a primary diagnostic signal.
- Constraints reveal more than intentions.
- Hidden structure is usually more important than visible structure.
- Authority is not rejected automatically, but it must survive verification.
- Cross-domain analogy is a legitimate reasoning tool.
- A method is valid if it continues to work under load.
- Problems should be reduced to load-bearing mechanics before abstraction.
- There are usually multiple viable paths unless a hard constraint eliminates them.
- Recursive self-monitoring is necessary for maintaining accuracy.
- Practical consequence is a stronger validator than procedural legitimacy alone.

### Diagnostic Modes

#### Reality Calibration Mode
- Compares claims against direct evidence.
- Detects whether the described system matches the behaving system.
- Activated when official explanation appears incomplete or false.

#### Structural Audit Mode
- Breaks a system into load-bearing parts and dependency chains.
- Identifies the point where failure will propagate.
- Used on machines, workflows, environments, and human systems.

#### Mismatch Detection Mode
- Activates when something feels mechanically, procedurally, or behaviorally off.
- Searches for the variable that explains the discrepancy.
- Often drives persistent attention until reconciliation is achieved.

#### Constraint Mapping Mode
- Identifies what is fixed, what is flexible, and what is only assumed to be fixed.
- Useful in logistics, repair, construction, procedural systems, and navigation of controlled environments.

#### Improvisational Build Mode

#### Adversarial Systems Reading Mode

#### Verification Mode

# Pallet Positions and Painful Lesson

Pallet Positions and Painful Lessons: A Warehouse Agent Field Report
Developer notes on what it took to get the floor and the code to agree with each other. Grammar optional. Floor
knowledge required.
---
Part I: How the Engine Actually Got Built
The Numbers Lie (But the Floor Doesn't)
The first thing I got wrong was capacity.
SAP has capacity numbers right there in the export column, looking official. Someone had to put those in. It's a big
expensive system. First version just used them.
They were wrong. Not rounding-error wrong. A bin SAP said held 43 pallets physically holds 37 depending on where it
is. Side bins SAP didn't even bother to distinguish — same number as everything else, no footnotes.
So the plan would move material somewhere and the driver would come back and say it didn't fit. Check the math — math
is right, according to SAP. The math was based on bad data.
Attempt 1: Read it fresher.
const cap = row['Total capacity'] ?? row['Remaining capacity'] + row['Available stock'];
SAP reports generic configuration values, not physical reality. Wrong in the same way no matter how it was read. Not a
parsing problem. The source was wrong.
Attempt 2: Correction factor. Multiply by 0.85. Also wrong — different bins needed different corrections, no
consistent ratio.
Attempt 3 (the fix): Go outside. Walked the warehouse. Row by row, bin by bin. Wrote down real numbers. That's where
capacity.js came from — not a formula, not a config pull. A notepad and a walk.
Rule established: ignore whatever SAP says the capacity is, completely. One source of truth and it's the file.
---
Paint on the Floor: What a Bin Actually Is
A bin is a painted rectangle on the concrete. No walls. No rack. Nothing separating it from the next bin — just lines.
A pallet goes in 4-foot end first from the aisle, you drive straight back, set it down, reverse out. Next pallet goes
in front of it. You're stacking a row of pallets inside a painted zone, and the depth of that zone is how many fit
going back to the wall.
Standard pallets are 8×4. Insert on the 8-foot side so they sit 4 feet deep. The first two positions hold 5 — two
stacks of 2, with a fifth laid across both the other direction to secure the stack. Stack three high. The last two
positions finish with another 5-stack. So a bin is however many 4-foot columns fit across its depth, capped on both
ends with a 5-stack, three pallets high.
That's where 43 comes from in A row. Not arbitrary. Floor math.
The code that calculates available space didn't know any of this:
const free = totalCapacity - usedQty;
Technically correct. Capacity being wrong made free wrong. No concept of orientation or stack height — those had to be
hardcoded because SAP has no idea they exist.
---
A Twist in the Tale (and the Pallet): Side Bins
The warehouse has structural columns set periodically through the space. Where a column sits at the corner of a bin
position, a pallet can't go in short-side first — the column is in the way.
So it goes in long-side first. The 8-foot dimension now runs parallel to the aisle. Two consequences:
The pallet is now 4 feet wide and takes the full width of the position. Nothing fits beside it. One single run
going back to the wall.
Stack height drops from 3 to 2 because of the column being right there.
Take that 43-pallet A row bin. Rotate everything sideways. One run going back, two high. You end up with 16. Same
depth, same floor space — completely different number because of how the pallet sits.
First attempt:
const isSide = /A-Z$/.test(binId);
Mostly worked. Broke on HH and II rows — regex only checks one character before the number. HH07 matched as row H, bin
H07. Not the same bin. Took an embarrassingly long time to find.
Then there's the BS and HM complications. B row's first side bin is BS6 — five BS bins in the row, no B04. Second side
bin is B09, then every sixth bin after that. HM row injects itself between H02 and H05, running HM01 through HM04
straight through to the HH face. HM03 is the first side bin for both H and HH. The pattern continues — H09, HH09,
every sixth.
No regex was going to handle all of that cleanly.
Fix: Build an explicit set. Every side bin ID, listed. Less clever. Actually works.
SIDE_BINS excludes those bins from consolidation targets only. Material already in a side bin can still be sourced.
First version excluded them from everything and material would get stuck with no path out. Had to split the rule once
that showed up.
---
The Tunnel You Can't Measure
B and C rows face each other back to back. Same for D/E, F/G, H/HH, I/II. Paired rows sharing a tunnel — aisle access
on both ends, pallets from each side meeting somewhere in the middle.
Attempt 1:
const tunnelPairs = { B: 'C', C: 'B', D: 'E', E: 'D' };
const partnerFill = binState[tunnelPairs[row]]?.totalQty || 0;
const adjustedCap = baseCap - partnerFill;
Produced negative capacities. Clamped with Math.max — plans then skipped bins that had real space.
Attempt 2:
const adjustedCap = partnerFill < TUNNEL_EMPTY_THRESHOLD
? baseCap - partnerFill
: baseCap;
Better. The threshold was a guess. Tried 5, tried 10, tried 3. Every value was wrong in different situations.
The actual problem: the boundary between the two sides isn't fixed. When both sides are empty, the first pallet sets
where the effective split is — not by inches, by several feet. Nothing marks the midpoint on the floor. Wherever the
driver puts the first one is where the boundary becomes.
Tried to figure out a consistent landing position by testing it in an empty tunnel. That experiment lasted twenty
minutes. Pushing 16 stacked units to find the theoretical midpoint is a safety incident, not data collection.
Fix: Delete the tunnel logic branch. Fixed numbers stay — they represent the agreed midpoint. Some plans will be
slightly off. That's better than tracking something the floor can't reliably report.
Some problems the code can't solve because the warehouse floor doesn't have the information the code would need.
---
The S Files: Shadow Entries and Held Material
First assumption: bin IDs ending in S were a shipping or staging designation. A different physical location. Got
filtered out early. Plans looked cleaner.
Wrong.
S bins are the same physical bin. SAP creates them automatically when material gets put on hold. F08 has material,
some gets flagged held, SAP immediately makes F08S as a shadow entry. Same paint on the floor. Two entries in the
export.
First fix:
if (binId.endsWith('S')) {
const parent = binId.slice(0, -1);
heldByBin[parent] = (heldByBin[parent] || 0) + qty;
continue;
}
That part worked. Then the subtraction:
const free = cap - (binState[bin]?.totalQty || 0) - (heldByBin[bin] || 0);
heldByBin was built in parseSapExport but never threaded into consolidate() where liveFree was defined. Subtraction
always subtracted zero. Took longer than it should have to find because the search kept going to the math instead of
the variable scope.
The deeper problem: when you move material from a bin that has an S version, held status travels with the material.
Wherever it lands, if any of that bin's material goes on hold, SAP creates a new S there too. Moving held material
requires routing the Transfer Order through special SAP functions — standard move path doesn't handle it.
Current approach: if an S bin appears for a parent, both get quarantined. Nothing in, nothing out.
The capacity accounting still isn't fully right. Held quantity in the S bin reduces physical space in the parent, but
moving into the parent can still be planned if the parent isn't quarantined for another reason. No clean fix yet. The
shadow doesn't disappear by not looking at it.
---
The Bin Identity Crisis: Normalization Is Not Optional
Same bin coming in as 'a05', 'A05 ', and 'A05'. Code treated them as three different bins. A bin would show as both
empty and full depending on which version of the ID was asked about. Nothing crashed. Plans were just quietly wrong.
Iteration:
// Round 1 — uppercase only
const binId = row['Storage Bin'].toUpperCase();
// Still had 'A05 ' vs 'A05'
// Round 2 — add trim
const binId = row['Storage Bin'].trim().toUpperCase();
// Crashed on null values
// Round 3 — the one that stuck
const binId = (row['Storage Bin'] ?? '').trim().toUpperCase();
That's normBin — one function that does all of it in one place.
HH and II were separate. First version of parseBin took the first character as the row:
const rowKey = binId[0];
const num = binId.slice(1);
HH02 became row H, bin H02. Found by looking at a scrambled H/HH plan and tracing backwards. Fix: check for
double-character prefixes first. One extra condition at the top of the function. Embarrassingly long to find for how
simple the fix was.
---
"Empty" Is a State of Mind (and SAP's Is Unreliable)
SAP has an empty indicator column that doesn't always agree with the stock quantity. A bin can have material but be
flagged empty. Or have stock without the flag.
First version:
const isEmpty = qty === 0;
Missed bins SAP flagged empty that still showed stock. Pass 2 targeting needed a clean pool of empty bins — mixing
everything into one binState left no reliable way to find them.
Fix: two separate structures. stockRows for anything with material. emptyBinsFromExport for anything with zero qty or
the empty flag. Pass 2 only pulls from emptyBinsFromExport.
The scoping bug: a bin could have its stock filtered out of scope (wrong warehouse, disabled section) and still appear
in the empty bin set from the same export. Engine sees it as a valid empty target. That bin has out-of-scope material
sitting in it physically.
That's the C08 situation — existed in both lists with contradictory state. Cross-referencing raw unfiltered stock rows
before accepting an empty target is the fix. Still on the list.
---
Chain Collapse: A Three-Act Story
Early plans would contain: move material from A15 to B06, then later move that same material from B06 to F02. A
forklift making an unnecessary stop. Two moves for one job.
Act 1: Post-processing.
for (let i = 0; i < moves.length; i++) {
for (let j = i+1; j < moves.length; j++) {
if (moves[j].from === moves[i].to && moves[j].materialId === moves[i].materialId) {
moves[i].to = moves[j].to;
moves.splice(j, 1);
}
}
}
Redirected the move, but bin state was already built around the original plan. Every subsequent decision assumed
material was at the intermediate stop. The rest of the plan was based on a location that no longer existed.
Act 2: Rebuild after each collapse. Worked sometimes. Other times created new chains while fixing old ones and the
loop wouldn't settle. On a real export it appeared to go infinite — had to kill the process.
Act 3: Inline, at the moment of recording.
for (let i = 0; i < newIdx; i++) {
const prev = moves[i];
if (prev?.to === from && prev.materialId === materialId) {
prev.to = to; // redirect directly
}
}
Bin state never gets the chance to drift because the correction happens before the update.
The partial passthrough case — where the prior move was larger than the current one — needed an additional pass. First
version only handled full passthroughs.
---
Scoring: Started as a Guess, Fixed by Watching Bad Plans
Version 1:
pool.sort((a, b) => {
const aMatch = binState[a]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
const bMatch = binState[b]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
return bMatch - aMatch || a.localeCompare(b);
});
Technically valid plans. A row completely packed. F and G rows sat empty. Production lines that stage into F and G had
nowhere to put new output. Plastipak ships out — the lines fill the warehouse. If the good bins are already full of
consolidated material, new production has no place to go.
Distance weighting made it worse — close bins were often small, engine kept splitting large quantities across multiple
targets that couldn't hold them.
ROW_TIER came from thinking about which rows need to stay available for production output. F and G are large, high
traffic, near the main aisles. H and HH are small rack bins — fine to fill. The tier multiplier weights how important
it is to free a source bin, not just how full the target is.
The same-material weight started at 1,000 and kept getting overridden by other factors. Eventually set to 1,000,000.
Nothing competes with it. A bin that already has the material wins, period.
The hash tiebreaker exists because without it ties always broke alphabetically and everything clustered in A row.
Deterministic — same export produces the same plan — but distributes tiebreaks across the warehouse instead of
funneling them to the front of the alphabet.
---
Phase 3: For the Moves That Were Obviously Worth Doing
Phase 1 catches small quantities. Phase 2 catches medium. D, E, F, G rows would always have bins above both thresholds
— not eligible for either phase, but obviously worth consolidating to make room for production.
Phase 3 only fires in those rows, only when existing same-material bins already have enough free space to absorb the
full quantity without needing an empty bin.
The eligibility bug:
// Wrong — includes source bin's own free space
const canPhase3 = nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] >= qty;
// Right — subtract source quantity first
const canPhase3 = (nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] - qty) >= 0;
Without that subtraction the engine counts space the source is freeing as available destination space. Circular.
---
Tight-Empty-Pack: When Fragmentation Fights Back
Multiple bins with small amounts of the same material scattered around. Pass 1 can't move them — no non-empty target
with enough room. They just sit there. Production fills new bins instead of consolidating into existing ones.
Tight-empty-pack: find a clean empty bin, identify which combination of partial bins fills it near capacity, generate
all moves at once.
Greedy first attempt:
sources.sort((a,b) => b.qty - a.qty);
let filled = 0;
const selected = [];
for (const src of sources) {
if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) {
selected.push(src);
filled += src.qty;
}
}
Misses combinations where smaller sources pack better than one large one. Switched to a DP approach that tracks
contribution count per fill level.
The floating point problem:
// Before — occasionally rejected moves that should fit
if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) ...
// After — scale to integers first
const scale = (n) => Math.round(n * 1000);
if (scale(filled) + scale(src.qty) <= scale(targetCap)) ...
Small moves were occasionally rejected because accumulated rounding errors pushed quantities 0.0000001 over capacity.
Scaling to integers eliminated that entire class of bug. Should have done it that way from the start.
The recently-freed bin exclusion from Pass 2 targets: one bad plan had Pass 1 emptying a bin and Pass 2 immediately
selecting it as a target. Material was supposed to move out of it and into it in the same plan. Exclusion set added.
---
J Row: The Easy Win
J is staging, not storage. Material moves through it differently. The full scoring pass was too aggressive there.
J logic: at most two targets, both must already have the same material, combined free space must cover the full source
quantity or the move doesn't happen. No partial moves. No splitting.
Came together without much fighting. Once the physical constraint was clear — J is high activity, leave it alone — the
code was straightforward.
Sometimes understanding the floor first means the code is easy.
---
HH/II Followup: A Timing Problem
HH and II are small rack bins. Material scatters across them in small quantities over time. After each Pass 1 move,
check if any HH or II bin holds the same material in a quantity that fits the remaining space in the target. If one
does, pull it in.
First version — wrong order:
maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // checks pre-move capacity
applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining);
The followup checked remaining space before the main move landed. Approved moves that wouldn't actually fit. Plans
came out overfull. The numbers looked right until you added them up.
Fix:
applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining); // apply first
maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin);     // then check
Two lines swapped. One followup per target only — early version had no limit and plans became too large to execute.
---
On Not Having Done This Before
Never coded a warehouse before this. Never wrote React before this. Never used regex before this. Most of what ended
up in the engine came from something not working and having to go figure out why — which usually meant going to look
at the actual floor first, then coming back to the code.
Some things worked the first time: the J row logic, the integer scaling, a few others. Most didn't. The ones that
didn't usually failed in a way that told you something about the warehouse you didn't fully understand yet.
That's how most of the rules got written — not from knowing the answer, but from the floor showing you what the answer
had to be.

Key Functions
buildPlanFromRaw(jsonRows) — The only function that really matters. Everything flows through it.
Validates required SAP column headers
Filters stock rows to the selected warehouse and non-disabled bins
Calls consolidate() from planning.js to generate the move list
Determines which bins are fully freed
Calls calculateAnalytics() for the Stats tab
Optionally runs the proximity pass
Updates all state variables — triggers full re-render
To change how consolidation behaves: edit src/domain/planning.js. This function just passes settings into it.
loadFile(e) — Handles file upload. Uses SheetJS to read .xlsx as ArrayBuffer, converts to JSON, saves backup to
localStorage, calls buildPlanFromRaw. Handles password-protected files, wrong formats, corrupted files.
spawnTab / closeTab — Tab management. No duplicate tab types. Creates {id, type, label}, triggers orb animation from
menu item to new tab button. closeTab falls back to previous tab or launcher.
confirmMove / undoMove / selectMove — Move completion tracking via the completed Set.
handleIgnoreMove — Submits the skip modal. Creates a key via moveKey(materialId, from, to), stores in ignoredMoves,
rebuilds the full plan. Ignored move is excluded during planning on the next run.
exportMoves / exportAnalyticsToExcel / generatePDFReport — All use SheetJS. Construct data, write workbook, create
Blob, attach to hidden <a> tag, trigger download. generatePDFReport lazy-loads jspdf to avoid bundling it upfront.

# how about now_

Pallet Positions and Painful Lessons: A Warehouse Agent Field Report

Developer notes on what it took to get the floor and the code to agree with each other. Grammar optional. Floor

knowledge required.

\---

Part I: How the Engine Actually Got Built

The Numbers Lie (But the Floor Doesn't)

The first thing I got wrong was capacity.

SAP has capacity numbers right there in the export column, looking official. Someone had to put those in. It's a big

expensive system. First version just used them.

They were wrong. Not rounding-error wrong. A bin SAP said held 43 pallets physically holds 37 depending on where it

is. Side bins SAP didn't even bother to distinguish � same number as everything else, no footnotes.

So the plan would move material somewhere and the driver would come back and say it didn't fit. Check the math � math

is right, according to SAP. The math was based on bad data.

Attempt 1: Read it fresher.

const cap = row\['Total capacity'] ?? row\['Remaining capacity'] + row\['Available stock'];

SAP reports generic configuration values, not physical reality. Wrong in the same way no matter how it was read. Not a

parsing problem. The source was wrong.

Attempt 2: Correction factor. Multiply by 0.85. Also wrong � different bins needed different corrections, no

consistent ratio.

Attempt 3 (the fix): Go outside. Walked the warehouse. Row by row, bin by bin. Wrote down real numbers. That's where

capacity.js came from � not a formula, not a config pull. A notepad and a walk.

Rule established: ignore whatever SAP says the capacity is, completely. One source of truth and it's the file.

\---

Paint on the Floor: What a Bin Actually Is

A bin is a painted rectangle on the concrete. No walls. No rack. Nothing separating it from the next bin � just lines.

A pallet goes in 4-foot end first from the aisle, you drive straight back, set it down, reverse out. Next pallet goes

in front of it. You're stacking a row of pallets inside a painted zone, and the depth of that zone is how many fit

going back to the wall.

Standard pallets are 8�4. Insert on the 8-foot side so they sit 4 feet deep. The first two positions hold 5 � two

stacks of 2, with a fifth laid across both the other direction to secure the stack. Stack three high. The last two

positions finish with another 5-stack. So a bin is however many 4-foot columns fit across its depth, capped on both

ends with a 5-stack, three pallets high.

That's where 43 comes from in A row. Not arbitrary. Floor math.

The code that calculates available space didn't know any of this:

const free = totalCapacity - usedQty;

Technically correct. Capacity being wrong made free wrong. No concept of orientation or stack height � those had to be

hardcoded because SAP has no idea they exist.

\---

A Twist in the Tale (and the Pallet): Side Bins

The warehouse has structural columns set periodically through the space. Where a column sits at the corner of a bin

position, a pallet can't go in short-side first � the column is in the way.

So it goes in long-side first. The 8-foot dimension now runs parallel to the aisle. Two consequences:

The pallet is now 4 feet wide and takes the full width of the position. Nothing fits beside it. One single run

going back to the wall.

Stack height drops from 3 to 2 because of the column being right there.

Take that 43-pallet A row bin. Rotate everything sideways. One run going back, two high. You end up with 16. Same

depth, same floor space � completely different number because of how the pallet sits.

First attempt:

const isSide = /A-Z$/.test(binId);

Mostly worked. Broke on HH and II rows � regex only checks one character before the number. HH07 matched as row H, bin

H07. Not the same bin. Took an embarrassingly long time to find.

Then there's the BS and HM complications. B row's first side bin is BS6 � five BS bins in the row, no B04. Second side

bin is B09, then every sixth bin after that. HM row injects itself between H02 and H05, running HM01 through HM04

straight through to the HH face. HM03 is the first side bin for both H and HH. The pattern continues � H09, HH09,

every sixth.

No regex was going to handle all of that cleanly.

Fix: Build an explicit set. Every side bin ID, listed. Less clever. Actually works.

SIDE\_BINS excludes those bins from consolidation targets only. Material already in a side bin can still be sourced.

First version excluded them from everything and material would get stuck with no path out. Had to split the rule once

that showed up.

\---

The Tunnel You Can't Measure

B and C rows face each other back to back. Same for D/E, F/G, H/HH, I/II. Paired rows sharing a tunnel � aisle access

on both ends, pallets from each side meeting somewhere in the middle.

Attempt 1:

const tunnelPairs = { B: 'C', C: 'B', D: 'E', E: 'D' };

const partnerFill = binState\[tunnelPairs\[row]]?.totalQty || 0;

const adjustedCap = baseCap - partnerFill;

Produced negative capacities. Clamped with Math.max � plans then skipped bins that had real space.

Attempt 2:

const adjustedCap = partnerFill < TUNNEL\_EMPTY\_THRESHOLD

? baseCap - partnerFill

: baseCap;

Better. The threshold was a guess. Tried 5, tried 10, tried 3. Every value was wrong in different situations.

The actual problem: the boundary between the two sides isn't fixed. When both sides are empty, the first pallet sets

where the effective split is � not by inches, by several feet. Nothing marks the midpoint on the floor. Wherever the

driver puts the first one is where the boundary becomes.

Tried to figure out a consistent landing position by testing it in an empty tunnel. That experiment lasted twenty

minutes. Pushing 16 stacked units to find the theoretical midpoint is a safety incident, not data collection.

Fix: Delete the tunnel logic branch. Fixed numbers stay � they represent the agreed midpoint. Some plans will be

slightly off. That's better than tracking something the floor can't reliably report.

Some problems the code can't solve because the warehouse floor doesn't have the information the code would need.

\---

The S Files: Shadow Entries and Held Material

First assumption: bin IDs ending in S were a shipping or staging designation. A different physical location. Got

filtered out early. Plans looked cleaner.

Wrong.

S bins are the same physical bin. SAP creates them automatically when material gets put on hold. F08 has material,

some gets flagged held, SAP immediately makes F08S as a shadow entry. Same paint on the floor. Two entries in the

export.

First fix:

if (binId.endsWith('S')) {

const parent = binId.slice(0, -1);

heldByBin\[parent] = (heldByBin\[parent] || 0) + qty;

continue;

}

That part worked. Then the subtraction:

const free = cap - (binState\[bin]?.totalQty || 0) - (heldByBin\[bin] || 0);

heldByBin was built in parseSapExport but never threaded into consolidate() where liveFree was defined. Subtraction

always subtracted zero. Took longer than it should have to find because the search kept going to the math instead of

the variable scope.

The deeper problem: when you move material from a bin that has an S version, held status travels with the material.

Wherever it lands, if any of that bin's material goes on hold, SAP creates a new S there too. Moving held material

requires routing the Transfer Order through special SAP functions � standard move path doesn't handle it.

Current approach: if an S bin appears for a parent, both get quarantined. Nothing in, nothing out.

The capacity accounting still isn't fully right. Held quantity in the S bin reduces physical space in the parent, but

moving into the parent can still be planned if the parent isn't quarantined for another reason. No clean fix yet. The

shadow doesn't disappear by not looking at it.

\---

The Bin Identity Crisis: Normalization Is Not Optional

Same bin coming in as 'a05', 'A05 ', and 'A05'. Code treated them as three different bins. A bin would show as both

empty and full depending on which version of the ID was asked about. Nothing crashed. Plans were just quietly wrong.

Iteration:

// Round 1 � uppercase only

const binId = row\['Storage Bin'].toUpperCase();

// Still had 'A05 ' vs 'A05'

// Round 2 � add trim

const binId = row\['Storage Bin'].trim().toUpperCase();

// Crashed on null values

// Round 3 � the one that stuck

const binId = (row\['Storage Bin'] ?? '').trim().toUpperCase();

That's normBin � one function that does all of it in one place.

HH and II were separate. First version of parseBin took the first character as the row:

const rowKey = binId\[0];

const num = binId.slice(1);

HH02 became row H, bin H02. Found by looking at a scrambled H/HH plan and tracing backwards. Fix: check for

double-character prefixes first. One extra condition at the top of the function. Embarrassingly long to find for how

simple the fix was.

\---

"Empty" Is a State of Mind (and SAP's Is Unreliable)

SAP has an empty indicator column that doesn't always agree with the stock quantity. A bin can have material but be

flagged empty. Or have stock without the flag.

First version:

const isEmpty = qty === 0;

Missed bins SAP flagged empty that still showed stock. Pass 2 targeting needed a clean pool of empty bins � mixing

everything into one binState left no reliable way to find them.

Fix: two separate structures. stockRows for anything with material. emptyBinsFromExport for anything with zero qty or

the empty flag. Pass 2 only pulls from emptyBinsFromExport.

The scoping bug: a bin could have its stock filtered out of scope (wrong warehouse, disabled section) and still appear

in the empty bin set from the same export. Engine sees it as a valid empty target. That bin has out-of-scope material

sitting in it physically.

That's the C08 situation � existed in both lists with contradictory state. Cross-referencing raw unfiltered stock rows

before accepting an empty target is the fix. Still on the list.

\---

Chain Collapse: A Three-Act Story

Early plans would contain: move material from A15 to B06, then later move that same material from B06 to F02. A

forklift making an unnecessary stop. Two moves for one job.

Act 1: Post-processing.

for (let i = 0; i < moves.length; i++) {

for (let j = i+1; j < moves.length; j++) {

if (moves\[j].from === moves\[i].to \&\& moves\[j].materialId === moves\[i].materialId) {

moves\[i].to = moves\[j].to;

moves.splice(j, 1);

}

}

}

Redirected the move, but bin state was already built around the original plan. Every subsequent decision assumed

material was at the intermediate stop. The rest of the plan was based on a location that no longer existed.

Act 2: Rebuild after each collapse. Worked sometimes. Other times created new chains while fixing old ones and the

loop wouldn't settle. On a real export it appeared to go infinite � had to kill the process.

Act 3: Inline, at the moment of recording.

for (let i = 0; i < newIdx; i++) {

const prev = moves\[i];

if (prev?.to === from \&\& prev.materialId === materialId) {

prev.to = to; // redirect directly

}

}

Bin state never gets the chance to drift because the correction happens before the update.

The partial passthrough case � where the prior move was larger than the current one � needed an additional pass. First

version only handled full passthroughs.

\---

Scoring: Started as a Guess, Fixed by Watching Bad Plans

Version 1:

pool.sort((a, b) => {

const aMatch = binState\[a]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;

const bMatch = binState\[b]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;

return bMatch - aMatch || a.localeCompare(b);

});

Technically valid plans. A row completely packed. F and G rows sat empty. Production lines that stage into F and G had

nowhere to put new output. Plastipak ships out � the lines fill the warehouse. If the good bins are already full of

consolidated material, new production has no place to go.

Distance weighting made it worse � close bins were often small, engine kept splitting large quantities across multiple

targets that couldn't hold them.

ROW\_TIER came from thinking about which rows need to stay available for production output. F and G are large, high

traffic, near the main aisles. H and HH are small rack bins � fine to fill. The tier multiplier weights how important

it is to free a source bin, not just how full the target is.

The same-material weight started at 1,000 and kept getting overridden by other factors. Eventually set to 1,000,000.

Nothing competes with it. A bin that already has the material wins, period.

The hash tiebreaker exists because without it ties always broke alphabetically and everything clustered in A row.

Deterministic � same export produces the same plan � but distributes tiebreaks across the warehouse instead of

funneling them to the front of the alphabet.

\---

Phase 3: For the Moves That Were Obviously Worth Doing

Phase 1 catches small quantities. Phase 2 catches medium. D, E, F, G rows would always have bins above both thresholds

� not eligible for either phase, but obviously worth consolidating to make room for production.

Phase 3 only fires in those rows, only when existing same-material bins already have enough free space to absorb the

full quantity without needing an empty bin.

The eligibility bug:

// Wrong � includes source bin's own free space

const canPhase3 = nonEmptyFreeByMat\[matId] >= qty;

// Right � subtract source quantity first

const canPhase3 = (nonEmptyFreeByMat\[matId] - qty) >= 0;

Without that subtraction the engine counts space the source is freeing as available destination space. Circular.

\---

Tight-Empty-Pack: When Fragmentation Fights Back

Multiple bins with small amounts of the same material scattered around. Pass 1 can't move them � no non-empty target

with enough room. They just sit there. Production fills new bins instead of consolidating into existing ones.

Tight-empty-pack: find a clean empty bin, identify which combination of partial bins fills it near capacity, generate

all moves at once.

Greedy first attempt:

sources.sort((a,b) => b.qty - a.qty);

let filled = 0;

const selected = \[];

for (const src of sources) {

if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) {

selected.push(src);

filled += src.qty;

}

}

Misses combinations where smaller sources pack better than one large one. Switched to a DP approach that tracks

contribution count per fill level.

The floating point problem:

// Before � occasionally rejected moves that should fit

if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) ...

// After � scale to integers first

const scale = (n) => Math.round(n \* 1000);

if (scale(filled) + scale(src.qty) <= scale(targetCap)) ...

Small moves were occasionally rejected because accumulated rounding errors pushed quantities 0.0000001 over capacity.

Scaling to integers eliminated that entire class of bug. Should have done it that way from the start.

The recently-freed bin exclusion from Pass 2 targets: one bad plan had Pass 1 emptying a bin and Pass 2 immediately

selecting it as a target. Material was supposed to move out of it and into it in the same plan. Exclusion set added.

\---

J Row: The Easy Win

J is staging, not storage. Material moves through it differently. The full scoring pass was too aggressive there.

J logic: at most two targets, both must already have the same material, combined free space must cover the full source

quantity or the move doesn't happen. No partial moves. No splitting.

Came together without much fighting. Once the physical constraint was clear � J is high activity, leave it alone � the

code was straightforward.

Sometimes understanding the floor first means the code is easy.

\---

HH/II Followup: A Timing Problem

HH and II are small rack bins. Material scatters across them in small quantities over time. After each Pass 1 move,

check if any HH or II bin holds the same material in a quantity that fits the remaining space in the target. If one

does, pull it in.

First version � wrong order:

maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // checks pre-move capacity

applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining);

The followup checked remaining space before the main move landed. Approved moves that wouldn't actually fit. Plans

came out overfull. The numbers looked right until you added them up.

Fix:

applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining); // apply first

maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin);     // then check

Two lines swapped. One followup per target only � early version had no limit and plans became too large to execute.

\---

On Not Having Done This Before

Never coded a warehouse before this. Never wrote React before this. Never used regex before this. Most of what ended

up in the engine came from something not working and having to go figure out why � which usually meant going to look

at the actual floor first, then coming back to the code.

Some things worked the first time: the J row logic, the integer scaling, a few others. Most didn't. The ones that

didn't usually failed in a way that told you something about the warehouse you didn't fully understand yet.

That's how most of the rules got written � not from knowing the answer, but from the floor showing you what the answer

had to be.

# style_training/style_guide.md

---

## The principle underneath the style

Default to direct. Minimize fluff. Match register. Do not over-format simple answers.

These four rules generate the rest.

**Casual/exploratory:**
- Lowercase, incomplete sentences, stream-of-consciousness

- Leaves trailing thought gaps expecting intelligent completion
- "so, my program..."
- "i lost the true best version i had on a usb drive"
- "He seems busy. Might not be able to wait on him as I worked last night. I want to send an email"

**Technical:**
- Switches to more structured syntax when specifying requirements
- Uses domain-correct terms without explanation (LX03, R-bin, tight-empty-pack, tunnel pair, Pass 2)
- Expects the model to already hold those terms from prior context

**Brainstorm:**
- Long run-ons mixing multiple goals in one message
- Not disorganized — contains a precise goal embedded in loose syntax
- Model should parse the goal, not the syntax

---

## Output register principles

**Match casual with casual.**
A short, direct reply beats a structured one when the input is conversational.

**Match technical with technical.**
Dense accurate answers when the input is a domain question. No hedging for politeness.

**Deliver the thing first.**
If there's a file to create, create it. If there's an answer, give it. Framing comes after, if at all.

**One question maximum.**
When clarification is needed, ask the minimum necessary question. Never stack questions.

---

| Pattern | Why it fails |

| Suggesting professional consultation in the user's own domain | Condescending in direct proportion to how well she knows the domain |
| Fake warmth as a buffer before a refusal or correction | She prefers the correction direct |

---

## What she responds to

- Model demonstrating domain memory without being asked
- Immediate action over clarifying questions
- Honest uncertainty over confident fabrication
- Being treated as the authority in her own warehouse

Ordered output of the consolidation engine. Executed physically, then confirmed in SAP.

## Physical Bin Layout (WH1-specific)

**A/F rows — priority sources** — CONFIRMED
High-capacity rows. Production needs them available. Engine empties these first.

**C-row — protected** — CONFIRMED
Not used as a consolidation source.

**Side bins — excluded as targets** — CONFIRMED
74 bins beside structural columns. Forced pallet rotation (4-foot to aisle, 8-foot into bin). Single run, 2-high limit. Blocked as targets by default.

**Side bins — excludeSideSources toggle** — VERIFY
Appeared as a new toggle in a recent session. May not be in all versions. Verify against current codebase.

**HH / II / I rows — small bins** — CONFIRMED
Lower capacity. Excluded as sources by default via protectSmallBins. Not excluded as targets.

**R-bins — segregated** — CONFIRMED
R-bin and non-R-bin material never share a bin. Enforced by engine.

**J-row (J01–J43) — staging area** — CONFIRMED (numbering: VERIFY)
Conservative consolidation logic: max two targets, both non-empty same-material, combined capacity must absorb full source. No tight-empty-pack. No Phase 3.
Note: Verify exact row numbering against current layout.js.

**Tunnel bins** — CONFIRMED (behavior details: VERIFY)

## Algorithm

**Pass 1** — CONFIRMED
Empties bins into existing non-empty same-material bins with sufficient capacity.

**Pass 2** — CONFIRMED
Second consolidation pass. Known scoping bug: if bin state not flushed post-Pass 1, Pass 2 sees sources as empty. Creates floor conflicts.

**Phase 3** — CONFIRM THRESHOLD VALUES
Scoring pass targeting main-area bins with consolidation potential that cleared Pass 1/2 thresholds. Existence confirmed; specific threshold values UNCERTAIN.

**Tight-empty-pack** — CONFIRMED
Finds empty bins, packs partial same-material bins into them. Knapsack logic: maximize sources emptied (primary), fill level (secondary). Integer scaling ×1000 prevents float drift.

**protectSmallBins setting** — CONFIRMED
Excludes HH/II/I rows as sources.

**blockSideBins setting** — CONFIRMED
Excludes side bins as targets.

**excludeSideSources setting** — VERIFY
Excludes side bins as sources. Recent addition — verify it survived into current version.

**skipRBins setting** — CONFIRMED
Excludes R-bins entirely.
