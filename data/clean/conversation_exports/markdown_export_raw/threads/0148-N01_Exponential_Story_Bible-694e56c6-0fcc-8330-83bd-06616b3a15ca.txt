# Build chapter packets: 3–6 scenes per chapter with required fields.
def chapter_packet(ch_num, title, pov, tide_phase, scenes, soulpulse_budget="0 uses"):
    md = [f"# CH{ch_num:02d} — {title}",
          "",
          f"**POV:** {pov}",
          f"**Time:** {tide_phase}",
          f"**Soulpulse budget:** {soulpulse_budget}",
          "",
          "## Chapter objective",
          scenes.get("chapter_objective",""),
          "",
          "## Chapter constraints",
          "\n".join([f"- {c}" for c in scenes.get("constraints",[])]),
          "",
          "## Scene list (beat-by-beat)",
          ""]
    for i, sc in enumerate(scenes["scene_list"], start=1):
        md.extend([
            f"### Scene {i}: {sc['name']}",
            f"- **Location:** {sc['location']}",
            f"- **Objective:** {sc['objective']}",
            f"- **Obstacles:** " + "; ".join(sc.get("obstacles",[])),
            f"- **Props/tools:** " + ", ".join(sc.get("props",[])),
            f"- **Sensory palette:** " + "; ".join(sc.get("sensory",[])),
            f"- **Decision point:** {sc['decision']}",
            f"- **Fail-state if wrong:** {sc['fail_state']}",
            f"- **Exit hook:** {sc['hook']}",
            ""
        ])
    md.extend([
        "## End-state (must be true when chapter ends)",
        "\n".join([f"- {e}" for e in scenes.get("end_state",[])]),
        ""
    ])
    return "\n".join(md)

Warm. Slightly too warm. Not heat—pressure. The collar sat where it should, neat enough to fool a lazy eye. The tag read clean. The seal looked intact.

Jhace didn’t trust tags. He trusted seating.

## CHAPTER 2 — THE MEASURE OF A SKY  
**Tide 0 | Kindling**  
**POV: Tiffani**

Glass told the truth even when people didn’t.

It told it the hard way—through stress lines and inclusions and the cruel honesty of fracture. It didn’t care about foremen or pride. It cared about heat, form, and the weight you forced it to hold.

Tiffani wiped her hands on her apron and watched the light at the glass-house doorway warp.

Not much. A hair. Like the air had a bruise.

She stepped closer, squinting through a simple sighting frame she’d built from scrap rod and a bent bracket—nothing official, nothing sanctioned. Official instruments belonged to people who’d never had to make certainty out of scraps.

The warp drifted again, same direction, same angle, too consistent to be a trick of tired eyes.

She set the frame on the bench, pinned it with a weight, and took three readings—each one a mark on slate, each one a refusal to be dismissed as “nerves.”

Outside, the terrace market muttered. Rumors ran faster than water. Somebody had heard a sky-scream from far terraces. Somebody’s cousin said the horizon flashed copper-gold.

Tiffani didn’t chase rumors. She chased repeatable.

She crossed the shop, heat licking her knuckles as she passed the furnace mouth. The furnace breathed steady, but even that breath felt… wrong. Not weaker. Not stronger. Misaligned. Like the rhythm was holding back a beat it didn’t know how to place.

She pulled a thin strip of cooled glass from a rack—clear, clean, annealed right—and held it near the doorway light. The strip was supposed to be dead quiet. No stress. No pattern.

A faint ghost line shivered inside it.

Tiffani’s throat tightened.

That line wasn’t from her work. That line was from the air.

She laid the strip down and grabbed her slate. If she walked this to an office and said, “The light is wrong,” they’d pat her head and tell her to go pour pretty things.

If she walked it in with numbers, they’d ask who approved her numbers.

If she walked in with something that changed in their hands, they’d have to touch the truth.

She drew a fast grid. Not pretty. Functional. Two sighting marks, a baseline, a drift angle, a rough path. She didn’t pretend it was perfect. She just refused to pretend it was nothing.

Her marks suggested the same thing every time she recalculated: whatever was happening wasn’t “falling” into Saint Merro.

It was passing through.

Enter-side elsewhere. Exit-side consequences here.

That meant Saint Merro wouldn’t see the object first. Saint Merro would feel the aftermath like a fist through stone.

She swallowed, tasting dust.

“No,” she whispered, because whispering didn’t change math.

She wrapped the clear strip in cloth and shoved it into her satchel beside calipers and a small fracture loupe. Then she added a second piece—thicker, blunt, with notch marks filed along the edge. If she was right, stress would show there first.

She hesitated, hand hovering over a small jar of copper-gold particulate—a vanity pigment some clients liked. She left it. This wasn’t decoration. This was witness.

Tiffani stepped out onto the terrace and headed for the administrative threshold.

A clerk sat behind a stone lip with ink-stained fingers and a face carved from procedure.

Tiffani set her slate down. “I need this read by someone who can order a shutdown.”

The clerk glanced at it like it smelled. “For what cause?”

“For what’s coming through the sky,” Tiffani said.

The clerk’s eyes flicked up, bored and already dismissing. “We don’t process ‘sky.’ We process damages and requests.”

“It’s not a request,” she snapped, then forced herself to breathe. Rage didn’t pass gates. Proof did. “It’s measurement.”

He tapped the slate with a finger. “Who certified this instrument?”

“My hands,” she said. “Same hands that keep glass from exploding in your face.”

His mouth twitched like he might laugh. “That’s not a certification.”

Tiffani leaned in. “Then certify this: if you delay, you’ll be certifying bodies.”

He looked past her, already reaching for the next petition. “Bring an approved report. Or take your concerns to a foreman.”

She stood there for a breath too long, waiting for him to become a person.

He didn’t.

Fine.

If the gate wanted approved, she’d make approval irrelevant.

She went back to her shop, heart hammering, and stoked the furnace harder. Heat rose. The glass-house filled with that bright throat-burn warmth that made sweat bead at the hairline.

She pulled a gather on her rod—thick, stubborn, glowing. She needed a piece that would hold stress like a ledger, a piece that would crack in a readable way when the air did whatever it was about to do.

She set the molten gather into a simple mold with notch marks. Pressed. Turned. Lifted.

The glass took shape—ugly, blunt, honest.

As she worked it, that faint warp at the doorway thickened. Light bent like it didn’t trust the world’s edges anymore.

The witness glass in her hand twitched—just a tiny internal shiver, a stress signature forming without any cause from her flame.

Tiffani’s breath caught.

It’s early, she thought. It’s already touching us.

She set the piece into the anneal box, slammed the lid, and scribbled on her slate: **If notch three ghosts, treat as high risk.**

Her hands didn’t shake. Her stomach did.

She packed three witness pieces into cloth, added her rough path grid, and stepped out onto the terrace again.

This time she didn’t go to gates.

She went downslope.

Toward the only kind of person who’d believe a hum and a taste and a pulse without asking who stamped it.

Toward the Underface.

## CHAPTER 3 — PIERCING: STREET-LEVEL  
**Tide 0 | Pouring**  
**POV: Jhace**

The first scream came from stone, not mouths.

Jhace had just cleared the ladder shaft into street air when the terrace beneath his boots lurched. Not a settling sway. A violent, unified shove—like the entire city had been struck from below.

People staggered. A cart toppled. Glass in a shopfront spidered in a single breath and then burst outward, raining knives.

Jhace ducked, throwing his forearm over his head. A shard skimmed his sleeve and bit skin. He didn’t feel it. The air tasted like iron filings.

A second beat rolled through—cleaner, deeper. The sound arrived late, a delayed roar that made the market stalls rattle and the terrace stones complain.

Somebody yelled, “Down! Down!”

Somebody else yelled a name like that name could stop physics.

Jhace’s head snapped toward the skyline—no fireball, no falling rock, no obvious threat. Just the light bending wrong, like the sky itself had been dented.

That was worse.

A woman near a fountain slipped, caught herself on the lip, and stared at her hand like it didn’t belong to her. Blood ran between her fingers—thin and bright.

“Move!” Jhace barked, grabbing a man by the shoulder and yanking him out of the street as a masonry bracket tore loose from an upper terrace and fell. It hit the stones where the man had stood, splitting rock like bread.

Panic poured through bodies like spilled oil.

He saw it—an overhead brace line that shouldn’t be moving, vibrating with that doubled deepening resonance he’d felt below. It wasn’t just shaking. It was answering the pulse, amplifying it, like a tuning fork hammered by the world.

A child stood under it, frozen, mouth open and silent.

Jhace didn’t think. He moved.

He sprinted, boots slipping on dust, and shoved the child sideways as the brace pin above them screamed and began to walk out of its seat.

The pin didn’t pop free yet. It just crept—slow, inevitable.

Jhace’s hands hit the stone support and the iron brace at the same time.

The world sharpened.

Soulpulse wasn’t a spell. It was a decision to spend yourself.

He drove the intent into his palm—hold. Just hold. Not forever. Not heroic. Enough.

The doubled resonance became layers he could separate. The brace line’s stress became visible as pressure in his bones. The pin’s seat became a map of micro-slips.

The brace stopped walking for a handful of breaths.

Cost punched him immediately—nausea rising like hot bile, tremor trying to invade his forearms, vision tunneling at the edges. He didn’t let go.

“GET AWAY FROM THE BRACE!” he shouted, voice tearing.

A second pulse hit—stronger, nastier, shaped differently. Not just a shove. A twist.

The hold broke.

The brace snapped free with a sound like the city ripping cloth.

Jhace threw himself forward and dragged the child under the nearest stone arch, rolling with him as the brace line whipped down and gouged the street, carving sparks out of stone.

He landed hard. Shoulder pain flared. His hands shook like they weren’t his.

People screamed. A stall caught fire from a toppled heat-lamp. Smoke rose. Above, an upper terrace wall cracked in a clean line, then began to shed stone in chunks.

Saint Merro was being asked to carry a load it hadn’t agreed to.

Jhace pushed up, trembling. He tried to breathe through the nausea. Every breath tasted like iron.

He saw the Underface access grate across the street—the mouth back into the bones. The pulses were traveling through everything. Street, stone, pipe, brace. The whole city was one instrument now.

He had to get back down. Not to hide. To stabilize.

Jhace stumbled toward the grate.

Then he saw her.

A woman moving against the panic flow, downslope, satchel tight to her side, face focused in a way that didn’t belong to terror. She wasn’t running. She was measuring.

Tiffani.

Another pulse hit.

Jhace’s legs threatened to fold. He had to spend again or he’d be useless.

It wasn’t “earthquake.” It was feedback.

He ripped his hand away, gasping, cost clawing through him—tremor now full, hands buzzing, vision flickering. He could barely close his fingers.

But he’d learned one thing worth the blood:

The next pulse would hit harder in the Underface, where the grid carried it like a throat carries a shout.

He looked at Tiffani and croaked, “Down. Now.”

She didn’t argue. She just moved.

And together they dropped into the city’s bones as Saint Merro screamed above them.

## CHAPTER 4 — ASH IN THE FLOW  
**Tide 1 | Shaping**  
**POV: Tiffani**

After the first terror, the city tried to pretend.

It swept shards into piles. It propped fallen braces with bad lumber. It called injuries “incidents” and cracks “settling.” It told itself a story where the worst was over, because admitting the worst was beginning meant admitting nobody was in charge.

Tiffani didn’t have the luxury of pretending.

Her shop smelled like smoke and cold ash. The furnace still breathed, but its rhythm wasn’t steady anymore. Heat came in uneven waves, as if the fuel itself was confused by the new pulses.

She laid her witness glass on the bench and watched stress lines crawl inside it like trapped lightning.

Not from her hand. From the air.

From the grid beneath the stone.

She had gotten downslope at the right time. Not because she was brave, but because she was done being ignored. She’d found the tunnel man—Jhace—half-trembling, eyes too sharp, mouth too tight, like he’d bitten down on the truth hard enough to crack teeth.

Notch one: baseline.  
Notch two: warning.  
Notch three: get the fuck out or bleed it now.

She watched notch two ghost. Then clear. Then ghost again.

A pulse was coming.

She didn’t have a clock for it. She had the hum. The taste. The way the furnace breath hit her chest.

She tried to steady her hands and did what she’d never admitted out loud she could do.

Soulpulse wasn’t just in tunnels. It wasn’t just for men who lived under stone. It was a tool some people carried in their blood like an inherited bruise.

Tiffani placed her palm on the indicator glass and pushed the intent into it—read. Not hold. Not force. Just read the stress before it became fracture.

Nothing.

No sharpening. No clarity. Just her own heartbeat in her fingers and the glass sitting there like dead sand.

Her stomach dropped.

She tried again, harder, anger rising with the push—read, damn you—

Pain lanced up her arm. Not heat. Not burn. A deep internal wrongness, like she’d twisted a joint against its seat. Nausea surged. Her vision blurred at the edges.

The Soulpulse failed, and it punished her for trying.

She yanked her hand back and leaned over the bench, swallowing bile. Her fingers trembled—fine tremor, not Jhace’s violent shake, but enough to ruin a pour.

“Fine,” she rasped. “Fine. I don’t need you.”

Soulpulse wasn’t a hammer. It was a clamp. A controlled pressure. You didn’t throw it. You set it.

She tried again, smaller. Narrower. One intention only: *separate noise from signal.*

Her palm touched the glass.

The world sharpened by a hair. Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just enough.

Stress lines inside the indicator resolved into layers—some from her anneal, some from the shop’s temperature drift, and one set that pulsed in time with the hum under the floor.

There.

That one wasn’t hers.

She held it for a breath too long and paid for it—tremor biting her fingertips, eyes watering, mouth filling with that metallic taste like she’d licked a coin.

But she got what she needed.

She snatched her slate and scribbled, hand shaking: **When notch two ghosts twice without clearing, pulse imminent. When notch three ghosts, bleed pressure or clear the corridor.**

She didn’t dress it up. She didn’t soften it. Workers didn’t need poetry. They needed survival language.

She gathered every thick indicator she’d made and wrapped them in cloth. She added a simple legend card: notch states, action steps. She grabbed her calipers and a fracture loupe and her anneal log, because if this became system, she needed records that outlived her.

Outside, the terrace wind carried ash and the distant sound of people trying to rebuild lies into shelter.

Tiffani headed downslope again.

Not because she wanted to live under stone, but because the stone was where the truth traveled first.

At the Underface access, guards tried to stop her. She didn’t argue. She held out an indicator piece and watched it ghost under their noses as the hum deepened.

They stared. One of them swallowed, face pale.

“Let me through,” she said. “Or you can stand here and pretend until the stone eats you.”

They moved.

Down below, the Underface breathed warm iron and wet rock. The hum was never quiet now. It layered itself like a warning that had learned how to speak.

Jhace stood near a junction map board, hands still trembling, eyes hollowed by cost. He looked up when he heard her boots.

“You’re back,” he said, voice rough.

“I’m not leaving,” she answered, and set the wrapped indicators on the stone lip like offerings to a god that didn’t deserve them. “This is a threshold system. Readable. Repeatable.”

He stared at the notch marks, then at her slate.

“You used it,” he said, and it wasn’t a question.

She didn’t lie. “Tried. Failed. Tried again. Got enough.”

Jhace’s mouth tightened—respect or fear, she couldn’t tell. “It’ll cost you.”

“It already did,” she said, flexing trembling fingers. “But now it costs *less* than bodies.”

A faint pulse crawled through the tunnel—small, but clean.

Not local.

Tunnel-wide.

Jhace’s eyes snapped to the indicators. So did hers.

Notch two ghosted.

Twice.

He looked at her slate, then at the branch corridor.

“Bleed,” he ordered, voice carrying. “Controlled. Now.”

Workers moved—fast this time. No arguing. No laughing.

Tiffani watched them go and felt something shift in her chest—not relief, not victory. Commitment. The ugly kind. The kind that meant your life stopped being yours.

Jhace leaned close, voice low. “If we’re doing this, we do it right. No soft words. No approval stamps.”

Tiffani met his gaze. “Good. Because I’m done asking permission.”

Another pulse came, heavier.

The indicators ghosted in unison.

And the Underface began to run their new method like it had always existed—because the alternative was Saint Merro breaking itself into graves.

Tiffani stood at the board with a slate, eyes scanning, mouth tight. Jhace stood beside her, hands shaking, jaw clenched.

“Three branches at notch two ghosting twice,” Tiffani said. “Two more approaching. One already at notch three.”

Bram shoved through the crowd. “Sector Nine is screaming for help. Sector Twelve too.”

Chloen’s voice cut in from the side. “We split crews too thin and we save nothing.”

That was triage. Ugly and real.

Tiffani drew a hard line on the slate. “Which chain prevents the worst cascade?”

Jhace stared at the map like it could confess. He knew load paths. He knew which branch fed which terrace. He knew what would happen if a main run sheared and dumped heat-grid pressure into old stone.

He pointed. “This chain. If it goes, it takes three terraces and the Underface access with it. We hold this, or we lose the ability to respond at all.”

Bram’s face twisted. “And the others?”

Jhace didn’t soften it. “They break.”

Silence hit like a punch.

Then Tiffani nodded once. “We hold the chain.”

She lifted her voice, carrying over the hum. “Listen. We’re stabilizing one critical chain. Everyone else: clear corridors on notch three, run verification, bleed where you can. Do not die for pride. Do not die for a foreman.”

People stared. Some hated her. Some loved her. Most just needed someone to decide.

Jhace barked assignments.

“Chloen—lead Sector Twelve. Bram—you’re with me. Salee—indicator calls at the weakest link. Kylar—runner. Don’t stop moving.”

Kylar’s eyes were huge, but he nodded and ran.

They moved as a distributed machine—call phrases echoing, indicator states shouted, valve tags slapped into hands, checklists passed like currency.

A pulse crawled under the stone—then another—then a stacked hit that made the tunnel lamps stutter.

At the chain’s weakest link—an old junction with a brace line that had been patched too many times—Salee lifted her indicator.

“NOTCH THREE—GHOSTING!” she shouted.

Jhace felt the iron taste rise. “Bleed!”

The valve crew moved. The hiss started.

The brace line groaned.

Bram looked at Jhace, eyes wild. “It’s walking—pin is walking.”

Jhace’s hands shook as he grabbed the pin puller. He didn’t trust his own fine control anymore, so he did the only smart thing:

He made it a two-person job.

“Bram—hold the brace. Chloen—wedge set. Salee—call pulses. Tiffani—watch the indicator rack. Loud.”

Tiffani’s voice cut through the noise. “STACKED HIT COMING—NOW!”

They replaced the pin under pressure, not because they were brave, but because the protocol told them exactly how to be effective without being stupid.

The stacked pulse hit.

Stone screamed. Dust fell in sheets. The brace line tried to whip.

The new pin held.

The chain held.

A breath later, far away through the stone, a different sound rolled in—lower, heavier, like a throat closing.

A district failing.

“The other chain—” Kylar gasped as he ran in, face gray. “Sector Nine collapsed a corridor. People trapped.”

Bram’s eyes shut. He swallowed hard. “Fuck.”

Tiffani didn’t let herself crumble. She wrote it on the slate anyway—ledger truth. “Sector Nine corridor collapse. Trapped. Response limited.”

Jhace’s stomach twisted. He wanted to run. He wanted to save everyone. He wanted to go back in time to before the city learned how to stack its punches.

He did none of that.

He looked at the chain they’d held—the access route still open, the method still alive—and forced himself to accept the brutal math:

If they’d chased Sector Nine, they might have lost everything.

People began to gather, anger rising like heat. “You chose wrong!” someone screamed. “You let them die!”

Tiffani stepped forward, face hard as cooled glass. “We chose to keep the response alive. We chose to keep the access open. We chose the chain that keeps the city from losing its spine.”

“You don’t get to choose!” a man spat.

Jhace’s tremor was visible as he pointed at the ceiling brace that had just held. “The city chose. We just read it.”

Harlen appeared at the edge of the crowd, flanked by clerks, eyes scanning like he was measuring how to turn this into an official narrative.

Tiffani saw him and felt rage flare, then compress into purpose.

“They’re going to seize this,” she said to Jhace, low.

“I know,” Jhace replied. “Let them try.”

They’d held the chain.

Now they had to hold the truth.

## CHAPTER 12 — THE GRIP THAT HOLDS  
**Tide 3 | Quench**  
**POV: Jhace + Tiffani**

Harlen’s meeting space smelled like ink and cold stone—two things that pretended permanence.

The council table was crowded with foremen who’d never set a wedge in their lives and clerks who’d never tasted iron and still believed paper could outvote physics.

Harlen stood at the head like he was about to bless them.

“We will formalize the procedure,” he said. “We will standardize language. We will—”

“We will not neuter it,” Tiffani cut in.

The room stiffened. A foreman scoffed. Another bristled.

Harlen’s eyes narrowed. “This isn’t negotiation.”

Jhace’s hands shook openly on the table. He didn’t hide it. He let them see what “cost” looked like. “It is if you want it to work.”

Harlen lifted a slate. “The revised document removes inflammatory wording. ‘If unsure, treat as high risk’ becomes ‘exercise caution.’”

Tiffani laughed, sharp. “That’s not caution. That’s permission to ignore.”

A foreman snapped, “Workers are panicking.”

“No,” Jhace said, voice flat. “Workers are surviving.”

Tiffani leaned forward, indicator in hand—thick, notched, ugly. “This stays worker-first. Verification stays. Escalation stays. You want to rename it? Fine. Call it ‘Harlen’s Holy Paper’ for all I care. But the steps stay intact.”

Harlen’s jaw worked. “You’re undermining authority.”

“Authority undermined itself,” Tiffani replied. “When it called pulses ‘settling’ and asked for output anyway.”

The room went quiet at that. Not because they agreed. Because they remembered.

Harlen set his slate down slowly. “If we distribute it your way, and unrest follows—”

“Unrest follows when people die,” Jhace said. “Competence follows when people live.”

Harlen stared at the indicator a long breath. Then he nodded once, tight and resentful. “Steps remain intact. Language stays direct. Verification stays.”

Tiffani didn’t relax. She didn’t trust concessions. She trusted repetition.

They left the hall and went straight to the Underface staging area, where workers were already gathering with indicator pieces in hand like they were part of their own bodies now.

Chloen ran the drill without being asked. Bram corrected stance. Salee called indicator states loud enough to make cowards flinch. Kylar ran messages with a runner’s fury, face set like he’d chosen a side.

Tiffani watched the motions repeat until they stopped looking like “training” and started looking like ritual—habit anchored in survival.

Jhace stood beside her, hands shaking, eyes tired, and said the simplest truth he’d learned in the bones:

“This isn’t mine anymore.”

Tiffani nodded. “Good.”

They moved away from the crowd to a quiet junction wall—stone scarred with old repairs, chalk marks layered like generations of warning.

Tiffani hung an indicator on a nail there, dead center, like a standard.

Jhace took a piece of chalk and drew a short mark beneath it—no symbol, no flourish. Just a line that meant: **we were here, and we chose to hold.**

Tiffani’s voice went low, steady. “No more asking permission.”

Jhace’s throat tightened. “No more hiding wrongness because it’s inconvenient.”

They stood in the hum, feeling the doubled deepening resonance ride under everything, tasting iron faintly like a reminder that the city wasn’t done.

A runner came hard down the corridor—Kylar, breath ragged, eyes wide.

“New junction flipping early,” he gasped. “Different branch. Indicator ghosted twice and didn’t clear.”

Tiffani’s hand was already on her satchel. Jhace’s lamp was already clipped. They didn’t look at each other for approval.

They moved.

Because that was the only definition of “the grip that holds” that mattered in a city learning new physics:

You show up.  
You read the truth.  
You do the steps.  
You pay what it costs—without pretending it costs nothing.

**CHAPTER 9 — DEBT TALLIES**  
*Tide 3 | Quench*  
*POV: Tiffani*

The city kept ledgers whether anyone wrote them down.

It tallied in cracked stone. In missing teeth. In a brace pin found three spans from the seat it swore it died in. In the way a corridor went quiet when a name was spoken because nobody wanted to confirm the answer.

Tiffani wrote anyway.

Not for comfort. For pattern.

She sat in the back room of the glass-house with a slate on her knees and ash still in her hair, writing the same harsh line until the letters stopped trying to crawl off the page.

Kiln-quarter held.  
Worker hurt.  
Pulse arrived early.  
Foreman pressed.

Across from her, Pella sorted indicator pieces into piles—good, borderline, scrap—with bandaged leg tucked close and jaw set like stubbornness could replace tendon. She worked one-handed when pain flared, then pretended it hadn’t.

Bram stood in the doorway, silent for once, as if noise might summon another collapse.

Chloen came in with a ragged strip of cloth wrapped around her forearm, eyes tired but locked. “Harlen called it ‘unfortunate collateral.’”

Tiffani’s jaw tightened until it ached. “That’s not accurate. It was predictable.”

Chloen’s mouth twitched. Not quite a smile. “He’s pushing a revised document. Wants to rename the protocol. Make it an ‘inspection improvement initiative.’”

Tiffani laughed once. It wasn’t humor. “So he can own it without owning what it means.”

Bram spat on the stone. “He can try.”

Tiffani tapped her slate with charcoal. “Trying is enough to kill it.”

She laid out three indicators on the bench and set her fracture loupe beside them—one from Junction Twelve, one from Sector Eleven, one from kiln-quarter.

All three had survived pulses.

All three carried stress lines that shouldn’t exist in a sane model.

“All right,” she said, low. “We revise thresholds.”

Pella didn’t look up from the piles. “Make it simple. People don’t read when they’re scared.”

“It becomes simple,” Tiffani said. She forced her breath down. Heat discipline. Emotional discipline. Same rule.

She traced the notch legend on slate, clean and brutal.

- Notch one clear: baseline.  
- Notch two ghosts: warning.  
- Notch two ghosts twice without clearing: immediate.  
- Notch three ghosts: clear corridor / bleed now.

Then she added one line in thick charcoal.

If unsure, treat as high risk. Verify fast.

Bram frowned. “That’ll cause more bleeds.”

“Yes,” Tiffani said. “And fewer funerals.”

Chloen leaned in. “Harlen will hate that line.”

“Good,” Tiffani said. “Harlen isn’t the one under load.”

A pulse crawled under the floor, faint but clean. The indicators on the bench ghosted in sympathy.

Still doing it.

Still repeating.

Still reshaping.

Tiffani pressed her palm to the stone and felt the hum change. Not louder. Different. Layered. A pulse signature riding beneath everything now like a second register.

Her body remembered Soulpulse.

Hated that it remembered.

Hated that it worked.

Hated what it cost.

But if she didn’t pin this new layer down, her thresholds would drift until they were lies again.

She set her hand flat on the stone, narrowed her intent until it was a single tool-edge, and clamped.

Separate signal. Hold long enough to compare.

Copper-gold light bled under her palm and ran into hairline seams. Vein-lines rose along her forearm, thin and bright, and the stone’s vibration separated into layers in her nerves.

Not magic. Mechanics.

Noise from furnace heat. Noise from street movement. And beneath both, the pulse layer that didn’t belong to either.

Metallic taste crept up.

Tremor bit her fingertips.

She slid her other hand over the indicators one by one, matching ghosting behavior to the pulse layer.

Kiln-quarter jumped early—heat wobble contamination. Sector Eleven tracked clean. Junction Twelve lagged—thicker glass, slower response.

She held the clamp one breath longer to confirm.

The cost hit hard. Nausea rolled up. Vision narrowed. Her hand buzzed as if it had been struck with a mallet.

She tore her hand back and swallowed bile, jaw clenched until her teeth ached.

“One use,” she rasped, wiping her mouth. “That’s it.”

Pella’s eyes narrowed. “Master Tiffani—are you—”

“No,” Tiffani said. “Still useful.”

She wrote the revision straight into the distributed checklist, hand shaking but legible.

Calibration note: thicker pieces lag. Treat lagging notch two as immediate if hum deepens and iron taste rises.

Bram stared. “You’re building rules for the taste.”

“I’m building rules for survival,” she corrected.

Chloen gathered the checklist cards—short, brutal, readable—and began stacking them. “We drill this.”

“Where?” Bram asked.

Tiffani stood, ignoring the wobble in her knees. “In public. Where management can’t bury it in ink.”

They went to a terrace staging area where workers from multiple sectors gathered—some angry, some terrified, some looking for someone to blame so they could sleep.

Harlen arrived with two clerks and a face set to neutral, which was his favorite weapon.

“This gathering is unauthorized,” he said, voice measured. “I understand the urgency, but—”

Tiffani lifted an indicator. “So is the city trying to kill us.”

He looked ready to speak. Then the glass ghosted in his line of sight as a pulse crawled under the stone.

His throat bobbed.

Tiffani didn’t let him recover. “We’re distributing worker-first steps. We’re drilling them. If you want to rename it, rename it on your slate. People will live by the real sequence.”

Harlen’s eyes flicked to the crowd. To a stretcher passing. To braces overhead that still trembled with the doubled resonance.

“You’re inciting unrest,” he said, because he had to say something.

“I’m inciting competence,” Tiffani replied.

Chloen started the drill.

“Indicator state?” she called.

A dozen hands lifted glass. Voices answered. Notch calls echoed. Verification steps were shouted. Valve sequences were rehearsed until they lived in wrists instead of brains.

A pulse hit mid-drill—heavier than the crawl—and the crowd didn’t scatter. They moved to the right spaces because the steps told them where safety lived.

When it passed, some people laughed. Some cried. Some just stared at their own hands like they’d been given them back.

Harlen watched and tightened. He understood the part that mattered.

The method had jumped the fence.

Tiffani looked at him, calm and ruthless. “You can’t bury this now. Too many hands have it.”

Harlen’s mouth went thin. “Then it becomes official.”

“Fine,” she said. “But the worker-first line stays. The high-risk line stays. And you do not cut the verification step.”

Harlen hesitated—just long enough for her to measure the size of his fear.

Then he nodded once. “Agreed.”

Bram muttered, “He’s gonna try to fuck us later.”

Tiffani didn’t argue. “Let him try.”

Because her ledger had a new entry.

Protocol spread.  
Management forced to adopt.  
City still pulsing.  
Cost accumulating.

And the cost she’d paid to sharpen the signal sat in her fingers as a warning she couldn’t ignore.

That was the last Soulpulse she could afford.

After this, it had to be method, not muscle.

**CHAPTER 10 — SECOND PULSE EVENT**  
*Tide 3 | Quench*  
*POV: Jhace*

The second real hit didn’t announce itself with spectacle.

It came as a correction.

Jhace was in the Underface when the hum shifted—subtle at first, like someone retuning a line under load. The doubled deepening resonance thickened until it wasn’t “second” anymore.

It was foundation.

He tasted iron before the indicator ghosted.

That was new.

He looked at the nearest worker—Salee—who held her indicator in a steady hand.

Notch two ghosted once.

“Verification,” Jhace ordered.

They ran it fast. Pin gauge. Collar seam. Brace seat. No skipped steps. No hero moves.

Notch two cleared.

Jhace’s shoulders eased by a fraction. “Stay sharp.”

Kylar came tearing down the corridor, face pale. “Sector Seven is flipping all at once. Indicators everywhere. It’s—”

The floor bucked.

Not a crawl. Not the clean shove from earlier. A layered impact that hit, then hit again inside itself, like the city had learned how to stack punches.

Lamps jittered. Dust fell. A brace line groaned overhead.

Jhace didn’t reach for Soulpulse.

Couldn’t. Not today. The tremor still lived in him from kiln-quarter, and spending on top of that was how you ended up on a slate.

He lifted his voice instead.

“CLEAR CORRIDOR!” he roared. “COVER! NOW!”

People moved.

Not perfectly. Not gracefully. But they moved to the right spaces because the drill had burned the steps into them.

The brace line snapped a fraction, scraping stone. A collar seam ticked—micro-slip trying to become catastrophe.

“Hold that bracket!” Jhace shouted.

Bram and two others slammed wedges in, driving them hard, trusting sequence over instinct.

Notch three ghosted in Salee’s hand.

“Bleed,” Jhace snapped.

The valve crew ran the sequence—no foreman permission, no Harlen signature. The hiss hit the corridor like an angry animal, then steadied as pressure dropped.

The stacked pulse hit again—harder—and the entire branch tried to twist out of truth.

Old quick fixes failed here. Wedges slipped. Pins walked.

The city demanded better work than “good enough.”

Jhace’s hands shook as he shoved a replacement pin into place. His own body was now an unreliable tool, and he hated it.

So he did the only smart thing.

He delegated.

“Chloen,” he barked, “take Sector Seven. Run protocol. Verify. Bleed only on notch three.”

Chloen met his eyes—no fear, just grim agreement. “On it.”

“Salee,” he said, “call indicator state. Loud. No shame.”

Salee swallowed, then lifted her voice. “NOTCH TWO—TWICE—IMMEDIATE.”

Good. Clear language. No embarrassment. No softness.

Bram shoved his shoulder into a brace support, face clenched. “This one’s walking!”

“Replace pin,” Jhace said. “Not patch.”

“We don’t have—” someone started.

“We do,” Jhace cut in, and kicked open the supply satchel where Tiffani had insisted they keep spares even when management called it waste.

A pulse hit, and the corridor screamed.

They replaced the pin anyway.

When the vibration eased, the brace line held. The collar seam stopped ticking. The branch stayed intact.

A win.

Earned by method. Not by one man’s palm.

Kylar crouched beside Jhace afterward, breath ragged. “You didn’t—”

“No Soulpulse,” Jhace said. “Not today.”

Kylar stared like that was either bravery or insanity.

Jhace didn’t care what it looked like. He cared what it did.

He climbed upslope after, through the ladder shaft into cold air that tasted less like iron but not like peace.

Tiffani waited at a staging point with Harlen nearby, both reading reports off slates as if the city was a problem you could file.

Tiffani’s eyes flicked over Jhace, reading tremor, posture, fatigue. “You held without spending,” she said, low.

“I had to,” Jhace replied. “We can’t keep buying windows.”

Harlen stepped in. “The official document will require—”

Tiffani cut him off without raising her voice. “It requires worker-first steps. It requires verification. It requires that you stop calling it ‘unrest’ when people are using it to stay alive.”

Harlen’s mouth tightened. “I’m trying to prevent panic.”

“You prevent panic by giving people competence,” Jhace said. His hands shook openly now. He didn’t hide it. “Not by hiding wrongness.”

Harlen looked away, jaw working. He had no clean answer.

Tiffani stepped closer to Jhace. “We go together from here,” she said. Not romantic. Not soft. Structural. “No lone hero work. No lone genius work. Method is bigger than either of us.”

Pride tried to argue. Pride could go choke.

“Agreed,” Jhace said.

And for the first time since the Piercing made Saint Merro a liar, Jhace felt something that wasn’t hope.

A standard. A sequence. A thing that might outlive them.

*Tide 3 | Quench*  
*POV: Tiffani*

The city kept ledgers whether anyone wrote them down.

It tallied in cracked stone. In missing teeth. In a brace pin found three spans from the seat it swore it died in. In the way a corridor went quiet when a name was spoken because nobody wanted to confirm the answer.

Tiffani wrote anyway.

Not for comfort. For pattern.

She sat in the back room of the glass-house with a slate on her knees and ash still in her hair, writing the same harsh line until the letters stopped trying to crawl off the page.

Kiln-quarter held.  
Worker hurt.  
Pulse arrived early.  
Foreman pressed.

Across from her, Pella sorted indicator pieces into piles—good, borderline, scrap—with her bandaged leg tucked close and her jaw set like stubbornness could replace tendon. She worked one-handed when pain flared, then pretended it hadn’t.

Bram stood in the doorway, silent for once, as if noise might summon another collapse.

Chloen came in with a ragged strip of cloth wrapped around her forearm, eyes tired but locked. “Harlen called it ‘unfortunate collateral.’”

Tiffani’s jaw tightened until it ached. “That’s not accurate. It was predictable.”

Chloen’s mouth twitched. Not quite a smile. “He’s pushing a revised document. Wants to rename the protocol. Make it an ‘inspection improvement initiative.’”

Tiffani laughed once. It wasn’t humor. “So he can own it without owning what it means.”

Bram spat on the stone. “He can try.”

Tiffani tapped her slate with charcoal. “Trying is enough to kill it.”

She laid out three indicators on the bench and set her fracture loupe beside them—one from Junction Twelve, one from Sector Eleven, one from kiln-quarter.

All three had survived pulses.

All three carried stress lines that shouldn’t exist in any sane read of load and stone.

“All right,” she said, low. “We revise thresholds.”

Pella didn’t look up from the piles. “Make it simple. People don’t read when they’re scared.”

“It becomes simple,” Tiffani said. She forced her breath down. Heat discipline. Emotional discipline. Same rule.

She traced the notch legend on slate, clean and brutal.

- Notch one clear: baseline.  
- Notch two ghosts: warning.  
- Notch two ghosts twice without clearing: immediate.  
- Notch three ghosts: clear corridor / bleed now.

Then she added one line in thick charcoal.

If unsure, treat as high risk. Verify fast.

Bram frowned. “That’ll cause more bleeds.”

“Yes,” Tiffani said. “And fewer funerals.”

Chloen leaned in. “Harlen will hate that line.”

“Good,” Tiffani said. “Harlen isn’t the one under load.”

A pulse crawled under the floor, faint but clean. The indicators on the bench ghosted in sympathy.

Still doing it.

Still repeating.

Still reshaping.

Tiffani pressed her palm to the stone and felt the hum change. Not louder. Different. Layered. A pulse signature riding beneath everything now like a second register.

Her body remembered Soulpulse.

Hated that it remembered.

Hated that it worked.

Hated what it cost.

But if she didn’t pin this new layer down, her thresholds would drift until they were lies again.

She set her hand flat on the stone, narrowed her intent until it was a single tool-edge, and clamped.

Separate signal. Hold long enough to compare.

Copper-gold bled under her palm and ran into hairline seams. Vein-lines rose along her forearm, thin and bright, and the stone’s vibration separated into layers in her nerves.

Not magic. Mechanics.

Noise from furnace heat. Noise from street movement. And beneath both, the pulse layer that didn’t belong to either.

Metallic taste crept up.

Tremor bit her fingertips.

She slid her other hand over the indicators one by one, matching ghosting behavior to the pulse layer.

Kiln-quarter jumped early—heat wobble contamination. Sector Eleven tracked clean. Junction Twelve lagged—thicker glass, slower response.

She held the clamp one breath longer to confirm.

The cost hit hard. Nausea rolled up. Vision narrowed. Her hand buzzed as if it had been struck with a mallet.

She tore her hand back and swallowed bile, jaw clenched until her teeth ached.

“One use,” she rasped, wiping her mouth. “That’s it.”

Pella’s eyes narrowed. “Glassmaster—are you—”

“No,” Tiffani said. “Still useful.”

She wrote the revision straight into the distributed checklist, hand shaking but legible.

Calibration note: thicker pieces lag. Treat lagging notch two as immediate if hum deepens and iron taste rises.

Bram stared. “You’re building rules for the taste.”

“I’m building rules for survival,” she corrected.

Chloen gathered the checklist cards—short, brutal, readable—and began stacking them. “We drill this.”

“Where?” Bram asked.

Tiffani stood, ignoring the wobble in her knees. “In public. Where management can’t bury it in ink.”

They went to a terrace staging area where workers from multiple sectors gathered—some angry, some terrified, some looking for someone to blame so they could sleep.

Harlen arrived with two clerks and a face set to neutral, which was his favorite weapon.

“This gathering is unauthorized,” he said, voice measured. “I understand the urgency, but—”

Tiffani lifted an indicator. “So is the city trying to kill us.”

He looked ready to speak. Then the glass ghosted in his line of sight as a pulse crawled under the stone.

His throat bobbed.

Tiffani didn’t let him recover. “We’re distributing worker-first steps. We’re drilling them. If you want to rename it, rename it on your slate. People will live by the real sequence.”

Harlen’s eyes flicked to the crowd. To a stretcher passing. To braces overhead that still trembled with the doubled resonance.

“You’re inciting unrest,” he said, because he had to say something.

“I’m inciting competence,” Tiffani replied.

Chloen started the drill.

“Indicator state?” she called.

A dozen hands lifted glass. Voices answered. Notch calls echoed. Verification steps were shouted. Valve sequences were rehearsed until they lived in wrists instead of brains.

A pulse hit mid-drill—heavier than the crawl—and the crowd didn’t scatter. They moved to the right spaces because the steps told them where safety lived.

When it passed, some people laughed. Some cried. Some just stared at their own hands like they’d been given them back.

Harlen watched and tightened. He understood the part that mattered.

The method had jumped the fence.

Tiffani looked at him, calm and ruthless. “You can’t bury this now. Too many hands have it.”

Harlen’s mouth went thin. “Then it becomes official.”

“Fine,” she said. “But the worker-first line stays. The high-risk line stays. And you do not cut the verification step.”

Harlen hesitated—just long enough for her to measure the size of his fear.

Then he nodded once. “Agreed.”

Bram muttered, “He’s gonna try to fuck us later.”

Tiffani didn’t argue. “Let him try.”

Because her ledger had a new entry.

Protocol spread.  
Management forced to adopt.  
City still pulsing.  
Cost accumulating.

And the cost she’d paid to sharpen the signal sat in her fingers as a warning she couldn’t ignore.

That was the last Soulpulse she could afford.

After this, it had to be method, not muscle.

**CHAPTER 10 — SECOND PULSE EVENT**  
*Tide 3 | Quench*  
*POV: Jhace*

The second real hit didn’t announce itself with spectacle.

It came as a correction.

Jhace was in the Underface when the hum shifted—subtle at first, like someone retuning a line under load. The doubled deepening resonance thickened until it wasn’t “second” anymore.

It was foundation.

He tasted iron before the indicator ghosted.

That was new.

He looked at the nearest worker—Salee—who held her indicator in a steady hand.

Notch two ghosted once.

“Verification,” Jhace ordered.

They ran it fast. Pin gauge. Collar seam. Brace seat. No skipped steps. No hero moves.

Notch two cleared.

Jhace’s shoulders eased by a fraction. “Stay sharp.”

Kylar came tearing down the corridor, face pale. “Sector Seven is flipping all at once. Indicators everywhere. It’s—”

The floor bucked.

Not a crawl. Not the clean shove from earlier. A layered impact that hit, then hit again inside itself, like the city had learned how to stack punches.

Lamps jittered. Dust fell. A brace line groaned overhead.

Jhace didn’t reach for Soulpulse.

Couldn’t. Not today. The tremor still lived in him from kiln-quarter, and spending on top of that was how you ended up on a slate.

He lifted his voice instead.

“CLEAR CORRIDOR!” he roared. “COVER! NOW!”

People moved.

Not perfectly. Not gracefully. But they moved to the right spaces because the drill had burned the steps into them.

The brace line snapped a fraction, scraping stone. A collar seam ticked—micro-slip trying to become catastrophe.

“Hold that bracket!” Jhace shouted.

Bram and two others slammed wedges in, driving them hard, trusting sequence over instinct.

Notch three ghosted in Salee’s hand.

“Bleed,” Jhace snapped.

The valve crew ran the sequence—no foreman permission, no Harlen signature. The hiss hit the corridor like an angry animal, then steadied as pressure dropped.

The stacked pulse hit again—harder—and the entire branch tried to twist out of truth.

Old quick fixes failed here. Wedges slipped. Pins walked.

The city demanded better work than “good enough.”

Jhace’s hands shook as he shoved a replacement pin into place. His own body was now an unreliable tool, and he hated it.

So he did the only smart thing.

He delegated.

“Chloen,” he barked, “take Sector Seven. Run protocol. Verify. Bleed only on notch three.”

Chloen met his eyes—no fear, just grim agreement. “On it.”

“Salee,” he said, “call indicator state. Loud. No shame.”

Salee swallowed, then lifted her voice. “NOTCH TWO—TWICE—IMMEDIATE.”

Good. Clear language. No embarrassment. No softness.

Bram shoved his shoulder into a brace support, face clenched. “This one’s walking!”

“Replace pin,” Jhace said. “Not patch.”

“We don’t have—” someone started.

“We do,” Jhace cut in, and kicked open the supply satchel where Tiffani had insisted they keep spares even when management called it waste.

A pulse hit, and the corridor screamed.

They replaced the pin anyway.

When the vibration eased, the brace line held. The collar seam stopped ticking. The branch stayed intact.

A win.

Earned by method. Not by one man’s palm.

“No Soulpulse,” Jhace said. “Not today.”

Kylar stared like that was either bravery or insanity.
