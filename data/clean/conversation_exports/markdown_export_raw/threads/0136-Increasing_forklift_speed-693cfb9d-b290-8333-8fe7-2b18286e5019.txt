How do I increase the maximum speed on a Toyota forklift?

[ASSISTANT]
You generally **shouldn’t** “increase max speed” on a Toyota forklift by bypassing limiters or safety controls. That’s how you end up with a tip-over, a smashed rack, or an OSHA-shaped headache. I can’t walk you through defeating governors/passworded settings or hacking the controller.

What I *can* do is tell you the legit, safe routes and the common “it’s slow because something’s wrong” checks.

## The legit ways (no sketchy tampering)
1. **Verify the truck is even spec’d for more speed**
   - Toyota sells different travel-speed configurations by model/class. Some trucks are intentionally capped for stability and braking distance.

2. **Have an authorized tech adjust travel-speed parameters (if available)**
   - Many Toyota forklifts have **programmable travel speed** and **acceleration** parameters in the controller, but they’re typically **dealer/service-tool + password** protected.
   - If your site policy allows it, a Toyota service tech can set it within safe limits for that model.

3. **If you truly need faster travel: get the right truck**
   - If you’re trying to make a warehouse lift behave like a yard truck, the answer is usually “wrong machine for the job,” not “turn it up.”

## The “it’s slow because something is limiting it” checklist
These are common and fixable, and they *feel* like a speed limiter when they’re really not:

### Electric trucks
- **Battery condition/voltage sag**
  - Weak battery = controller derates travel speed under load. Check battery health, connections, watering, and voltage under acceleration.
- **Speed mode set low**
  - Some have operator-selectable modes (turtle/rabbit, economy/performance). Make sure it’s not stuck in low mode.
- **Brake drag**
  - Jack it safely and check wheel drag. Dragging brakes will make it feel gutless.
- **Tire size/type**
  - Wrong diameter tires (or low pneumatics) change effective speed and can trigger traction limits.
- **Controller derate / fault codes**
  - Many systems reduce speed if they see certain faults (traction, temp, sensor, seat switch weirdness).

### IC (LP/Diesel) trucks
- **Engine not making power**
  - Dirty air filter, fuel restriction, bad LPG regulator, ignition issues = “won’t rev under load.”
- **Transmission/hydraulic issues**
  - Heat or low/incorrect fluid can cause sluggish response.
- **Brake drag / parking brake not fully releasing**
  - Very common.

### Both
- **Load/backrest/attachments**
  - Big attachments can trigger conservative handling or simply add rolling resistance.
- **SAS / stability behavior**
  - Toyota’s stability systems can limit behavior depending on conditions and model.

What you *can* do (and what warehouses usually do) is this:

## What the “process” usually is
### 1) Get the exact truck info
Go to the **data plate** (usually near the operator compartment/mast area) and write down:
- **Model**
- **Serial number**
- **Power type** (electric vs LP/diesel)
- **Any attachment** (sideshift, clamp, fork positioner, etc.)

Your dealer/service will ask for this.

### 2) Make sure it’s not just set to a slow mode
Before anyone touches parameters, check the obvious:
- Is it in **eco/turtle/low performance** mode (if your truck has mode selection)?
- Is there a **speed limit setting tied to key/fob/operator profile** (some fleets do this)?
- Any **seatbelt/seat switch** issues causing derate?
- If electric: is the **battery low/weak** or connections crusty (speed will derate hard)?

If the old truck is “slow,” it may be sick, not “turned down.”

### 3) Get approval internally (so it’s not on you)
This is the part your old supervisor probably handled:
- Superintendent + **EHS/safety** sign-off (even an email) stating: “Adjust travel speed to site standard.”
- Confirm the **aisle type**, pedestrian traffic, dock plates/ramps, and if you have **speed limits by zone** (some sites do).

This protects you.

### 4) Call Toyota Material Handling / your dealer service
Tell them exactly what you want:
- “We want the **travel speed and acceleration set to match our site standard** (or match Truck #X), within Toyota’s allowed settings for this model/attachment.”
They’ll either:
- Send a tech with the service tool, or
- If your company has an in-house certified tech program, they’ll tell you who has access.

### 5) After changes: quick verification
- Test **unloaded and loaded**, straight line + turns.
- Confirm **braking distance feels sane** and it doesn’t get sketchy on corners/ramps.
- Document the change (who/when/what setting category) for maintenance records.

## If your superintendent is pushing “just do it”
Your clean answer is:
“I can coordinate the dealer/service adjustment and do the pre-checks, but the speed parameters are dealer/service-tool controlled and need EHS approval.”

That’s not you being difficult—that’s you not being the fall guy.
