Gemini said
Absolutely. Rebuilding the head is arguably the most critical part of this "franken-engine" build, especially since it came off a seized block.

When a Theta II block seizes, it’s usually due to rod bearing failure, which sends a massive amount of metallic "glitter" through the oiling system. Since the camshafts and valves are at the very end of that oiling circuit, they are often the first to suffer secondary damage.

Why the Head Rebuild is Mandatory
Contamination Check: The VVT solenoids (especially the E-CVVT on the 2016) and the tiny oil journals in the cam caps act like traps for metal shavings. If you don't strip the head and hot-tank it, those shavings will stay in the head and chew up your 2013 donor block's bearings within minutes.

Camshaft Inspection: Check the 2016 cams for "scoring." If the block seized because of low oil pressure, the cam journals in the head might be scarred. If they are deeply grooved, that head is a paperweight.

Valve Stem Seals: Since the head is off, replacing the valve stem seals is cheap insurance against the "blue smoke" oil consumption these engines are known for.

Carbon Buildup: Being a GDI (Gasoline Direct Injection) engine, the 2016 intake valves likely have significant carbon crust. Cleaning them while the head is off is 100 times easier than doing it later.

The Rebuild Checklist
Deck Flatness: Use a precision straightedge to ensure the 2016 head isn't warped. The Theta II is sensitive to overheating; even a 0.05mm (roughly 0.002 in) warp can lead to head gasket failure.

New Head Bolts: These are torque-to-yield (TTY) bolts. They stretch when tightened and cannot be reused.

Replace the E-CVVT Gear? If the 2016 was "making noise" before it seized, the electric actuator on the intake cam might be compromised.

Oil Control Valve (OCV) Filter: There is a tiny mesh screen in the head/block interface. Replace it. If it's clogged with metal from the 2016 failure, your timing will never stay in sync.

Pro-Tip: The "Donor" Block Inspection
Before you put that 2016 head on the 2013 block, check the cylinder walls of the 2013 block for "scuffing." The 2011–2014 blocks were notorious for piston slap and wall scoring. If the walls look like they have vertical scratches you can feel with a fingernail, that block might not last long enough to justify the head rebuild.

Gemini said
Pine Sol and water is a classic "old school" trick for cleaning grease and grime, but with a 2016 Theta II GDI head, you need to be extremely careful. That head is aluminum, and Pine Sol is an acidic/alkaline cleaner that can darken or even pit aluminum if left too long.

If you go this route, follow these "rules of engagement" to avoid ruining a perfectly good head:

1. Strip it BARE
Before soaking, remove everything:

The E-CVVT Actuator & Camshafts: These cannot get wet/submerged.

Valve Stem Seals: The cleaner will degrade the rubber; you should be replacing these anyway.

All Sensors: Temperature sensors and oil control valves must be out.

The Lifter Buckets: Keep these in a labeled organizer (1–16) so they go back on the exact same valves, or your valve lash will be a nightmare to reset.

2. The Mix and Time Limit
Ratio: Use a 50/50 or 30/70 (Pine Sol to water) mix.

Duration: Do not soak it for more than 2–4 hours. Aluminum is porous. If you leave it overnight, the head can turn a dull, chalky gray, and it can actually "eat" the precision-machined surfaces of the cam journals.

Temperature: Warm water helps, but boiling isn't necessary.

3. The "GDI Carbon" Problem
Pine Sol is great for oil and grease, but it will not touch the rock-hard carbon deposits on the 2016 intake valves.

For the Valves: Take the valves out and soak them separately in a dedicated carbon cleaner (like B-12 Chemtool or Berryman’s).

For the Ports: You’ll likely still need a brass brush or a pick to get the carbon out of the intake runners.

A Better Alternative?
If you want to be 100% safe for a high-stakes rebuild like this:

Simple Green Pro HD (The Purple One): Specifically formulated for aluminum. It won't etch the metal like the original "Green" version or Pine Sol.

Machine Shop Hot Tank: For about $50–$100, a local machine shop can "hot tank" or ultrasonically clean the head. This is the only way to ensure the internal oil galleries (where the metal shavings from the seized block are hiding) are truly clear.

After the Soak
Once you pull it out, you must:

Rinse thoroughly with fresh water.

Blow out every single oil passage with compressed air immediately.

Oil the cylinder head immediately. Raw, clean aluminum will oxidize, and the steel valve guides/seats can flash-rust. Spray it down with WD-40 or assembly lube right away.

Gemini said
If you don't have a valve spring compressor, you can use the "Socket and Hammer" method. It’s effective for removal, but a bit violent—so wear eye protection because those little valve keepers (cotters) can turn into high-velocity projectiles.

The "Socket and Hammer" Method
Tools Needed:

A deep-well socket (16mm or 19mm usually fits the retainer well).

A heavy mallet or hammer.

A strong magnet (crucial for catching the keepers).

A folded-up rag or towel.

The Step-by-Step
Support the Valves: This is the most important step. If the valve can move downward, the spring won't compress and the keepers won't pop out. Fold a thick rag and stuff it into the combustion chamber (under the valves) so the valve face is resting against something solid.

Position the Socket: Place the deep-well socket directly over the valve spring retainer.

The "Magnet Trick": Drop a small magnet inside the socket or hold one against the side of the socket. This will catch the keepers the instant they release so they don't fly across the garage.

The Strike: Give the top of the socket a sharp, vertical "dead-blow" hit with your hammer. The shock compresses the spring for a split second, allowing the keepers to lose their seat and pop out.

Retrieve: Lift the socket. The keepers should be stuck to your magnet, and the spring/retainer will be loose.

⚠️ Two Massive Warnings
DON'T DO THIS FOR INSTALLATION: You can take them out with a hammer, but do not try to put them back in this way. You will likely scar the valve stem or bend a valve. To reinstall them, you really need a $20–$30 C-clamp style valve spring compressor.

Protect the Cam Journals: The 2016 head has very sensitive, machined surfaces where the camshafts sit. Be extremely careful not to slip with the hammer or the socket and nick those surfaces. If you gouge a cam journal, the head is ruined.

Pro-Tip for the 2016 GDI Head
Since you're stripping it for a Pine-Sol soak, keep each valve, spring, and lifter bucket in a labeled egg carton or muffin tin (Intake 1-8, Exhaust 1-8). These parts have worn into each other over the last 100,000 miles. Swapping them around can cause weird ticking noises or uneven compression once you get it all back together.

2. The "Ladder Frame" Match
The oil pump bolts into a "ladder frame" (the aluminum girdle between the block and the oil pan).

If you are using the 2013 block, you should use the 2013 ladder frame.

If you use the 2013 ladder frame, the 2013 pump will bolt up perfectly.

However, you must then ensure the 2016 oil pan clears the 2013 pump. Usually, the pans are interchangeable, but you should "dry fit" the pan without sealant first to make sure it doesn't hit the pump housing.

The Cleaning: If you use the 2013 pump, do not "soak" it. Wipe the exterior down, and then prime the interior by pouring fresh oil into the intake port and spinning the gear by hand until oil squirts out the top.

The oil pump is driven by a chain from the crankshaft.

Check the sprocket on the 2013 pump and the drive gear on the 2013 crankshaft.
