# N01: THE GRIP THAT HOLDS  
  
## Confluence Chronicles | Turning One: GLASS  
  
**Novella 1 of 30**    
**Word Count:** ~20,000 words    
**Timeline:** Tide 0 through Tide 20 (Post-Piercing recovery)

# CHAPTER 1: “IRON OFF-NOTE”  
  
The grid hum drops half a tone before Kindling ends.  
  
Jhace Torrin feels it through his palms before instruments register anything—pressure stacking somewhere deep in the Underface lines, load shifting where no load should go. He stands alone in the riser junction beneath Saint Merro, breath syncing unconsciously to the hum he’s memorized over seven Turns. Every seam, every weld, every heat-joint in this radius is a map inside his hands.  
  
this Tide, that map is wrong.  
  
He presses his palm flat against corroded iron. Vibration travels boneward—strained, uneven, the kind of deviation that doesn’t trigger alarms but precedes failures. The kind his parents never felt coming before the beam gave way.  
  
He moves with ritualistic precision. Junction to junction. Seam to seam. The Underface carries Kindling heat upward—normal warmth against skin—but beneath it, stress ripples in dissonant pulses.  
  
If he stops checking, something breaks.  
  
The thought isn’t panic. It’s premise. Structure. Habit carved into nerve.  
  
Bootsteps echo. Bram arrives with a tool bag slung over one shoulder, eyebrows already raised. Three Turns working with Jhace have taught him to distinguish between real danger and Jhace’s obsessive cycles. He’s still skeptical.  
  
“Tell me you slept,” Bram says.  
  
Jhace doesn’t lift his eyes from the weld line. “Read-lines are flat.”  
  
“They’re stable.”  
  
“They’re flat,” Jhace repeats. “Listen.”  
  
Bram listens. Hears the usual hum—low, consistent. Nothing alarming.  
  
“It sounds fine.”  
  
“It’s half a tone off.” Jhace moves to the next seam. “Load’s shifting.”  
  
“Variance is normal.”  
  
“Not this variance.” He checks the gauge. “Something above us is leaning.”  
  
Bram exhales through his nose—resigned. He unclips his tools. “All right. We verify.”  
  
They work in practiced silence. Jhace maps each junction with his hands while Bram confirms pressure readings. The hum refuses to settle. Every step Jhace takes reinforces the same conclusion: the system is compensating for weight it was never meant to carry.  
  
“There.” Jhace points to a junction that should be neutral. A thin distortion marks the edge—barely perceptible, but unmistakable.  
  
Bram checks the gauges again. “Still within tolerance. The seams will hold.”  
  
“Until the next phase change.”  
  
Bram hesitates. Then: “Reinforce?”  
  
“We reinforce.”  
  
They brace the weakest points. Redistribute what load they can. It isn’t enough—not if the shift keeps stacking—but it buys heartbeats, maybe breaths. Enough for evacuation if he’s right.  
  
The first tremor hits during Shaping.  
  
Thin. Sharp. Like the grid inhaling wrong.  
  
Jhace feels it through his boots before dust shakes loose from overhead pipes. The hum drops again—another half-tone, then another. He doesn’t wait.  
  
“Evacuate!” he shouts, already running. Bram sprints toward the upper passages, shouting warnings.  
  
Pressure folds through the corridor—air bending, gravity tilting in the wrong direction. The Underface vibrates in harmonic protest, every pipe and beam singing in frequencies that mean failure.  
  
Jhace moves worker to worker, clearing the radius. Every second stretches thin. Every vibration carries escalation.  
  
Somewhere up-slope, he knows, a glassworker is probably ignoring warning tremors to finish some mathematically perfect calibration. Tiffani Merrow—precision-obsessed, by Guild rumor. Rejects proposals for structural insufficiency, refuses shortcuts, calculates beyond practicality. Workers say she trusts numbers more than people.  
  
He’s never met her. Doesn’t plan to.  
  
The air pressure shifts again—harder this time. The hum fractures into a metal scream.  
  
By Striking phase, everything beneath Saint Merro is shaking.  
  
The Piercing has begun.

# CHAPTER 2: “WARPED GLASS”  
  
Tiffani Merrow recalibrates the alignment scope again. Not because it needs it—the calibration has held for three Tides straight—but because vigilance demands constant verification. Stillness is where errors form. If she stops observing, patterns shift in the gaps.  
  
This is premise, not paranoia. Structure carved from failure she refuses to repeat.  
  
The observatory crowns Saint Merro’s eastern rim. From here, horizon lines run unbroken across the district’s sprawl, each one mapped in her memory—angles measured, refractive behavior cataloged, deviations tracked to margins smaller than a fingernail’s width at distance.  
  
Most people see sky. She sees variables.  
  
Something bends them.  
  
A subtle warp interrupts the atmospheric line—a vertical seam where refraction skews wrong, the kind of distortion that does not belong to wind, heat, or weather. She watches it without blinking. Checks again. Same irregularity. Same interference.  
  
Kess approaches from behind, carrying water and wrapped bread. “You’ve been up since Kindling. Nothing changes that fast.”  
  
“Everything changes that fast.” She keeps her eye to the scope. “Whether we measure it or not.”  
  
He sets the water beside her. Doesn’t argue further. He’s learned her patterns—precision not as habit, but armor.  
  
The seam darkens.  
  
Tiffani shifts to the trajectory scope. Fingers move in clean sequences across her slate. Glass refraction index. Atmospheric density gradient. Angular displacement. Distortion velocity. She runs it twice. The anomaly intensifies.  
  
Then the spindle breaches the cloud deck.  
  
A descending object, long and smooth, cutting atmosphere with force that bends light around its mass. Not a ship. Not a storm. A projectile. Controlled descent, almost vertical.  
  
She calculates impact before she feels fear. Southern coastal region. Far from Saint Merro. Safe. For now.  
  
But the displacement pattern is wrong—too deep, too violent, as if the object intends to travel farther than surface.  
  
“Kess,” she says. “Evacuate the observatory.”  
  
He stares upward. “Will it hit—”  
  
“Evacuate.” Her voice stays even. Command calibrated to urgency without panic.  
  
He runs.  
  
She stays. Continues measuring. Records deviation curves. Documents angular drift. Every data point matters; understanding an event requires precision, not fear.  
  
Somewhere below, she assumes, a forge-worker is ignoring structural stress to finish some overdue project. Jhace Torrin—known for rejecting designs others accept, refusing commissions deemed “perfectly safe” by reasonable standards. She’s heard he trusts instinct more than analysis. A walking contradiction: a man who calls calculations “limitations” yet claims structural mastery.  
  
She’s wrong about him. Completely.  
  
But her attention remains fixed on the descending spindle. On the way light shears around its edges. On the deepening seam in the sky.  
  
Atmospheric pressure drops. The sky darkens along the vertical line, bending inward as though pulled by something massive.  
  
The projectile accelerates.  
  
She measures the angle to the horizon. Measures distortion rate. Measures the shift in thermal gradient. None of it predicts what will happen next.  
  
The Piercing is coming.  
  
She just doesn’t know what it means yet.

# CHAPTER 3: “THE PIERCING”  
  
The world breaks in a straight line.  
  
Shaping phase has barely settled when the first groan shudders through the Underface. Not a tremor—tremors flick and pass. This sound drags, a grinding pull from deep bedrock as if the planet is reconsidering its architecture.  
  
Jhace braces a hand to the riser wall. The grid hum drops half a pitch. Then another. The vibration running through the metal warps into an off-tone that coils in his teeth.  
  
“Up,” Bram says. No argument. “If the foundation starts choosing directions, we don’t stay to watch.”  
  
They climb.  
  
By the time they reach the street, the air already tastes of dust and hot metal. Shaping light cuts through haze that wasn’t there at Kindling. People stand in doorways—listening, calculating escape paths without admitting that’s what they’re doing.  
  
The second movement hits.  
  
The ground rolls in a long, slow wave. Buildings lean. Correct. Lean again. A cart overturns. Glass bursts in shimmering scatter. Far below, something deep in the Underface fails with a hollow boom.  
  
Jhace’s lungs pull grit. Copper tang on tongue.  
  
Then the sky splits.  
  
Directly above Saint Merro’s midline, a perfect vertical seam shears through blue. No clouds. No storm. Just a line too sharp for nature, too exact for coincidence.  
  
Light erupts along the seam—white at the center, edged in burning orange, haloed in darkness where atmosphere tears.  
  
People stare upward.  
  
The thing emerges.  
  
A spindle of dark metal punches out of the planet, rising through the torn sky with no deceleration, dragging a column of vapor and copper-gold particulate in its wake. No curvature. No arc. A straight-through trajectory that says this is not impact but exit.  
  
For three breaths it is visible—longer than any tower, fluted channels glowing white and copper inside its dark skin.  
  
Then it clears the last drag of atmosphere and vanishes into higher blue.  
  
The hole remains.  
  
A vertical column of absence extends from street to stratosphere—air stripped clean, color bent wrong at its edges. Saint Merro leans toward the void.  
  
Then the air slams back.  
  
The shockwave hits like a hammer. Pressure detonates outward from the column’s base. Windows implode. Roof tiles shear off. People are flung across stone. The sound follows—deep, rib-rattling, a single impossible note of the planet in pain.  
  
Jhace hits the ground hard. Stone tears skin from his palms. The world heaves sideways.  
  
His forge collapses in front of him—front pillars splitting, roof rolling, rear wall tearing off in a slow, inevitable fold. One apprentice sprints out the door as the support beam above her gives.  
  
Chloen.  
  
The beam follows her name.  
  
It crashes across her legs, pinning her thigh to stone. Blood pools fast. Heat radiates from the timber, saturated with forge-fire.  
  
Jhace runs.  
  
Around him, Saint Merro unravels. A tower buckles inward. A balcony tears free, taking screaming workers with it. A full street seam splits open, swallowing stalls and the people between them.  
  
He reaches Chloen. Her face is grey with shock.  
  
“Can’t feel—” She gasps. “—my legs.”  
  
The beam has crushed stone where it landed. Dragging her out tears the leg. Lifting wrong shifts the collapsing wall overhead.  
  
Grid diagrams flash unbidden—load paths, failure points. None match this chaos.  
  
If I don’t act, she dies.  
  
No space for anything else.  
  
Jhace wedges his shoulders under the beam. Copper-gold dust coats his palms, grit settling into every crease.  
  
He lifts.  
  
The beam does not move.  
  
Muscles strain. Vision narrows. Weight mocks effort.  
  
Hold. Just hold long enough.  
  
The command focuses so sharply that something beneath thought answers.  
  
Heat blooms in his palms—not from the timber, but from bone and tendon. Chest pressure surges outward. Copper-gold light threads along the beam’s cracks, seeping into splintered grain. Microfractures knit. Not fully. Enough.  
  
The weight redistributes.  
  
The beam rises—a fingerwidth, two.  
  
“Now!” Jhace grunts.  
  
Chloen drags herself free, teeth clenched against the scream. The crushed leg trails. When she clears the arc, his strength collapses.  
  
The beam slams down.  
  
Jhace stumbles back. Vision greys. His hands lock in spasms—violent tremor radiating up his arms and into his chest. Breath rasps. Muscles misfire. Strength drains in a hollowing rush.  
  
Stage 2 lands instantly.  
  
Around them, the Piercing’s shock continues ripping the district apart. To the west, grid supports cascade in failure—each collapse marked by plumes of dust and copper-gold ash. People run in chaotic vectors—some toward screams, some away.  
  
Chloen props herself against a wall, pale but conscious.  
  
“You—” She swallows. “You held it.”  
  
Jhace looks at his hands. Copper-gold tracery burns across his skin, following the same lines that lit through the beam. The tremor turns his fingers into unreliable tools.  
  
“I’m not done,” he says.  
  
Screaming rises from farther up the street. A half-dropped balcony. Pinned bodies. No time.  
  
One saved. Dozens not.  
  
Guilt hits heavier than the beam.  
  
Chloen manages a broken smile. “Go. I’m not going anywhere. You’re better at holds than walking right now.”  
  
He exhales—half laugh, half cough.  
  
Then he moves. Because stopping means letting more people die while he watches.  
  
Each step is a negotiation with legs that want to fold and lungs that want to fail. The tremor runs constant, a new baseline written into nerves.  
  
Above, the sky still bears the vertical wound the spindle carved. Clouds distort around it. Light bends.  
  
The Piercing has left.  
  
Its cost has not.

# CHAPTER 4: “FIRST MEETING”  
  
Glass sings before it fails.  
  
The observatory dome climbs past its tolerance pitch—a thin, rising note that cuts through Tiffani Merrow’s concentration. The alignment scope vibrates under her hands. Chalk lines blur on the slate.  
  
Pressure hits a breath later.  
  
Air slams against the eastern face of the dome. Frame bolts shear in sequence. The world lurches sideways as a shockwave ripples out from Saint Merro’s midline—the place where the sky tore and the spindle punched up through atmosphere and kept going.  
  
The floor tilts. The scope tears from its mount. The dome’s glass panels fracture along geometric lines she did calculate and still underestimated.  
  
The observatory collapses inward.  
  
Tiffani pushes off from the scope platform, aiming for the doorway. The floor shifts under her boots—angle changing faster than balance can. A support beam, wrenched free from its housing, drops across her lower leg right at the threshold.  
  
Bone gives before the wood does.  
  
Pain spikes—sharp, local, defining. Shin. Midpoint. Fracture, likely compound. The weight pins muscle to stone. Her body folds awkwardly, half inside the observatory, half in the open where the exterior stair begins.  
  
Debris follows.  
  
Glass rains around her in a glittering cascade. Timber groans overhead as the structure leans south, pulled by whatever the Piercing has done to gravity. Copper-gold dust from the planetary wound hangs in the air, coating everything—skin, glass, exposed wood—with faint metallic shimmer.  
  
Tiffani catalogs through the pain.  
  
Load path: beam resting on stone at one end, on twisted frame at the other, her leg trapped between. If she pulls without lift, flesh tears. If she tries to shove from this angle, force runs straight into the fracture.  
  
Extraction requires leverage she does not have.  
  
Error from missed variable kills people. Marisse’s blood in the glasshouse. This collapse now. If she misjudged the spindle’s trajectory, if distance or angle or exit vector were wrong—  
  
Not useful. She cuts the thought. Crisis allows no space for indulgent guilt.  
  
Hands find the beam. Rough grain, splinters, copper-gold dust ground into the fibers. She pushes. The beam does not move. Pain flares bright up her leg, but the timber only digs deeper into the already-broken bone.  
  
Intent narrows.  
  
Move. Just enough to change the angle.  
  
Heat gathers in her palms—not friction, not exertion, something deeper. The pattern of stress inside the beam lights up in her awareness: thin, bright lines running through wood and frame, showing exactly where load concentrates, where resistance accumulates, where a small change shifts everything.  
  
Form stops being abstract. It becomes perception.  
  
Copper-gold filaments trace along the beam’s internal fractures, starting where her skin touches wood. They don’t knit damage; they re-route it. Pressure flows away from the point crushing her leg into the beam’s farther supports.  
  
The weight changes.  
  
The beam lifts a finger-width. Two.  
  
She drags herself backward with her arms, hauling her leg free from the worst of the pinch. Bone grinds. Skin tears. She clears the beam’s direct press just as it settles back onto stone with a heavy thud.  
  
Her leg is no longer trapped under that load.  
  
But it’s broken in at least one place, maybe two. Blood soaks through torn fabric. The limb refuses to bear weight. Movement becomes drag—arms pulling, body trailing, splintered shin leaving a smeared line behind her.  
  
She makes it fully outside, onto the first stretch of stone stair. Behind her, the observatory creaks. Glass and frame slide, slow and unstoppable, inching toward the slope that will feed the whole structure into the distant vertical absence where the spindle exited the world.  
  
The district below is noise.  
  
Buildings failing. People screaming. Grid-hum gone, replaced by a lower city-wide groan as infrastructure shifts load toward the hole the Piercing left behind.  
  
Tiffani braces her back against the stair wall, breath measured in shallow pulls. Leg straightened as best the angle allows. Calculation runs whether she invites it or not.  
  
The observatory is lost. The data with it. The error margin on her last projections will never be recoverable now. The glasshouse. Marisse. The dome. How many collapses define a life?  
  
Footsteps crunch through broken glass.  
  
Someone moves up the stair from the street—methodical pace, not panicked. A broad-shouldered figure resolves through dust and copper-gold haze. Work-stained clothing. Hands marked with old burns and new copper-gold tracery that glints faint even without active Resonance.  
  
The tremor in those hands is visible even in rest.  
  
He stops two steps below her. Eyes take in the beam, the angle of her leg, the leaning observatory, the street beyond where part of the rim wall has already fallen.  
  
“Where is it worst?” he asks.  
  
Not Are you hurt? Not Can you stand?  
  
Where.  
  
Tiffani blinks once. Reorients. “Shin,” she says. “Midpoint. Bone broken. Pressure point was there—” she taps the air above her limb “—but I shifted the beam. The real problem now is here.”  
  
She indicates the timber still half-blocking the stair just above her, wedged against the doorway frame. “If it slips while you move me, the rest of the structure comes down onto both of us.”  
  
He steps up beside the beam, studies the geometry without touching it. The tremor in his fingers intensifies when he raises his hands, but his voice stays steady.  
  
“If I drag you straight back,” he says, “the leg tears. If I lift from the wrong point, I lose the housing and the beam jumps.”  
  
“Correct,” Tiffani answers. “The stress is running through that joint there.” She pulls chalk from her pocket—habit surviving even this—and marks a line along the beam, then taps two points where force can safely travel. “Load wants to pivot here. If you stabilize there, you buy us enough time to clear.”  
  
He doesn’t argue. Doesn’t say I know how to lift a beam. He places his hands exactly where she marked, tremor making first contact rough but settling as he braces.  
  
“On your count,” he says. “Tell me when.”  
  
She looks up the stair—measures the lean, calculates how much shift the doorway can survive before full collapse. “When I say ‘now,’ lift and hold. Do not chase the movement if it creaks. Let it lean into you, don’t fight the whole arc.”  
  
He nods once.  
  
Copper-gold glows under his palms, brighter where his skin meets wood. Wholeness-bias floods into the beam, sealing its worst cracks just enough that it behaves like a single piece for a few breaths.  
  
“Ready,” he says.  
  
“Now.”  
  
He lifts. The beam comes up a handspan, no more. The doorway squeals protest. Debris shifts above, but doesn’t fall.  
  
Pain spikes sharp as Tiffani drags herself downslope, using her arms and good leg. Broken bone grinds against itself, sending cold sweat across her scalp, but the limb clears the beam’s previous path.  
  
“Clear,” she gasps.  
  
He lets the weight go. The beam crashes down where her leg was, sending a fresh shiver through the already-damaged frame.  
  
For a moment, neither of them moves.  
  
His hands shake uncontrollably now—Stage 2 cost riding an already-bad baseline. Copper-gold tracery on his skin is brighter than before, fine lines etched deep as if the light carved them there.  
  
Her tremor is smaller but new—fine vibration starting in her fingers from the earlier self-extraction. Stage 1 pushing toward Stage 2.  
  
“You used my marks,” she says when she can catch breath fully.  
  
“You mapped the force,” he answers. “Most people shout at me to hurry. You told me where it actually matters.”  
  
His Underface accent sits thick on the vowels. His eyes are already tracking beyond her—down toward the district, where shouting and crashing continue.  
  
“You’re Jhace Torrin,” she realizes aloud. “The forge-master who refuses welded arches and rejects half the Guild’s rush work.”  
  
“Opinions differ on the reasons,” he says. “You’re Tiffani Merrow. The observatory problem.”  
  
She arches a brow. “Problem.”  
  
“Glassworker who calls half our supports inadequate and sends redesigns back with more chalk than original plans.” No heat in it—just fact. “You just saved us both a collapse. So either the rumors are wrong, or the complaints are.”  
  
She files that away for later. “Can you carry me?” she asks. “Leg won’t tolerate load.”  
  
“Yes.” He doesn’t hesitate. “But the district is worse downslope. If we go to the medical station, I won’t be back to the junction arches before the next failure.”  
  
She runs the calculus automatically. “Then route through the arches. Drop me at the first stable triage point. That lets you check the load on the way.”  
  
He studies her, measuring more than the break in her leg. “You’ll tolerate that?”  
  
“I prefer correct priorities to comfort,” she says. “Medical after infrastructure. Otherwise you’ll have more than one broken leg waiting.”  
  
A flicker of something close to approval moves across his face. “All right.”  
  
He gathers her with careful geometry—one arm behind shoulders, the other under thighs, keeping the broken shin aligned. He adjusts when she hisses breath through her teeth, shifting grip until forces on the limb equalize.  
  
“Say if the angle goes wrong,” he tells her.  
  
“I will.”  
  
They move.  
  
The city around them is half dust, half sharp edges. Smoke and copper hang in the air. Screams spike and fall. Twice Jhace slows to judge a failing wall or an overburdened brace; twice she gives rapid stress assessments, marking with words where she lacks chalk.  
  
“Load can hold another two Breaths,” she says at one sagging arch. “But only if you get support under that left-hand joint.”  
  
He grabs a passing Guild runner. “Timber under that joint, now. Or you lose the whole span.”  
  
The runner obeys without question. People have started to listen when he says hold and lose.  
  
By the time they reach the ad hoc medical station—a commandeered warehouse whose roof still sits mostly true—Tiffani is shivering with exhausted pain. Jhace’s tremor runs unchecked through his arms, each step a negotiation between strength and collapse.  
  
He sets her down gently onto a waiting pallet. A healer moves in with splints and bandages, hands already assessing damage.  
  
“You should sit,” Tiffani tells him. “You’re past safe cost.”  
  
“No.” He looks back toward the street. “There are more structures ready to fail.”  
  
“There are,” she agrees. “And you won’t be able to hold any of them if you fall in the next corridor.” Her voice stays even. “Go to ground now. Breathe. Water. Then go back out. That’s the sustainable pattern.”  
  
He meets her eyes. There’s sand there—grit and refusal and a calculating hesitance that wasn’t present before he lifted that beam on her marks.  
  
“You listened,” she adds. “To my geometry. I need you to listen to this too.”  
  
He exhales once, hard. “I hate that you’re right.”  
  
“Good,” she says. “You’re less likely to forget it.”  
  
He sits. Not gracefully. More like the decision pulls his legs out from under him as soon as he gives them permission. Someone presses a cup of water into his shaking hands. He drinks.  
  
The healer finishes the first layer of splinting on her leg. “You’re lucky,” they murmur. “Could have lost this.”  
  
Luck has little to do with it, in Tiffani’s estimation. Geometry and timing. A stranger’s hands on the right stress points. Form meeting Wholeness under a collapsing frame.  
  
Jhace glances sideways at her. “We’ll need to go back out,” he says.  
  
“Yes,” she answers. “But not alone.”  
  
Something settles between them then—not gratitude, not obligation, but recognition of matched edges. Where one fails, the other holds. Where one sees structure, the other feels it.  
  
The Piercing has cracked the world open.  
  
In the wreckage, two people who misjudged each other completely have just learned, under load, that together they can keep more standing than either could alone.  
  
The cost will grow.  
  
So will the work.  
  
So will this.  
  
Whatever it is.

# CHAPTER 5: “FIRST HOLD”  
  
One Tide after the Piercing, the mid-district arch groans like a wounded creature trying to stand.  
  
Jhace reaches it at a run, emergency timber bouncing against his shoulder. The arch spans the corridor that connects two dense blocks—stone residences on one side, wood-frame shops on the other. When the spindle tore through the world, the substructure shifted beneath everything. The arch stayed upright out of habit, not integrity. Now it’s leaning toward collapse.  
  
And people are still moving under it. Families clutching what they carried out. Guild retirees hauling injured neighbors. Every face glancing up once, silently begging the stones not to choose this moment to fall.  
  
Jhace drops the timber and assesses. A deep fissure runs through the eastern support—a vertical line where the keystone is trying to choose failure. The western joint is warping in sympathy. He can brace one side with raw strength, maybe, but not both.  
  
His tremor makes the first placement sloppy. Stage 2 baseline: the shake sits in his hands even before he lifts anything. He forces the timber into position anyway, shoves upward with his shoulder. The joint rises half an inch. No more.  
  
Footsteps behind him—uneven rhythm, brace clicking against stone.  
  
Tiffani arrives, leaning on a salvaged walking stick. Her face is bloodless with pain, but her attention is already mapping the arch’s angles. Her eyes move like surveying instruments.  
  
“You’re wasting effort,” she says.  
  
Jhace doesn’t look away from the joint. “People are still underneath.”  
  
“I’m aware.” She circles the arch, studying the fissure with practiced intensity. Then she pulls chalk from her pocket—habit surviving catastrophe—and marks the stone. Sharp lines. Clean geometry. Weight paths.  
  
Three stress points. Two redistribution options. One load arc.  
  
She doesn’t explain. She simply draws.  
  
Jhace watches the chalk like it’s revealing something he’s felt his whole life but never seen. He can sense where the fractures run; she shows him the exact picture of the thing he senses.  
  
“There,” she says, tapping two points. “And there. Hold both simultaneously. Seventy-thirty distribution. Not equal pressure. If you treat them evenly, the eastern joint will fail.”  
  
He places his hands on the points she indicated. The tremor makes initial contact rough, but the copper-gold dust from the previous Tide’s hold still clings to his palms—residue that conducts heat into cold stone.  
  
“Now,” she says.  
  
He focuses.  
  
Intent sharpens to two points at once—not lifting, but holding fractures in their current shape. Not letting them widen. Keeping collapse in pause.  
  
Wholeness-bias engages.  
  
Copper-gold traces crawl along the arch’s interior joints, following stress lines like running ink. Microfractures tighten. Not fully sealed—just enough for structure to listen.  
  
The arch steadies.  
  
Tiffani limps toward the corridor opening and signals the evacuation crew. “Move faster. We’re holding for a limited window.”  
  
People respond immediately, crossing under the stabilizing arch in a controlled surge. No panic—just the quiet discipline of a city that survived the Piercing by moving when told.  
  
Jhace’s arms shake more violently now. Tremor layered with fatigue layered with Soulpulse cost. His breath shortens. Vision narrows at the edges.  
  
“How many left?” he manages.  
  
Tiffani scans quickly. “Thirty. Hold.”  
  
He holds.  
  
When the last evacuee—a grandmother carrying a child—passes clear, the Guild bracing crew arrives with proper supports.  
  
“Release,” Tiffani says.  
  
Jhace does.  
  
The arch slumps but doesn’t fall—the crew slides timber and iron into place, bracing the weakened joints with engineering instead of desperation.  
  
Jhace staggers back. The tremor spikes to full-body intensity—Stage 2 brushing the edge of Stage 3. His knees go soft.  
  
Tiffani catches his arm with her free hand and guides him down until his back hits stone. Her own leg is trembling visibly from overuse.  
  
She grips his wrist. “Four counts in.”  
  
He inhales. One. Two. Three. Four.  
  
“Hold.”  
  
He holds.  
  
“Four counts out.”  
  
He exhales.  
  
They repeat the sequence until the catastrophic tremor retreats to its usual constant one. His vision steadies. Breath evens.  
  
Only then does she critique him.  
  
“You applied uniform force across both points.”  
  
His jaw tightens. “Yes?”  
  
“That’s wrong.” She gestures at the chalked stone. “Stress is asymmetrical. The eastern joint required seventy percent of your output. The western required thirty. Your fifty-fifty split under-supported one and wasted effort on the other.”  
  
The irritation spikes first—I just kept an arch from collapsing—but it’s swallowed quickly by clarity.  
  
She’s right.  
  
“If you shift to seventy-thirty,” she continues, “you achieve the same result with thirty percent less expenditure.”  
  
Thirty percent less cost. Thirty percent longer until Stage 3. Thirty percent more holds before collapse.  
  
“Show me,” he says.  
  
She blinks. A small, startled flick of surprise. “You want correction now?”  
  
“I want efficiency now.”  
  
Something eases behind her eyes—recognition, respect, a quiet alignment.  
  
She helps him stand and leads him to a partially collapsed wall. A safer test case. The mortar is cracked; the load path exposed.  
  
“Feel where the stress concentrates,” she says, placing his hands on the stone.  
  
He closes his eyes. Listens through bone, through tremor, through exhaustion. There—primary pressure point. And there—secondary.  
  
“Less here,” she says, touching the secondary. “More there. Intent follows geometry.”  
  
He directs his focus. More into the primary fracture. Less into the secondary.  
  
Copper-gold light follows instruction—uneven but accurate.  
  
The wall steadies under minimal force.  
  
“Better,” she says.  
  
And the word lands exactly as it should—not praise, not flattery. Calibration.  
  
Jhace looks at her—limping, chalk-stained, calculating—and realizes with sudden clarity:  
  
She doesn’t celebrate heroism. She optimizes it.  
  
He looks at his hands—trembling, cost-marked, copper-traced.  
  
“Can you continue?” she asks.  
  
Honestly? Another major hold will tip him into Stage 3.  
  
But people need stabilizing. Structures need reinforcing. The city is breathing unevenly.  
  
“Yes,” he says. “With breaks. And your geometry.”  
  
Tiffani nods once. “Then we continue. Together.”  
  
They move toward the next collapsing segment—her limping, him shaking—thinking in convergent lines neither had to articulate.  
  
Behind them, the arch stands solid on proper bracing.  
Not because one of them held it.  
  
Because both did.  
  
They’re learning.

# CHAPTER 6: “HEAT AND HAMMER”  
  
Six nights of the same dream.  
  
Chloen’s leg under the beam. His hands shaking too hard to grip. The structure sliding past failure while he watches, useless.  
  
On the seventh, Jhace stops pretending sleep will come.  
  
Kindling has barely started when he leaves the bunkhouse and walks to Vehr’s forge.  
  
The old master keeps his door unlatched—always has. Any smith who needs the fire can use it, so long as they leave the place better than they found it and hang a piece of work on the wall as payment. Jhace has hung eleven brackets there over three Turns.  
  
Tonight will be twelve.  
  
He pushes the door open. Dark interior, familiar shapes. Anvil, slack tub, tool rack. The smell hits first—coal dust, old ash, oil, iron. No people. Just potential.  
  
He lights a small lamp and checks the hearth.  
  
Too much ash has built up in the firebox. It clogs the grate, caking around the tuyere where air pushes into the coals. If he throws new fuel on top of that, the fire will choke before it starts.  
  
He takes the ash rake and pulls the dead gray weight forward, tipping it into the waste bin until the grate clears and the mouth of the tuyere shows dark and clean.  
  
Fire needs air. Air needs a path.  
  
Kindling first. Thin splits of dry hardwood, arranged loose enough for air to move between them. He stacks them in a small pyramid, instinctively building structure. Then a shallow ring of coal around, not on, the wood. Too much fuel too early smothers everything.  
  
He strikes flint to tinder, coaxes flame into the kindling. Wood catches, smoke curls up, then burns clear.  
  
He works the bellows.  
  
Slow, steady pulls. Air whispers through the tuyere, feeds the growing fire from beneath. The coals shift from dull black to red, then to orange as heat climbs through the bed.  
  
His hands tremble through each pull—Stage 2 baseline now, constant—but the motion eats the shaking. Push, pull, breathe. The tremor becomes part of the rhythm instead of fighting it.  
  
He watches the coals. Not the whole fire—specific parts.  
  
The center brightens toward yellow-white while the outer ring sits in deep orange. That central cone is the sweet spot: hot enough to move iron, not hot enough to burn it into useless scale. He adds coal around the edges, building a reservoir of heat that will feed the core when he works.  
  
Too much air and the fire roars, throwing heat he can’t use and wasting fuel. Too little and it sulks into smoke. The fire gives honest feedback. No lies. No politics. Just combustion, following rules it never breaks.  
  
He turns to the scrap rack.  
  
Most of the stock here is salvage from the Piercing—bent rail, twisted brackets, bars with stress cracks frozen mid-failure. He runs his fingers along three different lengths of iron, feeling for hidden fractures and slag lines. Scale flakes under his thumb where surfaces overheated and cooled wrong.  
  
He takes a file from the rack and scrapes the edge of the first bar. The file skates and chatters—too hard, likely cold-short, inclined to snap without warning. The second bar lets the teeth bite, taking a clean, even cut. Good structural iron. The third shudders under the file, grain catching in irregular pulses—too many inclusions in the metal.  
  
He sets the second bar aside. Flawed, but workable.  
  
Like him.  
  
He scrubs black scale from the surface with a wire brush. Any crust left on now will be hammered down into the metal and become a weak seam later.  
  
The bar goes into the fire—not buried in coals, but laid across the sweet spot so heat can wrap it evenly. One end points toward the tuyere, where the air pushes hardest. He rotates the bar every few breaths, watching color climb along its length.  
  
Black iron doesn’t move. Dull red moves reluctantly and tears. Orange begins to listen. Yellow-white is right. Pure white starts to throw tiny sparks—burning, not forging.  
  
The metal tells him when it’s ready. If he listens.  
  
He pulls it at yellow-white.  
  
Flat-jaw tongs grip the bar just behind the hot zone. He steps to the anvil—three strides, no wasted motion. The hammer waiting on the face is a cross-peen a little over three pounds. Heavy enough to move metal, light enough to swing all day if you know how to let the weight work for you.  
  
He grips the handle and the tremor surges, threatening to turn the first strike sideways.  
  
He raises the hammer anyway.  
  
Gravity does half the work. He lets it fall and guides the path. Steel meets iron on the center of the anvil’s face with a clear, ringing tone that travels up through his arms and into his ribs.  
  
The tremor vanishes into the blow.  
  
The iron bulges and flattens where he hit it, spreading away from the impact. He turns his wrist slightly, using the peen to draw the bar longer and thinner, working along the length in small, precise bites.  
  
He has maybe twenty heartbeats of good heat before the color drops and the iron refuses to move.  
  
He gets five clean strikes, then two less-clean ones that tell him the heat is leaving faster than he is shaping. Back to the fire.  
  
Heat. Color. Pull. Strike.  
  
The rhythm establishes itself. The shaking in his hands folds into the pattern—shake on the lift, steady on the drop, impact absorbing everything into the anvil’s mass.  
  
This he knows. This he can do.  
  
Third heat, he misreads the color and pulls too early. The iron is still deep orange when it hits the anvil. It moves, but grudgingly, edges crumbling instead of flowing. That gritty drag under the hammer is wrong.  
  
He stops after two blows. Forcing cold iron is how you drive cracks down into the grain where you can’t see them.  
  
Back to the fire. He rotates the bar more this time, making sure the underside matches the top. Uneven heating is as dangerous as not enough.  
  
Next pull, he overcorrects. Holds too long. Tiny sparks dance on the surface as he lifts the bar. The outer skin has started to burn—thin layer of metal turning to scale.  
  
He clamps down on the urge to curse. Burns can be worked around if they’re skin-deep. He shifts his target, working the area just behind the damage, drawing material forward to replace what he’s just ruined.  
  
Years of practice make the correction automatic. The iron doesn’t lie. It shows him exactly where he’s wrong and what it will take to fix it.  
  
The bracket grows out of the bar one heat at a time. He forges the corner over the rounded horn of the anvil, letting the metal bend around a curve instead of forcing it into a knife-edge that will crack under load. Each trip from fire to anvil, he works a few more breaths of shape into it—flange on one side for anchor bolts, back plate stout enough to carry serious weight.  
  
Heat time. Work time. Back to fire. Repeat.  
  
Sweat runs down his spine, sticking his shirt to his back. The front of his face prickles from standing near the open hearth. The air tastes of coal and hot iron, of scale burning away and water hissing off damp tools.  
  
His arms burn with the good fatigue that comes from a hundred deliberate strikes, not from holding a junction two Stretches past what his body could afford.  
  
Seventh heat, he notices one arm of the bracket pulling in tighter than the other as it cools. The bar wants to twist, shortening unevenly under its own tension.  
  
He catches it before it sets.  
  
He plunges just the warped leg into the slack tub. Water explodes into steam where hot iron hits. The bracket screams—a high, sharp sound as one side shrinks faster than the rest, pulling back toward true.  
  
He watches the shape from the corner of his eye, counting breaths. Too long in the tub and he’ll harden the whole thing into brittle glass. Too short and it won’t correct.  
  
He pulls it free. Steam trails from the surface. The warped arm sits closer to where it belongs now. He checks against a square—the bracket’s faces lie flush along both arms of the tool, inside radius kissing the metal without leaving daylight.  
  
Near enough for work that has to bear weight.  
  
Detail work next.  
  
He brings a smaller hammer—lighter face, more control—and uses the anvil horn to refine the inside of the corner, tapping instead of driving. Each small blow smooths another imperfection. He files along the length of the bracket, knocking down scale, rounding edges. Rough surfaces catch stress; tiny notches grow into cracks when load cycles through them a few hundred times.  
  
Smooth iron holds longer than rough iron. The material remembers every shape you force into it.  
  
One last heat. Not full forge temperature—just enough to bring the metal to a low glow. He draws it back from the heart of the coals and holds it near the edge, watching color creep along the surface: dark, then a hint of straw, then pale bronze.  
  
That’s tempering heat. Hard enough to carry load. Soft enough not to shatter under a shock.  
  
He lets it cool in still air, not water. Quiet cooling relaxes grain that quench hardened too far.  
  
When it’s dark again, he inspects it under the lamp.  
  
The bracket’s face is clean. No visible cracks from the quench. The corner holds its geometry. Both flanges lie flat and parallel. It carries its own weight without complaint.  
  
He made something that will hold.  
  
He turns it in his hands, feeling the faint tremor running through his fingers. The shaking hasn’t gone anywhere. It never does. But here, it doesn’t decide the work. The iron doesn’t care that his hands shake, only that the heat is right and the blows land where they should.  
  
Jhace crosses the forge and hangs the finished bracket on Vehr’s wall beside the others—eleven old shapes, each with its own story, and now this one. Payment for fuel and space. Proof he can still bring something from raw to right.  
  
He banks the coals, raking them back from the tuyere and dusting a layer of ash over the top to slow the cooling. Tools go back on their hooks. The floor gets a quick sweep—scale, slag, stray coal into the bin.  
  
Footsteps pause at the door. An uneven cadence. Familiar.  
  
Tiffani stands in the threshold, walking stick braced at her side. Shaping light from the street outlines her, but she doesn’t step in. She looks past him at the bracket on the wall, then at his hands.  
  
“Better?” she asks.  
  
He flexes his fingers. The tremor is still there—Stage 2 constant, copper-gold traces dull in the forge light. But his breathing has evened. His chest feels less like it’s being held in a vise.  
  
“Better,” he says.  
  
She nods once. No lecture about cost. No optimization. Just data accepted.  
  
“Vehr will be pleased,” she says.  
  
“He can complain to the bracket if he isn’t.”  
  
A faint curve touches her mouth. Not quite a smile. More an acknowledgment of shared language.  
  
She steps aside from the doorway to let him pass. They walk back toward the camp together, her stick clicking in rhythm with his bootfalls as the district shifts from Kindling into full Shaping.  
  
Behind them, the fire in Vehr’s forge cools at its own pace. The new bracket hangs on the wall, ready for whatever weight someone chooses to give it.  
  
It will hold.  
  
For tonight, that’s enough.

# CHAPTER 7: “STRUCTURAL THREAT”  
  
Two Tides after the Guild refusal, Saint Merro begins to fail in ways the Piercing did not cause.  
  
Jhace and Tiffani move together through the mid-district—a quiet, practiced tandem shaped by five Tides of crisis work. His tremor is Stage 2 constant, visible even at rest. Her leg has healed enough for weight-bearing, though uneven ground still anchors pain behind every step.  
  
The first collapse site looks normal for post-Piercing stress: shifted foundation, fractured stone, predictable load deviation.  
  
The second is wrong.  
  
Tiffani crouches beside a failed support bolt, her sleeve brushing ash. “This isn’t Piercing damage. Look.” She taps the bolt. The metal bends under her touch—softened, not snapped.  
  
Jhace presses his palm to the beam above it. Copper-gold residue on his hand hums faintly, responding to something unnatural embedded in the grain. “Entropy-bias. Someone weakened it on purpose.”  
  
The tilt of her head confirms the calculation she’s already running. “Structural sabotage. Targeted. Purposeful.”  
  
“And not amateur.” He gestures to the collapse footprint. “They knew which joints to weaken.”  
  
They check a third site. Then a fourth. All the same—entropy-softened bolts near valuable salvage caches. Each failure engineered to drop a building cleanly, forcing evacuation and panic. Perfect cover for theft.  
  
Evidence narrows their path toward a half-collapsed warehouse near the water main junction—one of the few structures still partially standing after the Piercing.  
  
Copper-gold particulate glows faintly on the doorframe.  
  
Fresh.  
  
Tiffani silently positions herself along the side access—eyes tracking load distribution, identifying every weak point in the structure.  
  
Jhace steps inside.  
  
The warehouse interior groans under strain. Half the ceiling sags, held by a single splintered beam. Crates lie burst open. Tools scattered. No figures. No movement.  
  
But the wrongness hums through the floor. Entropy traces smear along metal edges, pooled like fingerprints.  
  
“They were just here,” Jhace murmurs.  
  
Tiffani doesn’t answer. She’s studying the overhead structure with tense, mathematical attention.  
  
And then the sound comes.  
  
A sharp ping—subtle, almost polite.  
  
Then the ceiling shifts.  
  
A support beam gives way in an instant.  
  
Dust plumes upward. Ash-laced air implodes inward. The entire front half of the warehouse begins to fall.  
  
Tiffani moves first—no words, no warning. She slams her palm against the nearest wall joint and sends a precise Form-bias pulse through stone. It doesn’t stop the collapse. It changes its direction. Redirects the fall path by inches—just enough.  
  
Jhace sees it and moves into the redirected space before thinking. He catches the collapsing beam with both hands. Not to hold the whole structure—impossible. Just to slow it.  
  
Copper-gold flares beneath his fingers. Wholeness-bias pushes into cracked wood, sealing microfractures for the length of a breath.  
  
The beam hesitates.  
  
Tiffani uses that stolen second to pull a trapped worker out from beneath a splitting rafter. Jhace releases before his arms break under load and dives back as the warehouse collapses fully into a choking cloud of particulate.  
  
Silence follows—heavy, ash-thick, settling over ruin.  
  
The worker coughs painfully but stands. Alive.  
  
Tiffani meets Jhace’s eyes across the debris field. Both know: that collapse was engineered to kill anyone investigating. And they narrowly escaped because each predicted the other’s motion without speaking.  
  
She limps to him. He extends a shaking hand to steady her—not out of chivalry, but because coordination now exists as instinct.  
  
“They were watching us,” she says.  
  
“Or testing us.” His voice is low, rough. “Someone with entropy-bias wanted that ceiling to fall when we walked in.”  
  
“They know what we are.” Her gaze drops briefly to his tremor, then to the copper-gold traces along her own fingers. “That we’re using Soulpulse without oversight.”  
  
He exhales, breath shaking. “Tellis will use this. Say independent operators cause retaliation. Say we provoked it.”  
  
She doesn’t argue. Her silence is confirmation.  
  
Guild crews arrive shortly after to secure the perimeter. The saboteur is long gone—only entropy residue remains.  
  
Jhace and Tiffani walk away from the ruin side by side. His tremor worse than earlier. Her limp pronounced. Their shadows long against the broken street.  
  
“Are you hurt?” he asks quietly.  
  
“No,” she answers. “You?”  
  
“No.”  
  
They don’t separate as they walk. The distance between them stays small by choice, not necessity.  
  
Something unspoken has solidified—same shape as a brace under a failing beam, invisible until weight rests on it.  
  
They survived because they acted as one.  
  
And someone is now trying to break that.

# INTERLUDE I: **EDGES AND ECHOES**  
  
Salee presses both palms to the footbridge’s cracked timber and feels six hundred fears condensing into wood grain.  
  
Not metaphor. Perception.  
  
The commune’s dread runs through the structure like water through split stone—every parent who crossed this span carrying children, every elder who gripped the rail when balance failed, every Tide the flood rose another fingerwidth and someone wondered, *today?*  
  
The bridge holds their terror better than it holds their weight.  
  
She stands ankle-deep in mudwater, legs braced against the current’s sideways pull. The Piercing sent disruption rippling across Te’Oga in patterns no weather lore accounts for. This southern region took flooding. The bridge—old, necessary, splitting along its central beam—now carries hundreds of people’s only path between high ground and their stores.  
  
They asked her to look.  
  
She cannot leave.  
  
Wholeness hums in her blood. Not a choice. A compulsion. Where things fragment, where separation threatens, something in her marrow *pulls*—the way water finds cracks, the way roots find the soft seam in rock.  
  
She maps the bridge’s stress with her hands. Every splinter. Every rot-soft section. Every joint where metal should bind wood but doesn’t quite. The structure’s pain becomes hers—a diffuse ache spreading from fingertips to shoulders to spine.  
  
Most people would see: *Bridge broken. Needs replacement.* Walk away. Wait for proper engineers.  
  
Salee sees the old woman who crosses twice each Tide to check on her sister, hip too damaged for the longer route. Sees the family who stores grain on the far side because rats can’t swim. Sees the child who carries bread to her father’s workshop, small feet careful on warped planks.  
  
She sees all of them at once.
