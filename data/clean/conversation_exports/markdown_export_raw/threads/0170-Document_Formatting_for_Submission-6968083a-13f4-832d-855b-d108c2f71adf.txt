N01: THE GRIP THAT HOLDS

CHAPTER 1: THE BELL

The bell rang wrong. Not the slow warning cadence. Not the measured strike that meant "check your seals, tighten your brackets." This was the frantic peal of something already falling, the sound of a structure begging for a hold. Jhace was moving before the bell finished its third strike. He grabbed the wrong ring of collar keys on the first yank. Swore, dumped them into his palm, and sorted by feel while he ran. One key skittered off the stair edge and disappeared into the dark. He didn’t go back for it.

His kit bounced against his hip. Chalk. Slate. Collar keys. Tools for measuring failure, not stopping it. He didn't know why his legs were carrying him toward the sound instead of away. Didn't know why his heart counted the bell strikes like they were orders. One. Two. Three. Four. The doubled harmonic hit him at Junction Twelve. It skipped the pipe-hum entirely and punched straight into his sternum. His ribs buzzed like struck iron. His teeth hurt. His mouth flooded metal. Copper. Sharp and immediate, like he'd bitten through wire. He'd been tasting it for two Tides now. Faint at first. A ghost-flavor that came and went with the grid-hum. But this was different. This flooded his mouth so thick he wanted to spit. He didn't. Underface rule: waste nothing. His hands started shaking. Then the shaking cut out for three heartbeats and he thought, stupidly, his fingers had finally died. And then the tremor came back twice as fast.

It wasn't cold or exertion. It was a fine vibration that made his grip feel unreliable, like his muscles were half-taking orders and half-refusing them. He jammed them into his coat pockets and kept running. He forced his breath to slow. Counted to ten without looking at the numbers. The tremor kept going. He pulled one hand out. Squeezed it into a fist until the knuckles burned. Released. Still shaking. It wasn't aftershock. It was something settling into him—fine vibration under the skin—the kind that makes you drop a tool and swear at your own hand. He’d seen it on old manifestors in the deep tunnels. Hands that never found stillness. He’d called it age. Now he understood, because his fingers kept twitching against his thigh like a bad motor that wouldn't shut off. Tallow Street came into view through the access tunnel: upper terrace facade folded inward, stone slabs riding down on support beams that should have held twice that load. Dust cloud thick enough to choke on. People screaming from inside the collapse. Someone rang a hand-bell hard enough to crack its cadence. Emergency. Emergency. Emergency. A foreman waved at him, face streaked with ash. "We need a hold. We don't have time." Jhace looked at the structure. The failure line wasn't clean. The whole building was trying to become rubble, one breath at a time. Load shifts rippled through it like a living animal struggling to die. Stone groaned. Iron squealed. No crew could wedge that fast. The wedge bucket was still half‑full. Someone was still tying their apron when the stone started to go. The people inside didn't have margin. "I'm not—" Jhace started. The heat arrived. Not external. Internal. A sudden furnace-flare behind his sternum that made him gasp. Like someone had lit a coal bed under his ribs and told it to burn clean. Copper-gold dust swirled around his boots. He watched it climb his legs like static. Like it was looking for him. His chest burned hotter. The doubled harmonic snapped into alignment inside him—two mis-timed gears finally meshing. The wrongness in the grid-hum became rightness in his blood. His heartbeat and the city's failing pulse locked into the same rhythm. He didn't know what he was doing. He didn't know why his feet carried him under the broken lintel. He moved because the archway dust was already falling in steady sheets and somebody inside was screaming a name he couldn't ignore. He put both hands on the main support column. Light—thin, copper-gold, filament-fine—threaded from his palms into the stone seam. The building stopped moving. It was only paused—still wrong, still dangerous. Jhace's breath shook out of him. He could feel every shift in load through his fingertips: the weight of stone, the tension in iron, the panic-motion of bodies inside. The heat in his chest flared with each micro-adjustment, like his body was paying for every heartbeat of stability in increments he couldn't count. "Move," he rasped. "Get them out." Boots ran. Voices shouted coordinates. Someone coughed dust and kept crawling. A woman slid into his peripheral vision—practical coat, hair tied back, slate already in hand despite the chaos. She didn't look at him like a miracle. She looked at him like a system that could fail. "How long can you hold?" she demanded. Jhace's jaw clenched. The heat in his chest spiked. "I don't know." "Then we find out." She stepped into the dust, coughed once, and shouted to the evac crew. "Clear corridor A first. Count bodies out. No guessing." Jhace tried to turn his head. The moment he shifted his attention, the heat flared sharper—punishment for looking away. "I have to watch the load," he said through his teeth. "Then watch it," she snapped, already marking a line on her slate. "I'll watch you." People came out in bursts: three, then five, then a cluster carrying an older man whose leg bent wrong. Each time the load shifted, Jhace's hands tightened and the filament-light brightened in response. His heartbeat counted itself in his ears. One hundred. One hundred twenty. His arms started to tremble. Not from fatigue. From something deeper. A fine vibration that spread into his forearms, up to his shoulders, making his grip feel like it belonged to someone else. "Hold steady," the woman said, quieter now, close enough he could hear her through the dust. He didn't answer. Answering took breath. At two hundred heartbeats, the tremor spread into his chest. At two-twenty, his vision narrowed at the edges, copper-gold sparks flickering across it like welding flash. "How many?" he rasped. The woman didn't look up from the evac line. "Ten. Eleven. Twelve—" A scream from inside. A child. High and thin and suddenly cut off. Jhace's hands shook harder. "No," he hissed, and the filament-light surged, burning his palms numb. "Go," the woman shouted toward the breach. "Last sweep. Now." A rescuer crawled under the paused beam line and dragged a small body out by the shoulders. The child coughed dust and cried like she'd been holding it in for too long. The woman's voice cut clean through the chaos. "Twelve out. Corridor clear." Jhace felt the building try to move again the instant she said it, like it had been waiting for permission. "Terminate," she said. No softness in it. Not cruelty. Protocol. Jhace let the filament go. The building settled the last inch it had been denied. Stone grinding. Iron squealing. But the void where bodies had been was now empty. He staggered back into open air. His hands wouldn't stop shaking. The tremor was small—millimeters—but it was relentless. Like a ghost living inside his muscles, counting the dead he'd prevented and refusing to be quiet about the cost. The woman stepped beside him, dust streaked across her face. "Name." "Jhace Torrins." She wrote it without looking, chalk skating on sweat. "Duration: two‑forty—" The chalk snapped. She swore under her breath and wrote it anyway. "Don't," he snapped. Too hard. Too fast. She met his eyes. Her gaze was flat, measuring, absolutely certain. "If we don't measure it," she said, "we kill you next time." Between bells, they made it back to the workshop long enough to rinse dust off skin and reset slates. Jhace sat at his bench and waited for the chest heat to fade. It didn’t. Not fully. A banked warmth stayed behind his sternum—steady as a coal bed that refused to go cold. He pressed his palm to his chest. Felt nothing through shirt and ribs. Inside, the warmth answered anyway. Once. Twice. Like the world remembering how it had fit together when he held. His hand lingered there a heartbeat too long. Not thinking. Just reaching. He pulled it away like it burned. The tremor kept moving, but for one heartbeat—only one—the shake eased, like his body preferred the burn to the break. That was the first time the hunger felt like a vote. Jhace’s hands shook between them like evidence he hadn't wanted. He swallowed copper. Tasted grit. Looked at the child being carried past them on a makeshift stretcher—alive, crying, covered in dust but breathing. He nodded once. "Measure," he said. "Then make sure there isn't a next time." The woman's slate scratched. She wrote: Jhace Torrins. Inspector. First manifestation. Tallow Street. Tide 0. Behind them, the bell rang again. Different street. Same dust in his teeth. Same bell tearing its own throat. Jhace looked down and realized he was still gripping air. Copper sat on his tongue. He went anyway.

CHAPTER 2: THE PATTERN

The theodolite wouldn't settle. Tiffani had calibrated it three times in this Tide. Clean lens. Level base. Reference stars locked. Everything aligned the way it had been for twenty-six Turns of lens work. The crosshair still drifted. No shimmer. No warp. Just the star sitting wrong in the frame—like someone had nudged the sky with a thumb while she wasn't looking. She pressed her palm to the primary lens frame. Vibration. Faint. Constant. The doubled harmonic lived under the normal hum like a shadow heartbeat. It made her molars ache and her lens hand tremble. She pulled her hand away and wrote on her slate: Tide 0 — Kindling Lens frame vibration: doubled harmonic present. Star Seven-Three displacement: 0.34 marks off true. Neither atmosphere nor mechanism. Her mouth tasted copper. She'd been ignoring it for the last two Tides. Faint metallic tang that came and went with air currents from the ventilation shafts. But this was different. This arrived sharp and immediate, flooding her tongue like she'd been chewing wire. The light outside the lens port changed. The light didn’t dim or brighten. It bent. Tiffani looked up. for a heartbeat she couldn't move. Her brain offered one sentence, raw and useless: What the fuck is that? The sky over Saint Merro wasn't supposed to do anything except hold weather and stars. this Tide it held something else. A line. Straight. Dark. Moving too fast for her eyes to track at first, then slowing enough for her to understand what she was seeing. A spindle. Long and narrow and wrong, like someone had driven a forged bar through the world and walked away. Her breath stopped. For a stupid next, she couldn’t make her fingers obey. She stood there with her hand half-raised, as if she could physically stop the sky from doing whatever it was doing.

The spindle crossed her view in a single heartbeat. No friction fire. No sound. Just a displacement of light that made the air look thin. Then gravity lurched. Not a shift sideways. An up-and-down stagger, like the planet had been grabbed and shaken once. Her slate slid across the stone desk. A lens clamp snapped. Glass sang a sharp, stressed note and went silent. Tiffani caught herself on the observation frame and forced her eyes back to the port. The spindle was gone. Punched through. Somewhere below. Into stone or ocean or the Underface—she couldn't tell. Pressure arrived a breath later. Not a blast. More like the city inhaling and forgetting how to exhale. Dust fell from the ceiling mortar in a slow, dry drift. Then copper-gold particulate began falling outside. Glittering. Fine. Catching the lamplight like ground glass in a furnace beam. It coated the stone lip of the observation port and clung to the iron frame in flecks that shimmered when she moved. Tiffani touched it with her fingertip. It didn't smear like ash. It gritted. Abrasive. Her finger tingled where the dust touched skin. She wrote: Atmospheric intrusion event. Spindle-form object. Estimated velocity: extreme. Copper-gold particulate fall: immediate post-impact. Adheres to metal preferentially. Abrasive. Conductive? Her pencil slipped. The last word smeared into a dark streak. She stared at it, angry at her own hand, then rewrote the line harder than necessary.

A bell rang. Not the Glass-House alert. Distant. Down-terrace. The frantic peal of something already falling. Then another bell. Different direction. Same cadence. Emergency. Emergency. Emergency. Tiffani's stomach tightened. She grabbed her slate, shoved it into her coat pocket, and ran. The doubled harmonic followed her down the Glass-House stairs. She could feel it in the stone railing—vibration that hadn't been there earlier this Tide. The walls themselves were listening to something new. At the mid-terrace landing, she stopped. Tallow Street was visible from here: upper facade collapsed inward, dust cloud thick enough to choke on. People running. Voices screaming coordinates she couldn't parse. A third bell rang. Sector Nine. Grain district. Closer—close enough that the air changed when the facade flexed. Tiffani made her choice the way she always did: follow the data to its source. She ran toward Sector Nine. The copper taste got stronger with every stride.

CHAPTER 3: HOLD TWO

The grain warehouse sat in Sector Nine, where the terraces were newer and the beams were honest enough to still pretend they could be trusted. The collapse wasn't honest. Support columns had sheared at the base—clean breaks that didn't match load failure. The upper floor sagged like a shelf someone had stepped on. Dust poured from the breach in thick, choking waves. Jhace stood three strides from the entrance, hands shaking so hard he had to grip his belt to keep them still. He'd been here 300 heartbeats. His boot skated on spilled grain. He caught himself on instinct, palm slapping the wall hard enough to sting. Too close. Too human.

The tremor from Tallow Street hadn't stopped. It had spread from his hands into his forearms, up to his shoulders. A fine, relentless vibration that made every tool on his belt rattle. CHAPTER 4: PROTOCOL

CHAPTER 5: FIRST NETWORK
