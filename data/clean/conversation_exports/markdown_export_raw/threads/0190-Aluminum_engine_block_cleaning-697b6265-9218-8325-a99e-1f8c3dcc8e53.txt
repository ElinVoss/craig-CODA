Depends what you’re cleaning off (oil/grease vs rust/coolant scale vs baked-on gasket crap). Aluminum is picky: the “best” chemical is the one that **doesn’t pit/etch** it while still touching the mess.

## If you want one safest all-around choice (most situations)
**Aviation/Aluminum-safe water-based degreaser**  
- Examples: “aircraft/aluminum safe” formulas (Simple Green **Extreme** / “Aircraft & Precision” type products, Zep “aluminum safe” degreasers, etc.).  
- Why: Cuts oil/grease well, usually non-caustic or buffered so it won’t eat the block if you don’t leave it soaking for hours.

**How to use:** dilute per label, scrub, keep it wet 2–5 minutes, rinse thoroughly, blow dry.

## Best for coolant scale / mineral deposits (inside water jackets, radiator passages)
**Citric acid flush** (gentle, effective on scale)  
- Mix: ~2–5% by weight (roughly 20–50 g per liter of hot water).  
- Circulate/soak 30–60 minutes (don’t leave it overnight), then flush hard with clean water.  
- Optional: follow with a quick rinse of baking-soda water (light neutralize), then final water rinse.

## Best for gasket residue / RTV smears (deck surface, timing cover surface)
**Gasket remover (aerosol) + plastic scraper**  
- Works without gouging aluminum like a steel razor will.  
- Finish with **brake cleaner** (chlorinated *or* non-chlorinated) for a final wipe.

## Best for heavy greasy exterior grime (but still aluminum-safe)
**Kerosene/mineral spirits** (old-school, effective, low risk to aluminum)  
- Great if the block is out of the car and you can wash afterward.  
- Follow with soap/water rinse and dry immediately.

# What to avoid on aluminum (common ways people ruin blocks)
- **Oven cleaner / lye (sodium hydroxide, NaOH)**: will aggressively attack aluminum.
- **Strong acids** like **muriatic/hydrochloric**: can pit/etch fast.
- **Bleach / chlorine products**: bad corrosion issues.
- **High-strength “purple” caustic degreasers** (some are sodium hydroxide-based): can stain/etch unless explicitly labeled aluminum-safe and used briefly.

4. Final rinse, then blow out bolt holes/oil passages with air.

## The best chemical setup for an aluminum block (fully apart)

### 1) Main degreaser (inside + outside): **aluminum-safe water-based degreaser**
This is your workhorse for oil/grease sludge without eating the block.
- Use a product specifically labeled **aluminum/aircraft safe** (non-caustic or buffered).
- Mix per label (usually 4:1 to 10:1).
- Scrub with nylon brushes. Keep it wet 2–5 minutes. Don’t let it dry on the metal.
- Rinse hard with hot water.

**Why this is #1:** Most “industrial” degreasers are sodium hydroxide-based and will stain/etch aluminum. Aluminum-safe stuff avoids that.

### 2) Oil passages + galleries: **hot water + degreaser + brushes, then brake cleaner**
After degreasing:
- Run **rifle brushes / long bottle brushes** through every oil gallery you can access.
- Flush with hot water.
- Blow dry with compressed air.
- Finish the galleries with **brake cleaner** (short bursts), then air again.

**Rule:** If you can’t blow it out until bone-dry, you’re not done.

### 3) Coolant jackets / mineral scale: **citric acid**
If there’s crusty white/green coolant deposits or rust staining in jackets:
- Mix **2–5% citric acid in hot water**.
- Soak/flush 30–60 minutes.
- Rinse thoroughly.
- Optional quick neutralize rinse: a little baking soda in water, then final rinse.

**Why citric:** It eats scale without being an aluminum murder-acid.

### 4) Deck surface / gasket residue: **gasket remover + plastic scraper**
- Spray gasket remover, wait per can, scrape plastic.
- Repeat until clean.
- Final wipe with brake cleaner.

**Avoid:** hard steel razors on the deck if you’re not skilled; it’s easy to gouge.

### 5) Rusty steel bits (caps, crank, rings, etc.)
Not the aluminum block, but since you said “everything”:
- Use a **chelator** like Evapo-Rust on *steel parts only*.
- Keep that stuff off bearings.

## What NOT to use on the aluminum block
- Oven cleaner / lye / “purple” caustic degreasers unless explicitly aluminum-safe (even then, short contact only).
- Muriatic acid / strong wheel acids.
- Bleach or anything chlorinated as a “cleaner.”

## Order of operations (works, doesn’t trap dirt)
1) Scrape chunks & sludge first (dry).
2) Degrease + brush (inside/outside).
3) Hot rinse.
4) Oil galleries brush + flush.
5) Brake cleaner in galleries + blow out.
6) Citric acid in coolant jackets (only if scaled).
7) Final rinse.
8) Immediate dry: compressed air + heat gun or sunlight.
9) Protect machined surfaces: light oil film if it’s sitting.

---

## Two practical tips that matter
- **Don’t mix chemicals.** Rinse between steps like your life depends on it.
- **Dry immediately.** Aluminum corrodes fast when you leave water in crevices; steel parts flash-rust even faster.

## The correct cleaning workflow (block & head)
### 1) Knock the bulk off first
- Plastic scrapers, picks for corners, nylon/bronze brushes.
- Get rid of thick sludge before you introduce liquids.

### 2) Degrease (water-based, aluminum-safe)
- Spray/soak 2–5 minutes (don’t let it dry).
- Scrub with nylon brushes.
- Hot water rinse.

### 3) Solvent wash (mineral spirits) for varnish + stubborn oil film
- Brush on, scrub, wipe off.
- Follow with a detergent rinse (or another degreaser pass) so you’re not leaving solvent film everywhere.

### 4) Oil galleries (this is the bearing-saver step)
- Run **rifle brushes** through every oil passage you can access (block + crank if drilled + head galleries).
- Flush with hot water.
- Flush with brake cleaner.
- Blow out with compressed air until *absolutely dry*.

**If you skip brushing galleries, you’re gambling with your new bearings.** This engine sheds nasty sludge when neglected.

### 5) Coolant jackets (only if scaled)
- **2–5% citric acid in hot water**, soak/flush 30–60 minutes max.
- Rinse thoroughly.
- Blow dry.

### 6) Final clean and protection
- Final brake-cleaner wipe on machined surfaces.
- Blow dry.
- Light oil film on cylinder walls/deck/crank journals if they’ll sit.

## Parts-specific notes (Theta 2.4 GDI reality)
### Crankshaft
- Clean with mineral spirits + brushes; brake-clean final.
- Brush the oil holes hard.
- Blow through every drilling.
- Immediately oil the journals after they’re clean/dry.

### Cams
- Same deal: solvent + brush, brake-clean final.
- Don’t go caveman with abrasives on lobes/journals.

### Pistons/rings
- Carbon: use carbon cleaner + plastic/wood tools + brass brush lightly.
- Ring lands: use a ring-groove cleaner or an old ring (carefully) to scrape.
- Don’t soak pistons in harsh caustic.

### Bearings
- Do **not** “clean and reuse” old bearings for a rebuild—trash them.
- Do **not** blast brake cleaner onto new bearing shells and then touch them with dirty hands. Keep them surgically clean and dry until assembly lube.

### Block cylinders
- Chemical cleaning won’t fix rust/pitting/out-of-round. That’s hone/bore work.
- After honing, wash with hot soapy water until a white rag comes out clean, then oil.

Can I clean it with diesel?

[ASSISTANT]
Yes—**diesel works as a solvent degreaser**, especially on a bare aluminum block/head and steel parts. It’s not “best,” but it’s **usable** if you do it right.

## What diesel is good for
- Dissolving **oil sludge, varnish, greasy film**
- Soaking filthy steel parts (brackets, bolts, timing covers, etc.)
- Pre-cleaning before you move to a proper wash

## What diesel is bad at (and what can bite you)
- Leaves an **oily residue** that will hang in oil galleries and bolt holes
- Doesn’t touch **coolant scale** or **carbon** well
- If you stop at “diesel-clean,” you can contaminate **new bearings** on startup

## If you use diesel, do it like this
1) **Scrape first** (plastic scraper) to remove chunks/sludge.
2) **Brush with diesel** (nylon/parts brush). Don’t just dunk and call it done.
3) **Drain/wipe** thoroughly.
4) **Follow with a real wash**: hot water + dish soap or aluminum-safe degreaser. Scrub again.
5) **Flush oil galleries**: rifle brushes + hot water flush.
6) **Final rinse**: brake cleaner through galleries and machined surfaces.
7) **Blow dry hard** with compressed air until completely dry.
8) **Light oil film** on cylinders/deck/journals if it’ll sit.

That “wash after diesel” step is non-negotiable if you’re rebuilding bearings.

## Where I would NOT use diesel
- On/near **new bearings** or freshly cleaned bearing saddles right before assembly
- Any place you can’t rinse/flush afterward (hidden passages you can’t brush)

## Better cheap alternative if you can get it
- **Mineral spirits** or **kerosene**: similar cleaning power, usually less stinky and often leaves less “heavy” residue than diesel.

## The safe, effective workflow (bare block/head + teardown parts)

### 0) Set up so you don’t trap crap
- Keep the block/head **bare** (no bearings, no seals, no sensors).
- If you can remove **gallery plugs / restrictors** without drama, do it. If you can’t, no big—just spend more time brushing/flush + air.

### 1) Diesel pre-clean (cuts sludge/varnish)
- Brush diesel on, scrub hard (nylon + bottle brushes).
- Let it dwell 2–5 minutes max.
- Wipe what you can with rags.

**Do not stop here.** Diesel leaves an oily film that will end up in your new bearings.

### 2) Pressure rinse (water)
- Hit everything with your high-pressure nozzle to blast loosened sludge out of corners and cavities.
- Don’t be shy with volume—flow is your friend.

### 3) Soap/degreaser wash (this is what removes diesel residue)
Pick one:
- **Aluminum-safe degreaser** (best), OR
- Hot water + **Dawn** (works surprisingly well if you scrub)

Process:
- Apply, scrub, keep it wet, then pressure rinse thoroughly.

### 4) Oil galleries: brush + flush + air (bearing-saver step)
- Run **rifle brushes / long bottle brushes** through every oil gallery you can access.
- Pressure flush through every passage opening.
- Immediately **blow out with air** until you get *no mist*, just dry air.

### 5) Final “no residue” pass
- Spray **brake cleaner** into oil passages, threaded holes, and machined surfaces.
- Blow out again.

Brake cleaner + air is how you evict the last oily film and water.

### 6) Dry it like you mean it
- Blow every hole, crevice, and casting pocket.
- Then add heat if you can (sunlight, space heater nearby, or heat gun on low). You’re trying to drive out hidden moisture.

### 7) Protect machined surfaces if it’s going to sit
- Light wipe of clean engine oil on:
  - Cylinder walls
  - Deck surface
  - Main saddles (after final clean)
  - Crank journals (if already cleaned)

## Coolant jackets (only if crusty)
If you’ve got mineral crust/green-white scale inside water jackets:
- Do a separate step with **2–5% citric acid in hot water**, 30–60 minutes max.
- Rinse hard.
- Air dry.
(Do this **before** your final brake-clean pass.)

## What not to do with the pressure nozzle
- Don’t pressure-wash **new bearings** (or anything you already “final cleaned” for assembly).
- Don’t blast directly into areas with delicate pressed-in parts you intend to reuse (rare on a bare block, but still).

## Quick sanity checks before assembly
- After the final cleaning: wipe cylinders/deck with a white rag—**it should come off clean**.
- Blow through galleries: you shouldn’t see oily mist or dirty droplets.
- Threaded holes: dry, no pooled water.
