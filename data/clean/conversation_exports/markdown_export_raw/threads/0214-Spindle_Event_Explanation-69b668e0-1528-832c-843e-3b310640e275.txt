# Novella One — Section 1 

Jhace trusted edges more than faces.

Faces lied from habit. Edges lied only when someone had made them wrong.

He had the knife balanced on the anvil with its edge down and the spine up, a trick that looked foolish until you knew what it was for. A true edge would sit. A warped one would hunt. The steel would tell on itself if you gave it stillness and waited.

His left thumb steadied the handle. His right thumbnail traveled the bevel in one slow pass, feeling for the snag he still could not see. The blade held the same drag from heel to tip. Good. Not finished. Honest.

He turned it and checked the other side. Same line. Same resistance.

The tang sat square in the handle block. The rivet holes had lined up without persuasion. No forced fit. No future crack already hidden in the marriage.

A bad fit now is a broken hand later.

His father had said that over a vice with two fingers already starting to fail. Not as wisdom. As accounting. Work like this was always accounting. Force, surface, tolerance, debt.

Jhace set the knife aside and reached for the next blank.

The bell hit before his hand closed.

Fast warning. Street failure. Crowd hazard. Bodies moving where bodies should not.

He looked up before the second strike.

The apprentices by the bellows had gone still in the way boys did when fear and curiosity reached them at the same moment. One had his mouth open. The other was already looking toward the door like the answer might be waiting there.

"Out," Jhace said.

They moved half a beat late.

"Hands empty."

That got them. One dropped a tong. The other nearly grabbed for a cooling chisel and thought better of it.

Jhace caught the hot blank before it could roll, set it back in the cradle, slapped the quench lid shut, and jammed the nearest tools into the rack jaw-first. No loose iron on a floor that might tilt. No hot metal waiting to choose its own direction.

He snatched up the knife, wrapped it in a rag, and shoved it into his apron pocket. Because if he left it, someone would reach for it later in the wrong kind of rush.

By the time he hit the underface passage, the bell had picked up company.

One tower. Then another. Then a third somewhere farther upslope, the overlap wrong enough to make his shoulders tighten. Bell patterns were supposed to sort people. This one did the opposite. It said move and offered no shape to move in.

Stone corridors threw the sound back at him in hard pieces. Heat from the forge clung to his chest. Smoke and copper dust sat on the back of his tongue.

He should have gone up.

Up meant air and wider lanes. Up meant sight lines.

But his body had already chosen the route it knew: split arch, iron ladder, left at the old seam brace, shoulder through the bottleneck where two men could pass if neither of them insisted on dignity.

The crowd found him before daylight did.

Dock hands, terrace women, one runner still wearing tab colors, all of them compressed into the passage and trying to move faster than stone allowed. Jhace turned sideways, palms open, using bone and shoulder where he had to, never both hands at once. Two hands committed to one thing was how another thing caught you.

Someone shouted that the upper rail had gone.

Someone else shouted about glass.

A child cried once, then stopped, which was worse.

The floor rolled.

That part made sense.

A low shove through the masonry. Hooks rattling. Dust dropping out of old joints. Jhace widened his stance and kept moving. Quake meant arches first, then loose face, then anything tall enough to think it was permanent.

He had the list halfway through his head when the world did something new.

It pulled inward, as if the city had suddenly grown a center somewhere else and wanted everything to admit it.

His stomach lurched. The pressure in his ears changed. The man in front of him stumbled without tripping, arms windmilling at empty air. Dust lifted off the floor in a fine skin and streamed past Jhace's boots toward the plain.

He put one hand to the wall.

The stone hummed with a finer vibration, too even to be random, too ugly to call music. It carried none of the signatures of bells, carts, or any load he knew.

Then the passage spit him into daylight and for half a breath he had nothing to measure the world against.

The terrace rail was still there.

The lamp post was still there.

The upper walk still cut across the street where it had always cut across the street.

None of it sat right.

Angles had gone mean. The rail line leaned away from itself. A wall that should have met the next wall square was off by a whisper, and the whisper hurt more than a break would have. Breaks were simple. This looked like a bad repair done to the whole city at once.

People saw his face and looked where he looked.

That was when the screaming started to unify, many voices turning into one sound made of many throats.

Out on the plain, something was rising.

At first his brain did the kind thing and tried to call it weather. Dust wall. Smoke front. Heat bloom. Anything with rules.

Then the edge kept sharpening.

It came up out of the horizon with the steadiness of a tool already set in motion. Dark in bands. Bright in cuts. Too broad to take in cleanly. His eyes kept searching for its sides and failing, because every breath it was wider than the breath before.

The plain did not seem to produce it.

The plain seemed to be yielding.

Stone out there heaved. Dust vented from the ground in long gray spills. Heat flashed in places and vanished. Something inside the rising mass glinted glass-bright and then went iron-dark again.

Jhace tried to fit it to what he knew.

Slope failure.

Boiler burst.

Kiln face.

Nothing held.

Above it, the sky carried a black line so straight it looked made, not broken. The blue around it shivered with grit and heat, but the line itself stayed exact, hard-edged, unclouded, unfeathered into daylight—a narrow absence drawn across the world like a mark scored by something harder than weather.

Then the sound arrived.

Late.

Deep enough to come through the soles before it reached the ear. A rolling internal strike, as if somewhere under the city an iron pillar the size of a kingdom had just been driven through stone and kept going.

Windows burst up the terrace in a chain. Glass went outward and inward at once. A cistern tower three levels down split up its side and dumped half its water into the lane below. The spill came wrong, first hanging in a skin that caught the light like blown glass, then collapsing all at once and taking three people under it hard enough to break them against the curb.

A second pull hit.

Harder.

The crowd lurched as one body. Someone small disappeared at knee level. Jhace moved before he found the shape. He hooked fingers into a belt knot and hauled backward until the crushed body came free: a girl, maybe ten, gray with dust and trying not to cry because that was slower than breathing.

"Wall," he said, and shoved her toward the lee of a stair buttress.

A crack snapped overhead.

He looked up in time to see the edge of a stone facing peel loose from the terrace above. Big enough to kill. Flat enough to gather speed clean.

He had room for one thought.

Unknown load. Treat maximum.

Then he was under it, only far enough to get shoulder and forearm into the line of fall where the slab would have found momentum.

He did not try to lift. Lifting was for fools and stories. He braced, feet apart, elbow locked, back turned slightly so the force had somewhere to go that was not straight through him.

The slab hit his arm like the street had been dropped onto it.

Pain came white and clean. His boots slid half an inch. Stone grated. Mortar burst into his hair and eyes.

His right hand began to shake. He knew what strain felt like; this was finer than strain. A rapid misfire in the bones, as if something had taken hold of the line between wrist and fingertip and started plucking.

He clenched anyway.

The slab should have come down all at once.

It hesitated.

Only a fraction.

Only long enough to become impossible.

The stone shivered in the air against his forearm and answered him wrong, as if the load had momentarily forgotten which direction owned it. A woman beneath it got one more drag of breath. Two men got hold of her belt and pulled. A child came free by one arm and rolled screaming into the dust.

Three steps to Jhace's left, a cooper with both hands thrown up in pure animal refusal saw what was happening and tried to do the same thing with a hanging beam that had torn free of a balcony brace.

The beam slowed.

For one beautiful, hideous blink it actually slowed.

Then it twisted sideways, found a new angle, and smashed him flat across the chest hard enough to throw blood onto the wall behind him.

No one who saw both mistakes those two events for the same thing again.

The slab came down the rest of the way a fraction at a time, obedient to no one, but late. Late enough.

When it struck the street, the sound went through Jhace's legs and lodged in his teeth.

He stepped back.

His hand was still shaking.

He folded it once. Opened it once. The tremor stayed.

He pressed his thumb into the center of his palm as if pressure might tell him what had gone wrong. The answer came back as the same fine misfire, deep in the hand and nowhere he could brace against.

Around him, people stared with the ugly attention fear gave to anything that had interrupted death.

He hated that look.

Inventory.

The girl was still crouched against the buttress. Jhace caught her under the arm and set her on her feet.

"Can you stand?"

She nodded because children lied about pain the way men lied about weather.

"Then move."

She moved.

Good.

The lane failed in three more places before he took a full breath.

The upper parapet shed another skin of stone that shattered on the stair run. A door two houses down burst off its hinges and flew level across the street hard enough to break a man's jaw. Somewhere beyond the bend, crockery lifted out of a market rack in a glittering cluster, hovered, rang together like struck bells, and then exploded outward in white shards that sent everybody under the awning down bleeding.

Jhace looked up again because not looking did not make scale smaller.

The rising body had changed while he'd been under stone.

Its middle was finally giving way to taper. High against the black cut in the sky, the visible mass narrowed and kept narrowing, as if the world had been pierced by something wider at the center and that center had already passed. Fragments streamed around it—some incandescent, some dark, some shearing clear and some slowing and beginning to arc back toward the planet as if they could not decide whether they belonged to the thing or to the wound it had left.

The trailing end sharpened toward a point.

The thing kept going.

Jhace had the absurd urge to put both hands on the street and feel whether the whole city was still attached to the ground.

Another pull phase moved through.

Air thinned so fast his next breath came metallic. Dust rose in thin skins from every ledge and crack and flowed toward the plain. Somewhere to his left, a chimney emptied itself backward. Smoke went down instead of up.

Then water came.

Not from rain.

From above.

A terrace reservoir farther upslope had split its base and was vomiting runoff through a broken arch in short, violent surges. The first wave hit the stair lane at knee height, full of splinters, broken crocks, and two loose shutters slamming end over end.

People ran from that in the wrong direction and died for it.

A woman trying to drag two boys clear planted both feet, threw her arms out, and screamed at the water as if screaming were leverage. The surge lifted around her in a shining wall, climbed almost to shoulder height, and then lost shape all at once. It came down on her head and drove her face-first into the stone. The boys vanished into the churn. One came up. One did not.

Jhace grabbed the nearest rail post and let the wave break around his legs. Cold hit first. Then debris. Something wooden cracked his shin. The tremor in his hand jumped up his forearm and for one impossible instant he felt the rail answer him too, a faint held line in the iron straps before the force tore past and left him with nothing but pain and the taste of copper climbing into his mouth.

He let go at once.

The answer vanished.

He did not know what frightened him more: that the rail had answered, or that some part of him had already wanted it to.

When the water thinned back into channels and ruin, bodies began making choices again. Bad ones. Fast ones. The kind that came when terror wanted motion more than direction.

Some ran for open ground.

Some packed themselves under arches that were already thinking about failure.

Some tried their hands against what was breaking and discovered new wrongnesses there.

A cart-yard laborer with a pry iron in his fist shouted that he had it, he had it, and the pry iron rose under his grip, took him with it, and slammed him backward into a wall hard enough to leave him hanging there for half a second before both man and iron dropped together.

An old woman put her palm on a cracked pillar and the stone around her hand tightened, sealed, then fractured in a ring that bit through two fingers.

Consequence.

Stone breaking. People shouting names. Coughing. Water hitting steps in hard bursts. The kind of crying that came from grown mouths when they had not left room for it and it forced its way out anyway.

Chloen ran because the street was still throwing pieces of itself and because standing still felt like asking to be chosen.

She lost one shoe at the first turn and did not go back for it.

The ground bit through the stocking foot at once. Grit. Wet mortar. Something sharp she did not identify because naming pain made it larger.

She kept the surviving shoe because that was how children thought in disasters: badly, specifically, with the wrong loyalties.

At the next stair cut she stopped under an arch, then bolted out again when a crack walked across the underside in a sound like someone tearing cloth very slowly.

Too many bodies under one maybe.

That was a rule now.

No one had said it. The stone had.

A woman with blood drying black down one cheek clutched two boys by their sleeves and kept counting them aloud as if numbers could stop subtraction.

"One, two. One, two."

The smaller boy was crying without sound. The older one kept looking back upslope like he had forgotten something important and could not decide if it was a person.

Chloen looked too.

From here the sky-mark was only a slice between buildings, a black straightness where nothing straight should have been. Dust moved toward it in thin skins over the stone. Smoke bent the wrong way. The whole city looked as if someone had taken hold of one end and was trying to pull it through a hole too small to fit.

Then the stair wall beside her coughed dust and a bronze hook three handspans above her head slid sideways out of its anchor without any hand on it.

The woman with the two boys saw it move and slapped one hand against the stone in pure fear.

For one impossible blink the hook stopped in the air.

Then it turned toward her wrist as if it had remembered a better target.

The metal punched through the sleeve and pinned her to the wall by the forearm.

She did not even scream at first. The boys did that for her.

Chloen ran.

The bells struck again.

Then another bell answered. Then another. The sounds crossed each other until they stopped feeling like warning and started feeling like a net being thrown over a moving thing too large to hold.

People were being sorted by sound before anyone had decided what the sounds meant.

That scared Chloen worse than the hook.

Sorting meant waiting.

Waiting meant somebody else would decide what had happened to you before you found the words for it yourself.

She moved downslope with the rest because motion still looked more like life than standing did.

Nobody walked like themselves anymore. They traveled in jerks and checks, bunching at corners, breaking apart at cracks, all of them listening with their feet for the next wrong pull in the stone.

Chloen listened too.

It was not hard.

The city kept telling.

A slack phase came and everybody breathed at once.

A pull followed and the lane changed shape. Dust rose. A hanging cloth snapped inward. People leaned toward the plain without meaning to. Chloen grabbed a door handle to keep from stumbling and felt the metal buzz against her palm.

Enough to make her let go.

At the lower terrace mouth, no one had built a proper intake yet. They had only started pretending one into existence.

Chalk lines crossed stone where there had been no chalk that morning. White marks. Quick. Angry. Crooked in places where the hand had been running out of time. A rope lane had been strung between two lamp posts, a brace anchor, and a broken stair spindle. It sagged in the middle and people argued about which side of it they belonged on.

A captain with a split lip stood beside the chalk shouting lane numbers and making the same chopping gesture over and over, as if his arm had become the only part of him that still believed in order.

"Marked left! Children and breath-thin under cover! Carry hands stay visible! Keep away from the red!"

The red was a chalk circle around a collapsed awning where three bodies still lay under cloth and one hand kept twitching under the edge because nobody had checked whether twitching counted as alive today.

The words hit Chloen in pieces.

Marked. Children. Breath-thin. Carry hands.

She looked at her own wrist though she already knew it was bare.

So did the woman beside her. She pulled her daughter harder toward the children’s side without apology.

That hurt more than the pulling air did.

Chloen followed the bare wrists.

They were sent toward the edges.

Always the edges.

A man tried to duck under the rope with a bleeding shoulder and got shoved back by two registry boys no older than apprentices, each holding a slate like it gave them mass. The man started shouting. On the next pull phase, all the dropped nails around a brace bucket jumped six inches into the air and stuck themselves to his trouser leg in a glittering rash.

He screamed and clawed at them.

The boys with the slates backed up fast enough to trip over each other.

No one corrected the lane after that. They just left him to do his own removal with bloody fingers while the line bent around him.

That was when Chloen understood something ugly and grown:

a city in pain still loved lists, even when the lists were wrong.

She moved with the rope until the lane broke around a spill of fallen masonry. On the other side sat two women and one old man on overturned crates with slates on their knees and dust in their hair, turning people into columns as fast as the columns failed them.

Name.

Trade.

House mark.

Injured.

Can carry.

Can lift.

Can stay standing through pull.

The last one had been added later. The chalk beneath it was fresher and squeezed into a space meant for something smaller.

Ahead of her, a man kept saying his wife had gone back for his mother. The woman with the slate did not answer the sentence, only repeated, "House mark?"

He gave it.

She wrote it down.

A runner arrived with three new terms from somewhere higher up and made them scrape out two old ones because the old ones had already become inconvenient.

Chloen watched the chalk get erased.

Useful disappeared first.

Recovering disappeared second.

The new words were shorter.

Carry.

Wait.

Watch.

The woman with the slate wrote them without looking up.

That scared Chloen worse than blood.

Blood was present. Words lasted.

Somewhere behind the crate line, a little crowd cried out as one. Heads turned. A cooper's boy had both hands wrapped around a fallen kettle ring and was lifting himself onto his toes with it, heels kicking empty air while the ring climbed toward a torn bit of awning pole overhead. Two men rushed in. One got hold of the boy's belt and yanked. The ring dropped at once and split the stone between them.

The boy lived.

Chloen could tell from the way he screamed at his own hands after.

Nobody wrote down what had happened. They only wrote that he had to wait.

When Chloen reached the front, the slate woman looked at her once and then again, slower.

"Name?"

"Chloen."

"House mark?"

Chloen opened her mouth and nothing came out.

House mark meant the room under the terrace seam with the damp patch that looked like a bird if you squinted. It meant the cracked bowl by the wash basin. It meant a blanket folded into the wall niche and a door that stuck in wet weather and a corner where she kept the rag doll she had outgrown in public and still slept with in private.

House mark also meant the slab street.

The woman waiting for the answer watched her face and changed the question.

"Standing family?"

Chloen swallowed. Tasted copper. "Don't know."

The woman nodded as if uncertainty were an entry with its own box. She made a mark Chloen could not read.

"Any injury?"

Chloen thought of her bare foot. Her scraped palms. The bruise rising hot on her shoulder where the heel had clipped her.

"No."

That was a child's answer too. If you were still moving, it did not count.

The woman looked at the foot, looked at Chloen, and wrote anyway.

"Can carry?"

Chloen almost said yes just to avoid the next box.

Before she could, a voice farther down the line snapped, "Keep the little ones out of the pull mouth. That lane just ate two."

Everybody shifted at once.

The rope line moved. One crate slid. The old man with the third slate grabbed for his pencil and the pencil shot sideways out of his fingers, struck the cheek of the woman beside him, and fell quivering into the chalk dust. He stared at his own hand in bald terror.

No one stopped processing.

The woman touched the blood on her cheek, looked at it once, and kept writing with the red already drying there.

Across the lane, a woman in a dark coat was redirecting people away from a fresh crack with two fingers and a slate tucked under one arm. Her posture was straight enough to look unhurt until you saw the small hitch in one knee when the pull phase hit. She did not raise her voice. She did not need to. People moved when she pointed because she looked like the kind of person who had already counted the cost of disobedience and found it cheap.

For half a breath her eyes crossed the lane and caught on Chloen.

Assessing.

Then she was gone again into the movement, already cutting a new path through the crowd with nothing but hand signals and refusal.

The slate woman at the crate clicked her tongue and changed the mark she'd made.

"Wait lane. Rope three. Stay visible. If the air pulls thin, squat and cover your mouth."

She jerked her pencil toward the lane mouth.

That was all.

No comfort. No cruelty. Just placement.

Chloen moved where she was pointed because the city had become a mouth full of orders and some of them were better to obey than others.

Rope three ran along the broken edge of a lower terrace where people had started setting the displaced world into crude categories: broken stone here, salvage there, water there, children not currently attached to adults in the shade if shade could be found.

A little boy sat with a bundle in his lap that turned out to be a cat wrapped in an apron. The cat looked dead until it bit him. He smiled anyway.

A woman laughed at that and then covered her mouth like laughter had become too expensive without warning.

Chloen sat when she found a piece of curb not yet assigned another purpose.

From here she could see three things at once if she leaned the right way:

the black shaft in the slice of sky,

the rope lanes knotting people into order,

and, farther upslope, the man from under the slab moving through the work crews with a wedge bar in his hand.

His hands were steady on the bar.

Off it, they shook.

Chloen watched that longer than she meant to.

Because she had been under the stone when it stopped.

The city went on being rearranged around her.

Chalk scraped. Slates filled. Someone started handing out water by half-cup measure. Every time the air pulled thin, conversation broke in the same places and resumed with one less word than before.

A fight started near the carry lane when a quartermaster tried to take a pry iron off a man who had already learned his hand only settled while he held it. Three people joined the shouting. On the next pull phase, the pry iron leaped out of all of them and drove itself point-first into a timber post. The entire lane went still around it.

Then the quartermaster wrote something down.

That was what Chloen would remember later.

Not the shouting. Not the iron in the wood.

The writing.

How quickly the city took a scream and looked for the right box to trap it in.

Nobody said the world had ended.

That was too large.

They said things like lane, mark, wait, next.

That was how people kept living when the larger sentence would not fit in the mouth.

Chloen put her scraped hands between her knees and looked from the shaft to the shaking hands to the chalk lines that kept multiplying on the stone.

The street where she had almost died was only two terraces away.

It already felt like a place the city was trying to turn into paperwork while it was still killing people.
