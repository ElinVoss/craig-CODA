# The Chronicles of Iron and Glass

## Novella One

# Chapter 1 — Jhace

Jhace set the blade down on the anvil block and didn't look at the edge. That wasn't the part that failed in the hands. The handle was where people lied to themselves.  
The forge bay sat in the Underface—stone ribs, pipework stitched overhead, damp air that never fully warmed even with a fire burning. It was loud in the honest ways: bellows breath, hammer ring, quench hiss, the low murmur of the grid running through the walls like a sleeping animal.  
He kept his work at the left bench where the light was steady and the drafts didn't steal heat off steel.  
The blade itself was already done—ground true, spine straight, bevels even. A working knife, not a show piece. The tang ran full-length, squared shoulders clean where it met the ricasso. That meant the handle had no excuses.  
He chose a block of dark hardwood from the bin—close-grained, oily, heavy for its size. Not brittle. Not pretty, just stubborn. He checked it with his thumbnail, then with a quick tap against the bench: dull note, no hidden splits. Good.  
He cut it into two scales with a saw that needed oil yesterday. He cleaned the cuts with a plane until both faces lay flat against a straightedge. Any high spot became a future crack. Any gap became movement. Movement became failure.  
He set the tang on the first scale, traced it with a scribe, and marked three pin positions where the steel had enough meat. He clamped the scales together, drilled through both at once so the holes matched. Slow speed, steady pressure—let the bit cut.  
Guard came next.  
Most people treated a guard like jewelry. Jhace treated it like a washer that could save a hand.  
He pulled a strip of mild steel off the rack, measured, cut it, and squared the edges. Then he heated the guard plate to a dull red and drove it onto the tang shoulders with a wood mallet. The heat gave it just enough give. The tang burned its own fit, high spots confessing with smoke. He pulled it back off, filed the bright marks, and did it again.  
Drive, pull, file.  
On the third cycle the guard seated with a solid stop—no bounce, no gap, no twist. Jhace let out a breath he hadn't known he was holding.  
While the resin cured he went back to the guard and chamfered the edges. Not for beauty—sharp edges found skin. He rounded the corners until they wouldn't tear a glove.  
The grid hummed under his feet. Normal. Familiar. The quarter's old song: pipe-thrum, brace resonance, the gentle vibration that told you the city was still held up.  
He knew that song so well he heard problems in it without thinking.  
Mid-hammer, the song shifted—not louder or softer, just wrong.  
The hammer was in his hand—two-pound, long handle, head dressed smooth. He brought it down on the guard plate and the ring that came back was doubled. Like a note with a second note laid on top that didn't belong there. It prickled his teeth. It made his sternum feel crowded.  
He stopped the hammer in the air and listened with his hands on the bench.  
The pipe-thrum was still there, but something deeper was moving through it. Slow. Heavy. Sustained. Not a quake that rattled loose stone—this was the stone itself changing its mind about what it was.  
A tremor ran along the anvil block. The forge tools clicked against each other without being touched. Dust lifted from the floor in a fine, lazy drift as if gravity had loosened.  
Jhace's first thought was structural. A brace slip. A terrace shift. A pipe main failing somewhere it shouldn't.  
Then the bell rang.  
It wasn't the forge bell or the shift bell. The emergency bell, from above, from the streets.  
One strike. Uneven.  
Another. A different direction.  
He didn't stand there and decide. He moved. Chalk down, slate left open on the bench, hands already reaching for his kit by habit—wedge bar, line, quick clamps, a coil of cord, collar keys. Moving. Then thinking.  
The doubled hum thickened, like something was taking a breath through stone.  
He hooked the kit strap over his shoulder, scooped the keys, and went for the ladder shaft.  
More bells started.  
Different streets. Same urgency.  
The Underface corridor was tight, stone on both sides, pipework overhead sweating. People were already moving, faces pale in the lamplight, some running, some walking too carefully like they'd forgotten how to trust the floor. The air tasted dusty, and with every step he could feel the vibration climb from the soles of his boots up through his legs.  
He took the ladder two steps at a time. The rungs trembled under his hands.  
Halfway up, the pressure changed. It wasn't wind. It was absence. Like the air had been thinned with a knife.  
His ears popped hard. His chest tightened as if someone had set a strap around his ribs and pulled.  
He tried to inhale and got less than he asked for.  
Below, somewhere in the Underface, a torch flame bent sideways. Smoke that should have risen got pulled flat.  
Jhace swallowed and forced a breath through it. He didn't stop. Stopping didn't help.  
He came up through a service hatch behind a row of terraces into light that looked normal until his eyes adjusted and he realized normal was the insult. Blue kindling-light sky. People on the street. Shop signs. Dust in the air.  
And a deep, wrong hum running through all of it, doubling everything.  
The street had gone still in a way that wasn't calm. Tools lay where they'd dropped. A cart sat half-hitched, mule wide-eyed, nostrils flared, fighting the harness. Faces were turned in one direction as if there was only one thing in the world worth seeing.  
Jhace stepped out into the street and followed their gaze.  
On the plain beyond Saint Merro, something had risen.  
At first his mind tried to name it small. Wall. Cliff. A terrace collapse out beyond the far districts. Then the scale hit and the name fell apart.  
It wasn't a wall. It was the edge of the world being forced up.  
Banded strata—light over dark, dark over light—turned vertical like pages ripped from a book and held upright. The thing curved away left and right until it vanished into its own distance. Its base was lost in a brown-gray cloud that boiled without flame.  
Above it, the sky was wounded.  
A hole—no, a cut. A clean absence where blue stopped and black began. Stars inside it in full day. Not faint. Hard points of light like they'd been drilled through the air.  
The edge shimmered, not like heat, like the air couldn't decide where to exist.  
People made sounds behind him, but the air was thin enough that sound didn't carry right. It felt muffled, like cotton in the world.  
Jhace stood with his kit strap biting his shoulder and tried to make his eyes tell him something usable.  
The hum deepened. His teeth buzzed. Somewhere in the city, shutters rattled against their frames.  
Then—movement.  
Something flashed above the rising face, so fast his brain lagged behind his eyes. A shape crossing the open wound in the sky. Not a bird. Not a machine. Too big to understand, too fast to track.  
It turned as it moved. Not tumbling—rotating, like a spindle between invisible fingers. Veins of light ran along it in pulses—copper, gold, and colors he couldn't name cleanly because they weren't colors he'd ever seen in metal.  
No fire. No trail of burn. Just the violent fact of it.  
Behind it—following like torn cloth—debris.  
A smear of dark fragments and bright sparks that did not behave like falling. Some pieces continued after it, pulled along. Some lagged and began to arc back, caught between up and down.  
The air pulled again. Harder.  
Jhace's breath got taken, not by wind but by pressure. His throat opened and nothing came in for a beat. People clutched at their chests. Someone dropped to their knees and gagged without sound.  
Smoke that had been rising from a chimney across the street got yanked down into the stone mouth it came from.  
Then the pull shifted—not gone, just redirected. The wind stopped being a direction and became a demand. Everything loose went toward that horizon where the wound sat above the new stone face.  
Paper tore out of hands. Dust lifted in sheets. A woman's scarf whipped free and streamed away until it vanished into the gray.  
Far out near the rising face, water hung in the air.  
Too much of it. Too high. Too steady. A suspended mass that had no business staying up.  
For a few breaths it held. The hum underfoot made it quiver in place like it was listening.  
Then the world collected its bill.  
Water fell.  
Not a clean pour. It broke into heavy slaps and mist and sheets that flashed in the kindling-light as they came down. The sound reached the city late, a rolling crash that made the shutters jump and old mortar spit dust.  
The street around Jhace swayed. Not like a quake wave—like the stone itself had softened and shifted under the load of being itself.  
A building nearby answered the vibration with a groan.  
Jhace's eyes snapped left.  
The terrace edge above them—stone façade—had been weakened by the wrong hum. Mortar that should have been solid had behaved like paste for a moment and set again in the wrong shape. Now it was giving up.  
A slab tore loose. A big one. Old stone, thick as a man is tall, coming down toward the street where bodies still weren't moving fast enough.  
Jhace moved before he thought.  
He shoved two people back by the shoulders. "Move—\!"  
His arm came up, palm out, the stupid reflex of anyone who has ever watched a load shift and felt their body try to argue with gravity.  
And something in his chest answered.  
Heat behind the sternum. A pressure like a hand closing from the inside. Copper flooded his mouth so fast it tasted like biting a coin.  
The slab stopped.  
It hung in mid-fall like the world had stalled.  
Dust froze with it. Pebbles drifted instead of dropping. A hammer that had been knocked off a windowsill floated for a heartbeat before clattering down sideways, like gravity was returning in pieces.  
Jhace couldn't move.  
Not because he chose not to. Because the thing inside his ribs had tied itself to that stone and locked. He felt it like a line under tension—exact, punishing. The more his body tried to pull away, the more it tightened, teaching him without words that resistance had a cost.  
The street noise broke into fragments: boots, coughing, a name shouted and swallowed, the wet animal sound of panic trying to become speech in thin air.  
People ran under the suspended slab.  
Jhace couldn't turn his head to track them. He only felt the stone through the line—micro-shifts, strain, the constant insistence of gravity pressing down and being denied.  
His vision tunneled at the edges. The hum became everything.  
A small body stumbled in the dust under the slab—child-small, white with grit, hair stuck to her cheeks.  
She fell, hands scrabbling on stone that had turned slick with fine powder.  
Somebody reached for her and missed.  
Jhace's mouth filled with copper again. His jaw clenched without permission.  
The line in his chest screamed tighter, and the slab held.  
For one more breath. For one more step.  
The child cleared the shadow.  
The instant she did, something inside Jhace slipped—not a release he chose, a release like a knot letting go when the load is gone.  
The slab dropped.  
It hit the street with a crack that shook the quarter. Stone shattered. Dust punched up in a wave. The shock threw Jhace backward like a snapped cable. He hit hard on his shoulder and the world flashed white at the edges.  
He lay there with no air, throat open, nothing coming.  
Then his lungs finally pulled something in—thin, dusty, metallic.  
He rolled onto an elbow. His hands were steady for exactly one breath.  
Then they started shaking.  
Around him people were screaming now, full voice, as if the air had remembered how to carry sound. Someone dragged the child away from the rubble. Someone else vomited coppery spit into the gutter and sobbed like they'd been struck.  
Jhace stared at his own trembling fingers like they belonged to someone else.  
The hum under the street didn't stop. It held, doubled, wrong, as if the planet had been struck and would never settle back into a single note again.  
He forced himself upright.  
The horizon still held that impossible vertical face. The wound in the sky above it still showed stars in day. And high above, the debris smear had thinned, some of it vanishing after the spindle, some of it arcing away into a band that did not fall—caught, circling.  
Jhace didn't have words for ring or orbit. He only knew: pieces weren't coming down. Pieces were staying up.  
Bells kept ringing. Somewhere farther into the quarter a façade gave up and fell with a crash that made people flinch like whipped animals.  
Jhace swallowed copper because spitting felt like waste. He wiped his mouth with the back of his wrist and tasted metal on his skin too.  
He looked at the street—at the warped doors, the slumped corners of buildings that had softened and re-set wrong, at the dust that clung to everything like static—and he did the only thing his body knew how to do when the world started failing.  
He went toward the sound of the next bell.

# Chapter 2 — Tiffani 

Tiffani kept the observatory clean the way other people kept altars clean.  
It wasn't reverent or sentimental. It was functional.  
Dust on a lens was not "dust." It was scatter. Scatter became false stars, smeared edges, and bad numbers. Bad numbers killed boats and broke bridges and got people buried under stone they thought was stable.  
The glass-house sat on the high terrace where the city stopped pretending it wasn't built on stacked geology and old decisions. It was a skeleton of iron ribs and paneled glass, bolted into stone with braces that rang when you touched them. Inside, instruments stood like careful animals: theodolite on its plinth, transit scope, a long baseline level, a pendulum setup she'd built herself from salvaged brass and a weight she trusted.  
Slates leaned in rows along the west wall. Star charts rolled and tied with twine. A chronometer in a padded box ticked steadily, indifferent to everything else.  
Her knee ached. It always did. She didn't let it into the room.  
She sat at her workbench, slate open, recording the morning's calibration when the world shifted.  
It wasn't volume or pitch exactly. The kind of wrong you only noticed if you spent years listening to the same baseline through the same bones.  
It doubled.  
A second frequency laid itself over the first and refused to settle into harmony. It vibrated the glass panes. It made the brass fittings on the instrument rack buzz like insects.  
Tiffani froze with her pencil hovering over slate.  
She hummed again, longer, as if the sound could pin the moment down.  
The bubble in the level trembled, then steadied. The tremble was not from her hand. The whole plinth had moved.  
She looked at the pendulum.  
The weight swung without being touched.  
It wasn't wide or a quake swing. A tight, fast oscillation as if the air had become a medium for vibration instead of a passive gap.  
She wrote without thinking about handwriting.  
Grid baseline: compromised. Secondary coupled present.  
She underlined secondary twice.  
Then the air changed.  
Pressure dropped so fast it felt like someone had taken the room and pulled the lid off. Her ears popped hard. Her throat opened on instinct and got less air than it asked for.  
Outside the glass-house, chimneys did something they should not do: smoke reversed direction and poured down into stone mouths, pulled as if the sky had developed a hunger somewhere else.  
She didn't interpret. She adjusted.  
She snapped the chronometer open.  
Tick. Tick. Tick.  
She marked the time on slate and added:  
Pressure event: hemispheric.  
Because if it reached Saint Merro like this, it was not local.  
The hum deepened. The iron ribs of the glass-house took it and vibrated back. She felt it in her teeth. She felt it in the instrument screws. The city's pipework—normally a comfortable steady thrum—turned into something heavy and slow, like a giant hand pressing down in pulses.  
The emergency bells began.  
One sector. Then another. Then overlapping peals—uncoordinated, frantic, exactly what happened when systems designed for single failures got hit with a continent's worth of stress.  
Tiffani went to the theodolite.  
Her hands moved through a protocol she could recite in her sleep:  
Base lock. Level. Sight. Focus. Confirm seat.  
She put her eye to the lens and aimed at the fixed chimney marker.  
The crosshair drifted off target.  
A fraction at first. Then enough to be undeniable.  
She pulled back, checked the base again. Tight. Checked the bubble. Centered.  
She put her eye back to the lens.  
The marker slid sideways in her view as if the world beneath the instrument was rotating. Not fast. Not a spin. A steady, sick correction.  
She wrote:  
Orientation drift observed. Not instrument. Reference point stable. World axis shifting.  
She did not write "impossible." That word was for people who needed comfort. She needed numbers.  
She rotated the theodolite toward the horizon where the terraces fell away into the distant plain.  
The glass-house gave her a higher sightline than street level. Fewer obstructions. More sky.  
At first there was only the normal kindling-light—blue and bright and offensive in its steadiness.  
Then the horizon began to grow a wall.  
Not a wall. An edge.  
The ground in the far distance heaved upward in a curve too wide to hold in a single glance. The top climbed into the haze, and the haze itself became a moving thing—dust and pulverized stone rising without flame.  
The theodolite magnified enough that her eye could pick out banding: strata layers turned vertical. Light rock over dark rock over light, stacked wrong, exposed like a cut through a mountain turned sideways.  
She wrote faster.  
Exit-face event. Diameter: extreme. Strata vertical. Rim rise ongoing.  
The hum surged again and the glass panes shivered.  
Copper-gold particulate began to appear in the air, and she noticed because it clung to the wrong things.  
It didn't settle like ordinary dust. It floated, then stuck to metal edges—instrument screws, the brass collar of the theodolite, the iron ribs of the greenhouse—as if it had opinions about conductivity.  
She wiped a finger across the plinth and looked at the smear.  
Copper and gold in fine grit. Not paint. Not pollen. Not ash.  
She did not taste it on purpose. She still tasted it.  
Sharp metal at the back of the tongue, like she'd bitten down on a coin.  
She wrote:  
Particulate: conductive behavior suspected. Static attraction to brass/iron. Taste: metallic.  
Then she put her eye back to the lens.  
Above the rising face, the sky tore.  
Not with sound. With absence.  
A section of blue simply stopped existing. A circular-oval wound opened in the atmosphere, and inside it there was black.  
Not storm-dark. Space-dark.  
Stars.  
Visible in full day through the hole. Hard points of light with no haze.  
Her breath caught—purely mechanical, the kind of pause that comes when data breaks your baseline so completely you have to rebuild your assumptions in real time.  
She hummed once, thin and sharp.  
Then she wrote:  
Atmospheric discontinuity observed. Rift. Stars visible in daylight through void. Edges shimmer (boundary distortion).  
She underlined Rift and drew a neat box around it.  
Something moved.  
So fast her eye almost missed it, but the lens caught a slice—one breath, one clean frame of impossible data.  
A spindle.  
It wasn't round or a falling rock. A long, double-pointed body with a bulging center, rotating as it moved. It crossed the Rift's black like a needle passing through cloth.  
There was no fire. No wake. No friction burn, no streak of heat.  
Instead there were vein-lines along its surface that pulsed in bursts—copper, gold, green, purple, orange—bright enough to read as pattern, not glare.  
Light bent around it.  
She saw it as a slight lensing of the Rift's edge, a distortion that had geometry. Not random shimmer. A field.  
Then it was gone.  
The last she caught was the trailing end clearing the atmosphere and a smear behind it—debris pulled along, some pieces tight to its path, some lagging and beginning to arc back.  
Her hands did not shake. Her knee did. She ignored the knee.  
She wrote as fast as graphite allowed:  
Object observed: spindle/needle. Rotation confirmed. Surface vein-lines pulsing (Cu/Au/green/purple/orange). No thermal wake. No sonic signature. Lensing observed. Transit time: sub-second at Rift boundary.  
She marked the time again.  
Tick. Tick. Tick.  
Then she wrote:  
Trajectory linear.  
The inhale returned in pulses, not steady. Wind pulled toward the distant face, then slackened, then pulled again as if something enormous was breathing through a throat in the earth.  
She could see it at the far rim: dust at the base surged inward, then pushed outward, irregular but trending toward a pattern.  
Her instrument stand vibrated. The bubble in the level quivered. The pendulum's swing changed period, as if the planet's own timing had been struck and re-set.  
She forced her attention back to the Rift.  
The black remained. It did not close behind the spindle.  
That mattered more than almost anything else.  
She wrote:  
Rift persists post-transit. No closure observed.  
She set the slate down and replaced it with a fresh one.  
The next thing she wrote, in larger letters than she usually allowed herself, was not a theory. It was a directive to her future self.  
DOCUMENT EVERYTHING.  
Then the city below her answered the world's new baseline with human noise—cries, running feet, collapsing stone, and the first sharp, unmistakable sound of people discovering that the rules had shifted and their bodies could do things they did not understand.  
Tiffani didn't look away from the Rift.  
She watched the black wound sit in the blue sky like an error that refused correction, and she kept writing until her graphite stained her fingertips copper-gray.  
When she finally turned toward the stairwell, it wasn't to join the panic.  
It was to take her measurements to ground level—because the next phase would not be in the sky.  
It would be in the bodies.

# Chapter 3 — Tiffani

Tiffani descended the glass-house stairs two at a time, slate board under one arm and chronometer box under the other, careful not to misplace a step when the whole city was vibrating in two notes.  
The iron ribs behind her kept ringing. Somewhere in the frame a bolt had started talking—tiny, repeating clicks under load. She recorded it in her head: Glass-house frame: cyclic stress. Fastener chatter at NE rib.  
The air outside was not stable. It came in pulses—thin, then thick—like breathing through cloth. The wind pulled toward the distant Exit Face, then slackened, then pulled again. Not a storm pattern. A pressure differential trying to equalize through an impossible throat.  
Copper-gold particulate glittered along the terrace rail where the metal caught it. Stone got dull dust. Metal got the bright grit like filings. Conductive behavior wasn't theory anymore; it was visible.  
She ran her thumb along the iron rail and looked at the smear on her skin.  
Copper. Gold. A hint of green that didn't read as oxidation—too vivid, too clean.  
The first thing she saw when she reached the lower terrace was not the Rift or the wall.  
It was the buildings that had re-solidified wrong.  
Doorframes leaned a degree off square like a bad eye. A corner of a shopfront had sagged into a curve as if the stone had softened and been pushed from inside. Mortar lines bulged in places where they should have been hairline seams. A support post at the mouth of an alley had a bend that did not belong in a load-bearing member.  
The city looked like it had been briefly melted and then told to pretend it hadn't.  
People were in the street in clumps, all facing the horizon, mouths open, eyes fixed. Some prayed. Some laughed. Some stood with their hands at their sides as if they were waiting for instructions from the sky.  
Tiffani didn't give them any.  
She stepped into the flow like she belonged there, because she did. Not as comfort. As function.  
A bell rang—fast and uneven—then another from farther off, then a third that overlapped until the city sounded like it was trying to speak through broken teeth.  
Tiffani set the chronometer box on a stoop, opened it, and checked the tick.  
Tick. Tick. Tick.  
Still consistent.  
Good. If time was still time, she could work.  
She wrote on the slate as she walked:  
Local liquefaction response: permanent deformation. Expect delayed failures at stress points.  
She turned into Tallow Street by its stink and its narrow shop fronts, and the first collapse aftermath was already there.  
Dust hung in the air in thick curtains. It moved like it was slower than it should be, as if gravity was arriving late in some pockets. The particulate glittered in it, catching lamplight and daylight in different ways at once.  
A slab of stone lay broken in the street. Fresh fracture faces shone pale. People were gathered around it, some helping, some staring, some arguing.  
Her eyes went immediately to the failure pattern.  
She traced the break line up with her gaze: façade shear, mortar flow point, post-bend, then fracture. It matched her earlier forecast. Liquefaction, set wrong, then catastrophic shear under its own weight.  
She wrote:  
Collapse: delayed shear following re-solidification. Failure initiated at mortar seam—flow residue visible.  
Then she looked for the secondary phenomenon.  
A man was on one elbow near the edge of the rubble. Dust in his hair. Kit strap across his chest. His hands were held out in front of him as if he didn't trust them to behave. They were shaking. Not weak tremor. A full-body signal localized in the hands like the nerves didn't know where to settle.  
His mouth was tight, jaw working. He swallowed once, hard.  
She saw the copper stain at the corner of his lips.  
He had been the brace.  
Tiffani stopped at a distance that did not crowd him and crouched, not for kindness but to get her eyes level with his hands.  
The tremor had a pattern.  
It pulsed in sync with the doubled coupled underfoot, as if his body was still trying to match the new baseline and failing.  
She held her own breath still and listened with her skin. The air around him prickled. The copper-gold grit on her slate edge stood slightly proud, lifting on static.  
She wrote without looking away from his hands:  
Subject: post-event tremor synchronized to doubled coupled. Particulate attraction increased near subject.  
A woman a few steps away was already writing too—chalk on slate, numbers in a column, counting under her breath like it was prayer or punishment. But this woman had a small brass emblem pinned to her cloak—Crown Infrastructure. Her gaze wasn't just counting rubble. She was counting him.  
Tiffani noted it.  
Secondary observer: recording intervals. Fixation: subject's sternum/hand tremor. Crown affiliation suspected.  
Above the rooftops, the Rift was visible—black in blue, stars in day—still open, still wrong.  
On the horizon the Exit Face stood like a new continent turned vertical. Dust boiled at its base. The curvature at the edges was clearer now that her brain accepted the scale. It was not a wall. It was a rim.  
A faint band had begun to form across the sky. Not cloud. Not smoke. A thin arc that caught light differently, shifting brightness as it moved.  
The ring.  
She wrote:  
Orbital debris band visible. Arc across dome. Brightness varies with angle.  
The man's name cut through dust. "Jhace\!"  
He tried to stand and failed on the first attempt, his hands curling involuntarily as if they were gripping an invisible bar.  
Tiffani watched his sternum. His breathing pattern was wrong—short pulls, clipped, like his ribs didn't want to expand.  
"What did it feel like?" she asked, voice pitched low so it wouldn't carry into the crowd.  
He looked at her as if she'd asked him to describe a bolt with feelings.  
Then he swallowed again. Copper.  
"Like—" he started, then stopped.  
His hand shook harder for two pulses.  
"Like a line," he finished. "Tied through me."  
Tiffani nodded once and wrote:  
Phenomenology: "line tied through sternum" during suspension. Breath restriction. Copper taste surge.  
A person behind her retched and spat into the gutter, the spit dark with metallic flecks. Someone else wiped their mouth and looked at their own fingers like they'd found blood where there should not be blood.  
Copper taste: universal. Particulate present in sputum.  
Another bell pealed, closer. Movement became chaos. People surged down the street, away from the sound.  
Tiffani stepped into the flow, angling across it toward the next failure point.  
A stone arch over an alley had a hairline crack widening in time with the doubled hum. The crack opened on one pulse, closed on the next.  
She pointed and snapped, "Not that way."  
A few listened. The arch dropped a fist-sized chunk a beat later.  
Tiffani wrote:  
Crack propagation synchronized to coupled. Pulse-driven failure.  
She hummed quietly—one note, thin, steady—because sound was a stabilizer and her throat was still hers.  
The air pulled hard again toward the horizon.  
Tiffani took a measured breath on the slack phase.  
In. Out.  
Write it.  
Pressure pulse periodicity: irregular. "Breathing" confirmed at exit throat.  
A hammer lifted off a workbench and floated for a heartbeat, rotating slowly as if suspended in syrup.  
A scream went up.  
Then it dropped sideways. A bucket skated a handspan across stone, pulled toward the horizon as if it had found an invisible incline.  
Tiffani wrote fast:  
Localized suspension anomaly: tool levitation observed (1 heartbeat). Lateral drift toward exit vector.  
Ahead, water beaded upward along a retaining wall seam and lifted into the air in trembling strands before falling back.  
The crowd stopped like animals scenting a predator.  
Tiffani wrote:  
Water anomaly: upward seep \+ brief suspension. Particulate embedded.  
She heard it—two streets over—another person throwing hands out at a falling lintel. The lintel jerked mid-drop and held for half a breath.  
The person collapsed.  
The lintel fell harmlessly because the people under it had cleared in that half-breath.  
Tiffani didn't let it become myth. She let it become a list.  
M1: Jhace — slab suspension — tremor \+ copper surge \+ sternum lock.  
M2: unknown — lintel arrest — collapse.  
Space left for more.  
Across the sky, the orbital band brightened as the sun angle shifted, making the debris flash like a scar being shown off.  
Tiffani stopped long enough to look at the Rift again: black in blue, unchanged.  
She hummed once, steady, and wrote the last line on that slate page:  
New baseline established.  
Then she turned away from the horizon and back toward the city's breaking points, because whatever the sky had become, people were still made of stone and water and breath—and those were failing in ways she could, at least sometimes, predict.

# Chapter 4 — Jhace

Jhace kept moving because stopping made the shaking worse.  
Not always or forever—just right now.  
The bell sequence pulled him through the quarter like a hook in the ribs—fast peals from Brace Street, then the staggered answering ring from a lane he knew by smell more than signage. The kit strap sawed at his shoulder. His hands wouldn't hold still on the wedge bar, so he let them hang and used his legs.  
Copper still sat on his tongue. Every swallow scraped.  
The doubled hum underfoot stayed. It wasn't an aftershock. It was a new layer to the world, pressed through stone, through pipe, through bone. The city's old pipe-thrum was still there, but it had company now, and the company didn't know how to behave.  
People ran in bursts—start, freeze, start again—like the ground kept asking their bodies a question they couldn't answer. A man with a basket slammed into Jhace's shoulder and spun away without apologizing. A woman hauled a child by the sleeve, the kid's feet skidding, eyes wide.  
Jhace pointed once, short and sharp. "Open ground. Away from façades."  
Some listened. Most didn't. Bells made people think there was one safe direction. Bells were a lie on a day like this.  
He cut left at the corner by the tannery vents—hot, sour air—and almost ran into a knot of bodies jammed in the street. Not staring at the horizon now. Staring at something immediate.  
Fire.  
A building at the far end of the lane had lit up like a lantern. Two windows vomiting orange, smoke rolling out under the eaves in thick, oily layers. The flames weren't tall; they were hungry and spreading sideways along old wood and pitch. Heat shimmered above the door like glass trying to become water.  
The lane was a choke point: stone walls tight, terraces stacked, one way out blocked by that burning front.  
People were pressed against each other, coughing, trying to turn around in a space that didn't have room. Some were trying to push through anyway, as if speed could argue with flame.  
Jhace's brain stopped seeing "fire" and started seeing constraints.  
Narrow lane. Wind pulling toward the Exit Face in pulses. Smoke already leaning. Limited exits. Water sources? Pipe mains? Steam vents?  
He ran.  
Not into the crowd—into the edges, where he could move. He shoved through a gap, shoulder checking a man who was frozen mid-step. "Back," he snapped. "Back from the door. You'll feed it."  
A woman screamed at him, words useless. He didn't answer. He grabbed her elbow and hauled her away from the smoke line and pointed her toward an alley that dropped down two terraces. "That way. Now."  
She went because his hand was iron and her fear needed a direction.  
Jhace reached the front of the crowd where the heat hit his face like a slap.  
The door frame was charred. The upper lintel had started to sag—wood expanding, joinery failing. Smoke poured in a steady sheet, thick enough to hide what was inside.  
He looked left, right.  
A steam line ran along the wall two buildings over—an industrial feed from the Underface, used to warm vats. The pipe was sweating. The air around it was hotter than it should be. The hum in the stone made the fittings rattle.  
Steam was water. Steam was mass. Steam could smother if you could put it where it needed to be.  
Wind by itself wouldn't beat this. Not with the pitch and the tight lane. Wind would feed it as easily as it would starve it. He needed direction, pressure, and something wet enough to choke the oxygen without blasting embers deeper into the structure.  
His hands started shaking harder just from standing still.  
His sternum felt bruised from the inside. Heat lived there now, a memory of the line that had tied him to stone.  
A child coughed behind him, wet and tearing. A man tried to shoulder past Jhace toward the door, blind, and Jhace caught him by the collar and slammed him back into the crowd.  
"Not through," Jhace barked. "You'll die in it."  
"People are in—"  
"I know."  
Jhace stepped into the middle of the lane where the heat line was strongest. He turned his body so he could see both the burning doorway and the steam pipe.  
He raised both hands.  
Not palm-out like the slab. Different. Lower, open, fingers splayed as if he was shaping something in air.  
He listened with his skin.  
Pull phase came—air leaning toward the distant plain.  
Jhace moved with it.  
He swept his hands toward the fire, not fast—steady like pushing a load into position. His shoulders tightened. The heat behind his sternum flared, sharp, and his teeth buzzed in a way that matched the hum underfoot.  
The air answered.  
Not like a gust. Like a controlled shove.  
Smoke flattened. Flame tongues bent sideways, pressed back toward the interior instead of licking out into the lane. Embers tried to lift and got slammed down.  
It wasn't enough. Fire liked air too much.  
He shifted his aim.  
A joint on the steam line hissed, venting a thin ribbon upward.  
He hooked the shove of wind around that ribbon the way you'd redirect a flow in a pipe—curve it, carry it, throw it where it mattered.  
Left hand scooped. Right hand cut the air toward the burning doorway like guiding a stream into a funnel.  
The ribbon of steam thickened—maybe the joint failed more under the vibration, maybe the pull dragged it harder. He didn't care which.  
Steam rolled across the lane, white and dense, driven in a low sheet straight into the mouth of the building.  
It hit the heat and exploded into a wall of scalding fog. The flame inside coughed, then shrank. The orange at the windows dimmed, replaced by gray-white boiling.  
The crowd made a sound—half cheer, half scream—because steam looked like another disaster until it didn't.  
Jhace held his hands out and kept sweeping, smaller motions now, compressing and directing. He wasn't blowing the fire out. He was smothering it, stripping it of oxygen and drowning it in wet heat.  
His sternum locked. Breath cut. Vision narrowed.  
Copper flooded his mouth. It didn't fade this time. It clung.  
He forced the motion anyway. Tiny adjustments. Keep the steam sheet low. Keep it in the door. Don't let it backwash onto the crowd.  
The fire fought once—hungry, bright—then choked again as the steam packed the doorway.  
The orange dwindled to a pulse behind wet smoke.  
Jhace's arms trembled from shoulder to fingertips. Not effort like lifting a hammer—effort like holding a vibration in place while your bones tried to resonate with it.  
His lungs finally dragged in a thin breath. It tasted like copper and wet ash. He didn't spit. Spitting felt like giving something away.  
He let the wind go on the slack phase.  
The steam thinned. The smoke changed—less black, more gray. The doorway still radiated heat, but it wasn't spitting flame anymore.  
Jhace staggered one step back and braced a hand on the stone wall because his knees had decided they were optional.  
He looked at his hands.  
They were shaking again, worse than before—tiny jerks, out of sync, as if his nerves were trying to find the doubled coupled and missing. The tremor had spread up his forearms, a dull vibration under the skin.  
A cough tore out of him. Metal taste surged. He spat into the gutter without thinking and hated himself for it immediately.  
A woman pushed forward from the crowd, eyes wild. "Is it—?"  
"Not out," Jhace said. "But it's not eating the street. Get them away from the door. Now."  
She stared at him like he'd grown new bones. Then she turned and started shouting directions, and some people listened because a loud voice in a crisis becomes law.  
Jhace wiped his mouth with his sleeve and swallowed the rest of the copper anyway. It stayed. Like it was part of him now.  
He leaned close to the doorway's edge where the steam had cooled the stone enough to touch without skin sticking.  
He didn't go in yet.  
He listened.  
Through the wet hiss and the bell sequence and the planet's doubled hum, he heard something inside the building that wasn't fire.  
A cough. Human. Close.  
Jhace set his jaw.  
He raised his hands again—smaller this time—and shaped the air to hold the smoke back from the doorway like a curtain.  
His sternum burned. His throat tightened like the thought alone tried to pull the line into existence.  
He did it anyway.

# Chapter 5 — Chloen

Chloen didn't know the bell codes.  
She only knew bells meant grown-ups stopped pretending.  
It started like a feeling in her teeth.  
Not pain. A buzzing, like when you put your ear too close to the pipe runs in the Underface and the whole world sounds like it's inside a shell. She was on the mid-terrace steps with a bundle of rags under one arm because Aunt Lessa had sent her down to fetch clean wrap from the stitch shop, and Chloen had tried to walk like she wasn't late.  
Then the air got thin.  
Not cold. Thin.  
Her ears popped so hard it made her blink, and when she tried to take a bigger breath to fix it, her chest didn't fill right—like someone had pinched the air.  
Smoke did something wrong above the rooftops. It didn't rise. It pulled sideways and down, sucked back into chimneys as if the houses were drinking it.  
She stopped on the step and watched it, because her brain didn't know what else to do.  
People in the street stopped too.  
Not all at once. It was like a slow ripple—one person turning, then another, then everyone's heads going the same way.  
A cart horse stamped and tried to back up, eyes white.  
Chloen held her rag bundle tighter and followed the line of their faces.  
Far away past the terraces, something was happening on the plain. At first it looked like a storm wall, the kind that comes low and fast and eats the sky.  
Then it got taller. And taller. And it didn't move like weather.  
It rose.  
A wall. A cliff. A line where the world had been flat and now it wasn't. It climbed straight up, banded like the layers in cut rock—light, dark, light—except the layers were standing upright like someone had taken the ground and turned it on its side.  
Someone beside her whispered a prayer. Someone else laughed once, sharp, the wrong kind of laugh.  
The bell went again—one street over—fast, uneven.  
Chloen's stomach felt like it had dropped through her shoes.  
And then the sky tore.  
There was suddenly a hole in it. Not a cloud hole. A clean, black place in the blue.  
Stars inside it.  
In the day.  
That made her throat close even before the air tried to leave again.  
The grown-ups made a sound like one big breath being stolen.  
Chloen stared so hard her eyes watered. The black place shimmered at the edge, like the air was shaking because it didn't know where to stand.  
Something moved in that black.  
Too fast. Too long.  
She saw a flash of… not metal, not stone, not anything she'd ever held. It crossed the hole like a needle pulled through cloth. There were colors along it—bright veins that blinked copper and gold and green and another color that made her eyes hurt to try and name.  
Then it was gone.  
But the after of it wasn't.  
A streak of broken things followed behind it. Some pieces kept going with it. Some pieces fell back.  
And that was when Chloen's feet started to feel wrong.  
The street wasn't shaking like a cart going over bad stone. It was… soft, but not soft. Like standing on a drum skin that had been tightened too much and then someone hit it from underneath.  
The houses creaked.  
Mortar dust sifted out of seams like flour.  
People started moving all at once then. Not neat. Not ordered. They bumped and shoved and grabbed each other by sleeves.  
Chloen got pushed off the step and nearly fell. Someone's elbow hit her and she yelped, but the sound came out small and thin because the air was still wrong.  
Chloen tried to back up toward the wall of the nearest building because walls were safe.  
A crack sounded above her head.  
Stone was coming loose.  
A whole piece of building face—big and flat and heavy—tearing away from the terrace edge above and starting to fall.  
The street turned into screaming.  
Chloen ran.  
Her legs didn't feel like hers. The ground made her stumble like it was shifting under every step. Dust got in her mouth. It tasted like pennies.  
A man grabbed her arm—too hard—and yanked her sideways. "Keep moving\!" he barked, and shoved her forward.  
Chloen tried. But the crowd was thick and confused and the air kept pulling toward the plain like the sky had a mouth open somewhere.  
The slab shadow hit her.  
All light went wrong under it—dim and gray with dust swirling like smoke.  
Chloen's foot caught on something and she went down hard on her hands and knees. Her palms stung. The rags slid out of her grip.  
She looked up and saw the underside of the falling stone fill her whole world.  
She tried to scream and it stuck.  
She tried to get up and her arms shook like they'd forgotten how.  
Boots pounded past. Someone's heel clipped her shoulder and she fell sideways.  
The stone was too close. She could see the grain in it, old tool marks, dark seams where water had lived.  
She thought, stupidly, I don't want to die under a wall.  
Then the stone stopped.  
Not slowed. Stopped.  
It hung above her like a ceiling that had decided to hover.  
Dust froze in the air—enough that Chloen noticed. Copper-gold flecks glittered and stuck to her eyelashes.  
The street noise thinned into fragments. Boots. Coughing. A name being called.  
Chloen pushed up onto her knees.  
Someone's hand appeared—an adult hand, dusty, knuckles scraped—and pulled her elbow.  
Chloen tried to stand and her legs buckled. The hand yanked again and she got her feet under her.  
She looked up.  
The slab hovered above, held in mid-air. People ran underneath it, ducking even though it wasn't moving.  
Chloen's eyes tracked past the stone and found the man out in front of the crowd where nobody else stood.  
His arm was out, palm toward the slab.  
His face was tight. Concentrated like he was lifting something heavy and trying not to show it.  
His throat worked like breathing hurt.  
His eyes were on the stone. On the line of fall.  
On her.  
That made her move.  
Chloen stumbled forward out from under the slab.  
Her feet slid in dust. Her lungs pulled in air that tasted like metal. Her eyes watered so much she could barely see.  
She made it to the edge of the shadow.  
Light changed.  
One step, and she was out.  
She turned her head without meaning to, to look back.  
The man's arm was still out.  
For a heartbeat, nothing changed.  
Then his face flickered like something inside him slipped.  
The slab dropped.  
It slammed the street with a crack that punched up through Chloen's legs and into her stomach. Stone shattered. Dust exploded outward.  
The shock threw her forward. She hit the ground again, hard.  
Sound came back all at once.  
Screams. Shouting. Crying. The wet cough of dust in lungs.  
Chloen scrambled to her feet, half-blind, choking. Her tongue tasted copper like she'd been sucking on a nail.  
She looked back again.  
The man had gone down too—thrown backward like the stone had snapped a rope tied to him. He was on one elbow now, trying to sit up, and his hands were shaking so hard it looked like he couldn't hold them still even if he wanted to.  
A woman nearby—dust in her hair, slate in hand—was staring at him like she'd found a crack in the world and was trying to measure it.  
Chloen didn't know any of that. She just saw: he'd done something impossible, and it had hurt him.  
The bell rang again from somewhere deeper in the quarter.  
Chloen turned and ran toward the nearest doorway because doors meant shelter, even when shelter was a lie.  
She could still taste copper.  
She could still feel the hum in her bones—two notes fighting.  
And when she closed her eyes, she saw the man's outstretched palm holding up a slab that should have killed her.

# Chapter 6 — Jhace

Jhace kept his hands low where he could see them.  
Not because he thought they'd betray him again. Because they already had.  
Steam still clung to his sleeves. His kit strap bit his shoulder in a line that matched the sternum pressure like they'd agreed on a shape. The bell sequence had dragged them up terrace after terrace until the streets thinned and the city's edges stopped pretending they were edges and admitted they were a face—stone cut away into a drop so steep it made your stomach go light.  
They reached a break in the upper terrace wall where the parapet had cracked and slumped. Someone had already cleared rubble back from the lip. People stood in a row, shoulder to shoulder, staring down.  
Tiffani came up beside him without asking permission, slate tucked tight to her ribs. The copper grit on its edge lifted and settled in the wind pulses like filings on a magnet. Her knee hitched once. She ignored it and leaned forward.  
Chloen arrived a breath later, small in the gap between bodies, hair dust-matted, eyes wide in a way that wasn't just fear anymore. She didn't push. She slipped into the space that opened when people shifted away from the edge.  
Below them, the mountain face dropped into a basin of terraces and distant roofs. A city lay out there—larger than Saint Merro by a margin you could feel in the grid lines, in the breadth of its streets, in the density of its stacked stone. Smoke ribbons rose from chimneys. From this height it looked intact. From this height everything looked survivable.  
The sea did not.  
It came as a moving wall—water climbing out of itself, the surface torn into foam and debris. Not a wave you could swim in. A mass. A slab with no seams. It carried dark things inside it—wood, roof beams, something long and pale that might have been a mast or might have been a spine. It advanced with a certainty that made the bells feel pointless.  
Jhace watched the lip of the water where it met air. He watched the leading edge chew the ground ahead of it into mist. He watched the way the whole body of it leaned forward like it was being pulled by a hand you couldn't see.  
His teeth prickled on the doubled hum even from up here. The stone under the parapet vibrated in two layers. One old. One new.  
Chloen made a thin sound behind her teeth and swallowed it.  
Tiffani didn't speak. Her pencil moved.  
Jhace didn't look at her slate. He looked down at the city and counted what would fail first.  
Outer terraces. Lower supports. Any bridge span with a crooked seat from liquefaction. Any wall that had set wrong and was waiting for load.  
The wave kept coming.  
It should have hit the city like an axe.  
It didn't.  
Right before contact, the water changed direction.  
It wasn't slowly or like a tide choosing a channel.  
It turned.  
The leading edge of the wave kinked sideways as if a blade had cut a groove through it. Foam that had been rushing straight at the city snapped into a new vector, sliding along an invisible plane. The whole mass followed—tons of water re-routing as one body, redirected away from the densest district and forced into the open basin to the left where there was stone and scrub and nothing stacked for people to die in.  
Spray erupted where the redirected water scraped over ground. A new roar rolled up the face, late and heavy, vibrating through the parapet and into Jhace's sternum like a hand pressing to see if he'd learned.  
The city below still took damage—outer edges flooded, streets swallowed—but the central core, the thick-built heart, did not get hit straight on. The wave passed it like a fist missing a jaw by inches.  
A few people at the parapet cried out. Not words. Just noise. Relief and disbelief braided together.  
Jhace didn't make a sound.  
He watched the water. He watched the moment it obeyed something other than gravity.  
He felt his mouth fill with copper in anticipation, not after.  
That was the part that scared him.  
His hands twitched once, a small jerk toward the parapet, like the body remembered the line and wanted to throw it again. He clenched his fingers into his palm and held them there until the tremor settled back into its baseline pattern.  
Someone down there had done it.  
Someone had put a hand on a moving ocean and told it no.  
Jhace's stomach tightened with a hunger that wasn't food and wasn't heroism. It was the clean, brutal urge to be capable at that scale—the same itch that made him re-seat a guard until the bright marks stopped lying, only now the guard was the world and the mallet was whatever lived behind his sternum.  
He hated how much he wanted it.  
He glanced sideways without turning his head all the way.  
Tiffani's pencil was moving fast but controlled. Her slate was angled toward the horizon, not toward him. Her eyes were on the redirected flow, tracking, measuring. The copper grit on the slate edge lifted in tiny waves and settled again.  
Good.  
He was glad—suddenly, sharply glad—that she couldn't write down what he was thinking. Glad there wasn't an instrument that could read hunger and file it under M3.  
Because if she wrote it, it would become real in a way he didn't get to deny.  
Tiffani murmured, not to him, not to the crowd. To the slate. "Vector shift."  
She wrote it like it was enough.  
Then, quieter: "Force applied."  
Jhace swallowed copper. It scratched.  
Chloen leaned forward, gripping the broken parapet stone with both hands like she could keep herself from falling into the sight of it. Her knuckles were white under dust.  
"Did… did the water—" she started.  
"It turned," Jhace said. Flat. Confirming. No comfort.  
Chloen blinked hard and looked between the redirected basin and the city that had just not died.  
"How?"  
Jhace didn't answer because he didn't have one that wasn't a lie.  
Tiffani did not look up from her slate. "Not wind," she said, as if correcting herself before anyone else could. "Not terrain. Too late. Too clean."  
Jhace's jaw worked. He tasted metal again.  
He imagined his own hands doing it.  
His sternum tightened like the thought alone tried to pull the line into existence. Heat climbed under his ribs.  
He forced his breath into a measured pull on the slack phase. In. Out.  
He did not move his hands.  
A guard captain nearby was marking cracks with chalk. His slate-dust assistant watched Jhace with sharp eyes, making notes in a small book. Crown emblem on his sash. Jhace looked away, but the itch in his palms didn't stop.  
Down in the basin, the diverted wave slammed into rock and scrub and the underside of lower terraces. The impact threw up a fan of mist that glittered with copper-gold flecks even at this distance, catching the kindling-light in flashes that made the air look full of filings.  
Jhace watched the mist drift and settle and watched, for one heartbeat, a section of spray hang wrong—suspended, slow—before gravity reclaimed it.  
Field residue.  
A use.  
A cost.  
Somewhere down there, someone was paying.  
Jhace turned his head enough to look at Tiffani's slate at last—not to read it, but to see where she was looking.  
Her pencil stopped for a single beat.  
She wrote one new line, crisp as a stamped mark:  
M3: Mass-water vector deviation. Subject unknown.  
Then she added, smaller:  
Applied at last-second proximity.  
Jhace stared at the words and felt his hunger sharpen into direction.  
Somebody near the waterline. Somebody close enough to get drowned if they missed. Somebody who had touched the ocean and lived.  
He looked back down the face, scanning for a human scale point in the chaos. Too far. Too much spray. Too many moving pieces.  
Chloen's voice was small. "Is it… like what you did?"  
Jhace's hands flexed once, involuntary. The tremor tried to rise.  
He kept his eyes on the basin. "Bigger," he said.  
Tiffani's pencil ticked against the slate once—one controlled tap. A signal she used instead of swearing.  
"The same," she corrected, still not looking at him. "Only more."  
Jhace let that sit between his ribs like a weight.  
Below, the city's bells—distant, faint—began to ring, delayed by distance and wind. A new sequence, thin from far away, layered over Saint Merro's closer peals.  
Two cities ringing. One planet humming in two notes.  
Jhace took one step back from the parapet, not because he was done looking, but because if he stayed there he would start reaching.  
His sternum was already too awake.  
He wiped his mouth with his sleeve and tasted steam, ash, copper.  
He was glad Tiffani couldn't write what he was thinking.  
He wasn't glad he was thinking it.  
Somewhere down there, someone had moved an ocean.  
And now he knew it could be done.  
A small metal hook clattered on stone near his foot—something from a brace timber that had fallen loose. Jhace's hands flexed before he could stop them. For a fraction of a second, the hook lifted off the pavement, just an inch, then dropped again as he released the thought.  
No one saw it.  
But he felt it.  
And the copper taste surged again, sharp and insistent, like his body was trying to warn him about what he was becoming.

# Chapter 7 — Tiffani

Tiffani kept her slate closed.  
Not because there was nothing to write. Because there was too much, and the act of recording would change what people thought they were doing. If she raised graphite now, they'd start performing their panic for the page. They'd turn grief into testimony.  
She wasn't ready to be that kind of authority.  
The upper terrace had become a sorting place.  
Not formally or by decree—by physics.  
Rubble moved downhill when it could. People moved uphill when they could breathe. Anyone who could still walk drifted toward the wider stone flats where the terraces broadened and the parapets were intact enough to lean on. The broken streets below funneled survivors toward this ledge the way water found low points.  
Volunteers had formed lines. Hands passed stone from hand to hand. Smaller pieces went into baskets. Larger slabs were propped with scavenged timber and wedge bars. Someone had tied a length of rope across a crack in the paving to keep the crowd from stepping onto a section that shifted on the doubled pulse.  
A woman with a carpenter's hammer had marked doorframes with wax. A boy with a long pole tapped stones and listened for the hollow note of delamination. Someone—older, with a scarf over their mouth—had turned a smashed cart into a supply table and was rationing water in cups like it mattered, because it did.  
Tiffani moved through the worklines without stepping into anyone's path. Her eyes tracked loads and fractures the way her hands tracked stars. She counted by seeing.  
One: a retaining wall seam opened a hair each pulse.  
Two: a lintel with a new shear line, chalked in red.  
Three: a brace post that had set crooked after liquefaction—compensating now, but only by being loud about it.  
Chloen sat on the edge of a low wall with two other children, legs tucked up, hair still dust-matted. An older woman was wiping her face with a damp cloth. The girl's eyes tracked movement constantly, like she expected the stone to start falling again without warning.  
Tiffani watched her for three breaths, then looked away. She would not become a watcher who offered nothing.  
Jhace was farther off, near a collapsed arch, using a wedge bar and a section of pipe as leverage to lift a beam enough for two men to drag a body out. Alive. Coughing. Covered in soot and dust, but moving under their own command.  
Jhace's hands shook when he wasn't working.  
When he worked, they steadied.  
The tremor wasn't "fear." It was a signal problem. A resonance mismatch.  
Tiffani did not approach him.  
Not yet.  
The first reunions happened near the supply table. A woman with a split lip found her mother by accident and collapsed into her arms with a sound that was half laugh and half sob. A man held a child to his chest and repeated the child's name like the repetition could rewind time.  
Tiffani didn't write any of that down.  
It would be theft.  
The meeting assembled without announcement.  
A semicircle of people collected near the parapet where the ground was stable and the view kept everyone honest. No one wanted to look at the Rift constantly, but it sat above the Exit Face like a pressure in the corner of the eye, refusing to be ignored.  
A city factor arrived first—cloak dusted, rings on both hands, trying to stand like authority still meant anything. Two brace captains came with him, both with cracked knuckles and the practiced gaze of people who spent their lives keeping stone from deciding otherwise.  
A priest came too, not in full ceremonial robes—practical wool, a throat wrap, a small iron emblem at the collar. He wasn't smiling. He wasn't preaching. He looked tired in the way that meant he'd already done triage with words and found words insufficient.  
Two engineers from the pipe quarter took positions at the edge of the semicircle. One carried a ledger. The other carried nothing but kept rubbing his fingers together as if grit had gotten under his skin.  
And then there was Tiffani, standing slightly apart with her slate still closed, because the moment she began recording, the meeting would change shape around her.  
They spoke in fragments at first.  
"Entry?" one engineer asked, voice rough. "If the wound is an exit, where's the other end?"  
"Not here," the brace captain said. "We felt it before we saw it. The pull came from elsewhere."  
The factor turned his head, looking down the mountain face toward the far city and the basin where the water had redirected. "That wave should have—"  
"It didn't," someone cut in. A woman with dust in her hair and a wrist wrapped in cloth. She was not official. She was simply loud enough to earn space. "It turned."  
A murmur ran through the semicircle. Agreement. Disbelief. The kind of sound people make when reality refuses to behave.  
The priest lifted a hand slightly, not to silence them but to gather them. "We need to name what we can name," he said. "And leave the rest for later."  
Good instinct.  
Naming stabilized panic. But naming incorrectly was worse than leaving blank space.  
Tiffani opened her slate.  
Not to write everything. To anchor what mattered.  
She wrote one line at the top and let the people see the shape of it without reading it aloud:  
Observed: Rift persists. Exit Face stable. Pressure pulses. Doubled coupled baseline.  
She did not write M1, M2, M3. Not yet.  
The brace captain's eyes flicked to her slate. He swallowed once. "Doubled," he repeated, as if tasting the concept. "So it isn't settling. It's… permanent?"  
Tiffani didn't answer with certainty because certainty was a claim. She answered with measurement.  
"It hasn't decayed," she said. "Not in the time I've been listening."  
"How long?" the factor demanded.  
Tiffani glanced at the chronometer in her mind, not in her hand. She didn't need to open the box to know the intervals. "Since first pressure drop," she said. "It continues."
