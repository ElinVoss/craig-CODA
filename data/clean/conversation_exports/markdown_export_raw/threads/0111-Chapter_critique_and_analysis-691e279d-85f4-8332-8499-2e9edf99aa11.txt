Chapter One – A Door That Must Never Sag
The order slipped out from under the usual work and landed on Jhace’s boot.
He bent, wiped his fingers on his apron, and unfolded the oiled paper.
One hinge, load-bearing. For a door that must never sag. Weight: considerable.
— T. Merrow, Merrow Glass & Mirrors, Rue of Saint Merro.
A sketch accompanied it: a hinge with double pins, flared shoulders, a few fussy curves that understood weight but not iron.
“A door that must never sag,” Jhace muttered.
He could make a hinge in his sleep. But this wasn’t a barn door on a straight wall. If it was carrying glass, the slightest warp would crack it. He glanced at the window. Across the alley, between the cooper and the weaver, Merrow Glass & Mirrors leaned like every other old house on the street.
He should just make his best version, take the coin, be done.
Instead he took his coat, slipped the drawing into an inner pocket, and locked the forge behind him.
The glass shop’s sign was faded, its shutters scabbed with old repairs. He knocked. After a moment the door opened a hand’s width.
A woman looked out: dark hair bound back, sleeves rolled, faint burn marks along her fingers.
“T. Merrow?” he asked.
“Tiffani Merrow,” she said. “And you are…?”
“Jhace Torrin. The smith.” He tapped his chest. “I have your order. I don’t yet have your hinge.”
Her eyes narrowed. “Is there a problem?”
“Not if I see the door,” he said. “You wrote ‘must never sag.’ I don’t know the wall, the frame, the weight. If I guess wrong, your door fails and my name goes with it.”
She held his gaze a moment, then stepped back.
“Come up, then, since you care so much about my hinges.”
The shop smelled of dust and polish, sheets of glass stacked along the walls, mirrors giving back chopped pieces of his reflection. She led him up a narrow stair. Heat met him halfway like a solid thing.
The furnace room was long and close, windows on one side, kiln on the other. An apprentice scrubbed a table in the back. Near the front, propped in a brace, stood the door frame.
Not a solid door. A wooden frame with lead strips already soldered into a pattern, some spaces filled with colored glass, others empty.
“A window in a door,” Jhace said.
“A door that’s mostly window,” Tiffani answered. “It’ll hang there.” She nodded at the leaning wall. “Back entrance. I’m tired of working in the dark.”
He crouched, studying the old hinge-posts: tired iron, stone that had settled out of true years ago.
“You’re hanging lead and glass on a crooked wall,” he said. “People will come through all day. It will slam. It will swell in winter, shrink in summer. If the door fights the wall, something breaks.”
“Exactly why I don’t want a market-stall hinge,” she said. “The frame’s old. I’m not tearing it out. I can’t afford a mason on top of everything else.”
She said it simply, but he heard the strain in it.
“How many people use this door?” he asked. “Workers? Deliveries?”
“Us, and whoever brings sand and cullet when they remember we exist,” she said. “We prop it open when the kiln’s running hot. The boy—” she jerked her chin at the apprentice “—forgets himself and slams it when he’s got both hands full.”
“Once,” the boy muttered.
“Once so far,” she said.
Jhace ran his thumb along the drawn hinge in his mind. He looked at her without quite meaning to.
She stood with her feet set wider than her shoulders, weight low. When the floor dipped under a board, her knees flexed and her hips shifted, top half adjusting while her base stayed steady. Not stiff—ready.
“A hinge that moves like you stand,” he said under his breath.
“What?” she asked.
“You brace low, give high,” he said. “If something knocks you, you bend and come back, you don’t topple. The hinge needs to do the same. Broader shoulder. Two pins. A curve that lets the door swing forward a breath before it turns in, so the weight glides instead of grinding.”
She studied him like he was another tool on the rack.
“Can iron be that thoughtful?” she asked.
“If someone makes it so,” he said. “Metal will do what you shape it to.”
Her mouth twitched. “You’ve been watching from your window,” she said. “The furnace, I mean.”
“The light’s hard to miss,” he said. It came out more defensive than he liked.
“And the smith who crosses the street to ask about a hinge is hard to miss,” she replied. “All right, Master Torrin. Make it as you’ve described. Strong enough for my nonsense, kind enough not to tear my work off the wall. Try not to make it ugly.”
“I don’t make ugly hinges,” he said.
“We’ll see,” she said. “How long?”
“A week,” he said, before his sensible mind could interfere.
“A week, then.” She held out her hand.
He took it. Her grip was firm, palm rough in different places than his; heat scars instead of hammer thickening. It felt like shaking hands with another sort of fire.
“Thank you for coming in person,” she said. “Most would have guessed and blamed the wall when it failed.”
“I’d rather blame myself beforehand,” he said.
A voice called her name from the far end. She released his hand and turned away, already folding back into the pattern of her work.
Jhace went back down through the cool, dusty shop and into the street. The forge felt almost mild after the kiln. He woke the banked coals, laid a bar of iron across the growing orange, and pictured the leaning wall, the heavy door, the way Tiffani’s weight sat over her feet.
A door that must never sag.
He drew the bar out when it bloomed white at the edges and set it on the anvil. His hammer found its rhythm: brace, curve, give, hold.
The hinge would take that weight, he promised himself, even if he had to ruin three bars learning how.
He didn’t yet know how much more than a door he’d agreed to hold up.
Chapter Two – Pressure Lines
Tiffani watched Jhace from the upstairs window until he vanished into his forge.
The glass beside her broke his shape into blue and green fragments, then gave him up. When the door below closed, the workshop’s sounds shrank to the soft tick of cooling glass and the low breath of the banked furnace.
“Marc,” she called down the stair.
He appeared halfway up, cloth in hand. “Yes, mistress?”
“That’s enough for today,” she said. “If you stay any longer you’ll melt. Go home. We’ll pick up tomorrow.”
He blinked, then grinned with relief. “Yes, mistress.”
A few minutes later she heard him gather his things and the front door close.
Silence settled, the particular kind that follows the last other person leaving a room. No off-beat footsteps, no muttering, no clatter that wasn’t hers.
She turned from the window and walked the length of the workshop.
Furnace at one end, banked low. Bench of tools—rods, paddles, shears—all in their places. Sacks of sand stacked by the wall, pressing inward. The unfinished door frame by the window, lead river sketched and half-filled, waiting on glass and a hinge she didn’t yet have.
Ledger downstairs, she thought. Rent. Coal. Guild. Hinge—one week.
She circled back to the door frame, laid her knuckles lightly against the wood. It answered with a dull, honest knock.
“You and that wall,” she told it. “You’re all I’ve got. Try not to drag each other down.”
Her own voice sounded too loud.
She drew breath to say more—and froze.
Something sang at the edge of hearing. A thin, high note, like the rim of a glass very softly struck.
She held still, head tilted.
Not the furnace. Its usual murmur was a low animal sound, and it was barely awake. No wagon rattled under the window. No one in the street was dragging metal along stone.
The sound lingered, faint, almost nothing.
Then it grew.
Not louder so much as nearer, as if it had stepped from outside the walls to just behind her eyes. Her teeth began to ache, a slow pressure along the roots.
She pressed her tongue against them. Copper bloomed in her mouth, sharp and metallic. No cut. No blood. Just taste.
“The kiln,” she said under her breath, turning toward it.
The furnace door sat shut, light banked to a steady orange. The bricks were still. No rattle in the flue, no hiss.
The note climbed, so high it stopped being a note and became a tension. The hair on her arms lifted.
On the racks, a few glass sheets began to tremble.
Not rattle—there was no shaking in the air—just a fine, constant shiver, as if they were set humming by a hand she couldn’t see. Light from the window smeared across their surfaces.
“Stop,” she said, uselessly.
The glass did not stop.
Under the high thread, a low hum joined in, deep enough she felt it in her chest. The floorboards picked it up. The boards under her boots seemed to buzz.
Her heartbeat fell into step with it, too fast, then too slow.
A small pane cut for the river door snapped with a sharp tik. A spiderweb of cracks shot across it from a flaw that hadn’t been there a moment before.
She flinched like she’d been struck.
“All right,” she said through her teeth. “Enough.”
The workshop didn’t listen.
The pressure in her ears thickened, as if a storm sat right on the roof. The air smelled wrong—less of smoke and sweat, more like lightning, sharp and mineral.
She moved toward the window simply because it was somewhere to go. Each step landed a half-fraction late, like the floor had to think about meeting her.
She grabbed the sill with both hands.
Outside, the Rue of Saint Merro had tilted.
The cooper’s shop across the way slouched further to one side. Rooflines slid against each other by a degree her eye knew they shouldn’t. The whole street seemed very slightly off-level, as if the world had decided to lean.
No one else noticed.
A woman shifted her basket from one hip to the other. A carter shook his reins. A child dashed past, laughing, running at an angle that did not match the pull she felt in her own body.
The note climbed again, higher than hearing, until her vision blurred at the edges.
Her gaze snagged on a small round mirror hanging near the window. Her own face looked back—stretched, pale, eyes too wide.
The mirror’s surface rippled once, like water under a dropped pebble.
Her fingers dug into the sill. Wood bit into her palm. It felt reassuringly solid, which only made everything else worse.
“Hold,” she whispered—to the room, the glass, the street, herself.
The hum thickened until it was everything: air, bone, heartbeat. A single, sustained pressure that seemed to be looking for something to crack.
The unfinished door frame at her side began to buzz in its braces. The lead lines shook. The empty spaces where glass would go looked suddenly like holes cut into nothing, not waiting for anything, just opening onto absence.
Her stomach lurched as if the floor had dropped, though her boots stayed planted. For a breathless instant the world felt unmoored—no up, no down, only the sensation of falling without moving.
She could not see a bottom to that fall. Only the sure knowledge that whatever it led to was not the same city that had been here a moment before.
Chapter Three – When the World Was Pierced
 

Jhace found his rhythm again.

Iron on anvil, coals breathing, the city’s noise weaving through the open shutters in a familiar, careless braid.

The hinge lay half-formed on the anvil: spine thick, arms curved, shoulders broad enough to argue with a crooked wall. He’d worked the first pin socket true, bored it clean, and now he was drawing out the second arm, matching it to the first by eye and instinct.

He fed the fire in small, steady offerings. A shovel of coal, rake it level, bellows until the heart of it went from orange to a hungry, almost-white yellow. The heat licked his face, sweat carving paths through soot on his skin.

Outside, the Rue of Saint Merro woke properly.

Hawkers called bread and fish and cheap cloth. The distant hiss of a kettle at the bathhouse let out its thin steam whistle, a shrill, familiar punctuation to the morning. Somewhere, a potter’s kiln vented with a coughing roar. Wagon wheels thudded over the same loose cobble they always hit, followed by the same curse.

Jhace’s hammer fell between those sounds, steady and sure. The city drew its cadence, and he fit his work into the gaps.

He lifted the glowing iron, turned it, struck. The metal yielded, edges soft under the hammer’s face. Sparks jumped and died around his boots.

Curve. Shoulder. Pin.

He quenched the piece briefly to read its color, steam snatching at his arm.

The kettle whistle down the street let out another shriek. A second answered it from somewhere further uphill, then a third, as if the city itself were clearing its throat.

Jhace frowned. Three boilers at once? The bathhouse, the tannery, and the guild kitchens all venting in the same breath was possible, but it set his teeth on edge.

He shoved the hinge back into the coals and reached for the bellows.

As he worked the pedals, the usual roar of rising fire took on a strange overtone. A higher note, thin and taut, threaded through the rush of air. Like the sound of a blade singing when you strike it—but stretched out, held far beyond comfort.

He paused, foot hovering.

The bellows wheezed once more, then stopped. The fire settled, but the note did not.

It hung there at the edge of hearing, too pure to belong to anything with gears or wood in it.

Jhace straightened slowly, hand still on the bellows handle.

The hair on his arms prickled.

He listened.

The city had not gone silent; not yet. A woman laughed outside. A cart creaked past. Someone shouted for a boy to get out of the street.

Over it all—threading through it, around it—came that high, drawn-out sound.

A glass edge, he thought, though that made no sense. Stroked, not struck.

His teeth began to ache.

He put his tongue to the back of them. They were solid. The ache wasn’t. It came from deeper, along the roots, down into his jaw.

He tasted metal. Not the honest tang of the forge, but the sharper flavor of bitten tongue. He hadn’t bitten anything.

“The kiln,” he muttered, even though there was nothing in his own shop that should sing like that.

He checked anyway. The small tempering oven in the corner was cool. No kettles, no pipes. Nothing that made steam except sweat.

The note climbed higher.

The usual sounds of the Rue dipped, voices faltering as people, one by one, stopped mid-word to listen without meaning to. The hum tightened, as if someone had taken an invisible string and begun to pull.

On the rack by the window, a row of unfinished nails began to tremble. Just a whisper of motion, shy shivers against the wooden pegs.

Jhace watched them, forgetting to blink.

“Stop that,” he told them.

They did not.

The water in the quench trough rippled. Not from any draft; the air was still. Concentric rings spread out from no visible source, as if the trough itself remembered being struck.

The hammer in his hand felt faintly alive.

The high note thickened, became something he felt in bone more than heard in ear. Under it, a second tone rose—low, deep, a pressure that seemed to push up from the floor.

The boards under his boots buzzed.

His heart stuttered, then fell into rhythm with that deeper hum: too fast, then too slow, then strangely in time.

“All right,” he said under his breath. “Enough.”

The world, for once, did not listen.

He crossed to the shutters and shoved them wider.

Light knifed in. The street lay below, tilted—not visibly, not yet, but he felt the wrongness in his knees.

People stood with heads tipped back, mouths half-open, looking up.

Jhace followed their gaze.

The sky was being punctured.

Far above the rooftops, the blue had gone hard and bright, like enamel fired too long. Through it pushed a point of something that was not cloud, not storm.

At first it looked like a star at noon, impossibly small and bright. Then it grew. And grew.

A lance.

No—it had thickness, a central bulge tapering away to needle-sharp ends. Even at that impossible height he could feel its size, some animal part of him taking the measure of it and flinching.

Glass, his mind supplied, because the surface caught the sun and broke it into hard shards of color. Not just glass. Metal glinted along it—iron? Silver? Bands of something duller, pewter-grey. And streaked along its length, frozen waves of sand and stone fused into ripples.

An alien fusion of everything it had eaten on its way here.

It wasn’t falling. It was arriving.

The note in the air climbed with it, higher, tighter, as if the thing itself were drawing the sound behind it like a tail.

Someone screamed in the street. Somewhere else a horse reared, eyes white, bridle jangled uselessly as the rider fought.

Jhace gripped the windowsill until his knuckles went white.

The lance—no word he had felt right for it—thickened in the sky as it descended. Its central body must have been leagues across, but perspective hammered it flat. He could see now that both ends tapered to vicious points, slightly asymmetrical, as if it had lost pieces of itself to something he’d never see.

The city bell in the tower of Saint Merro began to ring. Once, twice. Then more, the pattern collapsing into a frantic clamor as whoever pulled the rope forgot the proper calls and simply hauled.

The note from the sky swallowed it.

He did not see it hit.

There was no single strike, no neat moment of contact he could point to.

One heartbeat, the spear hung above the world, point angled slightly north as if considering.

The next, the planet shuddered.

The hum underneath everything surged. Jhace’s stomach dropped, though his feet stayed planted. The horizon flexed. For a breath, every angle in the world felt wrong—streets, walls, the line of the opposite roof.

The floor lurched.

Jhace grabbed the window frame with both hands as his workshop jumped sideways, then down, then up in a sequence that made no sense. Tools slid from the bench. The hinge clanged off the anvil and skittered across the floor, ringing like a bell.

The building groaned, timbers complaining in long, tortured creaks. Somewhere below, he heard crockery explode.

The high note—they’d lived with it for what felt like a lifetime and might have been only moments—broke.

In its place came a sound like the city exhaling all at once.

Then noise, all kinds at once.

Screams. Crashes. The roar of something big collapsing—stones slamming into each other, beams snapping.

He pulled himself back from the window as the frame itself shifted, warping under strain.

The quench trough overturned behind him, water surging across the floor. It ran at a slant, pooling in one corner instead of the usual two.

“Out,” he told himself. “You need to get out.”

The doorway looked narrower than it had that morning. The ceiling above it had developed a new crack, jagged and dark.

He took one step and was thrown sideways as the workshop jerked again. The far wall seemed to surge toward him; his shoulder slammed into the anvil hard enough to drive the breath from his lungs.

For a heartbeat he thought the building would go over. That the whole narrow house would peel away from its neighbors, slide into the street, and lie there on its face like a felled tree.

It didn’t.

It leaned, complained, dropped part of the ceiling in a storm of dust and lath, and then held.

Slowly—too slowly—it stilled, trembling like a living thing that had been hit and wasn’t yet sure if it was dead.

Jhace coughed, wiped grit from his eyes with the back of his hand, and staggered for the door.

He reached the street to find the Rue of Saint Merro half-collapsed into itself.

Houses that had leaned politely for generations now leaned rudely, jostling each other like drunks desperate for support. Some had lost the argument and lay in broken heaps, front walls crumpled into their own rooms. A plume of dust hung over everything, turning the air into thick, choking cloth.

People stumbled through it like ghosts.

A woman clutched a child, both white with plaster. A man stood screaming at a sinkhole where the cobbles had simply dropped away, taking his cart and horse with them. Somewhere, someone called a name over and over until it broke.

Jhace shoved past them all, eyes tracking up.

The sky was wrong.

Not in the obvious way—no hole gaped where the spear had passed. The blue was still there, but stretched, drawn, as if some huge hand had dragged color and cloud sideways and left them slightly off.

A line cut across it.

Hair-thin from here, but too straight to be natural, a faint seam that ran from one horizon toward the other before the haze swallowed it. He couldn’t say what it was. He only knew it hadn’t been there that morning.

Something was falling.

Not rain. Not ash.

Dust, at first: a fine, glittering powder that settled on hair and skin and stone. It looked like ground glass and sand and metal filings all mixed into one, catching the weird light in colors that didn’t quite belong to any he knew.

It tasted of copper and heat when it landed on his tongue.

Bigger pieces followed. Shards. Flakes.

Bits of the thing that had passed above them, scraped off by whatever violence had just happened, now coming down wherever they pleased.

A piece the size of his thumb struck the cobbles near his boot and stuck there, half-fused into the stone as if it had always been part of it. Its surface was glassy, veined with threads of iron and something that looked like pewter and something that looked like no metal he’d ever seen.

It pulsed.

Very faintly. Not with light, exactly, but with a suggestion of brightness somewhere inside it, as if it were thinking about glowing.

He stared.

All around, similar specks and shards embedded themselves in wood and brick and skin. Some bounced away. Some didn’t. People cursed, brushing at their sleeves and hair, not noticing which flecks refused to be brushed off.

One grain, no larger than a seed, struck Jhace on the back of his right hand.

He hissed, more from surprise than pain, and swatted at it. It should have bounced. Instead it sank, heatless and sure, into his skin.

He stared at the spot.

A faint, pale thread of light chased the path of his veins for a finger’s width, then faded. His hand tingled, like a limb held too long in one position.

He flexed his fingers. They obeyed, stiff but working.

No time for that now.

Across the alley, Merrow Glass & Mirrors still stood.

The front wall had cracked, sign hanging by one chain, but the building was upright. The upper shutters were askew; one had torn clean off and lay shattered in the street. Through the gap he could see the gleam of glass and the slow, half-falling sway of something inside finally giving up its grip.

“Tiffani,” he said.

He didn’t remember deciding to move; he was already running, dodging a fallen beam, skidding in dust and spilled water. The world lurched under him again, not from a new quake but from the realization that the ground itself now had a lean he didn’t know.

He hit the glass shop’s door with his shoulder. It gave, frame warped, and he shoved his way in.

The air inside was thicker, full of powdered plaster and the fine, glittering residue. The smell of hot brick and broken things slammed into him.

“Tiffani!” he shouted, voice raw.

Above him, something groaned.

He took the stairs two at a time toward the broken light above, the taste of copper thick on his tongue, the city still shaking itself apart below—unaware of anything except the need to reach her before whatever was still moving finally came down.
Chapter Four – Shards That Listen
The bottom did not come.
The feeling of falling without moving stretched, thin as the sound in her skull. Tiffani clung to the window sill, lungs locked, while the world around her decided which way was down.
Then it chose.
The workshop dropped.
Not far—half a handspan, perhaps—but every board, brick, and beam lurched at once. Her boots slammed back into the floor. Pain shot up her knees. Plaster cracked with a brittle, tearing noise; a strip of it tore free from the ceiling and burst across the workbench in a chalky wave.
The high note in the air snapped lower, then climbed again, tighter.
Glass began to go.
It started with a single clear pane on the rack by the furnace, shivering until a fine starburst leaped from its center. The fracture spread faster than her eye could follow. The sheet came apart with a sound like a hundred little screams, pieces raining down in a bright, deadly curtain.
Tiffani flinched back, throwing up her hands.
Shards hissed past her, an invisible knife-edge of wind chasing them. One slashed the air where her throat had been a heartbeat before. Another skimmed her forearm, cutting through sleeve and skin with neat, impersonal precision.
Pain flared hot. Warmth ran down to her palm.
She grabbed for the furnace to steady herself, leather glove taking the heat. The kiln felt wrong—vibrating, not with its own fire, but with that thin, keening sound driving through the brick.
“Hold,” she gasped at it, because there was nothing else to say.
The furnace held.
The floor did not. It rolled under her feet, a slow, nauseating heave, as if the whole house were a boat shoved sideways by some enormous unseen wave.
Beyond the window, the Rue of Saint Merro buckled.
Tiffani saw roofs dip and jerk, chimneys snap, one whole facade peel away and crumble into the street. People pinwheeled, arms flailing for balance that did not exist. A cart toppled, wheels spinning uselessly in the air.
Above it all, the sky was wrong.
She caught only flashes through the window frame and the dust—something bright, elongated, too straight to be cloud, cutting from one side of the heavens to the other. For a moment it seemed to hang there, a spear of fused brightness.
The sound in her skull peaked.
The world shuddered so hard her teeth clicked. Somewhere deep beneath her feet, a long, grinding roar rose—the sound of something enormous being forced through something larger that did not want to move.
Te’Oga itself, she thought wildly, and then she had no room for thought at all.
The river door tore loose from its brace.
The half-finished frame swung, leaded pattern yawning toward the floor. Glass that had survived the first jolt exploded now, pieces spinning out like thrown coins.
She lunged.
Her shoulder slammed into the wood. Weight crashed into her, trying to drag her down. She locked her arms and dug her heels in, boots skidding on glass.
“Hold,” she snarled through her teeth. “You are not falling before you’ve even hung.”
The frame bucked. For a sick instant she knew she would lose it.
Something stung the back of her hand.
She felt it: a pinprick cold, then a slipping, a sinking that was not weight but absence. A grain of something—dust? glass?—passed straight through her skin without resistance.
Her veins lit.
A pale thread of light raced from the sting up across the back of her hand, toward her wrist, then faded. A fizzing, urgent sensation bloomed in her palm, like her hand had been asleep for hours and was waking all at once.
The sound in the room changed.
It didn’t lessen. It… focused.
She could feel, with unbearable clarity, where the door’s weight pulled hardest, where the wall flexed, where the lead wanted to fold. The strain mapped itself into her bones, a pattern of lines and angles that had nothing to do with vision and everything to do with pressure.
She shifted her grip without thinking, sliding her left hand along the frame to a point that suddenly felt right.
The weight shifted.
Not much; the door was still heavy, the wall still crooked. But the force of it slid away from the weakest joints, distributing along steadier lines. The frame stopped pitching toward the floor and settled against her shoulder instead, quivering, then easing by a fraction.
“Good,” she hissed, breath tearing in her throat. “Good. Breathe with me.”
The door, being wood and lead and glass, did not breathe. It did, however, refrain from crushing her.
Shards continued to fall around them, a bright, lethal rain. She heard glass slicing wood, snapping under boots, chattering across stone.
One narrow sliver darted toward her face, catching what little light remained. Reflex jerked her head aside. Her left hand flared hot, that new buzzing rising sharp.
“No,” she snapped.
The shard twitched.
It did not stop, not entirely—but it veered, strike glancing off the door frame instead of burying itself in her eye. It fell at her feet with a disappointed chime.
Her heart hammered in her ears. Sweat and blood stung her eyes.
This is not happening, she thought. Glass does not listen. Doors do not breathe with you. Dust does not sink into your skin and teach your hand where to stand.
Another heave rolled through the workshop, less violent now but longer, a deep, bone-level sway. Somewhere below, a beam gave up with a crack. The building sagged, then settled into a new, crooked shape.
The high note in the air began to fray.
It dropped, not vanishing but loosening, as if whatever string had been tightened past reason was finally, reluctantly, being allowed some slack. The lower hum under her feet shifted from violent shove to steady, unpleasant throb.
Tiffani eased the door back into what remained of its brace. Her arms shook with the effort. When she let go, it stayed more or less where she’d put it, listing but upright.
She staggered away, boots crunching glass, and grabbed the edge of the workbench to stay standing.
Half the workshop was wreckage.
The rack nearest the furnace had collapsed, a tangle of warped iron and broken sheets. The window had lost most of its panes; those that remained sat at wrong angles, some fused with new, faintly glowing grit glittering along the cracks. A length of ceiling had come down near the stair, leaving beams exposed like ribs.
In the middle of the bench, half buried in plaster dust, lay a chunk of something that had not been there before.
About the size of her fist. Smooth as melted glass in some places, rough as cooled slag in others. Veins of dull metal—iron, pewter, and some third thing she had no name for—ran through a body the color of old sand turned to stone.
It pulsed.
Not brightly. Not even visibly, at first. More a suggestion of light under the surface, in time with nothing she recognized.
As she stared, a veil of fine dust drifted down through the broken ceiling. It sparkled as it fell, catching odd, sharp colors even in the ruined light.
Some of it landed on her arms, her face, the bench, the door frame, the furnace bricks. The rest vanished into cracks, into hair, into open mouths across the city.
Another grain struck her, this time at the corner of her jaw.
Cool. Then gone.
Her skin tingled. Sound seemed to jump closer—not louder, but clearer. The creak of a beam three houses down, the distant shattering of a window, the uneven cadence of running feet in the street all snapped into painful focus.
She clapped a hand to her ear, uselessly, then dropped it. It didn’t help.
She listened anyway.
Screams, now, mixed with shouted names. The heavy, final crump of something large collapsing nearby. The river, perhaps, out of sight but suddenly loud in her bones, crashing against its banks as if someone had shoved the world underneath it.
And over it all, like an aftertaste, a faint, persistent ringing—as if the spear she had glimpsed in the sky were still humming its way through the planet, even though it was already gone.
The floor under her boots had a lean it hadn’t had that morning. She adjusted, feet finding a new balance.
Fine, she thought. The city tilts, we tilt with it.
She forced herself toward the stair, picking her way around fallen beams and drifts of glass. Every shard her eyes touched seemed to jump a little in her awareness, as if waiting for some command.
“Stay,” she said to them all, voice low and sharp. “You’ve done enough.”
They were glass. They stayed.
She reached the head of the stairs and gripped the rail. It creaked, but held.
