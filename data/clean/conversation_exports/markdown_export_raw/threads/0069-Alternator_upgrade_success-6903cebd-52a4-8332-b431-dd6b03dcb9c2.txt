Got it—spike + stall means the charging system isn’t just “annoying,” it’s actively upsetting the PCM. On an ’06 S197 the alternator is PCM-controlled (“Smart Charge”), so a wiring/regulator/control issue can make it go full-field, over-voltage for a moment, then the PCM resets and the engine dies.

Here’s a tight, do-this-now diagnostic plan that’ll actually chase the root cause:

1) Battery first (it can kill alternators)
- Resting: ≥12.6 V after sitting 30+ min.  
- Cranking drop: shouldn’t dip below ~10.0 V.  
- If the battery fails a load/conductance test, replace it before anything else. A weak battery causes regulator overshoot and will nuke remans.

2) Catch the spike
- DMM on the battery, MIN/MAX capture, engine idling, then shift through gears and into Park (with big electrical loads on: headlights, rear defrost, blower).  
- Normal: ~13.5–14.7 V steady.  
- If you see >15.5 V even briefly, that’s a regulator/command issue—not just a “light.”

3) Check AC ripple (bad diodes = false “OK” on many store tests)
- DMM to AC Volts across the battery at 1,500–2,000 rpm with loads on.  
- Acceptable: ≤0.2–0.3 V AC.  
- ≥0.5 V AC = failing diode pack → alternator/regulator is bad even if “new.”

4) Voltage-drop tests (find hidden resistance that fools the system)
- B+ path: red lead on alternator output stud, black on battery +, engine at 1,500 rpm, loads on. You want ≤0.2 V drop. More = corroded lug, loose nut, or a tired megafuse/junction.  
- Ground path: red lead on alternator case, black on battery –, same test. ≤0.1–0.2 V. More = ground issue (even with “new” cables). Verify the engine-to-chassis braided strap.

5) Smart-Charge control plug (the common S197 gotcha)
You mentioned replacing the harness twice—aftermarket pigtails are frequently mis-pinned on these years.
- Inspect the 2- or 3-pin alternator control connector closely: terminal tension, corrosion, and—most important—pin order. Compare wire colors/positions to a factory diagram; many pigtails ship with GENCOM/GENMON swapped. A swapped/open control wire = full-field, lamp on, voltage spikes, and stalls.  
- Tug test each wire; no movement. If in doubt, repin to match OEM.

6) Correct alternator type matters
- The ’05–’06 wants the Motorcraft-spec smart-charge regulator (6G family). Some parts-store units are the wrong internal regulator (or reman junk) and will always flag “Check Charging System.” Given you’re on alternator #4, I’d strongly try a Motorcraft unit (not just “compatible”).

7) Unplug test (helps separate PCM vs alternator)
- With the engine idling, unplug the small smart-charge connector from the alternator (do **not** disconnect the battery).  
- If voltage immediately settles around a fixed ~13.5 V and the stall/spike behavior goes away, suspect the control circuit/PCM command (GENCOM) or mis-pinned pigtail.  
- If it still spikes or wanders, the alternator/regulator itself is faulty/wrong for the car.

8) Pull the right codes (generic OBD-II won’t show them)
Use a Ford-aware scan tool (FORScan is perfect) to read **PCM and instrument cluster** modules. Look for:  
- P1246/P0620/P0622 (generator control/field faults)  
- P0562/P0563 (system voltage low/high)  
- U-codes for loss of comms with generator  
If you see control-circuit codes and your pinout is good, the PCM may need a reflash (calibration) or, rarely, repair.

9) Fuses/feeds to check
- Under-hood megafuse for the alternator output (replace if heat-stressed), and the PCM/GEN control fuses/relays in the Battery Junction Box/Smart Junction Box. A weak feed to the regulator or PCM will cause surging and the lamp.

10) Don’t keep driving it like this
Those over-voltage spikes can cook modules, coils, and the cluster. Fix before more testing miles.
