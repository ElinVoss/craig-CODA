The Research Phase — Google as a Teacher
They don't know what to search yet. That's the real beginning of learning anything — learning what words to use.

They start with obvious searches. "Parts tracking software." "Inventory location system." They find enterprise tools that cost $40,000 to implement. They find spreadsheet templates. They find forum posts from 2014 that link to software whose websites no longer exist.

They search Reddit. This is where the education actually starts. Someone in a subreddit for small business owners is asking the same question. The top comment says "we just use a Google Sheet." The second comment says "we tried [Big Software] and it was overkill." The third comment says "honestly someone should just build a simple web app for this."

That third comment lands differently than it would have six months ago.

They start searching differently. "How do people build web apps." "What do developers use to build tools like this." They find the word React. They find the word CRUD. They find a YouTube video titled something like "Build an inventory app in React — beginner friendly." They watch the first four minutes during their lunch break.

The YouTube Rabbit Hole
The video makes it look achievable. That's the dangerous part. The person thinks: I could follow along with that. They don't think "I'm going to become a developer." They think "I could probably make a thing that does the thing I need."

They start freeCodeCamp at night. Not because someone told them to — because they googled "learn React free" and that's what came up. They do the JavaScript fundamentals section. It's boring but they push through because the goal isn't to learn JavaScript, the goal is to build the thing. The JavaScript is just in the way.

They hit their first real wall around the time they encounter asynchronous functions. They don't understand why console.log is printing undefined when they just set the value two lines above. They spend an evening on this. They find a Stack Overflow answer from 2017 that sort of explains it. They find a YouTube comment where someone asks the same question and the reply is condescending. They eventually find one sentence in a blog post that makes it click.

They write that one sentence in a notes app on their phone.

The First Scrappy Build
They open VS Code. They've watched enough to know that's where you write the code. They use create-react-app because that's what the tutorial used even though if they knew more they'd use something else. It doesn't matter.

They build the dumbest possible version of the idea first. A hardcoded JavaScript array with five fake items. A text input that filters the list as you type. They show it to nobody. They open it in their browser and type a part number and the list filters and they feel something disproportionate to what they've actually built.

They add more fields. They add a simple form to add new items. They break it three times. They fix it. They google "how to delete item from array React without mutating state" at 11pm on a Tuesday and find the answer and it works and they close the laptop and go to sleep feeling like they did something real.

The Slow Realization
A few weeks in, they start to see the shape of what this actually is. It's not a spreadsheet replacement. It's a location lookup tool. The key insight is that the problem isn't tracking how many of something you have — the POS system already does that. The problem is purely spatial. Where is it? Which bin? Which shelf? What do you do when one bin overflows into a second location?

They realize the data model matters more than the interface. They spend a Saturday not writing code but writing on a notepad: what fields does a part actually need? They look at actual part numbers. They notice the cross-reference problem — the same physical part has multiple numbers depending on who made the aftermarket version.

This is the moment where the real-world experience from working the job becomes worth more than any tutorial. They know things about the domain that no YouTube video could teach them. They build those things into the structure.

The Part Nobody Talks About
Progress gets slower. The first features come fast because they're simple. The later features are hard because they require understanding things that have dependencies on other things.

They start a feature, get stuck, open three browser tabs, read documentation that assumes they already know things they don't know yet, close the laptop, come back two days later, re-read what they wrote, and somehow it makes more sense. This cycle repeats more times than they expected.

They learn that most of learning to build something is just refusing to quit during the part where you don't understand what you don't understand.

They google things they feel embarrassed googling. "What is an API." "Difference between props and state." "Why is my map function not rendering." They find out those are the same questions everyone asks. They stop feeling embarrassed.

The Leaving
Then life moves, the way life does. Another opportunity. A better offer. A conflict. A change of plans. Something shifts, and they're no longer at the place that had the problem.

The app is sitting in a folder on their computer. It has no logo. No company name in the code. No database — just data that lives in memory and disappears when the browser tab closes. It's maybe sixty percent of what they originally imagined.

For a few weeks they don't touch it.

Then one day they open the folder again. They run it. It still works. They look at the code and understand it — actually understand it — in a way they didn't when they wrote some of it. They see exactly what's missing and they know how to add it now.

They open a new browser tab and search: "how many powersports dealerships in the US."

The story is just starting.

That's how it actually happens. Not a eureka moment. Not a business plan on a whiteboard. Just a person noticing something broken, refusing to accept that it has to stay broken, and learning just enough to do something about it.

The first thing I got wrong was the capacity.
SAP has capacity numbers in the export. Right there in the column, looks official. So the first version of this just used those. Why wouldn't you, its a big expensive system someone had to put those numbers in for a reason.

They were wrong. Not a little wrong. A bin SAP said held 43 pallets physically holds 37 depending on where it is. Side bins SAP didnt even bother to distinguish, just gave them the same number as everything else.

So the plan would move material somewhere and the driver would come back and say it doesnt fit. You check the math, the math is right according to SAP. The math was based on bad numbers.

First thing i tried was to read the capacity column out of the export dynamically. Thought maybe if i just pulled it fresh every time it would be accurate. Wrote something like:

const cap = row['Total capacity'] ?? row['Remaining capacity'] + row['Available stock'];

That didnt work because SAP reports generic configuration values not physical reality. The number was always wrong in the same way no matter how i read it. Wasnt a parsing problem. The source data was just wrong.

Tried applying a correction factor next. Like multiply by 0.85 across the board to get closer to real. That was also wrong, different bins needed different corrections, there was no consistent ratio.

The fix wasnt code at all. Went out on the floor and counted. Row by row, bin by bin. That's where the capacity roster came from — not configuration, not a formula. Just walking the warehouse writing down real numbers.

After that the rule became: ignore whatever SAP says the capacity is, completely. One source of truth and its the file.
Bins are paint on the floor. That took a while to code correctly.
A bin is a painted rectangle on the concrete. No walls. No rack. No barrier between one bin and the next — just lines. A pallet goes in 4-foot end first from the aisle, you drive straight back, set it down, reverse out. Next pallet goes in front of the first one. You're building a row of pallets inside a painted zone and the depth of the zone is how many fit going back to the wall.

Standard pallets are 8 by 4. You insert them on the 8-foot side so they sit 4 feet deep into the bin. The first two spaces hold 5. Two stacks of 2, with a 5th  laid across both the other direction securing the 1st stack. Then pallets are stack 3 high. The last two spaces of the bin are secured with an additional 5-stack. So a bin is however many 4-foot columns fit across its depth, between both 5-stacks, 3 high. That's where 43 comes from in A row. Its not arbitrary. Its floor math.

The code that calculates available space didnt know any of this at first. It was just:

const free = totalCapacity - usedQty;

Which is technically right but the capacity number being wrong made the free number wrong. And there was no concept of orientation or stack height — those are physical facts that the code had to have hardcoded in because SAP has no idea about them.
Side bins aren't smaller. They're rotated. That's the whole difference.
The warehouse has structural columns holding the building up, set periodically through the space. Where a column sits at the corner of a bin position the pallet cant go in short-side first because the column is in the way on the side.

So it goes in long-side first. The 8-foot dimension now runs parallel to the aisle instead of going back into the bin. That one change does two things.

First — the pallet is now 4 feet wide in the bin. Takes up the full width of the position. Nothing fits beside it. One single run of pallets going straight back to the wall. That's it.

Second — you can only stack 2 high instead of 3 because of the orientation and the column being right there.

So take a 43 pallet A row bin. Rotate everything sideways. One run going back, two high. You end up with 16. Same depth, same floor space, completely different number because of how the pallet sits.

const isSide = /A-Z$/.test(binId);

That mostly worked but broke on HH and II rows because the regex was only checking one character before the number. HH07 would match as H, row H, which is wrong. Took an embarassingly long time to figure out why the HH row capacities were all coming back as standard instead of side.

The hardest part of this was determining how the  BS and HM bins changed the logic of three rows: B, H and HH. All of the side bins were started with 07. B row stars it;s side bin with BS6. So, the HM row injects itself in the same position as it does in H row for HH. And HM 03 is the first side bin for both those rows. The second is H09 and HH09 and then follows the same pattern as B.

Fixed it by just building an explicit set of every side bin ID instead of trying to detect them with a pattern. Less clever, actually works.

SIDE_BINS is a hard exclusion from targets only. Material already in a side bin can still be sourced. First version excluded them from everything and material would get stuck with no way to plan it out. Had to go back and split the rule once that problem showed up.
The tunnel logic was the hardest thing to accept couldn't be solved.
B and C rows face each other back to back. Same for D and E, F and G, H and HH, I and II. Paired rows sharing a tunnel, aisle access on both ends, first units from each side meeting somewhere in the middle of the space.

First attempt at tunnel logic: track each pair as a shared object, split the total depth between the two sides based on how full each one was.

const tunnelPairs = { B: 'C', C: 'B', D: 'E', E: 'D' };
const partnerFill = binState[tunnelPairs[row]]?.totalQty || 0;
const adjustedCap = baseCap - partnerFill;

That produced negative capacities. If both sides had a lot of material the math would decide there was less than zero space available. Clamped it to zero with Math.max but then plans would just skip those bins entirely even when they had real space.

Second attempt: only apply the tunnel adjustment when one side was below a certain fill threshold. Otherwise use the base capacity.

const adjustedCap = partnerFill < TUNNEL_EMPTY_THRESHOLD
  ? baseCap - partnerFill
  : baseCap;

That was better but the threshold was a guess. Tried 5, tried 10, tried 3. Every value was wrong in different situations. The core problem wasnt the threshold, it was that the boundary between the two sides isnt fixed.

When both sides of a tunnel are completely empty, the first pallet that goes in sets where the boundary actually is. Not by a few inches. By several feet. There's nothing on the floor marking the midpoint — just paint on the outer edges of each bin. Wherever the driver sets the first pallet is where the effective split becomes.

Tried to work out a consistent landing position by actually pushing a bin around in an empty tunnel to see where it naturally stopped. That experiment lasted about twenty minutes. I wouldn’t dare attempt to push 16 units all  stack together and push that entire bin to the threshold. That is someone else hurt.

Deleted the whole tunnel logic branch. The fixed numbers stay. The fixed numbers are the agreed midpoint — what you get when the first unit lands roughly where it's supposed to. Some plans will be slightly off because of a weird tunnel boundary. That's better than trying to track something that cant reliably be tracked.

S bins are not staged lanes. Got that completely wrong.
First assumption was that bin IDs ending in S were some kind of shipping or staging designation. A different physical place. So they got filtered out early and the plans looked cleaner without them. Seemed fine.

Wrong.

S bins are the same physical bin. SAP creates them automatically when material gets put on hold, and is placed into a bin. I speculate that the location in tagged with an ‘S’  F08 has material, some of it gets flagged held, SAP immediately makes F08S as a shadow entry for the held portion. Same floor paint. Two entries in the export.

First thing I tried when i figured this out was to merge the S entry back into the parent before building the bin state:

if (binId.endsWith('S')) {
  const parent = binId.slice(0, -1);
  heldByBin[parent] = (heldByBin[parent] || 0) + qty;
  continue;
}

That part worked fine. The held qty was being tracked. But then i tried to subtract it from liveFree during planning:

const free = cap - (binState[bin]?.totalQty || 0) - (heldByBin[bin] || 0);

And the problem was that heldByBin wasnt getting passed into the consolidate function correctly. It was being built in parseSapExport but not threaded through to where liveFree was defined. So the subtraction was always subtracting zero. Plans that ran showed no difference from before.

Traced it for a while before realizing the variable just wasnt in scope where i needed it. The fix was straightforward once i found it but it took longer than it should have because i kept looking at the math instead of the variable passing.

The bigger problem is what happens when you move material from a bin that has an S version. Wherever it lands, if any of that bins material goes on hold, SAP creates a new S there too.  Unless the bin it gets moved to is empty, then I think it becomes blocked for putaway. The issue travels with the material. And you must change the order request so the TO gets ran through the calling “Move Held Material” through special functions.

Current approach: if an S bin appears in the export for a parent, both get quarantined. Nothing in, nothing out.

The capacity accounting still isnt fully right. The held qty in the S bin reduces the physical space in the parent bin but moving into the parent is still possible if the parent itself isnt quarantined for another reason. Dont have a clean fix for this yet. The quarantine handles the most obvious cases but the shadow doesnt disappear just because we're not looking at it.
Bin IDs in SAP are not consistent and that broke everything quietly.
Same bin would come in from the export as 'a05', 'A05 ', 'A05'. Code treated them as three different bins. Bin would show as empty and full simultaneously depending on which version of the ID you asked about. Nothing crashed. Plans were just wrong in ways that were hard to see.

First attempt at fixing it was just .toUpperCase() at the point where the ID was read:

const binId = row['Storage Bin'].toUpperCase();

That helped but didnt catch the whitespace. Still had 'A05 ' and 'A05' as different bins. Added .trim():

const binId = row['Storage Bin'].trim().toUpperCase();

Better. But then null values started crashing it when a row had no bin ID at all. Added a fallback:

const binId = (row['Storage Bin'] ?? '').trim().toUpperCase();

That's basically what normBin became. A function that does all of that in one place so you dont have to remember to do it everywhere.

The HH and II row parsing was a separate problem. Two character row prefixes. First version of parseBin just took the first character:

const rowKey = binId[0];
const num = binId.slice(1);

HH02 became row H, bin H02. Completely different bin. Found it by looking at a plan where H and HH rows were completely scrambled and tracing backwards. Fix was checking for double-character prefixes first before falling back to the single character.

That one took an embarrassing amount of time to find for how simple the fix was. One extra condition at the top of the function.
'Empty' in the export doesn't always mean empty on the floor.
SAP has an empty indicator column that doesnt always agree with the stock quantity. A bin can have material listed but be flagged empty. Or have stock and not be flagged. Both had to be handled.

The first version just checked the quantity:

const isEmpty = qty === 0;

That missed bins flagged empty by SAP even though they showed stock. And the empty bins needed to go into a separate pool for Pass 2 targeting — the first version just mixed them all together into one binState object and then Pass 2 had no reliable way to find clean empty targets.

Split them into two separate structures. stockRows for anything with material. emptyBinsFromExport for anything with zero qty or the empty flag. Pass 2 only pulls from emptyBinsFromExport.

The bug that came from a scoping problem: a bin could have its stock filtered out of scope — wrong warehouse, disabled section — and still appear in the empty bin set from the same export. Engine would see it as empty and plan a move into it. That bin had material from a different scope sitting in it physically.

That's the C08 situation. Existed in both lists with contradictory state. Cross-referencing the raw unfiltered stock rows before accepting an empty target is the fix — still on the list to add properly.
The chain collapse took three tries.
Early plans would have: move material A15 to B06, then later in the same plan move that same material from B06 to F02. Forklift making an unnecessary stop. Two moves for one job.

First fix was post-processing. After all moves were planned, loop back through and look for chains:

for (let i = 0; i < moves.length; i++) {
  for (let j = i+1; j < moves.length; j++) {
    if (moves[j].from === moves[i].to && moves[j].materialId === moves[i].materialId) {
      moves[i].to = moves[j].to;
      moves.splice(j, 1);
    }
  }
}

That redirected the move but bin state was already built around the original plan. Every decision made after the original move assumed material was at the intermediate stop. Collapsing it after the fact meant the rest of the plan was based on a location that was now wrong.

Second attempt: after each collapse, rebuild bin state from scratch and re-evaluate. That worked sometimes. Other times it would create new chains while fixing old ones and the loop wouldnt settle. Ran it on a real export and it went into what felt like an infinite loop — had to kill the process.

Third attempt: do it inline, at the moment each move is recorded. Before binState updates, check whether the destination was a prior move's target for the same material. If it is, redirect right there.

for (let i = 0; i < newIdx; i++) {
  const prev = moves[i];
  if (prev?.to === from && prev.materialId === materialId) {
    prev.to = to; // redirect directly
  }
}

That worked. Bin state never gets the chance to drift because the correction happens before the update.

The partial passthrough case — where the prior move was larger than the current one — needed another round after that. First version of the inline fix only handled full passthroughs. Found it by watching a plan where a source bin had more material than the destination had room for and the chain reappeared.
Scoring started as a guess and got fixed by watching bad plans.
First version: same material yes or no, then alphabetical.

pool.sort((a, b) => {
  const aMatch = binState[a]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
  const bMatch = binState[b]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
  return bMatch - aMatch || a.localeCompare(b);
});

Technically valid plans. A row completely packed. F and G row sat empty. Production lines that stage into F and G had nowhere to put new output. That's the real problem — these bins need to be available for production, not inbound shipments. Plastipak ships out. The lines fill the warehouse. If the good bins are already full of consolidated material, new production has no place to go.

Added distance weighting next. Closer bins scored higher. That made things worse in a different way — close bins were often small, engine kept trying to move large quantities into bins that didnt have enough room then splitting across multiple small targets.

ROW_TIER came from thinking about which rows actually matter to keep clear for production output. F and G rows are large, high traffic, near the main aisles — production needs those. H and HH are small rack bins, fine to fill those. The tier multiplier weights how important it is to free a source bin, not just how full the target is.

The same-material weight started at 1,000 and kept getting raised because other factors kept accidentally overriding it. Eventually set it to 1,000,000. Nothing else competes with it. A bin that already has the material wins, period.

The hash tiebreaker exists because without it ties always broke alphabetically and everything clustered in A row again. The hash is deterministic — same export always produces the same plan — but distributes tiebreaks across the warehouse instead of alphabetically.
Phase 3 was added because obvious consolidations kept getting skipped.
Phase 1 catches small quantities. Phase 2 catches medium. But D, E, F, G rows would always have bins with material that didnt qualify for either because the quantity was above the threshold, but consolidating them was obviously worth doing to make room for production.

Phase 3 only fires in those rows, only when existing same-material bins already have enough free space to absorb the full quantity without needing an empty bin.

The eligibility check took a couple tries. First version:

const canPhase3 = nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] >= qty;

That sometimes approved moves that werent possible because nonEmptyFreeByMat was including the source bins own free space in the total. The source bin cant receive itself.

const canPhase3 = (nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] - qty) >= 0;

Subtracting the source quantity first fixed it. Without that the engine counts space the source is freeing as available destination space. Circular.
Tight-empty-pack came from watching fragmentation happen in real time.
Multiple bins with small amounts of the same material scattered around. Pass 1 cant move them because there's no non-empty target with enough room. They just sit there. Production runs fill new bins instead of consolidating into existing ones because the engine doesnt have a path.

Tight-empty-pack: find a clean empty bin, figure out which combination of partial bins fills it near capacity, generate all the moves at once.

First version of source selection was greedy:

sources.sort((a,b) => b.qty - a.qty);
let filled = 0;
const selected = [];
for (const src of sources) {
  if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) {
    selected.push(src);
    filled += src.qty;
  }
}

Works sometimes. Misses combinations where smaller sources pack the bin better than one large one. Switched to a DP approach that tracks how many sources contribute to each fill level.

The floating point problem: small moves would occasionally fail because the remaining quantity would be 0.0000001 over capacity due to rounding errors accumulating across calculations. Moves would get rejected that should have fit.

// before
if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) ...

// after — scale to integers first
const scale = (n) => Math.round(n * 1000);
if (scale(filled) + scale(src.qty) <= scale(targetCap)) ...

Eliminated that whole class of bug. Shouldve done it that way from the start.

The recently-freed bin exclusion from Pass 2 targets came from one bad plan where Pass 1 emptied a bin and Pass 2 immediately selected it as a target. Material was supposed to move out of it and into it in the same plan. Added the exclusion set.
J row always needed its own rules. That one worked pretty quick.
 Material moves through it differently. The full scoring pass was too aggressive there.

J logic: at most two targets, both must already have the same material, combined free space has to cover the full source quantity or the move doesnt happen. No partial J moves. No splitting.

That part came together without too much fighting. Once the physical constraint was clear — J is high activity, dont mess with it aggressively — the code was straightforward. Sometimes understanding the floor first means the code is easy.
HHII followup logic — got the timing wrong the first time.
HH and II are small rack bins. Material scatters across them in small quantities over time. After each Pass 1 move, check if any HH or II bin holds the same material in a quantity that fits the remaining space in the target. If one does, pull it in.

First version fired the followup check before applyMove updated the bin state:

maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // too early
applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining);

The followup would check how much space was left in the target and see the pre-move number — the full remaining capacity before the main move landed. So it would find HH bins and approve followup moves that wouldnt actually fit once the main move was applied.

Plans would come out with more material moving into a bin than the bin could hold. Took a little while to find because the numbers looked right until you added them up.

applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining); // apply first
maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // then check

Swapping those two lines fixed it. The followup now sees the bin state after the main move lands and checks real remaining space.

One followup per target only. Early version had no limit and plans got two or three times larger than needed. I would look at the list and just not do it.

And the problem was that heldByBin wasnt getting passed into the consolidate function correctly. It was being built in parseSapExport but not threaded through to where liveFree was defined. So the subtraction was always subtracting zero. Plans that ran showed no difference from before.

Traced it for a while before realizing the variable just wasnt in scope where i needed it. The fix was straightforward once i found it but it took longer than it should have because i kept looking at the math instead of the variable passing.

The bigger problem is what happens when you move material from a bin that has an S version. Wherever it lands, if any of that bins material goes on hold, SAP creates a new S there too.  Unless the bin it gets moved to is empty, then I think it becomes blocked for putaway. The issue travels with the material. And you must change the order request so the TO gets ran through the calling “Move Held Material” through special functions.

Current approach: if an S bin appears in the export for a parent, both get quarantined. Nothing in, nothing out.

The capacity accounting still isnt fully right. The held qty in the S bin reduces the physical space in the parent bin but moving into the parent is still possible if the parent itself isnt quarantined for another reason. Dont have a clean fix for this yet. The quarantine handles the most obvious cases but the shadow doesnt disappear just because we're not looking at it.
Bin IDs in SAP are not consistent and that broke everything quietly.
Same bin would come in from the export as 'a05', 'A05 ', 'A05'. Code treated them as three different bins. Bin would show as empty and full simultaneously depending on which version of the ID you asked about. Nothing crashed. Plans were just wrong in ways that were hard to see.

First attempt at fixing it was just .toUpperCase() at the point where the ID was read:

const binId = row['Storage Bin'].toUpperCase();

That helped but didnt catch the whitespace. Still had 'A05 ' and 'A05' as different bins. Added .trim():

const binId = row['Storage Bin'].trim().toUpperCase();

Better. But then null values started crashing it when a row had no bin ID at all. Added a fallback:

const binId = (row['Storage Bin'] ?? '').trim().toUpperCase();

That's basically what normBin became. A function that does all of that in one place so you dont have to remember to do it everywhere.

The HH and II row parsing was a separate problem. Two character row prefixes. First version of parseBin just took the first character:

const rowKey = binId[0];
const num = binId.slice(1);

HH02 became row H, bin H02. Completely different bin. Found it by looking at a plan where H and HH rows were completely scrambled and tracing backwards. Fix was checking for double-character prefixes first before falling back to the single character.

That one took an embarrassing amount of time to find for how simple the fix was. One extra condition at the top of the function.
'Empty' in the export doesn't always mean empty on the floor.
SAP has an empty indicator column that doesnt always agree with the stock quantity. A bin can have material listed but be flagged empty. Or have stock and not be flagged. Both had to be handled.

The first version just checked the quantity:

const isEmpty = qty === 0;

That missed bins flagged empty by SAP even though they showed stock. And the empty bins needed to go into a separate pool for Pass 2 targeting — the first version just mixed them all together into one binState object and then Pass 2 had no reliable way to find clean empty targets.

Split them into two separate structures. stockRows for anything with material. emptyBinsFromExport for anything with zero qty or the empty flag. Pass 2 only pulls from emptyBinsFromExport.

The bug that came from a scoping problem: a bin could have its stock filtered out of scope — wrong warehouse, disabled section — and still appear in the empty bin set from the same export. Engine would see it as empty and plan a move into it. That bin had material from a different scope sitting in it physically.

That's the C08 situation. Existed in both lists with contradictory state. Cross-referencing the raw unfiltered stock rows before accepting an empty target is the fix — still on the list to add properly.
The chain collapse took three tries.
Early plans would have: move material A15 to B06, then later in the same plan move that same material from B06 to F02. Forklift making an unnecessary stop. Two moves for one job.

First fix was post-processing. After all moves were planned, loop back through and look for chains:

for (let i = 0; i < moves.length; i++) {
  for (let j = i+1; j < moves.length; j++) {
    if (moves[j].from === moves[i].to && moves[j].materialId === moves[i].materialId) {
      moves[i].to = moves[j].to;
      moves.splice(j, 1);
    }
  }
}

That redirected the move but bin state was already built around the original plan. Every decision made after the original move assumed material was at the intermediate stop. Collapsing it after the fact meant the rest of the plan was based on a location that was now wrong.

Second attempt: after each collapse, rebuild bin state from scratch and re-evaluate. That worked sometimes. Other times it would create new chains while fixing old ones and the loop wouldnt settle. Ran it on a real export and it went into what felt like an infinite loop — had to kill the process.

Third attempt: do it inline, at the moment each move is recorded. Before binState updates, check whether the destination was a prior move's target for the same material. If it is, redirect right there.

for (let i = 0; i < newIdx; i++) {
  const prev = moves[i];
  if (prev?.to === from && prev.materialId === materialId) {
    prev.to = to; // redirect directly
  }
}

That worked. Bin state never gets the chance to drift because the correction happens before the update.

The partial passthrough case — where the prior move was larger than the current one — needed another round after that. First version of the inline fix only handled full passthroughs. Found it by watching a plan where a source bin had more material than the destination had room for and the chain reappeared.
Scoring started as a guess and got fixed by watching bad plans.
First version: same material yes or no, then alphabetical.

pool.sort((a, b) => {
  const aMatch = binState[a]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
  const bMatch = binState[b]?.materials?.has(materialId) ? 1 : 0;
  return bMatch - aMatch || a.localeCompare(b);
});

Technically valid plans. A row completely packed. F and G row sat empty. Production lines that stage into F and G had nowhere to put new output. That's the real problem — these bins need to be available for production, not inbound shipments. Plastipak ships out. The lines fill the warehouse. If the good bins are already full of consolidated material, new production has no place to go.

Added distance weighting next. Closer bins scored higher. That made things worse in a different way — close bins were often small, engine kept trying to move large quantities into bins that didnt have enough room then splitting across multiple small targets.

ROW_TIER came from thinking about which rows actually matter to keep clear for production output. F and G rows are large, high traffic, near the main aisles — production needs those. H and HH are small rack bins, fine to fill those. The tier multiplier weights how important it is to free a source bin, not just how full the target is.

The same-material weight started at 1,000 and kept getting raised because other factors kept accidentally overriding it. Eventually set it to 1,000,000. Nothing else competes with it. A bin that already has the material wins, period.

The hash tiebreaker exists because without it ties always broke alphabetically and everything clustered in A row again. The hash is deterministic — same export always produces the same plan — but distributes tiebreaks across the warehouse instead of alphabetically.
Phase 3 was added because obvious consolidations kept getting skipped.
Phase 1 catches small quantities. Phase 2 catches medium. But D, E, F, G rows would always have bins with material that didnt qualify for either because the quantity was above the threshold, but consolidating them was obviously worth doing to make room for production.

Phase 3 only fires in those rows, only when existing same-material bins already have enough free space to absorb the full quantity without needing an empty bin.

The eligibility check took a couple tries. First version:

const canPhase3 = nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] >= qty;

That sometimes approved moves that werent possible because nonEmptyFreeByMat was including the source bins own free space in the total. The source bin cant receive itself.

const canPhase3 = (nonEmptyFreeByMat[matId] - qty) >= 0;

Subtracting the source quantity first fixed it. Without that the engine counts space the source is freeing as available destination space. Circular.
Tight-empty-pack came from watching fragmentation happen in real time.
Multiple bins with small amounts of the same material scattered around. Pass 1 cant move them because there's no non-empty target with enough room. They just sit there. Production runs fill new bins instead of consolidating into existing ones because the engine doesnt have a path.

Tight-empty-pack: find a clean empty bin, figure out which combination of partial bins fills it near capacity, generate all the moves at once.

First version of source selection was greedy:

sources.sort((a,b) => b.qty - a.qty);
let filled = 0;
const selected = [];
for (const src of sources) {
  if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) {
    selected.push(src);
    filled += src.qty;
  }
}

Works sometimes. Misses combinations where smaller sources pack the bin better than one large one. Switched to a DP approach that tracks how many sources contribute to each fill level.

The floating point problem: small moves would occasionally fail because the remaining quantity would be 0.0000001 over capacity due to rounding errors accumulating across calculations. Moves would get rejected that should have fit.

// before
if (filled + src.qty <= targetCap) ...

// after — scale to integers first
const scale = (n) => Math.round(n * 1000);
if (scale(filled) + scale(src.qty) <= scale(targetCap)) ...

Eliminated that whole class of bug. Shouldve done it that way from the start.

The recently-freed bin exclusion from Pass 2 targets came from one bad plan where Pass 1 emptied a bin and Pass 2 immediately selected it as a target. Material was supposed to move out of it and into it in the same plan. Added the exclusion set.
J row always needed its own rules. That one worked pretty quick.
 Material moves through it differently. The full scoring pass was too aggressive there.

J logic: at most two targets, both must already have the same material, combined free space has to cover the full source quantity or the move doesnt happen. No partial J moves. No splitting.

That part came together without too much fighting. Once the physical constraint was clear — J is high activity, dont mess with it aggressively — the code was straightforward. Sometimes understanding the floor first means the code is easy.
HHII followup logic — got the timing wrong the first time.
HH and II are small rack bins. Material scatters across them in small quantities over time. After each Pass 1 move, check if any HH or II bin holds the same material in a quantity that fits the remaining space in the target. If one does, pull it in.

First version fired the followup check before applyMove updated the bin state:

maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // too early
applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining);

The followup would check how much space was left in the target and see the pre-move number — the full remaining capacity before the main move landed. So it would find HH bins and approve followup moves that wouldnt actually fit once the main move was applied.

Plans would come out with more material moving into a bin than the bin could hold. Took a little while to find because the numbers looked right until you added them up.

applyMove(materialId, from, targetBin, remaining); // apply first
maybeApplyHhIiFollowup(materialId, targetBin); // then check

Swapping those two lines fixed it. The followup now sees the bin state after the main move lands and checks real remaining space.

One followup per target only. Early version had no limit and plans got two or three times larger than needed. I would look at the list and just not do it.

i had never coded a warehouse before any of this. never wrote react before this. never used regex before this. most of what ended up in the engine came from something not working and having to go figure out why, which usually meant going and looking at the actual floor first and then coming back to the code.
some things worked the first time. the j row logic. the integer scaling. a few others. most things didn't. the ones that didn't usually failed in a way that told you something about the warehouse you didn't fully understand yet. that's how most of the rules got written — not from knowing the answer but from the floor showing you what the answer had to be.

---

The Research Phase — Learning What to Even Call the Problem

At the beginning, the problem doesn’t look like a system problem. It looks like annoyance.

You’re walking the floor. You need something. You know it exists. SAP says it exists. But knowing something exists and knowing where it actually is are two completely different things.

So you start the way everyone does. You search.

“Inventory tracking software.”  
“Warehouse bin system.”  
“Parts location tool.”

You find enterprise systems. Expensive ones. The kind that assume the problem is already clean and well-defined. You find spreadsheets. You find half-dead tools someone abandoned five years ago.

None of them match what’s actually happening on the floor.

So the search changes.

Not “what tool do I use,” but:

“How do you build something like this.”

That’s where the real shift happens.

You start seeing different words:
- React
- CRUD
- API

You don’t care about any of them. You’re not trying to become a developer. You just need something that answers a simple question:

**Where is it, and will it fit there?**

That’s it.

---

The First Build — Something That Barely Works

The first version isn’t a system. It’s a proof that the idea isn’t impossible.

Hardcoded data.  
A few bins.  
A few materials.  

Type something into a search box and it filters a list.

That’s enough.

Because the moment it works—even a little—you realize something important:

This doesn’t need to be perfect to be useful. It just needs to be more honest than what you’re using now.

So you keep going.

You add the ability to insert data.  
You break it.  
You fix it.  
You Google things you don’t understand yet.

You hit walls:
- why something returns undefined
- why state doesn’t update
- why the same code works in one place and not another

You don’t learn it all at once. You pick up pieces. One sentence at a time until something clicks.

---

The Shift — From “Tracking” to “Reality”

At some point, the idea changes.

It stops being about tracking inventory.

Because inventory isn’t the problem. SAP already tracks inventory.

The problem is physical.

**Where does it go?**  
**What happens when it doesn’t fit?**  
**What happens when one bin becomes two without anyone updating anything?**

You stop thinking about software and start thinking about space.

You start looking at the floor differently:
- how pallets actually sit
- how people actually place them
- what rules exist that nobody wrote down

That’s when the real work starts.

---

The Realization — The System Is Lying

You assume the data is right.

Why wouldn’t you? It’s SAP. Someone had to configure it. Those numbers exist for a reason.

So you build around it.

You trust the capacity column.  
You trust the structure.  
You trust the idea that the system reflects reality.

And then the plans start failing.

Not in code. In real life.

A move gets generated.  
A driver goes to execute it.  
It doesn’t fit.

You check the math. The math is correct.

Which means the math is wrong.

---

The Pattern — Failure Teaches the Rules

This becomes the loop:

1. The engine makes a decision  
2. The floor rejects it  
3. You figure out why  

Every failure exposes something the system doesn’t know.

Not a bug in code—a missing piece of reality.

- A bin isn’t actually the size SAP says it is  
- A “different” bin is actually the same physical space  
- Two sides of a row aren’t independent  
- Some constraints don’t exist until a forklift creates them  

You stop trying to make the code smarter.

You start making it **less wrong**.

---

The Rule That Changes Everything

Eventually it becomes obvious:

**The warehouse does not contain enough information to be modeled cleanly.**

Some things:
- aren’t recorded
- aren’t consistent
- aren’t even decided until someone physically moves something

And that means something uncomfortable:

**There are problems the code cannot solve.**

Not because the code isn’t good enough.

Because the information doesn’t exist.

---

That’s when the approach changes.

You stop chasing perfect logic.  
You stop trusting upstream data.  
You stop trying to generalize everything.

You start:
- hardcoding what’s real
- ignoring what’s wrong
- and accepting that some answers will always be approximate

---

And that’s where the actual system starts.

Right after the first thing that turned out to be wrong.

The capacity.

## The System — What This Actually Is

This isn’t a web app.

There’s no server. No database. No API sitting somewhere in the background pretending to be reliable.

It’s a local machine tool.

An Electron shell wrapped around a React app that reads a file, builds a plan, and tells someone on a forklift what to do next.

That’s it.

You drop in an SAP export.  
It parses it.  
It builds a model of the warehouse.  
It generates a plan.  

Then it gets out of the way and lets the floor decide if it was right.

Everything lives in memory. If you close it, it’s gone—except for a few saved settings.

Which is intentional.

Because the source of truth isn’t the app.

It’s the floor.

---

## The Shape of the Codebase

At a glance, it looks like a normal React project.

It isn’t.

Most React apps are UI-first with logic glued on. This is the opposite.

The UI is just a control panel for a set of domain rules.

The actual system lives in a handful of files:

- **bin.js** — how bins are interpreted  
- **sap.js** — how SAP lies get turned into usable data  
- **planning.js** — the engine  
- **capacity.js** — the rules SAP got wrong  
- **proximity.js** — secondary optimization  
- **analytics.js** — what changed after the plan  

Everything else is just:
- showing it
- clicking it
- exporting it

- every tab gets rendered

This file *is* the backend.

## State — The System in Motion

Every piece of state exists because something went wrong without it.

- `rawSapJson` — so you can rebuild without re-uploading  
- `stockRows` — filtered reality, not raw input  
- `emptyBinsFromExport` — because “empty” lies  
- `moves` — the plan  
- `freedBins` — what you actually gained  
- `completed` — what the floor has confirmed  
- `ignoredMoves` — what reality rejected  

None of these are theoretical.

## The Only Function That Matters

Everything runs through one function:

### `buildPlanFromRaw()`

This is the pipeline.

Not conceptually. Literally.

```
Excel → JSON → filtered reality → plan → analytics → UI
```

Step by step:

1. **Validate the file**
   If SAP changes column names, everything breaks here first.

2. **Parse the export**
   Separate:
   - bins with material
   - bins that claim to be empty

3. **Build the plan**
   Call into `planning.js` — this is where decisions happen.

4. **Figure out what actually gets freed**
   Not what moved—what changed.

5. **Calculate stats**
   Because “we moved stuff” isn’t useful. “we freed 18 bins” is.

6. **Optionally run proximity**
   Secondary pass. Not required. Sometimes useful.

Then it pushes everything into state.

React re-renders.

The UI updates.

That’s the loop.

## The Engine — Where Decisions Get Made

`planning.js` is the only part that thinks.

Everything else just feeds it or displays what it returns.

The process is simple on paper:

1. Build current bin state  
2. Pick a source bin  
3. Find the best target  
4. Apply rules  
5. Record the move  

Repeat until no more valid moves exist.

But the “apply rules” part is where all the real complexity lives.

That’s where:
- capacity lies get overridden
- side bins get excluded
- production rows get protected
- thresholds get enforced
- ignored moves get skipped

It’s not optimizing in a clean mathematical sense.

It’s navigating constraints that weren’t designed to be consistent.

## The Second Pass — Fixing What the First Missed

After consolidation, there’s an optional pass:

`proximity.js`

This one exists because consolidation alone doesn’t solve everything.

You can end up with:
- material technically consolidated
- but still scattered across the warehouse in inconvenient ways

So proximity asks a different question:

> “Can we pull this material closer to where it’s actually used?”

It:
- finds the most scattered materials
- scores aisles
- generates moves toward better locations

It’s not required.

But when it works, it cleans up what the main engine can’t justify on its own.

## Capacity — Where Reality Overrides the System

SAP has numbers.

They look official.

They are wrong.

`capacity.js`

This is where:
- real pallet counts live
- overrides happen
- edge cases get hardcoded

You can tweak it in settings.

But the default behavior is based on what actually fits, not what someone typed into SAP.

## Parsing SAP — Turning Noise Into Something Usable

`sap.js` does three things:

1. **Checks headers**
   If the format changes, you catch it immediately.

2. **Splits reality**
   - stock rows (actual material)
   - empty bins (claimed emptiness)

3. **Builds bin state**
   Aggregates everything into:

```
bin → {
  totalQty,
  materials,
  per-material quantities
}
```

That structure is what the engine operates on.

Everything before this is just cleanup.

- casing issues
- whitespace
- invalid values
- multi-character rows (HH, II)
- warehouse grouping

Without this layer, the same bin becomes three different bins.

Nothing crashes.

Everything just becomes wrong.

It has `collapsed`, `allDone`, `progress`, and it maps over `cluster.moves`. That means one section can represent a chunk of work that moves through the app as a unit instead of as a thousand disconnected little table entries.

Then there is the state list, which is long because the app is long in the places that matter.

`xlsxReady` tells the app whether SheetJS has loaded yet.  
`xlsxRef` keeps the SheetJS object without forcing rerenders.  
`supportOpen` decides whether the support panel is visible.  
`rawSapJson` is the raw uploaded spreadsheet in memory.  
`stockRows` is the cleaned version after filtering the warehouse and disabled bins.  
`emptyBinsFromExport` is the set of bins SAP claims are empty.  
`emptyBinTypes` maps empty bins to storage types.  
`moves` is the consolidation plan.  
`freedBins` is what ends up fully cleared.  
`completed` is the set of move IDs the user has marked done.  
`ignoredMoves` stores skipped moves, reasons, timestamps.  
`ignoreModalMove` is the move currently being skipped.  
`ignoreReason` is the text box value for that reason.  
`analytics` holds the stats.  
`showBeforeAfter` opens the comparison modal.  
`initialBinState` and `finalBinState` are the snapshots before and after.  
`loadError` is the red failure message.  
`openTabs` is the list of tabs that are currently alive.  
`activeTabId` is the tab that is actually visible.  
`settingsOpen` decides whether the drawer is out.  
`uploadFeedback` is the little success or failure message after a file load.  
`orbAnimation` drives the animated flying orb between menu and tab.  
`newTabId` triggers the entrance animation on a newly opened tab.  
`searchTerm` is the move list search box text.  
`hideCompleted` hides completed moves from the list.  
`pageSize` controls how many moves are shown per page.  
`page` is the current page number.  
`warehouse` is the warehouse filter, usually `WH1`, `WH2`, `WH3`, or `ALL`.  
`excludeRbins` handles automatic disabling of R-row bins.  
`globalThreshold` is the minimum PAL count for a source bin.  
`thresholdMode` says whether that threshold is absolute or percentage-based.  
`allowSrc110`, `allowTgt110`, `allowTgt111` control storage type permissions.  
`excludeHISource` blocks HI-prefixed bins from being used as sources.  
`excludeCustomBins` toggles the hardcoded cross-aisle exclusions.  
`lineBins` stores one bin per production line.  
`finderQuery`, `finderQty`, `finderLine`, `finderResult` belong to the PutAway drop finder.  
`capOverrides` holds custom user-set capacities.  
`disabledBins` stores manually or automatically disabled bins.  
`proximityMoves` holds the second-pass moves.  
`proximityAutoRun` controls whether that second pass runs automatically.  
`selectedMoveId` tracks the active move the user has focused.

That list looks excessive because it is. It has to be. Every one of those states exists because at some point not having it caused the app to lie to the user.

The main function that matters in `App.jsx` is `buildPlanFromRaw(jsonRows)`. That is the point where the file becomes a plan. It validates headers, filters stock rows, calls the consolidation engine, determines freed bins, computes analytics, optionally runs proximity, and then updates everything so React can redraw the world. If the plan is wrong, this is where the chain of custody starts. Either the file was bad, the filters were wrong, or the rules in the domain layer were wrong. `buildPlanFromRaw` itself is just the pipeline throat. It does not invent the warehouse. It just forces the data through the pipe.

`loadFile(e)` handles the upload event. SheetJS reads the file as bytes, converts it to JSON, saves the raw result to localStorage as a backup, and then passes it into the rebuild flow. If the file is password protected, malformed, missing columns, or just ugly enough to trigger a parser tantrum, this is where the error surfaces.

`spawnTab(type, menuItemId)` and `closeTab(tabId)` manage the little tab system. `spawnTab` checks whether a tab of that type already exists, creates one if it does not, and kicks the orb animation from the launcher item to the new tab button. `closeTab` removes the tab and falls back to whatever should be visible next. It sounds like window dressing, but without tab discipline the whole UI becomes a junk drawer.

`confirmMove(id)`, `undoMove(id)`, and `selectMove(id)` are about execution tracking. A move is not done just because the app said so. The user has to mark it. The app stores that truth in `completed`. `selectMove` chooses the current row. `confirmMove` marks it done. `undoMove` takes it back out. This matters because the plan is not the execution. The floor is.

`handleIgnoreMove(e)` is what happens when the user says, no, that move is garbage. It stores the move key in `ignoredMoves`, with the reason and the timestamp, and then rebuilds the entire plan without that move. That is not cosmetic. That is the system accepting a correction from the person using it.

`exportMoves()`, `exportAnalyticsToExcel()`, and `generatePDFReport()` are the output layer. They build arrays, write workbooks, create blobs, trigger hidden downloads, and clean up after themselves. `generatePDFReport()` lazy-loads `jspdf` and `jspdf-autotable` so the app does not carry that weight until it needs it. That is one of the few places where restraint actually won.

The memoized computed values are there because the app would otherwise keep recomputing the same things and wasting time for no reason.

`memoizedBinState` updates when `stockRows` changes.  
`allMoves` combines regular moves and proximity moves.  
`filteredMoves` applies search, completion, and visibility filters.  
`visibleMoves` slices the current page.  
`moveClusters` groups the visible moves with their progress.  
`totalMoveQty` totals all pallets across all moves.

Those are not extra conveniences. Those are the difference between a live app and a CPU furnace.

`renderPaneContent()` is the switchboard. It looks at the active tab type and decides what to draw. If the tab is `CONSOLIDATOR`, it shows the move execution table. If it is `PUTAWAY`, it shows the drop finder. If it is `STATS`, it shows metrics and charts. If it is `MAP`, it hands rendering off to the warehouse map component. If it is `HELP`, it opens the guide panel. Everything the user sees goes through that one decision point.

The domain files deserve their own explanation because that is where the actual logic lives.

`src/domain/bin.js` is the place where raw bin names get turned into something sane. `normBin(v)` trims, uppercases, and rejects garbage. `isIgnoredBin(v)` checks whether the name ends in `S`, which in this system means a staging or shadow entry, not a physical bin you should treat the same as a real target. `isPhysicalBin(bin)` checks the master layout. `toNum(v)` strips commas, converts strings, and returns `0` when the value is useless. `parseBin(bin)` splits `HH09` into `{rowKey: "HH", num: "09", upper: "HH09"}` instead of pretending every row is one character long. `getWarehouse(bin)` figures out which warehouse the bin belongs to. `inWarehouse(bin, selected)` applies the user’s filter. If the warehouse ever adds a new naming convention, this is where it gets taught to the app.

`src/domain/sap.js` is where the export gets interrogated. `validateSapHeaders(rows)` checks the first rows for the required headers so the app can fail early instead of failing after the plan is already built. `parseSapExport(json)` walks every row, separates stock from empties, and handles the ugly case where a bin appears occupied and empty in the same export. `buildBinState(stockRows)` turns rows into a bin map with quantities, materials, descriptions, and storage types. The required columns are the spine of the file: `Storage Bin`, `Material`, `Material Description`, `Available stock`, `Storage Type`. If SAP changes names, this file is where the damage gets repaired.

`src/domain/planning.js` is the part that makes the app worth using. `consolidate()` builds the current bin state, scores source and target bins, evaluates every rule, and returns the move list and the final state. That file is where thresholds live, where storage type rules live, where source and target scoring live, where ignored moves are respected, where locked bins are skipped, where capacity is enforced. `findBestBin()` is the PutAway helper. `moveKey(materialId, from, to)` creates the unique ID for move tracking. `LINE_PREFERRED_ROWS` is the mapping that keeps production lines from being fed into nonsense bins. If consolidation behavior changes, this is the file.

`src/domain/proximity.js` handles the second pass, where the system tries to clean up scatter by aisle. `DRIVE_AISLES` maps aisles to the rows they serve. `findMostScatteredMaterials(binState, limit)` finds the materials spread across the most different aisles. `scoreAisleCandidate(aisle, materialId, state, empties, capOverrides)` ranks a target aisle. `generateProximityPlan(materialId, targetAisle, ...)` builds the actual moves. It exists because “consolidated” is sometimes still too messy to be useful.

`src/domain/analytics.js` is the reporting brain. `calculateAnalytics({moves, initialBinState, finalBinState, stockRows, freedBins, capOverrides})` returns totals, row breakdowns, material breakdowns, utilization before and after, and freed capacity. Those numbers are not decoration. They are how you prove the plan did something real.

`src/domain/storage.js` is just localStorage with a seatbelt. `loadCapOverrides()` and `saveCapOverrides(data)` persist custom capacities. `loadDisabledBins()` and `saveDisabledBins(data)` persist the disabled bin set. Both use JSON parse/stringify with try/catch because browser storage is not something you trust blindly.

`src/domain/capacity.js` is where `baseCapacity(storageType, bin)` decides how much a bin can hold based on its type and its physical identity. That file exists because the warehouse is not uniform, and SAP’s generic answer is too blunt to use as-is. The Settings drawer can override those values too, because sometimes the system needs to admit the floor knows better.

The configuration files are just the frame around the whole thing. `vite.config.js` exists so the app bundles properly and loads assets correctly in Electron mode. `tailwind.config.js` defines the industrial color palette — `ppBlue`, `steel`, `forge`, `danger`, and the hazard stripe animation. `package.json` is the dependency spine: `react`, `react-dom`, `xlsx`, `jspdf`, `jspdf-autotable`, `lucide-react`, `tailwindcss`, `electron`, `vite`. That is the whole stack. No mystery. Just the tools needed to make the thing behave.

The data flow from end to end is ugly in the best possible way.

User uploads Excel file.  
`loadFile()` reads bytes with SheetJS.  
JSON rows get handed to `buildPlanFromRaw()`.  
`validateSapHeaders()` checks the columns.  
`parseSapExport()` splits occupied and empty state.  
`consolidate()` generates the move plan.  
`calculateAnalytics()` computes the stats.  
Optional proximity pass runs if it is turned on.  
State updates.  
React re-renders the tabs.  
The user works through the move list.  
Moves get selected.  
Moves get confirmed.  
Ignored moves trigger a rebuild.  
Exports get generated when needed.

That is the loop.

It keeps going because it has to.

The common edit scenarios are the map for future maintenance: new warehouse means `bin.js` and `layout.js`. Different SAP columns means `sap.js`. Different prioritization means `planning.js`. Capacity change means `capacity.js`. New tab means `App.jsx`. Color scheme means `tailwind.config.js`. More production lines means `planning.js`. Different excluded bins means `App.jsx`. Different PDF layout means `App.jsx` again because the report still lives there.

And underneath all of it is the real point, which never changes no matter how much code gets written around it:

This app is not trying to outsmart the warehouse.

It is trying to make the warehouse legible.

SAP says one thing.  
The code thinks another.  
The floor decides the rest.

Most of the software exists because those three did not agree, and the only honest way forward was to stop pretending they did.
