# The Chronicles of Iron and Glass — Novella One

# Chapter 1 — Underface Forge

The knife was balanced.

Jhace had set it on the anvil face with the edge down and the spine up, a stupid trick if you didn't know what you were doing and a useful one if you did. A true edge would sit without rocking. A crooked edge would hunt for its own angle and tell on itself.

His hands were steady.

He dragged his thumbnail along the bevel in one slow pass, feeling for a snag he could not see. The steel was still warm enough to carry a faint give under the nail. Good. Not soft—just not finished.

He turned the blade and repeated it on the other side. Same resistance. Same line. The tang sat square in the handle block, and the rivet holes lined up without force.

A bad fit now is a broken hand later.

He'd learned it from his father over a vice, the words delivered like a measurement and nothing more. Back then the old man's fingers had still closed true. Later they hadn't.

He didn't say it out loud. You didn't waste breath on sayings when the work already proved them. He just set the knife aside and reached for the next blank, already moving through the steps his body knew without needing permission.

Then the bell rang.

The bell in the underface ran a fast warning—street failure, crowd hazard, bodies in motion. Not the slow pull for shift change. Not the long call for a fire seam. This meant something had already started coming apart and wanted company.

Jhace's head came up before the sound finished making sense. He classified it the way he classified a crack: where it started, what it would take with it, and who would be under it when it let go.

Hazard. Crowd. Fast.

He set the hot blank back into the cradle instead of tossing it—no loose metal on a floor that might tilt. He slapped the quench lid down, then shoved the nearest tongs into the rack with their jaws closed. The shop didn't get to become another failure.

"Out," he snapped at the apprentices by the bellows. Not a lecture. A directive. "Hands empty. Go."

One of them hesitated, eyes on the knife. Jhace caught the look and shook his head once. If it isn't held, it falls on someone smaller. The rule wasn't about metal. It was about everything.

The boy moved.

Jhace took the knife anyway—wrapped it in a scrap of leather and pushed it into his apron pocket. Not because it mattered now. Because leaving it would make someone reach for it later, and reaching for things in a surge was how fingers got lost.

He ran for the exit passage with his shoulders turned to slip through bodies, not collide with them. The underface swallowed sound into stone and damp heat. Smoke and coppergilt dust clung to his throat.

He should have gone up.

Up meant air, sight, wider lanes. Down meant tunnels and choke points. But his feet had already chosen. The route was in his legs from a hundred shift runs and back-hauls, and trusting it was faster than verifying it.

Left at the seam brace. Down the iron ladder. Through the split arch where the mortar always shed grit.

The crowd found him halfway out, a press of shoulders and elbows moving because the bell told them to move. Someone screamed a name. Someone else screamed back. Jhace kept his eyes low and his hands up, palms open, not grabbing unless he had to.

The city shifted.

The first shove was familiar: a low roll through stone that turned hanging tools into clattering hazards and made people widen their stance like the world owed them balance. Jhace braced and kept moving, already running the usual list in his head—arches first, loose brick next, glass third.

It was just another quake.

Then the next shove hit wrong.

Not a roll. A pull.

It took the weight out of his stomach like a hook had caught him under the ribs and lifted. Bodies lurched forward, then back. Dust rose in sheets from joints that had held through generations and suddenly didn't feel like holding anymore.

He stopped long enough to look up.

When he reached the street, there was nothing to orient to.

Lines he knew—roof edges, tower corners, the familiar cut of the terrace rail—had shifted out of alignment as if someone had taken the city in both hands and twisted. A street lamp leaned at a new angle. A wall that should have been square looked slightly wrong, and the wrongness made his teeth hurt.

A scream became a roar. Not voice. Not wind. Pressure tearing through space.

The exit-face rose like the planet had decided to exhale.

Stone lifted in a slow heave. Dust poured out of seams like breath. The sky overhead split in a line so straight it looked cut, not cracked. The edges didn't crumble. They parted.

Jhace reached for the old classification anyway—quake, settle, aftershock—and tried to lay it over what he saw.

It didn't fit.

The sky opened.

Not in color, not in light, but in structure: a seam overhead widening like something had pried it apart. Behind it was depth that didn't belong. Cold that didn't match the air. A slice of elsewhere pressed against the world like it had always been there and was only now being acknowledged.

The pull hit again, harder, and the crowd became a single mass. Someone went down near his knee—small, swallowed immediately by larger bodies stepping where they could to avoid falling themselves.

Jhace dropped his center of gravity and moved toward the collapse point without thinking about it.

He hooked two fingers into a belt knot and hauled, getting the smaller body out of the crush and into the wall shadow before the next surge could take her again.

Unknown load.

Treat it as maximum.

A beam overhead groaned and shifted. Jhace put his shoulder under the edge, not lifting it—just stopping it from finding momentum. His forearm locked against stone. The beam bit down, and pressure ran through his bones like a file.

His hand started shaking.

A fine vibration at first, easy to blame on shock, on dust, on the pull in the air. It didn't stop when the slack came. It didn't stop when he forced his fingers to tighten.

Not now.

He held anyway.

People spilled past under the beam's shadow. Some ducked. Some didn't notice. He held until the pull passed and the beam settled into a new seat that would hold for the next breath-count or the next failure, whichever came first.

When he stepped out, eyes landed on him—wide, hungry for an explanation, hungry for a reason the beam had listened.

He did not ask for the stares.

He turned away from them and looked back up at the rupture overhead, forcing his eyes to take the whole shape instead of the parts that made his stomach twist.

The seam in the sky stayed open. It didn't heal like a crack. It didn't close like a door. It just remained, a statement no craft could argue with.

And below it, the city began to adapt—badly, quickly, with bodies as the first material spent.

# Chapter 2 — Glass-House Observatory

Tiffani kept the observatory clean the way other people kept altars clean.

Not reverent. Not sentimental. Functional. Dust on a lens was not dust. It was scatter. It was error. It was someone else's mouth later, saying her numbers failed because she was careless.

She did not give people that kind of gift.

She opened the glass-house shutters a finger-width and watched the city's upper terraces breathe smoke in slow pulses. Fine grit hung in the air. Coppergilt, already—too early for that, if the grid was behaving.

She set the shutters back, latched them, and went to work.

The bench held two instruments and one rule.

Two instruments, because one instrument could lie and still look clean. One rule, because clean numbers were only safe if they were repeatable.

She checked the bubble on theodolite one. Centered. She checked the bubble on the spare. Centered. She ran the pendulum once, timed the swing against the chronometer, then ran it again with her fingers off the base.

*Baseline confirmed. Drift within tolerance. Ambient hum: steady.*

She wrote it without decoration. Just enough to make it real.

Another tick entered the glass.

Not a sound. Not a bell. A pressure in the panes, a sympathetic vibration you felt in your teeth when you held them still.

Tiffani paused with the cloth in her hand.

Secondary frequency, low amplitude. Unmodeled.

Her first thought was not *impossible*. Her first thought was *instrument resonance*.

She put the cloth down and checked the mount bolts on the theodolite base. Tight. She checked the bench anchors. Tight. She checked the shutter latch, in case the wind was making a lie.

Everything held.

The vibration doubled, then split—one line steady, the other wandering as if it could not decide what to couple to.

She put her eye to the lens.

The reference marker on the far ridge slid sideways in her view, smooth as grease. Not a jump. Not a tremor. A continuous rotation.

Base shift, she thought. She pulled back, checked the bubble. Centered. She checked it again anyway.

She put her eye back to the lens.

The marker kept sliding.

Not the instrument.

Not her.

The world.

She opened the chronometer and counted the drift in ticks because her mouth wanted to do something else—say a word that belonged to panic.

*Orientation drift observed. Reference point stable. World-frame shift suspected. Confidence: medium.*

She added the confidence tag because she could hear the future already: *You guessed. You assumed.* She would not be trapped by her own handwriting.

A pressure drop punched the room so hard the shutters flexed. The air thinned. Her ears popped.

Outside, smoke reversed direction.

It went back into chimneys.

It went back into mouths.

Tiffani's hand went for the slate automatically.

Vent backflow, she labeled it, because the label gave her an action: log ticks, mark direction, note onset.

*Pressure event onset. Smoke reversal. Tick-count: one—two—three—*

The glass-house seam hissed. The pendulum's swing went wide.

Her label did not fit. The air did not behave like air.

She crossed out *vent* and wrote *unknown pressure inversion* without pausing her count.

*—four—five—six—*

The emergency bells began.

One bell, then another, then a third, overlapping the first. Not a drill cadence. Not a fire cadence. The wrong pattern, the pattern you used when you did not know what you were ringing for.

Tiffani held still long enough to listen to the overlap.

Her throat tightened. She swallowed it back down.

Bells were not data. Bells were people trying to move bodies.

Bodies moved badly when they moved blind.

She wrote a single line at the top of the page, larger than the rest.

*OBSERVATION ≠ PERMISSION.*

Then, underneath it, smaller:

*Record only. Do not assign cause. Do not prescribe response.*

She had written something like that once, in a different collapse. Procedural. Correct. The inquiry called it *negligent delay* anyway and used her notes to stack liability on her name until it hurt to hear it spoken.

She did not forget that.

She would not give them clean language again.

The drift continued.

Tiffani returned to theodolite one and rechecked the bubble a third time. Still centered. She moved to the spare instrument and took the same read.

The marker slid in both.

The ridge did not move.

The frame did.

She logged three readings, spaced by breath-count, and tagged the confidence again.

*World-axis drift persists. Confidence: high.*

Outside, over the terraces, something rose.

At first she thought it was a dust wall, a pressure front, the kind of rolling face you got when a structure failed at distance and pushed its own air ahead of it.

Then the face sharpened.

It was not dust. It was geometry.

A plane lifting through the skyline like the city had been cut and a new surface was being revealed behind it—smooth enough to look false, bright enough to hurt the eye.

Exit-face, her mind supplied, because her mind needed a noun.

She wrote it the way she wrote everything else.

*Exit-face emergence observed. Angle of rise:—*

She did not finish the angle because the sky tore.

A section of blue stopped existing. Not dark. Not cloud. Absence. A void with hard edges that did not bleed into the air around it.

Her reflex tried to save her: atmospheric refraction, heat shimmer, an optical artifact created by the exit-face's glare.

She put her eye to the lens.

Stars.

Hard points of light in full daylight, fixed as nails.

Her breath caught—one sharp inhale she did not intend to take.

She did not let it become a sound.

She forced her hand steady on the adjustment knob and logged coordinates anyway.

*Rift aperture present. Stellar visibility through aperture. Confidence: high.*

She took another read. Then a third. Spaced. Tagged.

*Parallax absent across three reads. Confidence: medium.*

Medium, because her hands were shaking now, and she would not pretend they weren't.

A shape crossed the Rift.

Not a falling thing. Not a bird. Too straight, too long, too sure.

A spindle—glass and iron and something in between—moving with no regard for air. Colors bled through its fractures the way heat bleeds through a kiln seam: green, purple, pink, blue, orange—irrational in their placement, bright enough to look like a warning.

Behind it, the sky smeared.

Debris, she thought. Vapor. A trail.

Then the smear kept moving after the object was gone, like the world had been pushed out of alignment and was still trying to settle back into itself.

She wrote until the graphite cut grooves in the slate.

*Object transit observed. Shape: spindle. Surface: mixed shard. Fracture glow present. Transit speed: unmeasurable by eye. Debris smear persists post-transit.*

Below, the terraces answered with human noise. Cries. Running feet. Stone collapsing. Orders shouted and not obeyed because the bell cadence was wrong and the air was wrong and the sky had become a hole.

Tiffani stayed at the lens.

If she sounded the bell herself, she would be choosing a response. If she chose a response and it failed, it would become her fault in a way no measurement ever could.

That was the trap. She could feel it closing.

She wrote *DOCUMENT EVERYTHING* across the page anyway, because if she did not, the next part of this would belong to whoever survived long enough to lie about it.

Then she closed the chronometer.

She picked up the slate.

This time she would bring the measurements to the people who thought they were allowed to make calls.

She turned toward the stairwell.

Not to join the panic.

To put the numbers into the bodies.

# Chapter 3 — Ground-Level Continuation

By the time Tiffani reached the lower terraces, the air had already learned a new way to sit in the lungs.

It went in sharp. It came out metallic.

Smoke lay in pockets between walls as if it had weight. Coppergilt grit clung to damp faces. People ran past her with their hands over their mouths and their eyes wide, and none of them looked up because the sky was the wrong place to look now.

Her slate stayed against her chest until she found a surface that would not move under her.

That was the first protocol: no baseline on a shifting floor.

She stepped onto a block of intact stonework—old, heavy, load-bearing—and set the slate down. She opened the chronometer, then closed it without starting it.

Not yet. Not until she had something to time.

She took three breaths and forced the tremor out of her fingers by pinning her hand flat to the slate edge. Not a prayer. A clamp.

*Zone baseline required. No carry-over.*

She wrote it as a rule, not an observation.

She looked for the simplest measurable in the mess.

Sound. Dust fall. Pendulum drift, if she could hang it.

There was no place to hang it.

So she used bodies.

A runner staggered by, coughing hard enough to fold himself. The cough produced coppergilt taste in the air like a filament snapping.

Tiffani did not ask him if he was hurt. She did not have that kind of comfort in her mouth.

She watched his sputum hit the stone.

Color. Density. Spread.

*Symptom cluster: cough—metallic taste—particulate in sputum. Confidence: medium.*

She tagged confidence because her eyes kept darting to the places where stone had fractured and she needed to admit it to the page.

A brace team yelled for hands.

Two men were under a half-fallen slab at the corner of a collapsed archway, their shoulders jammed against stone that should have already finished falling.

The crowd around them kept saying the same phrase in different voices, trying to make it true.

"Hold. Hold. Hold."

Tiffani's eyes went to the failure pattern before the shouting resolved into meaning.

Bracing artifact, her mind labeled it. Timber wedge out of sight. A hidden prop. A trick of angle.

Label first, because label gives you an action.

She wrote fast.

*Load arrested. Brace present. Failure delayed.*

Then she looked.

No timber.

No wedge.

No brace line. Nothing set against the slab except two bodies and a third man with his hands out, palms under stone as if his skin could negotiate with weight.

His jaw worked like he was swallowing grit. His forearms trembled. Not from exertion alone—something finer, something patterned, as if the tremor had a pattern that did not belong to muscles.

Tiffani's pencil stopped mid-stroke.

She crossed out *brace present* so hard the graphite tore the slate's surface.

She wrote:

*Load paused mid-fall. Mechanism unknown. Constraint observed: stone does not descend while subject contact maintained. Do not assign cause.*

Constraint only. That was the doctrine she could live with. Mechanism claims made targets.

Her eyes flicked to the man again—just long enough to register the wedge bar on the ground beside him, unused, and the steadiness in his stance that did not match the shaking in his hands.

Not relevant to baseline.

She forced her focus back to the slab geometry and counted the fracture lines.

One of the brace team shouted for a name.

"What's your name—"

The man did not answer. Or he did. Tiffani couldn't hear it over the crowd.

She did not look up. She did not want to know.

Naming him would make him a point on a ledger later.

She wrote *subject* and moved on.

A crack ran along the arch's remaining foot like a sentence. She traced it with her eyes, then with the pencil tip hovering a hair above the slate.

*Fracture propagation: lateral. Rate: slow. Ambient hum: elevated.*

She needed a baseline. The sky-frame was gone. The world-frame was drifting. All she had were local behaviors.

So she built a local grid.

*Zone baseline established: lower terrace—south wall cluster. Ambient hum: high. Particulate: high.*

She took three readings spaced by breath-count and tagged confidence.

*Confidence: medium. Retest required if bell pattern changes or if crowd density shifts.*

The bell pattern changed.

A new bell joined, higher and faster, the kind used for registry lines. The kind used to make people stand still in their fear so someone could count them.

Tiffani's throat tightened.

She turned her head without turning her body.

A Crown runner stood at the edge of the crowd with a slate of his own and a stylus poised like a hook.

A Crown recorder was there. He was watching the held slab, not because he cared about the stone, but because he cared about who was under it.

Liability pressure rose in the back of her skull like heat.

Her language tightened with it.

She angled her slate closer to her body and wrote smaller.

*Institutional observer present. Reduce interpretation. Use subject identifiers only.*

She did not write the man's name. She did not write *held*. She wrote *constraint observed* and let it be ugly.

The runner's stylus moved.

Tiffani felt it as a physical thing, like a hook catching cloth.

She did not let her eyes drift back to the man again. The micro-pull was there—attention trying to return to the only thing in the chaos that looked like a rule—but she pinned it down and kept measuring stone.

Observer bias, she thought, not as confession but as warning.

She stepped away from the slab and into the flow of the crowd, keeping the wall to her left, keeping her slate angled down.

*Transition protocol: move to next zone. Re-baseline on arrival. Do not carry assumptions.*

She wrote it, because if she did not, she would forget and trust the last protocol like it was law.

The world did not respect law.

# Chapter 4 — Firebreak

Between bells, the city tried to pretend.

It stacked brace timber. It dragged bodies out of lanes. It patched cracks with wet mortar like the mortar still mattered. It tried to live in the slack.

Jhace worked until the forge went cold.

Not because he believed the work fixed anything. Because keeping tools moving kept hands busy, and busy hands didn't reach for panic. He sharpened edges, set rivets, and repaired hinges from doors that no longer closed. He made everything fit as if fit still meant safety.

Then the next call came.

Bells again—higher and harsher, meant to move bodies out of open lanes and into braced pockets before the next pull found them.

A runner cut across the terrace with a slate held up like a shield.

"Firebreak," the runner shouted. "Parapet lane. Now."

Jhace didn't ask what failed. If a runner was sent, the failure had already started. He only checked his hands.

The tremor was a fine vibration under the skin, easy to pretend was heat or exhaustion. Pretending didn't change what iron did when you asked it for precision.

He flexed once. The vibration stayed.

He went anyway.

The firebreak line was a strip of cleared stone between stacked brace timber and a wall already showing hairline shear. Brace teams moved in short bursts, setting wedges, pulling slack, then resetting before the next pull hit.

Jhace scanned for a tool that could take the argument out of his hands.

He found a wedge bar.

Plain iron. Flattened chisel end. Grip worn smooth by other palms. Not a weapon. Not a badge. A lever for making stubborn things move the way they should.

He wrapped his fingers around it and felt his hand settle.

Anchor. Not comfort.

He tested the weight with a short lift, then hooked it under the nearest brace lip like it belonged there.

# Chapter 5 — The Child Under the Slab

Chloen saw the woman with the slate before she saw the wrongness in the air.

Not a runner. Not a brace hand. Clean coat, sleeves kept back from dust like she expected the stone to obey. She stood on the terrace edge with her body angled away from the lane traffic, eyes on a crack line that Chloen couldn’t see from here.

The woman’s pencil moved without her looking at the page.

“Not an eruption,” she murmured. “Not a fall.”

Graphite ticked. She wrote a short line, then crossed part of it out—too hard. The slate surface dimpled under the stroke. She wrote again tighter, as if smaller letters could make the world behave.

Chloen caught the page for half a blink when the woman shifted her wrist.

BREACH VECTOR: THROUGH.  
ENTRY: unknown.

# The Chronicles of Iron and Glass — Novella One

# Chapter 1 — Underface Forge

The knife was balanced.

Jhace had set it on the anvil face with the edge down and the spine up, a stupid trick if you didn't know what you were doing and a useful one if you did. A true edge would sit without rocking. A crooked edge would hunt for its own angle and tell on itself.

His hands were steady.

He dragged his thumbnail along the bevel in one slow pass, feeling for a snag he could not see. The steel was still warm enough to carry a faint give under the nail. Good. Not soft—just not finished.

He turned the blade and repeated it on the other side. Same resistance. Same line. The tang sat square in the handle block, and the rivet holes lined up without force.

A bad fit now is a broken hand later.

He'd learned it from his father over a vice, the words delivered like a measurement and nothing more. Back then the old man's fingers had still closed true. Later they hadn't.

He didn't say it out loud. You didn't waste breath on sayings when the work already proved them. He just set the knife aside and reached for the next blank, already moving through the steps his body knew without needing permission.

Then the bell rang.

The bell in the underface ran a fast warning—street failure, crowd hazard, bodies in motion. Not the slow pull for shift change. Not the long call for a fire seam. This meant something had already started coming apart and wanted company.

Jhace's head came up before the sound finished making sense. He classified it the way he classified a crack: where it started, what it would take with it, and who would be under it when it let go.

Hazard. Crowd. Fast.

He set the hot blank back into the cradle instead of tossing it—no loose metal on a floor that might tilt. He slapped the quench lid down, then shoved the nearest tongs into the rack with their jaws closed. The shop didn't get to become another failure.

"Out," he snapped at the apprentices by the bellows. Not a lecture. A directive. "Hands empty. Go."

One of them hesitated, eyes on the knife. Jhace caught the look and shook his head once. If it isn't held, it falls on someone smaller. The rule wasn't about metal. It was about everything.

The boy moved.

Jhace took the knife anyway—wrapped it in a scrap of leather and pushed it into his apron pocket. Not because it mattered now. Because leaving it would make someone reach for it later, and reaching for things in a surge was how fingers got lost.

He ran for the exit passage with his shoulders turned to slip through bodies, not collide with them. The underface swallowed sound into stone and damp heat. Smoke and coppergilt dust clung to his throat.

He should have gone up.

Up meant air, sight, wider lanes. Down meant tunnels and choke points. But his feet had already chosen. The route was in his legs from a hundred shift runs and back-hauls, and trusting it was faster than verifying it.

Left at the seam brace. Down the iron ladder. Through the split arch where the mortar always shed grit.

The crowd found him halfway out, a press of shoulders and elbows moving because the bell told them to move. Someone screamed a name. Someone else screamed back. Jhace kept his eyes low and his hands up, palms open, not grabbing unless he had to.

The city shifted.

The first shove was familiar: a low roll through stone that turned hanging tools into clattering hazards and made people widen their stance like the world owed them balance. Jhace braced and kept moving, already running the usual list in his head—arches first, loose brick next, glass third.

It was just another quake.

Then the next shove hit wrong.

Not a roll. A pull.

It took the weight out of his stomach like a hook had caught him under the ribs and lifted. Bodies lurched forward, then back. Dust rose in sheets from joints that had held through generations and suddenly didn't feel like holding anymore.

He stopped long enough to look up.

When he reached the street, there was nothing to orient to.

Lines he knew—roof edges, tower corners, the familiar cut of the terrace rail—had shifted out of alignment as if someone had taken the city in both hands and twisted. A street lamp leaned at a new angle. A wall that should have been square looked slightly wrong, and the wrongness made his teeth hurt.

A scream became a roar. Not voice. Not wind. Pressure tearing through space.

The exit-face rose like the planet had decided to exhale.

Stone lifted in a slow heave. Dust poured out of seams like breath. The sky overhead split in a line so straight it looked cut, not cracked. The edges didn't crumble. They parted.

Jhace reached for the old classification anyway—quake, settle, aftershock—and tried to lay it over what he saw.

It didn't fit.

The sky opened.

Not in color, not in light, but in structure: a seam overhead widening like something had pried it apart. Behind it was depth that didn't belong. Cold that didn't match the air. A slice of elsewhere pressed against the world like it had always been there and was only now being acknowledged.

The pull hit again, harder, and the crowd became a single mass. Someone went down near his knee—small, swallowed immediately by larger bodies stepping where they could to avoid falling themselves.

# Chapter 2 — Glass-House Observatory

Tiffani kept the observatory clean the way other people kept altars clean.

Not reverent. Not sentimental. Functional. Dust on a lens was not dust. It was scatter. It was error. It was someone else's mouth later, saying her numbers failed because she was careless.

She did not give people that kind of gift.

She opened the glass-house shutters a finger-width and watched the city's upper terraces breathe smoke in slow pulses. Fine grit hung in the air. Coppergilt, already—too early for that, if the grid was behaving.

She set the shutters back, latched them, and went to work.

The bench held two instruments and one rule.

Two instruments, because one instrument could lie and still look clean. One rule, because clean numbers were only safe if they were repeatable.

She checked the bubble on theodolite one. Centered. She checked the bubble on the spare. Centered. She ran the pendulum once, timed the swing against the chronometer, then ran it again with her fingers off the base.

*Baseline confirmed. Drift within tolerance. Ambient hum: steady.*

She wrote it without decoration. Just enough to make it real.

Another tick entered the glass.

Not a sound. Not a bell. A pressure in the panes, a sympathetic vibration you felt in your teeth when you held them still.

Tiffani paused with the cloth in her hand.

Secondary frequency, low amplitude. Unmodeled.

Her first thought was not *impossible*. Her first thought was *instrument resonance*.

Everything held.

The vibration doubled, then split—one line steady, the other wandering as if it could not decide what to couple to.

She put her eye to the lens.

The reference marker on the far ridge slid sideways in her view, smooth as grease. Not a jump. Not a tremor. A continuous rotation.

She put her eye back to the lens.

Not the instrument.

Not her.

The world.

*Orientation drift observed. Reference point stable. World-frame shift suspected. Confidence: medium.*

She added the confidence tag because she could hear the future already: *You guessed. You assumed.* She would not be trapped by her own handwriting.

It went back into chimneys.

It went back into mouths.

*Pressure event onset. Smoke reversal. Tick-count: one—two—three—*

The glass-house seam hissed. The pendulum's swing went wide.

Her label did not fit. The air did not behave like air.

*—four—five—six—*

Bodies moved badly when they moved blind.

*OBSERVATION ≠ PERMISSION.*

*Record only. Do not assign cause. Do not prescribe response.*

She had written something like that once, in a different collapse. Procedural. Correct. The inquiry called it *negligent delay* anyway and used her notes to stack liability on her name until it hurt to hear it spoken.

The ridge did not move.

The frame did.

*World-axis drift persists. Confidence: high.*

Anchor. Not comfort.
