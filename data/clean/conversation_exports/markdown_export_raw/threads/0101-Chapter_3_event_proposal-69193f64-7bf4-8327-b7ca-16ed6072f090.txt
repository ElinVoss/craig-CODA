The air slammed back, pressure pounding his eardrums. A distant roar rolled through the stone—rubble, collapsing somewhere above, maybe everywhere. The Underface hum didn’t come back clean; it shuddered into a new key, uneven, like a wounded animal still trying to run.

Far off, something massive gave way with a wet, grinding crack.

Heat surged past, drawn toward the damage. The proof-wall flared bright enough that he had to look away, its inner surface boiling with orange and white, shapes moving behind it as conduits flexed and realigned under forced load.

He realized he was still clutching the column, fingers dug so deep into the ridges his knuckles ached. The torque key lay on the floor at his feet, forgotten.

Brin swore softly. Not the usual Underface curses, not at him, just at the sheer scale of whatever had just happened. His face was the color of old chalk.

“What was that?” he rasped.

Jhace swallowed. His mouth tasted like metal filings and hot dust.

He didn’t have words for it. No training scenario had ever mentioned the sky tearing or the world tipping. This wasn’t a line failure, a boiler blowout, a mis-set valve. It was all of those things at once, scaled up until the numbers stopped meaning anything.

He bent to pick up his torque key and almost fell as his body overcorrected, still arguing with the new orientation of gravity. Even the simple act of reaching felt strange, distances warped by that barely-there tilt. Not enough to see. Enough to *feel*.

The grid’s new note crawled along his burned-in instincts, an off-true vibration that refused to settle.

The world had been square his whole life. Plumb. Heavy in predictable directions. Now some invisible hand had taken hold of Te’Oga and set it upright by force, like a craftsman straightening a crooked beam with a final, brutal twist.

Jhace pressed his palm flat to the column again.

The metal hummed back at him, unfamiliar. Like it was waiting to find out whether this was the way things were going to be from now on, or just the moment before everything finally broke.

### CHAPTER 3 – JHACE (continued)

The world had been square his whole life. Plumb. Heavy in predictable directions. Now some invisible hand had taken hold of Te’Oga and set it upright by force, like a craftsman straightening a crooked beam with a final, brutal twist.

Jhace pressed his palm flat to the column again.

The metal hummed back at him, unfamiliar. Like it was waiting to find out whether this was the way things were going to be from now on, or just the moment before everything finally broke.

A siren wailed somewhere above, strangled at the start, then rising into a full-throat howl. Not the usual grid alarm. Deeper. Broader. *Spire* alarm.

Brin flinched. “That’s surface-side, isn’t it?”

Jhace didn’t answer. He already knew.

The proof-wall’s glow shifted, no longer just heat in neat lanes. It smeared, brightened, shapes moving like a storm behind frosted glass.

“Move,” he said. “Check the junctions. If the Spire took a hit, everything down here’s going to—”

The floor jumped.

Not a roll, not a shudder. A single, vicious jolt that threw both of them sideways. Jhace hit the column again, shoulder first, as a section of ceiling farther down the corridor split with a crack like a gunshot. Stone sheared loose in slabs, smashing into the lane and bursting into grit.

Hot wind blasted through the new opening, carrying the stink of scorched stone and—beneath that—something brittle and sharp: burned glass.

Not grid glass. Not proof-wall. Furnace glass.

The Glass Quarter.

He didn’t remember starting to run. One breath he was in the lane with Brin shouting something behind him; the next he was sprinting toward the cross-cuts that led up toward the Spire’s lower galleries, boots slipping on fresh dust, every support they passed groaning as it took new load.

The tilt hadn’t stopped. It was just slow now, subtle, the world still settling into its new angle. His body hated it. Every step felt like it veered just a fraction off where he’d aimed.

He forced his eyes to the work instead of the wrongness.

Turn. Up. Past a blown-out inspection hatch vomiting heat. Up again, hand to the wall to keep balance as a second jolt rolled through the bones of the Spire. Shrill shouting echoed down from above, mingled with a sound he’d only heard at the furnaces on Kindling days when someone opened a door at the wrong time—a wet, sucking roar as air met heat that shouldn’t have been exposed.

He hit the last landing at a half-stumble and slammed his palm into the crash-bar of a heavy door.

It juddered, stuck.

“Come on,” he hissed, shoving. Hinges squealed protest, then something beyond it gave with a grind. The door lurched open into chaos.

The Glass Quarter workfloor had never been his domain; he’d only seen it twice, both times from the safe stretch of a marked walkway. Now every rule sign might as well have burned.

Half the vaulted ceiling had caved, beams punched down at ugly angles. Sunlight knifed in through a ragged tear where stone used to be, a bright square full of dust and the angry white glow of something still falling far above. One of the big proof-walls on the far side had spidered and collapsed inward, spilling shards across the floor like broken ice.

And in the middle of it all, one of the casting platforms had lost a fight with whatever had fallen. Its steel support had buckled, dumping its weight sideways. A girded beam the size of a tree pinned it—and everything under it—to the floor.

Someone was under it.

For a heartbeat, all he saw were hands. Pale against dark steel, fingers curled under a crossbar as if trying to bench-press the world.

Then his eyes adjusted, found the rest of her.

She lay half-twisted, one leg free, the other trapped at the thigh beneath the beam. Her protective skirts had ridden up; molten spill from a shattered channel had kissed exposed skin. The glass had cooled in a heartbeat-thin skin but glowed still underneath, a hunger of heat licking up around the meat of her leg.

She wasn’t screaming.

She was breathing hard, jaw clenched, eyes open and *angry* as she pushed up on the crossbar with both hands like that would matter. Dark hair stuck to her forehead; sweat burned paths through a fine dusting of glass powder.

“Don’t stand there,” she snapped, voice hoarse. “Either run or help.”

That was his first impression of Tiffani Merrow: not the precision, not the neat, measured lines of her work, just the fact that she met a catastrophe with an argument.

Jhace was moving before she finished.

He dropped his torque key, slid in on his knees at the edge of the spill. Heat hit him like opening a furnace door to the face, breath stealing. The cooling glass had formed a glossy, cracked sheet over her thigh, the beam pressing it deeper into flesh.

“You move that wrong, it’ll shear my leg,” she said, rapid, clipped. “We need lift *here* and brace *there.* You see the load paths?”

He did. Different trade, same language. The beam’s weight ran along a twisted line to two points of contact. Move one without the other and it would bite down like a jaw.

“The spill?” he asked.

“Don’t worry about the spill.”

The smell said he should. Burned hair, cooked meat, sharp tang of glass.

Jhace stripped off his right glove in one jerk. The skin beneath was slick with sweat.

“Brin!” he yelled over his shoulder, not checking if the man had actually followed. “Find a lever. Long as you can. And something solid to wedge under.”

“T-panels by the north wall!” someone else shouted—a younger voice, high with panic. “He took a—”

“Do it!” Jhace snapped, cutting across.

He planted his bare palm on the glass.

It was like pressing his hand to the mouth of a stove. For a heartbeat his nerves lied, told him it was just very hot proof-wall. Then the real heat came through, the kind that didn’t feel like heat so much as the concept of *burn* being scribbled into his bones.

Tiffani hissed, not in pain but warning. “You don’t need—”

“Shut up,” he said, teeth bared. “Push when I say.”

The glass flexed under his hand, thin crust cracking to reveal the molten under-layer. He could *feel* it wanting to flow, to slough, to obey a gravity that no longer quite pointed straight down. He pressed harder, trying to spread the contact, to keep it from running in a line that would carve new damage along her leg.

His skin went from hot to gone—no temperature, just raw input. Fingers, palm, wrist, all turned to white noise.

“Brace,” he grunted.

Tiffani set her jaw and pushed against the crossbar again, muscles standing out. Somewhere behind them, boots pounded. Brin slid in with a T-panel, expression sick as he took in the scene.

“Under there,” Jhace managed. “When I say.”

He counted it in his head. Load, angle, give. The world still felt wrong—the tilt turning every simple calculation into something with an extra hidden term—but he did what he’d always done underground: trusted the metal more than his stomach.

“Now.”

He heaved. Not trying to lift the beam—he wasn’t a god, he knew the numbers—but to change the way its weight sat, just enough to open a pocket. Brin jammed the panel in where he’d pointed. The beam shifted, a groan of protest running along its length.

The pressure on Tiffani’s leg eased by a breath.

“Again,” Jhace said, voice strangled.

They did it twice more. On the third, Tiffani jerked her leg free with a choked sound that might have been a scream if she’d given it air. The glass crust shattered where it caught, tearing at the cooked flesh. For an instant Jhace saw bright red under the cooling sheen.

He let go.

His hand came away slower than it should have, skin sticking, then tearing free. He didn’t look at it. The nerves weren’t reporting anything useful anyway.

“Can you walk?” he asked.

Tiffani’s breath sawed in and out. She looked down at her leg once, a quick, clinical glance, then away.

“I can move,” she said. “Walking is later’s problem.”

Brin stared at Jhace’s hand. “Foundry’s ash, Jh—”

“Not now,” Jhace snapped. The world was still falling, and somewhere in the back of his mind a different alarm had been screaming since he’d stepped onto this floor.

Chloen.

He didn’t remember seeing her on the way in. She should have been with Bram, down near Junction Seven. She should have been *anywhere but here*—

“Jhace?”

The voice was small, thin, wrong. Not from the open space, but from under it.

All three of them froze.

“Did you—” Brin started.

“I heard,” Jhace said.

The sound came again, muffled, from the far side of the ruined casting platform. He staggered to his feet, his right hand hanging stupid and red at his side, and picked his way over wreckage and spilled shards, the world continuing its slow adjustment under his boots.

“Chloen!” he bellowed. “Call again!”

A cough. A sob. “Here!”

He found the edge of it: where the platform’s catwalk had sheared away, its metal grating twisted down into a slope of rubble and shattered frames. A section of mezzanine had dropped like a guillotine and lodged in a cracked V between two support columns. Under that, in the narrow, mean space the collapse had spared, he saw movement.

A hand. Smaller than his. Fingers clawing at powdered stone.

He slid down the slope on his good knee, catching himself with his left hand before he face-planted.

“There,” Tiffani said behind him, voice clipped with pain but surprisingly steady. “Beam’s hung. If it slips, it’ll crush that gap flat.”

“I know,” Jhace said, throat tight.

Chloen’s face appeared for a moment as she turned toward his voice. Dust streaked, blood at her hairline. One eye already swelling.

“Can’t… move my leg,” she said, breathing too fast. “It’s stuck.”

“Don’t,” he said. “Don’t move. I’ll get you out.”

He took in the geometry. Beam weight held by stressed stone on one side, twisted frame on the other. Rubble wedged in a way that made the whole thing look stable until you thought about the tilt, about the wrong pull of gravity.

Any piece he shifted could trigger the rest.

His right hand throbbed now, finally, the nerves catching up and deciding to complain all at once. He flexed it and tasted bile. The skin was an angry, wet red, blisters already forming where the glass had cooled against it.

Not the hand he was used to working with. Not the grip he trusted.

Good. Use the other.

He jammed his burned hand against his stomach, out of the way, and started clearing the smaller rubble with his left. Stones, twisted rods, shattered glass, all dragged away and flung back up the slope.

Every touch made the beam groan.

“Stop,” Tiffani said once. “That piece is carrying more than you think.”

“You have a better idea?” he snapped.

“Yes,” she said. “Don’t pull that one. You pull *that* one, you get all of us killed. There—see? Where it catches the flange? Load’s running through it.”

She was right. Damn her, she was right, even half-crushed and half-cooked. He adjusted, took a different piece, worked around.

Chloen coughed. The sound was wet now. That scared him more than the creaking steel.

“Talk to me,” he said, not looking up. “What hurts?”

“Head,” she said faintly. “And—uh—everything.”

He risked a glance. Blood ran down from her hairline in a slow sheet, dripping onto the stone. There was too much of it for comfort.

He reached for a chunk of grating braced across her trapped leg and felt the entire structure shift under his fingers. Not much. Just enough that every instinct he’d ever honed down here screamed *no*.

Stupid thing to do in a collapse. You were supposed to watch the cracks, watch the dust, watch everything. But his eyes were lying to him right now, feeding him a world still pretending to be square. His bones knew better.

The hum of the grid wasn’t just under his feet anymore. It was in his palm, in the metal skin of the frame, in the stone teeth it bit into. It buzzed up his arm in a ragged rhythm, fighting the thud of his own pulse.

Under his hand, the frame shifted.

Not a slide, not a fall. A *correction*. The twisted metal unbent just enough to change how its weight ran. Stones under it settled into new pockets that hadn’t existed a blink before. The big beam overhead sighed as its load redistributed along a path that wasn’t possible in the old, square world.

The rubble around Chloen didn’t explode outward. It settled away from her, small pieces rolling aside as if nudged by a patient, invisible hand.

Her trapped leg came free by degrees. The pressure eased. She sucked in a breath that turned into a sob halfway through.

Then the warmth hit his veins full-on.

It ran down both forearms in a straight, merciless line, carving tracks under the skin. He could see the glow through his eyelashes even with his eyes closed, copper-bright against the washed-out light of the ruined floor.

He ripped his hand back like he’d grabbed live current. The connection broke with a snap that left him dizzy. The world crashed in all at once: the stink of dust and blood, the groan of wounded metal, the keening of someone sobbing nearby—maybe Chloen, maybe him.

He opened his eyes.

Chloen lay clear of the worst of the rubble, leg free. Blood still streaked her face, but the flow from the main cut along her hairline had slowed to a sticky ooze. Where he’d swear her skull had been laid open a moment before, the skin now pulled together in an ugly, fresh seam, red but closed.

She blinked up at him, pupils huge. “That… felt weird.”

Jhace stared at his own arms.

Two clean lines of angry, new color ran from palm to elbow on each, just under the skin. Copper-gold at the center, fading to raw, inflamed flesh at the edges. They throbbed like someone had driven hot rods through his veins and left them there.

“Grids and gutters,” Brin whispered. “What did you do?”

“I don’t—” Jhace started, then stopped.

Because he did know, in the way you know when a bolt is set right without checking the gauge. He’d wanted her whole. The world had obliged. At a price.

A shadow moved at his shoulder. Tiffani limped into view, one hand gripping a bent rail for balance, the other pressed to her burned thigh. Her face was gray around the mouth, but her eyes were sharp.

Carefully, as if approaching a live furnace, she reached out and took his left wrist, turning his arm to see the line.

“That wasn’t heat,” she said quietly. “Not just heat.”

He swallowed. His tongue tasted like metal again.

“Grid’s different,” he managed. “Since the sky… since whatever that was. It’s all… off. But it listened.”

Tiffani’s gaze flicked to Chloen—breathing, alive, bloody but very much *not* dead—then back to his veins.

Her thumb brushed just shy of the burning line, not quite touching. “We’re going to need,” she said, voice still raw but now very, very precise, “to understand what you just did. Before it kills you.”

Jhace let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding. His legs chose that moment to give up. He sat down hard on the broken floor between them, hand throbbing, veins singing, the Spire’s new, crooked hum threading through all of it.

The world had tilted. Something in him had tilted with it.

And for better or worse, it was not going to tilt back.
