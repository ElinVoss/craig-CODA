Here’s the reality:

**What you’ve got**
- MAPP (or MAP-Pro) burns hotter than propane (~3,700°F+), which is great for glass work.
- Handheld torch heads are designed for quick jobs (plumbing, brazing), not long stationary burns on a desk.

**The main risks**
- **Tip-over hazard** → those cylinders are top-heavy. One bump = rolling flame.
- **Overheating the cylinder** → long continuous use can heat the tank, which is bad news.
- **Carbon monoxide** → indoors + open flame = invisible problem if ventilation sucks.
- **Fire spread** → desktops + clutter + radiant heat = ignition waiting to happen.

**If you’re going to keep using it, do this minimum setup:**
- Lock the tank upright (vise, clamp, or a weighted base — don’t trust it standing free)
- Fireproof surface (ceramic tile, cement board, or a soldering/fire brick)
- Clear at least **2–3 feet** around it — no paper, solvents, or dust piles
- Ventilation: window + fan pulling air out (not just blowing around)
- Keep a spray bottle + fire extinguisher within arm’s reach
- Don’t run it nonstop for long stretches — give the cylinder time to cool

**What’s honestly better (if you plan to keep doing this):**
- A **bench torch setup** with:
  - Separate fuel tank (propane or MAP)
  - Hose + stable torch head
  - Optional oxygen mix for cleaner flame control  
- Even a basic entry setup is **way safer and more controllable** than a handheld bottle torch

Ah—that’s a weird one, but it actually makes sense electrically. What’s happening is basically a **voltage spike / EM interference issue**. Here’s the breakdown:  

- The ignitor on a MAPP torch is basically a **piezoelectric spark**. It generates a very short, very high-voltage pulse to jump the gap and create the spark.  
- That pulse **radiates as electromagnetic interference (EMI)**. It’s not huge, but it’s sharp and fast.  
- Your PC → HDMI/TV combo is sensitive to fast EMI spikes. The HDMI cable acts like an antenna, so the spike hits the video line and makes the TV **lose sync temporarily**, which is why it flashes black for a couple seconds.  
- Once the spike decays, everything resyncs, and the display comes back.  

Basically, your torch is sending a tiny “zap” down the airwaves that your HDMI signal freaks out over.  

**Solutions / mitigations:**  
1. **Distance:** Move the torch farther from the PC and cables. Even a foot or two helps a lot.  
2. **Shielding:** Run the HDMI cable away from the torch and ideally wrap it or use a ferrite bead on the cable.  
3. **Grounding:** Make sure the PC, TV, and any metal surfaces are properly grounded—helps dissipate spikes.  
4. **Alternative ignitor:** Some glass burners switch to a **battery-powered push ignitor** instead of the piezo, which avoids the spike.

### **Containing the spark**
The key is stopping the EMI from radiating into nearby electronics:

1. **Faraday cage / shielding**
   - Surround the torch tip and ignitor circuit in a metal enclosure connected to ground.
   - Even a thin aluminum box works for high-frequency spikes.
   - This stops the pulse from radiating into HDMI/USB lines.

2. **Twist and shield cables**
   - Run all sensitive cables (HDMI, USB, power) in **shielded cables** or twist them tightly.  
   - Add **ferrite cores** at each end—these choke high-frequency spikes.

3. **Grounding**
   - A proper ground plane for the table/bench reduces “floating voltage” spikes that reach your PC/TV.
   - Metal table + wire to grounded outlet can absorb the discharge.

4. **Spark gap modification**
   - Moving the piezo electrodes slightly closer to the torch nozzle and away from cables reduces radiated EMI.  
   - Adding a **small capacitor (pF-nF range)** across the piezo terminals can soften the spike so it doesn’t radiate as much.

---

### **Using the spark**
A single piezo spark is tiny energy-wise (~0.1–1 mJ), but it’s **high-voltage, very short pulse**, which is what makes it interesting:

1. **Igniting flammable gasses**

3. **Triggering circuits**
   - That voltage spike can **trigger a sensitive electronic input** if properly isolated and scaled down with resistors or voltage dividers.

4. **High-voltage capacitor charging**
