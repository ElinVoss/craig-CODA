# **Chapter 1: Narrowvale Pilot**

Jhace comes in with the rain. Narrowvale’s three-lane choke gleams slick, flags the color of old brass slapping on their cords. Steam from kettles shelves low, the smell of onion and wet rope. He lifts the placard kit to the posting wall and sets his boot on the bottom rung of a bent ladder that doesn’t trust him. The board he needs to cover is crowded: last‑season tax notice, a chipped fish‑day prayer tile, someone’s hand‑ink arrows pointing nowhere.

“Two hands,” he says, not loud, only for the crew near. Kylar anchors the rung with a shin and his palm flat. Brighlee waits at the side with the bell card and an oilcloth roll of ties.

Jhace measures with fingers first. Two knuckles from the bolt head, three from the seam in the stone. The Map‑as‑Set board will live here or it will die here; there’s no point being precious somewhere else. He chalks the rectangle, an honest outline that catches the spit of weather, then opens the kit.

Icon plates in their slots: droplet, wedge, ring, arrows, bell marks. The Conclave stamp sits in its felt pocket, weighty as a small vise. Ridger tool wrapped in cloth, teeth fine as a fishbone comb. He checks the ink’s drag with a thumb. Viscosity good; rain won’t thin it into lies.

The steward arrives like a brass hinge grinding dry. Jall Var carries a ledger in two hands as if it is a baby that might curse. He takes in the chalk, the board, the crew. “Not text,” he says, meaning to cut the thing short. He points with the ledger’s corner. “This lane reads. We post words.”

“Bell windows are color,” Brighlee murmurs, looking nowhere.

Jhace gives the steward the polite weight of a glance and no more. The lane is already crowding. Carts at his flank drip, mule ears flick. Vendors fold tarps, then unfold them again when the gust shifts. Authority noise rides the rain: a council runner up the back way, someone calling about a missed fee, three men rehearsing an argument so it feels practiced. The choke is hot metal without air.

He marks the first anchor. The hammer shows its clean face, and Kylar’s small breath answers each tack with a ready tie. The board goes up steady, strip by strip, not a show. Jhace steps off the ladder, calves buzzing, and wipes rain off the board with the inside of his sleeve. The kit’s latch ticks open again. He slides out the plate for the lane map and lays it to the wood.

Map‑as‑Set replaces words. He sets the three arrows for the three streams of traffic in the choke: left, heavy carts; center, foot and hand‑barrows; right, return flow and runners. The arrows’ tails meet in a simple tooth that suggests the pinch point. He presses ink and pulls clean. The black sits proud without shine. Water beads gather, refuse to smudge, and roll.

“Those are lines,” Jall Var says. “Not law.”

Jhace checks the spacing to the caller post he chalked earlier. Brighlee is already there, tying a small square to the post that announces bell windows. Blue for Four, yellow for Two, the squares clipped to spin when the wind wants them. She gives him a look that says: simple holds.

“Law is posted,” Jhace says, soft. He doesn’t say it to the steward; he says it to the board. He fits the ring glyph at waist height, the one that marks the home line for hands. The ring must be obvious but not intrusive. Too high and people miss it; too low and children worry it like a toy. He measures from his belt to the board, sets the ring, inked and honest.

The wedge glyph sits where it must sit, upstream of a Veil zone that will arrive in two chapters’ worth of work for other people. For now, the wedge simply points to the corner turn standard ahead, a promise the board makes and the crews must keep. He places the wedge and pulls the plate. Clean edge, no feathering. Rain tracks around it like water around a polished stone.

Jall Var is not done. “Without text, my clerks must interpret. You invite mishandling. Fines fall wrong. You will cause mistakes.” A vendor nearby, hearing fines, goes quiet. The rain doesn’t.

Kylar keeps his hands busy at the ties, learning the icons by touch as he has been taught: arrow is a long bone, wedge is a tooth, ring is a smooth coin, droplet is a tear that points down to where oils belong. Jhace approves the habit by not stopping it. He sets the bell marks in a neat row along the board’s bottom edge, two short stripes, one long, for the cadence that will regulate changes. He hears the future taps in his bones, a steadying thought in the weather’s noise.

He drifts the ridger out of its cloth and holds it near the bottom right of the card. Tempermark isn’t the ritual today, but a ridge at the legend will keep counterfeits from slipping in early. He presses the teeth with a palm heel until they bite a relief into the inked field. Not a flourish, just a fact you can feel.

“Your people can leave the words to the court?” Jall Var asks, voice climbing. “We are literate. We will not be reduced.”

Jhace’s voice is tired from the morning’s pressline run across Grindheim’s span. It comes out hoarse, the way a hinge sounds after salt air. “The corridor reads the icons,” he says. “Clerks can read as much as they like elsewhere.” He lifts the Conclave stamp and sets it under the wedge. The stamp’s face is a small square with the quill mark. He inks it and presses until the wood says yes with a small change in tone. When he pulls away, the black square sits like a seal on a crate. Lawful enough for any steward who bothers to look.

“Who signed that?” Jall Var asks.

“Corlexi,” Brighlee answers, because she was there. She taps the ledger strip that lives in her roll where the approval number sits dry and precise. She doesn’t offer it to the steward. Rain and paper are enemies; this crowd will see it later when it can be seen.

The choke flexes. A mule pushes its shoulder into the wrong lane. Jhace steps into the center without fanfare, one palm out for gentle stop, one finger to the new map, then a pivot of the wrist that returns the animal to left flow. He doesn’t shout. He doesn’t need to. The driver reads the arrows and yields. Rain runs off the brim of the man’s hat and finds its way to the gutters instead of down the corridor’s spine.

Pressure builds again at the back as a cart’s wheel catches on a loose cobble. The sound is a dull metal waver. Jhace feels his calves complain as he half‑crouches to reset the stone with his foot and a pry of the hammer’s handle. Kylar is there with a wedge of wood to keep the repair honest. They don’t exchange words. The work is a unit of actions that fit without comment.

“Card needs warnings,” the steward says, raising his voice to cover the crowd. “Words about penalties. Words about exceptions.” He enjoys the shape of exceptions.

Jhace sets the final icon: the droplet. A small mark at the board’s lower left that directs oil use to the annex and keeps the corridor free of false cures. Salee will love it when she sees it. He cleans the plate on the cloth’s dry edge and racks it home. Cost shows in the small shake at his wrist as he unwinds from the ladder one more time. He is thirsty. He is nearly done.

He steps back until his shoulders touch the posting wall. Brighlee lifts the caller square and flicks Blue to face the lane. “Bell Four weather,” she says, low, so the world can arrange itself. She ties it off with a knot that can be changed with wet fingers. Kylar rechecks the ties in sequence, then palms the ring glyph to test its height, an apprentice ritual they made up in a dry moment and kept.

Jall Var opens his mouth, maybe to announce a stay. A gust blows his ledger’s pages into his face, and he takes a small step back. The crowd laughs, the soft kind that makes the temperature of a place rise half a degree.

Jhace takes the moment. He lifts the ridger once more and bites a thin ridge along the legend line, the place where a thumb will go if the eyes don’t trust yet. He breathes the way he teaches: in for four, out for six. The breath burns at the top of his chest where the morning left a rasp. He can feel the ache ripening in his calves, a clean soreness like metal cooling right.

“Post is live,” he says to the board. To the lane. Not to the steward. He puts the ridger away, seals the kit, and lets the tools rest.

Behavior changes first at the edges. A barrower reaches the choke and pauses, uncertain, then sees his lane in the arrow and steps into it without being told. A boy with a coil of cord at his waist drifts his hand to the ring glyph as he walks and smiles like something fit in his head without pain. To the right, a pair of runners align themselves and stop fighting space they do not own.

The steward tries one last time. “If someone asks, where do they read the clause?”

Jhace tips his chin at the Conclave square. “There,” he says. “The clause is that this is the clause.” He doesn’t wait for the answer. He turns to the board and rubs the lower corner with the pad of his thumb to see if the ridge holds under rain and flesh. It does.

Brighlee unrolls a small strip with the caller symbol and slips it over the post so the next crew knows where to stand and when. Kylar moves through the first three carts like he was born a knot‑opener, touching nothing, only pointing. A mule snorts, a wheel creaks, steam lifts. Air finds a path again. The corridor’s tone drops half a note.

Jhace steps away, hoarseness a scratch that won’t drink water until the work is fully done. He lets the lane show him if it learned something. It did. He feels it in the way the pressure at the choke goes from thrust to glide.

He sets the stamp again just once, a second bite beside the first, not required, only present for the people who count such things. Then he does the last check: he runs his own thumb over the little Tempermark ridge where the legend lives, skin finding the raised line by instinct rather than sight.

A woman with a market cart does the same, tentative, then sure. Her thumb finds the ridge. The cart eases forward without a shouted order.

# **Chapter 2: South Annex Veils**

Tiffani had the benches warm and the lamps turned down to a honey dim so the glass would tell the truth. The South Clinic Annex breathed its own weather: oil smell, thread kits lined like small bones, veils stacked in rolls that caught the light at their edges. She chalked the wedge glyph on the teaching board, angled toward the corner work, and set the metronome bell two beats soft.

The hot racks arrived off schedule, metal shrieking against metal. A runner and his partner came shouldering through the Annex arch with the first frame still giving off heat. Steam lifted when rain from their coats met the rack’s bars. Salee swore in the doorway, one clipped syllable, then claimed the oil shelf from a curious trainee with a wrist tap that was gentle and absolute.

“Wedge before Veil,” Tiffani said. She kept her voice low and clean, the way you speak beside reactive material. “Corners three‑count. We practice with dead panes first.” She nodded toward the practice frames, the ones with score marks to show where old failures began.

Kylar stood at the edge with a coil of ties he didn’t need for this class but didn’t put down because hands need a task. Valaryn, sleeves rolled, laid out return‑to‑work slips where trainees could see them and not touch. Tiffani let their competence be background muscle. The work in front of her was three apprentices new to Annex discipline and one old hand who had learned in a shop with more shouting than standard.

She hooked the first dead pane into the frame and traced the route with the raw edge of a wedge plate. The wedge glyph points, the wedge tool speaks; the two must agree. “The wedge is not decoration,” she said. “It is the turn. You set it before you lay a veil. If you veil a live corner without a wedge, the crack you earn is your fault. This will not be argued.”

The metronome bell ticked twice, soft as fingernails on a jar. She let the sound land in the room’s nerves.

“Three‑count corners,” she said. “Listen first.” She held the corner of the dead pane under the lamp and breathed on it until mist printed. The edge haloed. “One to find the heat,” she counted, finger following the inner edge. “Two to move it.” Her hand drew a small circle with the wedge’s flat. “Three to hold and leave.” She stepped away and the mist thinned to nothing, a clean matte that said, safe.

The first trainee, a boy whose hands were too strong for their own good, reached for the veil roll too fast. Tiffani took his wrist, not a correction that hurt, only an anchor. “Wedge first,” she said. He switched tools, the small sting of embarrassment serving as a better primer than talk.

The hot rack thudded into its notch. Heat hummed through the bench, a tone that lived in the steel more than the air. Off schedule meant the glass would come through before she had finished training speed on cold material. She moved the metronome a hair faster with a knuckle. “We adjust but do not skip,” she said to no one and everyone.

Salee set the oil vials above the benches, purity seals intact and obvious. A neat square of color sat on the shelf edge to match the lot’s true hue. Tiffani eyed it once and looked away. Oils lied when people lied; today they would not.

Trainee Two ran her wedge along the edge, angle wrong, pressing too hard. The sound told on her, a dry scrape where the metal should sing. “Listen,” Tiffani said. She took the wedge and made the same pass, a small correction of wrist. The sound shifted to a whisper that lived in the room’s bones. “If it scratches your head, it’s wrong. If it makes you breathe out without knowing, it’s right.”

Valaryn cleared her throat softly, not to speak, but to draw a pair of watchers to the posted slips. Paid rest, return‐to‐work: the clinic’s part of Veinwork, visible, not a rumor. Tiffani caught the gesture’s neatness with a brief nod and returned to the bench.

“Corners,” she said. “Three counts.” She placed a fresh pane on the jig. The lamp showed the corner’s gloss a little like ice. “You will want to drag the heat,” she said. “You will not. You will ask it to sit.” She breathed across the corner again and traced the ring where mist laid. “One. Two. Three.” On three she lifted the wedge and set the veil, not touching the bright edge with bare skin, not clouding the flux with damp.

The old hand leaned forward. “I was taught to veil first,” he muttered, like a confession a person offers right before he decides to ignore you. Tiffani did not look up.

“You were taught wrong,” she said, not unkind. “Your panes failed at the corners and you blamed weather. We have weather every day.” She tilted the frame so the light told the truth. “The wedge makes the corner a decision. The veil protects that decision. That is the order.”

The second rack arrived out of breath, carried by a team that should have been somewhere else on the clock. Steam again. The sound of wet canvas slapping a wall. Tiffani’s back tightened from hours of leaning into benches across the day; something in her lower spine ticked like a cooling plate. She drank a mouthful from the tin at her hip, the water metallic from the tap and good.

She moved the class to live glass because time had said so. The hot frame was a patient that would not wait its turn and would punish vanity. “Hands washed, gloves dry,” she said. “If your breath wobbles, step away and call.” No one wobbled. The room was made of people who hated to be the cause.

She nodded to Trainee Three, the quiet one who had placed her tools in a line and would not start without being told. “Set,” Tiffani said. The wedge went down. The angle was true, the pressure light. She watched the girl hear the room, saw the small slackening in her mouth when the wedge sang across the glass correctly for the first time.

“Corner,” Tiffani said. “Count like you mean it.” The metronome bell ticked. One. Two. Three. The trainee’s breath synced without drama, the body learning before the mind wrote anything down.

The veil came next, laid with the roll’s leader aligned to the wedge’s exit. No chatter, no flare. The edge took matte like frost receding under sun. Salee, satisfied that no one would waste oil or knock a vial, turned and posted a small reminder square above the shelf: one drop icon, black; a wedge; a quill. Inventory truth is policy.

The old hand tried to move faster to prove he could. He caught the corner driver just enough to smudge gloss. Tiffani’s palm met his on the tool and took it away. “Stop,” she said. Not a scold. A door closing. “Again. The wedge listens first. Your hands do not outrun heat.”

He swallowed and stepped back into the beat like a person finding the right place in a song that was already going. His second attempt arrived in the room as quiet as a decent apology. The wedge whispered. The corner held. The veil did not lie.

Tiffani felt the day in her back like a wire pulled too tight. She shifted her stance and let weight ride through her heels into the floor. The Annex’s stone took it and gave her steadiness back. She moved bench to bench, checking. Thumb above a corner to call mist, then letting it fade to judge rate. Palm near a seam to sense radial heat. Breath in, count, breath out. “Again,” she said when again was what was required. “Good,” she said once when it would teach better than silence.

The door admitted a gust that made the lamp flame lean. Air skittered across a finished edge and left it unmoved. Tiffani watched for counterfeit behavior and saw none: inks right, ridges true, oil smell clean. She waved Kylar to the posting wall with the teaching card blank. He held the ties but didn’t tie. That was her part.

“Last pane,” she said. The room had the neat hunger of people about to finish well. She took the tools herself, not to prove anything, only because it was faster and time was a material there was ever less of. Wedge set. Angle clean. The wedge plate kissed the edge like metal speaking to glass in a language both respected. Corner: one, two, three. Veil laid with the leader aligned as if a line drawn on air. The lamp’s cone showed the gloss give up its shine and settle.

She nodded once, enough for the room to release the held breath. Back complained, mouth dry, hands steady.

At the wall, she inked the teaching card: wedge glyph large and honest at top left, three short bars stacked beside it to mark the counts, a small ring glyph at waist height to remind of home line even here where benches and bodies could crowd truth out. Bell marks in the lower field for the cadence they practiced. Conclave square with quill stamped in the corner, authority quiet and visible. The ridger tool left a fine bite across the legend strip so fingers could tell if the board was real.

She tied the card to the board at the Annex’s end, knots set so a wet hand could change them. Kylar checked the ties in sequence with a fingertip, reading the icons like raised letters, then stepped back.

The class drifted close. No one touched the card. They didn’t have to. The lamps hummed in their cages. Tiffani leaned over the last pane and breathed one more time across the corner. The gloss surrendered. Matte held. In that soft light the edge looked like it had exhaled. She turned her head and the lamplight ran clean along the line.

On the posted card, the wedge glyph sat clear as a coin. Beneath it, in that same honey dim, the pane’s edge showed safe matte while the brief mist thinned and was gone.

# **Chapter 3: Pressline Cadence**

Jhace reached the Narrowvale choke ahead of the bell change and found the weather already doing the work of a poor foreman. Rain hit in sheets that turned cobbles to polished iron. Kettle steam bent under the gusts and laid a low fog at shin height. The lane held like a frayed rope in a hand that hadn’t decided to let go or pull. Bell Two’s window was about to end; Bell Four would raise its face. That seam was where lanes came apart.

He slung the short bar across his shoulder. It was nothing more than a length of tempered rod with a coin‑thick cap for the taps. The pressline lives in small, clear sounds. He kept the placard kit under his cloak to keep the ridger teeth dry. Brighlee stood already at the caller post, Blue square still showing, fingers at the tie knot, eyes on him. Kylar held a coil of cord and the little hammer like a young man who had understood that hands idle at a choke were a liability.

Vendors shoved for last sales before a window shift. A man with red sleeves decided the center lane was his private invention and angled a cart into foot traffic. A girl carrying oil tins bumped a shoulder and swallowed her pain so it wouldn’t leak. Authority noise gathered on the edges like a storm building its own weather. The bell rope twitched down the corridor where it hung from its housing.

Jhace set his palm on the caller post to feel the lane’s tone through the wood, the way a bridgewright learns to listen to his spans. The energy had turned granular; there was no flow, only a crowd of particles insulting each other. He took a breath that hurt a little from the morning’s chill and let it out slow. Then he lifted the bar.

Two short taps.

They landed clean, coin on rod, a sound with borders. The first found the crowd’s ears. The second told them the first wasn’t a mistake. He waited a breath and delivered them again. Two short taps. He kept the spacing with his ribs, not his head.

Brighlee flipped the caller square from Blue to Yellow with a quick twist and tied it so it could be cut fast if weather asked. Kylar drifted along the left boundary, pointing the front axle of Red Sleeves back to the heavy lane with a patient hand and the plain authority of someone who belonged to the board.

A vendor near the steam carts shoved the man in front of him to prove a point to a third party. The shove shook the middle lane like a wire. Jhace did not look there. He looked at the heads down the way and counted four, then six, that had started to move in sympathy with the taps. Two short. Two short. The cadence did not tell them what to do; it taught them when to do what the board already asked.

He used his left hand for the bar, the one that took its orders more reluctantly, to preserve the right for fine work if he needed it. The weather’s needles pushed into the cuff of his sleeve. The rod’s cap rang true; the sound bounced once off the stone face and returned corrected. He saw the first knot slacken where runners had tangled with a barrow and tried to share space.

The bell in its housing made up its mind and let go of Two. The last notes hung. People habitually surge at the seam, wanting to capture an advantage that doesn’t exist. He sent the taps again, a hair faster, still paired. Two short. Two short. The pressline is not a marshal. It’s a metronome you can feel in your ankle bones.

Red Sleeves caught his eye, not with a challenge, only because he had been forced to notice. Jhace touched the map‑as‑set board with the rod’s cap to point and then lowered the rod and tapped. Tap tap. The driver’s chin dipped once. He backed his wheel an inch and the jam that had been a problem turned into a problem that had edges. Edge problems you can work.

The lane breathed. Drops hit puddles in complex polyrhythms until they started to organize themselves around the tapping like iron filings finding a field. He felt the odd ache begin in his right wrist anyway, the cost of a day that had been more arm than leg. It would be a dull throb by night, the sort you soak out with heat and small patience. He kept the left hand working and let the right hang loose at his side.

Brighlee called nothing. She didn’t need to. The cadence carried the message that the window had turned and the rules of the board continued. She held up two fingers toward the bell housing for the ringer to see through the steam. The ringer answered with a nod that didn’t care if anyone else witnessed it. Work acknowledging work.

Somewhere behind the soup stall, a barked complaint rose, tried to find allies, failed, and ran out of energy against the taps. Two short. Two short. Jhace moved three paces toward the center and touched the map with his thumb at the corner where earlier he had set the wedge glyph. There, the knife‑point of the choke where flow decided whether the corridor was a lane or a fight. He tapped once against the board as a courtesy to his own method and stepped back to hear what would happen without him.

It happened. The knot by the barrow opened at the edges first. A wheel nudged into a space that had not been space thirty seconds ago. A child holding a depth of cloth recognized that the pressline was older than his inclination to dart. He waited. Two short. Two short. He moved during the spaces and the moment he chose belonged to him because the corridor had lent it.

Kylar made his loop at the front, the runner’s light step that touches every square of the problem without owning any of it. He set the little hammer’s face lightly to a tie that had sagged at the caller post and took the slack out without ceremony. The young have gifts; this one’s was reading icons by finger in the rain, correcting small things before they borrowed money from big things.

The shove from the vendor line tried to become a practice. Jhace put three beats of silence between tap sets and watched the shove fail in the quiet because there was no rhythm it could break. Then he resumed. Tap tap. Tap tap. His calves remembered last week’s climb and protested at the stance he kept that balanced weight across slick stone. He shifted, preserved his hinge, and let that be the adjustment.

Red Sleeves put his palm briefly to the ring glyph at the map’s waist height as he passed it, the unconscious confession of a man who had been angry and now wasn’t sure at what. The ring’s paint had been polished by many such hands already this week. It gleamed even under rain, a small truth in a wet world.

When the surge was ninety percent solved, which is the number that lets a place heal itself the rest of the way, Jhace lowered the rod and let the cadence live in the crowd for a few breaths without him. It did. He felt unnecessary, which is how a standard should make the person who sets it feel on a good day.

He turned to the board. The Map‑as‑Set carried the arrows, ring, wedge, and the bell marks he’d placed earlier. Today needed words without words: a clause in icon language that taught the taps to outlive the man who held the rod. He opened the kit under his cloak and kept the inner lining between the ridger and the rain.

The clause was a strip that would live at the board’s lower field: two short bars paired, then a small gap, then the pair again, with a quill‑square to show Conclave authority. He set the plate, checked the spacing with a thumb, and inked with care. The first pull sat true. The second did too. He breathed. His wrist complained when he forced the ridger to bite the legend line’s edge so the strip couldn’t be lifted clean without telling on itself. He accepted the cost and kept going.

Brighlee stepped close and tilted her oilcloth so his work stayed honest. She did not speak. Kylar kept the crowd’s edge from licking at the posting space by simply existing where he existed, a body that said the board was not a negotiable surface.

Jhace lifted the stamp and set the small Conclave square with the quill mark at the clause’s right margin. He pressed until the wood changed tone under his palm and then he released. The square sat with the comfortable authority of a pin that had been set cooler than birth. He didn’t need a witness for this. The witness would be behavior.

He tapped the rod once against the post to clear water and set cadence again, this time standing in front of the new clause strip as if it had always been there. Tap tap. Tap tap. He matched his sound to what the board now declared.

A vendor near the edge who loved exceptions more than food opened his mouth and then closed it because his feet had already obeyed. The ringer at the housing let the first of Bell Four’s notes out. They rode over the tapping rather than against it. Rain smacked the ground like small coins. The pressline held.

Jhace finished the last pass with the rod when the window’s seam had fully laid down. He let the bar rest against his shoulder and rolled his right wrist with the small grimace you allow when no one is looking, felt the tendon’s heat, and made a private note about wrapping it later with clinic salts.

He wiped a sleeve across the lower board where water had tried to pool and then stepped back to see the whole like he always did, against the habit of zooming in that had saved him earlier lives and would kill this one if he let it have the reins.

The clause lived where it should. Two short bars, a measured space, two short bars again. No text about penalties. No room for misreading. Conclave square biting the corner. The ring glyph shone where hands had made it honest. The arrows told their quiet story.

He let the crowd show him the result. It did. The center lane shifted from personal to communal; the left tightened to a competent squeeze; the right carried return flow without flinching. A runner passed with knees high to keep his shoes from taking in water, hand brushing the ring in passing, eyes already on the next choke.

Jhace set the rod down against the post, secured by a simple tie so the next person could find it without hunting. He pressed the ridger once more into the clause edge to leave that fine bite for fingers that asked if it was real.

On the board beside the arrows, the cadence clause sat newly posted, ink glossy in the rain. At his feet, puddles took the two short taps and answered. Rings spread, matched the beat, and then melted into each other as the knot opened.

# **Chapter 4: Counterfeit Placard**

Tiffani met the choke at first bell after rain, when ink tells on itself. The posting wall carried the Map‑as‑Set card Jhace had set, the cadence strip, a clinic notice square from Valaryn, and—new overnight—a Tempermarked caution plate someone had tucked in at the edge like a helpful afterthought. The edge was wrong. The board wanted to be believed too badly.

She palmed the ridger in her pocket to keep her hand honest and stepped into the spray. Floor hands drifted closer with the look people wear when they suspect a thing and hope to be wrong. A man with damp apron and a printer’s nail bed stood within ear, attention too careful. Forger nearby, she marked, and let the note sink without changing her face.

Action first: she touched the plate. A proper Tempermark gives the thumb something to argue with. This one was smooth, shine like wet glass not ink. Her fingertip skated. Flat.

Brighlee arrived at her shoulder with the ties and the Conclave stamp packet, eyes already on the strangers who had made themselves temporarily busy. Kylar hovered by the caller post, one hand on home line like a boy setting himself to be reliable if called. The lane was thin now, early carts rolling in the clean after-rain light. Good time to pull a lie.

Tiffani curled the corner of the suspect plate and checked bite. A real board resists then yields; this peeled without complaint, like a sticker sold to children. She didn’t rip it yet. Public corrections live better if you show the why first.

“Fingers,” she said, not loud, only to the three nearest vendors who’d stopped selling to watch. She held the counterfeit where it could be reached and held up the real board’s legend strip beside it. “Here, then here.” She pressed her own thumb first along the posted clause on the real board, where the ridger’s teeth had laid a fine bite yesterday. The tiny ridge met her skin with the respectful push of a truth refusing to be ignored. She slid to the fake. Smooth. “Feel the difference,” she said.

They did. First vendor’s face changed before he had words. Second closed his eyes for the test and nodded like a person listening to a song that proves itself in the first bar. Third hissed half a breath through teeth.

“Counter,” Brighlee said low, confirming the call and shifting her stance so her body made a small wall against a sudden wind. The printer’s nail bed man looked at the sky like he admired weather and had a place to be. He didn’t move away. People who had good paper rarely needed their courage to keep them still.

Tiffani kept her hand steady. The day had already taxed it with bench work at the Annex; the small muscles along her thumb complained at the fine control. She locked her wrist and let arm bone carry the work.

## **﻿Prologue — The Year the World Took a Breath**

The world ended once and decided it was too much trouble to stay dead. It didn’t break apart; it flexed, like a lung taking air after being punched. A pull went through the crust—straight, fast, absolute—and stopped just short of tearing clean through. What it left was a tunnel with no opposite mouth. People called it the Bore, because naming the wound makes it sound finished.  
From the Upper Crown, it looks like an even circle of shadow on maps. From the Underface, it’s a black horizon that presses in your teeth when the air gets heavy. Nobody pretends it’s a mystery anymore. We live around it, not above it. Every crack, every bridge, every load-bearing inch answers to what the Bore changed.  
It began with water taking new routes. Rivers turned, cliffs sagged, docks listed without warning. For a year the wind came up from the ground instead of the sky, and you could smell iron on your skin no matter how often you washed. People called it divine; the rest of us called it gravity. When the air stopped shaking, a handful of us were still standing, and we started measuring before we started praying.  
We found the veins by accident. They ran under the ground like nerves under skin, thin lines where force preferred to travel. When the veins slipped out of their old paths, they bled heat and pressure and made Harrow—the kind of rupture that doesn’t care if you’re watching. A few people, tired of being erased, learned to touch the veins and guide them. Those hands became Veinwrights. Their work became the city’s language: Trace. Set. Bridge. Veil. Slake. Tempermark.  
The Bore taught one rule that never stopped mattering: everything carries stress, and stress remembers. You can’t erase a fault; you can only give it a safer way to move. That’s what the craft is—negotiation between material and will. Every building stands because someone convinced it not to fall.  
Six months after the pull, the first bridges started breathing. At night you could see vapor lifting from the joints like ghost breath. No one remembers laying the mains that kept them warm, but the warmth stayed. It rose through basements and clinics, through pipes that had no valves, through the Conclave halls where the Veinwrights kept their ledgers. The city learned to live with the heat as a fact, not a gift. It kept people alive in winter, and it kept them honest—because if a line failed, everyone nearby heard it.  
When the air cooled, the Bells came. Not temple chimes—work signals. Long clear rings for reopen, two short for brace, fast beats for Harrow risk. Each Bell came with a board stamped in copper and sealed in glass. You could tell a city’s health by how clean its Tempermarks were.  
The first posted rule was ugly and perfect: One hand on the home ring. Hold something that won’t move while you touch what might. It was less philosophy, more survival.  
Then came the rest, written by people who’d already bled once for the knowledge: — Trace wet. Dry lines please eyes and lie to Veins. — Wedge before Veil. Cool the draft, then the glass. — Half-beat Slake. — Countermark cooler than birth. Pride bends things wrong. — No White. — Brace taps only in Bells Two and Three. You are not special in Four. — If it isn’t on the board, it didn’t happen.  
They’re not prayers. They’re methods. Watch a street when a blue HALT goes up and every hammer stops midair. Watch the crowd hold breath until the setter witness signs the clear. That’s how cities stay alive—by honoring pause more than noise.  
We forbid Overbind, because a clean disaster still kills you. We forbid Whitequench, because a lie in glass cuts deeper than heat. We measure by what doesn’t fail, not by what shines.  
After the Bore, Bells multiplied. Every trade learned its rhythm. The Couriers’ Bell marked motion; the Conclave’s marked repair. You could walk a full day and never be out of hearing range. It was noise that meant survival—call, answer, repeat.  
Six hundred and twelve rounds later, Te’Oga still leans a fraction toward the Bore. Maps mark the tilt with a single diagonal line through the city’s name. Children in the Underface draw circles in ash to guess the next drift. Crown engineers add decimal places to comfort themselves. Neither side is wrong.  
The Bore doesn’t threaten anymore. It just exists. We’ve made a kind of peace with the wound: keep it measured, keep it known, keep it held.  
Work here isn’t sacred. It’s necessary. The Conclave says “Trace wet.” The Couriers answer, “One hand on home.” Together, they make the city stay where it is.  
If you need a prayer, you use your hands: Trace wet. Wedge before Veil. Slake on the half-beat. Tempermark what you promise. Post it. The world isn’t healed. It’s held. That’s enough.

# **Chapter 1: Narrowvale Pilot**

The board he needs to cover is crowded: last‑season tax notice, a chipped fish‑day prayer tile, someone’s hand‑ink arrows pointing nowhere.

“Two hands,” he says, not loud, only for the crew near. Kylar anchors the rung with a shin and his palm flat. Brighlee waits at the side with the bell card and an oilcloth roll of ties.

Jhace measures with fingers first. Two knuckles from the bolt head, three from the seam in the stone. The Map‑as‑Set board will live here or it will die here; there’s no point being precious somewhere else. He chalks the rectangle, an honest outline that catches the spit of weather, then opens the kit.

Icon plates in their slots: droplet, wedge, ring, arrows, bell marks. The Conclave stamp sits in its felt pocket, weighty as a small vise. Ridger tool wrapped in cloth, teeth fine as a fishbone comb. He checks the ink’s drag with a thumb. Viscosity good; rain won’t thin it into lies.

The steward arrives like a brass hinge grinding dry. Jall Var carries a ledger in two hands as if it is a baby that might curse. He takes in the chalk, the board, the crew. “Not text,” he says, meaning to cut the thing short. He points with the ledger’s corner. “This lane reads. We post words.”

“Bell windows are color,” Brighlee murmurs, looking nowhere.

Jhace gives the steward the polite weight of a glance and no more. The lane is already crowding. Carts at his flank drip, mule ears flick. Vendors fold tarps, then unfold them again when the gust shifts. Authority noise rides the rain: a council runner up the back way, someone calling about a missed fee, three men rehearsing an argument so it feels practiced. The choke is hot metal without air.

He marks the first anchor. The hammer shows its clean face, and Kylar’s small breath answers each tack with a ready tie. The board goes up steady, strip by strip, not a show. Map‑as‑Set replaces words. He sets the three arrows for the three streams of traffic in the choke: left, heavy carts; center, foot and hand‑barrows; right, return flow and runners. Jhace checks the spacing to the caller post he chalked earlier. Brighlee is already there, tying a small square to the post that announces bell windows. Blue for Four, yellow for Two, the squares clipped to spin when the wind wants them. She gives him a look that says: simple holds.

“Law is posted,” Jhace says, soft. He doesn’t say it to the steward; he says it to the board. He fits the ring glyph at waist height, the one that marks the home line for hands. The gloss surrendered. Matte held. In that soft light the edge looked like it had exhaled. She turned her head and the lamplight ran clean along the line.

On the posted card, the wedge glyph sat clear as a coin. Beneath it, in that same honey dim, the pane’s edge showed safe matte while the brief mist thinned and was gone.

# **Chapter 3: Pressline Cadence**

Jhace reached the Narrowvale choke ahead of the bell change and found the weather already doing the work of a poor foreman. Rain hit in sheets that turned cobbles to polished iron. Kettle steam bent under the gusts and laid a low fog at shin height. The lane held like a frayed rope in a hand that hadn’t decided to let go or pull. Bell Two’s window was about to end; Bell Four would raise its face. That seam was where lanes came apart.

He slung the short bar across his shoulder. It was nothing more than a length of tempered rod with a coin‑thick cap for the taps. The pressline lives in small, clear sounds. He kept the placard kit under his cloak to keep the ridger teeth dry. Brighlee stood already at the caller post, Blue square still showing, fingers at the tie knot, eyes on him. Kylar held a coil of cord and the little hammer like a young man who had understood that hands idle at a choke were a liability.

Vendors shoved for last sales before a window shift. A man with red sleeves decided the center lane was his private invention and angled a cart into foot traffic. A girl carrying oil tins bumped a shoulder and swallowed her pain so it wouldn’t leak. Authority noise gathered on the edges like a storm building its own weather. The bell rope twitched down the corridor where it hung from its housing.

Jhace set his palm on the caller post to feel the lane’s tone through the wood, the way a bridgewright learns to listen to his spans. The energy had turned granular; there was no flow, only a crowd of particles insulting each other. He took a breath that hurt a little from the morning’s chill and let it out slow. Then he lifted the bar.

Two short taps.

They landed clean, coin on rod, a sound with borders. The first found the crowd’s ears. The second told them the first wasn’t a mistake. He waited a breath and delivered them again. Two short taps. He kept the spacing with his ribs, not his head.

Brighlee flipped the caller square from Blue to Yellow with a quick twist and tied it so it could be cut fast if weather asked. Kylar drifted along the left boundary, pointing the front axle of Red Sleeves back to the heavy lane with a patient hand and the plain authority of someone who belonged to the board.

A vendor near the steam carts shoved the man in front of him to prove a point to a third party. The shove shook the middle lane like a wire. Jhace did not look there. He looked at the heads down the way and counted four, then six, that had started to move in sympathy with the taps. Two short. Two short. The cadence did not tell them what to do; it taught them when to do what the board already asked.

He used his left hand for the bar, the one that took its orders more reluctantly, to preserve the right for fine work if he needed it. The weather’s needles pushed into the cuff of his sleeve. The rod’s cap rang true; the sound bounced once off the stone face and returned corrected. He saw the first knot slacken where runners had tangled with a barrow and tried to share space.

The bell in its housing made up its mind and let go of Two. The last notes hung. People habitually surge at the seam, wanting to capture an advantage that doesn’t exist. He sent the taps again, a hair faster, still paired. Two short. Two short. The pressline is not a marshal. It’s a metronome you can feel in your ankle bones.

Red Sleeves caught his eye, not with a challenge, only because he had been forced to notice. Jhace touched the map‑as‑set board with the rod’s cap to point and then lowered the rod and tapped. Tap tap. The driver’s chin dipped once. He backed his wheel an inch and the jam that had been a problem turned into a problem that had edges. Edge problems you can work.

The lane breathed. Drops hit puddles in complex polyrhythms until they started to organize themselves around the tapping like iron filings finding a field. He felt the odd ache begin in his right wrist anyway, the cost of a day that had been more arm than leg. It would be a dull throb by night, the sort you soak out with heat and small patience. He kept the left hand working and let the right hang loose at his side.

Brighlee called nothing. She didn’t need to. The cadence carried the message that the window had turned and the rules of the board continued. She held up two fingers toward the bell housing for the ringer to see through the steam. The ringer answered with a nod that didn’t care if anyone else witnessed it. Work acknowledging work.

Somewhere behind the soup stall, a barked complaint rose, tried to find allies, failed, and ran out of energy against the taps. Two short. Two short. Jhace moved three paces toward the center and touched the map with his thumb at the corner where earlier he had set the wedge glyph. There, the knife‑point of the choke where flow decided whether the corridor was a lane or a fight. He tapped once against the board as a courtesy to his own method and stepped back to hear what would happen without him.

It happened. The knot by the barrow opened at the edges first. A wheel nudged into a space that had not been space thirty seconds ago. A child holding a depth of cloth recognized that the pressline was older than his inclination to dart. He waited. Two short. Two short. He moved during the spaces and the moment he chose belonged to him because the corridor had lent it.

Kylar made his loop at the front, the runner’s light step that touches every square of the problem without owning any of it. He set the little hammer’s face lightly to a tie that had sagged at the caller post and took the slack out without ceremony. The young have gifts; this one’s was reading icons by finger in the rain, correcting small things before they borrowed money from big things.

The shove from the vendor line tried to become a practice. Jhace put three beats of silence between tap sets and watched the shove fail in the quiet because there was no rhythm it could break. Then he resumed. Tap tap. Tap tap. His calves remembered last week’s climb and protested at the stance he kept that balanced weight across slick stone. He shifted, preserved his hinge, and let that be the adjustment.

Red Sleeves put his palm briefly to the ring glyph at the map’s waist height as he passed it, the unconscious confession of a man who had been angry and now wasn’t sure at what. The ring’s paint had been polished by many such hands already this week. It gleamed even under rain, a small truth in a wet world.

When the surge was ninety percent solved, which is the number that lets a place heal itself the rest of the way, Jhace lowered the rod and let the cadence live in the crowd for a few breaths without him. It did. He felt unnecessary, which is how a standard should make the person who sets it feel on a good day.

He turned to the board. The Map‑as‑Set carried the arrows, ring, wedge, and the bell marks he’d placed earlier. Today needed words without words: a clause in icon language that taught the taps to outlive the man who held the rod. He opened the kit under his cloak and kept the inner lining between the ridger and the rain.

The clause was a strip that would live at the board’s lower field: two short bars paired, then a small gap, then the pair again, with a quill‑square to show Conclave authority. He set the plate, checked the spacing with a thumb, and inked with care. The first pull sat true. The second did too. He breathed. His wrist complained when he forced the ridger to bite the legend line’s edge so the strip couldn’t be lifted clean without telling on itself. He accepted the cost and kept going.

Brighlee stepped close and tilted her oilcloth so his work stayed honest. She did not speak. Kylar kept the crowd’s edge from licking at the posting space by simply existing where he existed, a body that said the board was not a negotiable surface.

Jhace lifted the stamp and set the small Conclave square with the quill mark at the clause’s right margin. He pressed until the wood changed tone under his palm and then he released. The square sat with the comfortable authority of a pin that had been set cooler than birth. He didn’t need a witness for this. The witness would be behavior.

He tapped the rod once against the post to clear water and set cadence again, this time standing in front of the new clause strip as if it had always been there. Tap tap. Tap tap. He matched his sound to what the board now declared.

A vendor near the edge who loved exceptions more than food opened his mouth and then closed it because his feet had already obeyed. The ringer at the housing let the first of Bell Four’s notes out. They rode over the tapping rather than against it. Rain smacked the ground like small coins. The pressline held.

Jhace finished the last pass with the rod when the window’s seam had fully laid down. He let the bar rest against his shoulder and rolled his right wrist with the small grimace you allow when no one is looking, felt the tendon’s heat, and made a private note about wrapping it later with clinic salts.

He wiped a sleeve across the lower board where water had tried to pool and then stepped back to see the whole like he always did, against the habit of zooming in that had saved him earlier lives and would kill this one if he let it have the reins.

The clause lived where it should. Two short bars, a measured space, two short bars again. No text about penalties. No room for misreading. Conclave square biting the corner. The ring glyph shone where hands had made it honest. The arrows told their quiet story.

He let the crowd show him the result. It did. The center lane shifted from personal to communal; the left tightened to a competent squeeze; the right carried return flow without flinching. A runner passed with knees high to keep his shoes from taking in water, hand brushing the ring in passing, eyes already on the next choke.

Jhace set the rod down against the post, secured by a simple tie so the next person could find it without hunting. He pressed the ridger once more into the clause edge to leave that fine bite for fingers that asked if it was real.

On the board beside the arrows, the cadence clause sat newly posted, ink glossy in the rain. At his feet, puddles took the two short taps and answered. Rings spread, matched the beat, and then melted into each other as the knot opened.

# **Chapter 4: Counterfeit Placard**

Tiffani met the choke at first bell after rain, when ink tells on itself. The posting wall carried the Map‑as‑Set card Jhace had set, the cadence strip, a clinic notice square from Valaryn, and—new overnight—a Tempermarked caution plate someone had tucked in at the edge like a helpful afterthought. The edge was wrong. The board wanted to be believed too badly.

She palmed the ridger in her pocket to keep her hand honest and stepped into the spray. Floor hands drifted closer with the look people wear when they suspect a thing and hope to be wrong. A man with damp apron and a printer’s nail bed stood within ear, attention too careful. Forger nearby, she marked, and let the note sink without changing her face.

Action first: she touched the plate. A proper Tempermark gives the thumb something to argue with. This one was smooth, shine like wet glass not ink. Her fingertip skated. Flat.

Brighlee arrived at her shoulder with the ties and the Conclave stamp packet, eyes already on the strangers who had made themselves temporarily busy. Kylar hovered by the caller post, one hand on home line like a boy setting himself to be reliable if called. The lane was thin now, early carts rolling in the clean after-rain light. Good time to pull a lie.

Tiffani curled the corner of the suspect plate and checked bite. A real board resists then yields; this peeled without complaint, like a sticker sold to children. She didn’t rip it yet. Public corrections live better if you show the why first.

“Fingers,” she said, not loud, only to the three nearest vendors who’d stopped selling to watch. She held the counterfeit where it could be reached and held up the real board’s legend strip beside it. “Here, then here.” She pressed her own thumb first along the posted clause on the real board, where the ridger’s teeth had laid a fine bite yesterday. The tiny ridge met her skin with the respectful push of a truth refusing to be ignored. She slid to the fake. Smooth. “Feel the difference,” she said.

They did. First vendor’s face changed before he had words. Second closed his eyes for the test and nodded like a person listening to a song that proves itself in the first bar. Third hissed half a breath through teeth.

“Counter,” Brighlee said low, confirming the call and shifting her stance so her body made a small wall against a sudden wind. The printer’s nail bed man looked at the sky like he admired weather and had a place to be. He didn’t move away. People who had good paper rarely needed their courage to keep them still.

Tiffani kept her hand steady. The day had already taxed it with bench work at the Annex; the small muscles along her thumb complained at the fine control. She locked her wrist and let arm bone carry the work.

“Bite pattern,” she said, for the floor hands to hear. She lifted the corner again and showed the back. The adhesive spread was too clean, the fiber too thin, the ink an unconvincing black that had gone gray in the wet. She set the counterfeit down on the board’s shelf and took the ridger out where anyone could see it. “Tempermark is a ridge made with teeth,” she said. “Ink sits proud. Water beads and walks off. Counterfeits share shine and lie flat.”

She pressed the ridger into the fake’s lower edge. Teeth left a surface scratch and no ridge. The tool’s voice on the material was wrong, a papery squeak instead of the soft cut of ink and wood agreeing. She let the sound travel.

A murmuring started in the ring of watchers like a kettle before boil. Floor hands, anxious, wanted her to name the forger. She didn’t. Standards bite the object, not the man, until the board makes it necessary to write names. She set the counterfeit’s corner back down with care, then lifted it clean and tore it along the field so the false ridge could not be repurposed. The tear showed fiber that belonged to cheaper paper than any Conclave stock. She held the torn piece beside the real board one last time and felt both with two thumbs to anchor the lesson in skin.

Kylar stepped in with the cord and pinned the safe side of the posting wall so a gust wouldn’t turn the work into a sail. Brighlee laid a dry oilcloth on the shelf and opened the stamp packet. The Conclave quill square sat in the felt as if it had no opinions beyond gravity.

The printer’s nail bed man shifted his weight, then tried a small smile at the vendor next to him as if to be included in a safer story. The vendor did not offer him one.

“Posting a warning square,” Tiffani said. She took the spare card from the kit: a small field with no text, only a ridged legend strip and the quill square space. She set it to the wall where the counterfeit had been and pressed two ties through with Kylar’s help. The board accepted its new inhabitant with the same bored dignity as stone accepts rain.

She inked the quill square and pressed. The wood changed tone under her palm. She lifted and checked for clean bite. Good. She set the ridger to the legend strip and pressed a fine ridge the width of a thumbprint. The teeth hummed. Raised ink stood like a tiny levee that water could admire but not conquer. She turned the card sideways and pressed a second bite to form a small crosshatch patch in one corner—an agreed place for the public to test without destroying the card’s working surface.

Brighlee took the torn counterfeit piece and pinned it lower on the wall under the warning square, a museum of dishonesty. She set a small red thread through it, not recall slash—this wasn’t a lot code matter—but a visual cue that strangers would read as wrong. The Conclave had allowed that tiny thread for this exact purpose on Corridor cards. Not a penalty, a lesson.

“Why not fine him,” a floor hand asked, unable to keep the question in his chest. Eyes ticked toward the printer. The man’s jaw lay loose, too careful, listening for the exact shape of his name in public air.

“Because we fix the corridor first,” Tiffani said, and let the rest be obvious and later. She set the fake’s remaining piece into the burn tray Kylar produced and shook a pinch of drying sand over it to keep ink from wandering. “And because the next one watching will stop if the ridge stops them.”

She lifted the warning square’s edge and set the small Conclave serial number at bottom right. The stamp bit clean again. She did not look up to meet the steward’s eye because the steward hadn’t earned it yet today.

The printer twitched toward departure. Brighlee, casual as a rain gutter, flowed to occupy the air in front of him so his first step would be a collision. He reconsidered and stayed. The lane watched itself, which is a pleasure when it happens.

Tiffani’s hand shook once, a fine tremor from the steady‑hand taxes of the Annex and now this. She flattened it by laying it over the posted card’s ridged patch until her skin taught itself again what true felt like. Breath in, four. Out, six. The tremor listened.

Valaryn’s return‑to‑work slips fluttered in a breeze at the wall’s far end and settled. The lane carried its own business for a minute without asking anyone’s help. The steam carts hissed. The ring glyph at waist height had a new polish where people touched it to think.

Tiffani let the crowd see the sequence again in two motions: thumb to real ridge, thumb to counterfeit’s flat. She held still so they could memorize the difference by watching her skin behave. Then she tipped the burn tray and fed the torn counterfeit to a small dish flame the clinic used for sample sterilizations. Paper darkened, curled, and failed. Ink turned to a thin smoke that the rain pushed down and away.

“Posted,” Brighlee said, tying the last tie of the warning square so a wet hand could change it later if it needed moving. Kylar checked the ties in order because that’s what he does when nervous: he lets sequence save him.

The printer breathed like a man adjusting his story and stepped back half a pace. No one followed. No one needed to. The board had more authority than anyone’s mouth.

Tiffani lifted the ridger again and pressed a small second bite at the warning card’s lower edge as if she were signing with a line only fingers can read. She tucked the tool away and rolled her shoulders once. The cost would arrive later as long lines in her hand that only heat and rest could flatten. For now, the room’s nerves had calmed one notch.

A boy with a cord at his waist reached out with theatrical caution and touched the crosshatch patch, face serious. He looked up at her and she nodded. He did it again, lighter, then pressed the flat of the burned scrap’s cooled ash against his fingertip and found nothing to feel. He said nothing. He didn’t need to.

The forger‑adjacent man made himself small and slid toward the edge where crowds dissolve. He would be spoken to later, not here, not with weather watching. This moment belonged to the board.

Tiffani looked once more along the wall. Map‑as‑Set, cadence clause, clinic square, and now the warning. She stepped back until rain traced the rim of her hood and opened her palm to the two surfaces that mattered.

On the left, the posted warning square stood with its ridged patch proud, droplets beading and lifting. On the right, between her fingers, the last unburned scrap of counterfeit showed ink that lay flat and dead. She ran a fingertip across both to teach feeling to strangers who might watch. One surface pushed back like good steel under cloth. The other offered nothing at all.

# **Chapter 5: Countermark Dispute (Yard)**

Jhace stepped into the Reclaim Yard with wet shoulders and the taste of iron in his teeth. Pins and plates leaned in ranks against the wall like a graveyard of decisions. A crowd held to the rain shadow of a bent canopy, watching a single lane pin clamped to the public anvil. The pin wore a mark that had no business on a working corridor: a proud flourish stamped deep and glossy, wider than standard, the ego‑tooth of a legacy brast.

Kylar stood at the stanchion with the countermark bundle cradled in his arms as if it might try to climb away. He’d posted the small “inspection” square on the yard board, quill‑boxed and dry. Brighlee, wind‑chapped, had brought the witness ledger and was taking the weather’s measure with one eye and the crowd’s with the other.

The legacy brast himself waited with chin forward, coat open to make sure everyone saw the mark he’d carried through three owners and a flood. The set on the pin had been decent in its day, but the bite pattern had lied about what was safe. The map updates from Narrowvale demanded a different tooth, cooler, narrower, truer.

“It’s mine,” the man said, not loud, not offering it for debate. Pride spoken like a stamped letter.

Jhace didn’t answer him. He set his palm on the pin’s neck and felt the cold in its core. He watched a bead of water sit and not travel. He bent close and looked at the mark’s shoulder where the bite should have feathered to guide force. It ended in a flashy shoulder instead, a place where stress would start a hairline and then argue its way into a crack at the worst time.

He nodded once to himself and reached for the countermark slip on the board’s peg. Thin paper stiffened with wax at the edges so rain would not unmake procedure. He set it on the anvil’s flat with a coin for weight and inked the reason code: **R‑3: Legacy ego‑mark nonstandard; unsafe shoulder.** His handwriting was blocky from cold. He rubbed his knuckles and set the range box: **cooler‑than‑birth: 120–140** with a small tick mark at **131**, the number he expected to like today.

Kylar fed the small brazier at the anvil two thumb‑thick sticks and coaxed a tight bloom of heat. No white quench anywhere near. No overbind. This was a language of degrees and patience. Jhace lifted the pin with the tongs and set its mark face toward the glow, not close enough to bully the metal, just near enough to teach it to forget.

He felt the yard gather behind him. People like to watch obsolete pride bend. He didn’t mind being their instrument as long as they learned the standard and not the story. Brighlee turned the witness ledger to a dry page and tucked a strip of oilcloth under the bottom edge so the rain could pretend to be helpful elsewhere.

Jhace held a sliver of temper wax to the pin’s shoulder. The wax softened at a pace he trusted. Cooler than birth, close enough to memory to let the metal admit correction without screaming. He checked the angle twice, then a third time, and let the heat run in one clean breath along the mark. The old shine went cloudy at the edge like breath on glass.

The legacy brast stepped nearer, the audience narrowing itself to one face. “You erase me,” he said. It wasn’t a question. The words had the hunger of a man who expected to be fought.

“You lied about shoulder,” Jhace said, not unkind. “The corridor pays for that. Not today.” He took the set tool from the kit, the one with the honest Conclave tooth, cooler than birth and certified. He showed the tooth to the air and then to the crowd. “Witness,” he said to Brighlee.

She stepped to the anvil’s other side, ledger open. Kylar moved to have eyes on the pin’s back and the brazier both. The yard’s noise dimmed.

Jhace set the tooth, checked the alignment by feel rather than sight, and gave the mark three taps that were not for cadence, only for forming. The sound went into the metal and did not return confused. He lifted, wiped moisture from his eyebrow with the back of his wrist, and set again for the feather. Tap. Tap. He rotated the tool a degree and breathed in. He held it there and breathed out, ceding heat to air.

The old flourish began to fail under the new bite, not vanishing, but lowered from command to memory. The glossy shoulder dulled to a working face. The tooth seats clean when you get the range right; it sits like it has been waiting.

Kylar touched the pin’s cool side and nodded to himself, an apprentice’s private book adding a line he would carry. The crowd rustled. Someone at the back changed their mind about saying something clever and let the moment keep its adult quiet.

Jhace checked the wax again. It touched 132 and then relaxed. He set the tool for the final bite, a correction line that would refuse to accept the old shoulder’s lie if a tired crew in rain tried to repeat it later. He pressed, not with strength but with stubborn geometry. Tap. He lifted.

He let the metal talk to him through the tongs. The new mark sat honest. Not pretty, never pretty. He took the light from the brazier with his hand and moved it across the face to see how the sheen broke. The old gloss had become a soft dull like storm water settling in a pan. The bite remained like a notch in a bar you check with your fingernail each time because you respect it.

The legacy brast’s throat worked. He didn’t say sorry; a man like that spends apologies like gold and usually never has any in his pocket. He did drop his chin an inch in a human acknowledgment of a standard older than any single person. It was enough.

“Consent,” Brighlee said, that one word shaped into a ledger line. She held the slip and the oilcloth edge in a way that made it impossible to pretend you didn’t hear.

Jhace set the pin down on the anvil’s wood and took the quill. He signed in the setter line, hand cramping, letters honest enough to be recognized in bad light. He set the cooling range box again with a dot and wrote **tool: Conclave bite A‑2** on the small space for equipment. Brighlee turned the page toward the legacy brast without offering comfort.

The man looked at the crowd, which looked back the way a span looks at a person who jumps on it to see if it will hold. He took the quill. The nib made a small scratch when it found a fiber in the paper that didn’t love him. He signed under **consent**. Kylar’s mouth didn’t move but his shoulders eased three grains.

“Witness,” Brighlee said. She signed the witness line with her travel‑small hand, tight and neat, then set the ledger aside and took out the Conclave serial stamp for the slip. The square bit, inked, in the lower left where rain could not lift it. She pressed the cooling range with a tiny ridge from the ridger teeth so fingers could audit it later.

Jhace could feel fatigue now not as a particular pain but as a hollowness behind the ribs. He reached into his pocket for the small clinic salts wrapped in paper and bit off half a square. It woke his mouth with clean bitterness and dragged his focus back to the yard’s plane.

He lifted the pin again with the tongs and set it on the bench for air to finish what the brazier began. He pressed his thumb to the new bite, then to the dulled shoulder, then held up his hand so the crowd could read something in his skin: metal told truth when asked correctly.

Kylar ran to the posting board with the finished slip while Brighlee set the rejected flourish’s fragment—one thin curl of squashed gloss—into the yard’s small burn pan. She touched a match, and the curl darkened and split. No drama. Honest materials accepting end of life.

Jhace turned to the legacy brast. “That pin goes back into lane work when Chloen’s maps say its tooth belongs,” he said. The man took it without a word and without the visible shame of a person corrected in public. Maybe the correction had landed on the metal and not on his throat. That’s where it should live.

The yard exhaled the way steel sings after strain. Tools returned to hooks. The brazier shut its eye. Rain slurred into a steady line instead of a curtain.

Jhace wiped the anvil face once out of habit. He washed his hands at the barrel and let the chill make its case. Then he walked to the posting board without hurry.

The countermark slip hung where it should, ties slanting just enough to shed water. The quill square sat dark. Two names dried under the clause: his under **setter**, Brighlee’s under **witness**, the legacy brast’s under **consent**, precisely where it needed to be for the paper chain to carry weight.

He touched the lower corner where the ridger had left its fine patch for fingers and found the bite he expected. Beside it, the pin lay on the bench, old sheen dulled to a working face. In that flat light the new tooth caught nothing but purpose. The posted slip took the rain and didn’t blur; the ink held like a coin pressed into a palm and kept.

# **Chapter 6: Oil Purity Check**

Tiffani reached the South Annex before the bell and found the oil crates stacked like squat bricks against the inner wall, water beading on waxed lids. Salee had chalked a neat box on the bench: samples to test, seals to break, seals to replace. The room carried the good smell of iron racks cooling in another hall and the neutral smell of clean glass. Ration slips fluttered from a nail near the door, three already torn and gone before noon.

“Hot rack in twenty,” Salee said, not a complaint. “We clear these first.” She tapped the top crate with the back of a knuckle. The tap made a small wet thud: heavy enough, not sloshy. She lifted the lid with her thumb on the droplet icon stamped in red, then stopped. “Seal’s wrong,” she said.

Tiffani leaned in. The seal color should have been umber to match the lot year. This one leaned two shades light. Under rain, colors lie; under lamps, truth returns. She slid a lamp closer and turned the wick down so the light went gentle. The seal still looked wrong.

Pressure crept in from the corridor like heat through a thin wall. Jall Var’s voice ran a lane over, enumerating rations to anyone who needed the theatre. The Annex didn’t need it. Tiffani kept her mouth closed until usefulness was available.

Salee set a shallow dish on the bench and opened the crate anyway, steady. Vials nested in straw, glass shoulders gleaming under wax. Lot code stenciled on the inside lid in a neat hand: **L‑72 / P‑9**. She lifted one vial and rolled it in her fingers to check for bubbles that shouldn’t be there. The oil clung to the glass with a slightly fast retreat. Not proof, not yet.

“We test,” Tiffani said. She took a clean pane from the practice frame bin. It was a square of truth no one could argue with. She wiped it with a lintless cloth and set it on the bench. Salee unsealed the vial with a soft twist that told the wax was brittle. She let one drop fall from the glass lip onto the pane.

Viscosity answered in how a drop spreads and breathes. Pure oil sits proud, then relaxes like a quiet lung. Cut oil takes the edge first then hurries into itself. This drop rimmed, hesitated, then rushed an indecisive half‑inch and stopped like a person changing stories mid‑sentence.

Tiffani lifted the pane to the lamp and watched the edge move. “Fast,” she said. Salee made a mark in the ledger beside **L‑72**: a small angled slash that meant, watch.

She set the pane down and took out the oil chalks. They lived in a leather roll wrapped against moisture, three sticks: white for drag, gray for feather, black for flow. She drew a line through the drop with the white chalk. The mark should have parted smooth and left a clean channel. Instead, the chalk stuttered, then broke into a bead at the exit like water pushed where it didn’t belong.

“Again,” Salee said. She liked to see a wrong show itself twice. Tiffani repeated with the gray. The feather at the line’s edge went ragged. Salee set another mark in the ledger, then reached for a second vial from deeper in the crate so a single misfilled bottle wouldn’t call the tune. Different vial, same lot. Wax better. She let a drop fall on fresh glass. The behavior matched: a quick sulk outward, then obstinate stillness.

The door let in Jall Var and two steps of his impatience. He brought rain with him like it was a credential. “Rations are lean,” he began. His tone expected a discount for urgency. His eyes went to the racks that were not his business and then to the vials that were.

Rain slicked the Narrowvale post. Jhace climbed, glove sticking on wet rung, shoulder ache flaring and ignored. He set the backboard to the iron straps and drove the first screw until the plate rang level. Air pressed from the choke like a held breath finding a seam.

“Cordon two,” he said down into the noise.

Brighlee clipped the caller strip high and clean, the small symbol bright against fog. She didn’t decorate it with talk. Kylar walked the first three carts with two fingers and a stare, turning axles, making space without touch. A mule rattled harness. Steam lifted. The lane started to remember how to flow.

Jhace chalked the map onto the board in four lines, not a drawing, a route. Map-as-Set. He marked the exits, the cut-in, the lift that always snagged. No sermon about why it worked. He set it to work. “Caller window here,” he said, tapping the square. Brighlee flipped the cloth to Yellow and tied it loose so a knife could pull it fast.

He checked the ridger. Teeth clean. A clause plate waited in his pocket, edges blunted so it would teach without cutting. He took it out and pressed its lip to the top left, then felt for the bite. Good. He tamped once with the heel of his hand and the ridge took its seat.

Two short taps on the post with the coin cap of his rod. The sound carried. Someone in the crush answered by shifting half a step. Then someone else. Kylar’s hands showed a cart where to live. The lane’s pressure eased from shove to glide.

He kept moving. Chalk. Tap. Set. He cut a notch for the corner pinch two carts down and posted a second small plate beside the hazard icon: no words, just the wedge glyph pointing the right way. The board wasn’t a prayer. It was a set of actions people could make in time.

Hoarseness scratched at his throat. He didn’t drink. He steadied his hand and placed the Conclave clause where it would be seen without being stared at, lower right. He seated the Tempermark die and put weight behind it. Ring, bite, hold. He lifted the stamp and checked the ridge with the side of his thumb. Honest.

A call flared near the tea stalls and tried to start trouble in the wrong lane. Jhace didn’t look over. Two taps again, same spacing, and Kylar slid his palm along a cart’s shaft like he was balancing air; the trouble bent along the posted line and became nothing. A wheel creaked, then found quiet.

“Board reads,” Jhace said.

Brighlee had the pen already writing. Functional. She posted the small slip with the clause number and stepped back. The crowd, without quite thinking, matched her distance.

Jhace knelt on the midstep and set a small wedge into the pavement, low and flat, where barrows always drifted. One tap. The wedge talked to the wheel before the wheel could pretend not to hear. He checked the notch with his thumb. Set.

The choke widened in the way a throat widens when pain loosens. He listened for ring under stone. Even. No chatter. The ear ring from earlier faded to a dull, acceptable hum.

Costs: glove sticks; hoarse caller. He didn’t name them out loud. He kept the work moving.

A man in red sleeves tried to invent a lane. Kylar pointed once, and the man’s idea fell apart without needing its pride killed. Brighlee flipped the caller back to Blue, ticked a half-knot into the tie, and clipped a single narrow slip under the larger clause: window closed at Bell. Posted.

Jhace wiped rain from the clause plate with the back of his wrist and gave it one more seat. He checked the ridge again, thumb across. He didn’t love the ink’s shine. He wanted the matte that meant it would read in bad light. He took a breath, set the stamp again beside the first mark, not required, but present for people who count. Bite. Done.

He held still long enough to let the lane tell him if it had learned. Pressure at the choke went from thrust to glide. He stepped back.

“Done,” he told Brighlee.

She closed her book without flourish.

Kylar drifted back in with a spare rope coil, not needed anymore. He stood at the edge of the board and read it once like a person memorizing a route he would need later in a different city.

Jhace tipped his chin toward the square. “There. The clause is that this is the clause.” He didn’t wait for agreement. He rubbed the plate’s lower corner with his thumb to see if the ridge held under rain and flesh. It did.

Brighlee unrolled a fresh caller strip and slipped it over the post so the next crew would know where to stand and when. Kylar moved through the first carts like he had been born a knot-opener, touching nothing, only pointing. A mule snorted. A wheel creaked. Steam lifted. The corridor’s pitch lowered.

Jhace stepped away, hoarseness a scratch that wouldn’t drink water until the work was done. He let the lane show him it had learned. It had. The choke’s push faded to glide.

He set the stamp one last time, small bite beside the first, then ran his own thumb along the Tempermark’s ridge where the legend lived. Skin found raised line by instinct, not sight.

A woman with a market cart did the same, tentative, then sure. Her thumb found the ridge. The cart eased forward without a shouted order.

# Chapter 2

Tiffani lowered the lamps until the glass told the truth and chalked the wedge glyph on the board angled to the corner work. The metronome bell ticked two beats soft. “Wedge before veil,” she said, voice clean the way you speak beside reactive material. “Corners, three-count. Dead panes first.” She hooked the first pane and traced its route with a raw wedge plate, letting the edge print the turn into her wrist.

Hot racks arrived off schedule, bars still giving heat. Steam lifted when rain off coats met metal. Salee took the oil shelf with a wrist tap that said touch nothing you can’t put back. Kylar stood quiet at the edge with ties he didn’t need. Valaryn laid return-to-work slips where eyes could read and hands could fail to touch.

“The wedge points; the tool speaks; they must agree,” Tiffani said. She seated a corner and pressed until the pane stopped lying. The bell ticked twice. The room learned to hold its breath and then to let it go.

A porter at the clinic door fumbled the brace. The sill had a proud point that had taught itself to rise under years of wet and yesterday’s surge. Tiffani knelt to sight along timber and stone. “Left brace a thumb higher. Right, feather. Don’t drag.” Tap tap. The porter’s wrapped hand shook, then steadied when his palm found the ring again. The door moved through its last third; the creak dropped from raw to shallow scrape. Salee touched oil to the pin and wiped the extra so grit wouldn’t take the gift back later.

Inside, a trainee tried to help by pulling. Helping is sabotage with manners. Tiffani raised a palm in the hinge gap. “Outside binds. Inside waits.” The girl stitched her hands into her belt. They held the door half-open for a count; heat went out, iron cooled a degree. Tap tap. Through the full motion now. The timber let go a long, thrummed sigh and came to seat in silence.

Jhace appeared at the arch’s edge, water sliding from his hem to the stone where it belonged. He set one hand on the home ring and steadied the brace with the other without talk. Their eyes met for a beat. The spark she had shelved put a warm bead in her throat and slid lower. She kept it; she kept working.

“Card,” she said. Salee had the kit open. The old door drill card on the inner wall showed wedge small, ring at waist height, three bars for count, oil droplet, clinic ⭕ in the corner. It lacked the bind clause the room had just learned with its shoulders.

Tiffani pulled a blank, inked the wedge larger, and moved it upstream of the ring to teach set corner first even at a hinge. Under it she laid a simple bind: a bar crossing a ring, two small hands as half-circles. **Micro-Bind: one unit, caller pairs** lived in icon plates, not text. She stamped the clinic ⭕ lower right and the small Conclave quill square bottom left. She pressed the ridger across the legend so the board would tell on itself if anyone tried to lift it. Thumb across: bite.

Jall Var’s mouth shaped a flourish request near the lower margin. She fed it nothing. “East gate,” she said, and walked there because the wedge angle had been trying to lie. She corrected it. The room felt the correction like a knuckle cracked clean; the next cart took the turn without shriek. Sound fell from the lane like a dropped habit.

“Board plates match,” Chloen said, setting three overlays on the shelf in a row. Arrow tooth here, chalk there. “West, Center, East: the same story.” Tiffani stood back until all three fit in her head, eyes blur, then sharp. The maps spoke one language. People began to behave like citizens of a single corridor instead of ambassadors from their own weather. She let herself feel the cost finally: grit at the corners of her eyes, not grit at all, only muscle asking to rest. She put a thumb to Center’s ridged patch and took its small stubbornness back into her hand.

“Done,” she told the boards.

Brighlee tied a small caller note under each map, icon for **one hand on home** beside a bell mark for night crews. Kylar rubbed the ring glyph under West with his whole palm and grinned at no one when the paint shone more honest.

Tiffani pressed the quill square on East one last time and tied the lower left corner again because corners are where lies start. The bell down-lane gave its seam. Crews paused and then chose lanes the maps had already given them. Pitch lowered. Steam drew polite ribbons. The clerk trio that had tried to be important wandered off to inspect somewhere else.

She slid the kit under her coat. Two fingertips to Center’s ridged patch. Those fingertips to the temple. The world sharpened a fraction. She breathed once and let the corridor answer if it had learned.

On the wall, three Map-as-Set boards hung at West, Center, and East, identical: arrows meeting at a tooth, ring at waist height, wedge set honest, bell pairs along the bottom, quill squares biting dark. At each caller post, a small square sat at the same height, marked and ready. In the slick light, the three maps read like one drafted sentence, and a market cart’s thumb found the ridge and moved.

# Chapter 3

Rain turned Narrowvale’s cobbles to iron plates and pushed steam low along the shins. The choke was already fraying: carts angling for advantage, runners trying to split the same lane, voices building pressure for no reason except habit. Jhace felt it all through the caller post, palm flat to the old wood. The grain carried the lane’s trouble back into his bones, granular, no flow.

He lifted the short bar. Tempered rod. Coin-thick cap. Work, not theater.

Two short taps.

Edges found ears. The second proved the first wasn’t stray. He breathed to keep the spacing honest and did it again. Two short. The weather needled his sleeve and the cap gave back a clean ring off the stone face. Brighlee flipped the caller square from Blue to Yellow and tied fast for quick cut if the wind asked. Kylar drifted the left boundary, patient hand on a red-sleeved vendor’s axle, nudging him back to the heavy lane like it was always his idea.

Two short taps.

A shove rose near the steam carts and shook the middle like a wire. Jhace didn’t look. He counted heads downstream: four, then six, moving in the cadence. No orders. Just timing. The board already told them what. He gave them when. He kept the bar in his left to save the right for fine work.

The first knot loosened where runners had tried to share space with a barrow. He shifted the beat one rib wider and let the sound bounce and return corrected. The lane’s breath caught, then evened. “Yellow holds,” Brighlee said, voice flat and functional from the post. Her pencil worked in the wet, steady.

The bell rope twitched down the corridor. Window shift coming. He set the bar under his arm and shouldered to the center seam, a step without apology. Two short taps. Pause. Two. The pause mattered. Crowds hate blank. He gave them a thin one that would hold.

A girl with oil tins misjudged a shoulder. She swallowed the pain and kept her feet. He kept his eyes on distant heads. Reaction travels. He would rather shape it than chase it.

Kylar’s cord line kissed the ground in quick loops. He didn’t try to make a fence out of string. He made suggestions. Runners read suggestions faster than speech. Red Sleeves tried to angle again and met the cord lightly on his shin. He looked for a human to quarrel with and found none. He settled.

Rain thickened. The lane answered.

Two short taps.

At the far curb a boy popped a kettle lid for steam and took the corner of the world with it. Fog rolled. Sound pressed flatter. Jhace felt his rib tempo get tempted and denied it. He set his boot against the post and let the ring run through tendon rather than skull. Ear ring rose like a weather front and sat there, a cost he’d pay later. Not now.

A cart wheel slid on iron wet. He changed nothing. Panic rewards itself. The driver corrected to the cadence and only then heard his own breath. Jhace shifted the bar to mark a side-pulse for foot traffic. Two short, then a half-beat ghost for walkers. Heads turned like birds. The wire-hum of argument at the edge fell a measure.

He palmed his glove to wipe rain. The leather stuck to his knuckles. Glove sticks, noted. He didn’t look at the ache blooming in the shoulder where yesterday’s brace had asked more than a day’s worth. Shoulder ache, noted. The work paid attention to those who didn’t ask it to pretend.

Yellow bought him half a Bell. Yellow was all he wanted.

“Clause?” Brighlee asked without lifting her eyes. The pencil did its own work.

“Ridge the caller, not the crowd,” he said. She didn’t need the why. He wasn’t sermonizing. He was naming parts. She set her ridger teeth through waxed strip and wrote what would be posted if it held.

Two short taps.

A row of kettles chose to squeal at the same note. Tuning didn’t matter. What mattered was borders. He gave the edge back to them with the bar. She wrote: Pressline cadence in effect. Two-short marks. Obey Yellow. Brace taps only in Bells Two and Three. If it isn’t on the board, it didn’t happen. She underlined the Tempermark space and left it blank. He had to earn ink.

A second knot built at the mouth where the street narrowed around a bolted drain. He tracked the cause: a boy avoiding an ankle-high puddle, forcing a woman to step into the heavy lane, forcing a cart to lose angle, forcing four shoulders to close. All of it small and enough. He changed the measure to two short, long breath, two. The breath gave the puddle room to be water instead of law. The knot unwound with the laziness of a salvaged thread.

He set his palm to the post again. Tone came back even. The lane had stopped insulting itself. He took a shorter stance. Test whether it was him or the board. He halved the volume, then halved it again. The lane carried itself. That’s what you wanted.

A body crashed into his shoulder. Red Sleeves. He’d found a human after all and didn’t like the absence of quarrel. Jhace didn’t step back. The bar remained a tool, not a weapon. He didn’t look at the eyes. He looked at the axle. Two short taps, and his ribs stayed ruler-straight. Red Sleeves’ anger slid off the edges of timing. The man retreated because there was nowhere for his noise to live.

Kylar’s coil shortened. He’d learned something today. Good.

Brighlee lifted a fresh strip from the kit and added one line beneath the clause: Caller square changes set cadence. Watch the cloth. She kept it functional, not clever. When she clipped it for a test fit to the lower board, the ink bled but held.

Bell Four’s face came up. The rope rang, light and fast along the corridor. A shiver went through the whole shape of the choke. Jhace narrowed the spacing and moved two paces toward the bridge side without breaking count. He did not fight the bell. He escorted it. Two short taps. The sound laid a rail for the new flow to choose. It chose.

A runner skated, caught himself on a post, swore until he saw the Yellow and read his own mouth shut. The square isn’t a suggestion. It’s a contract.

The ear ring finally took its tax. A low thrumming behind his right eye made the bar’s cap look brighter than it should. He swapped hands for a few beats to spare the burn he’d hidden with wrap. Blister bite woke under the glove. He could admire pain later. Now he counted and listened.

Two short taps.

The choke thinned from crush to queue. The fog lifted to knee height. The puddle at the drain stopped bossing the lane. Red Sleeves became red sleeves again.

Brighlee stood with her pencil paused. “Status?” she said, five letters of mercy. He put the barhead to the post and gave three quick, quiet kisses of brass to wood, the board’s home code for Steady under new face. She wrote it, plain.

Kylar let the cord lie slack and then reeled it in, loops flat, not tangled. He’d make a decent clerk someday if the world didn’t eat him first.

Jhace stopped tapping.

The lane kept moving.

He gave it ten counts to prove it would live without him. It did. He leaned the bar against the post and flexed the web of his hand. The glove’s seam had raised a ridge. He pressed it flat with his thumb until the sting settled into something he would remember later.

“Post it,” he said.

Brighlee pulled the clause sheet and slid it into the glass sleeve on the caller board. She wiped the pane with the heel of her hand and stamped the Tempermark into the wax with a single, level bite. Ink haloed and went still. Kylar tied off the Yellow square at half-staff for the next team’s eyes.

Jhace walked the first twenty paces downstream, not as a guard, only to test. Foot traffic matched the two-short in their own heads now, shoulders falling into the same slow angle of mercy people rediscover when the lane reminds them it’s shared. He checked the tricky drain one more time. The water had taken a longer path. Good.

He came back to the post and let his shoulders drop, a weight setting into the bracket cut for it. Hoarse caller burned the top of his throat. He drank from the bottle and tasted glass.

Brighlee wrote the last lines without adornment: Pressline cadence posted at Narrowvale choke, Bell Two to Four seam. Clerk Brighlee; Setter Jhace. Witness on observation only. She clipped the copy to the backer board and didn’t add a flourish where none was due.

Kylar raised a hand like a question and put it down without asking. He didn’t need to. He’d seen the shape and kept it.

“Costs?” Brighlee asked.

“Ear ring. Glove sticks. Shoulder ache,” Jhace said. “Call it posted.”

She nodded once, said nothing, and finished with the stamp.

On the board, the new cadence card sat dead center under the Yellow square. Rain beaded on the glass, then ran to the bottom rail, leaving the pane a dull matte with the print clean inside. The square held color in the wet and didn’t move.

# Chapter 4

Bell Eight rolled slow under Alderline, sound caught in steam and stone. The lower conduits ran hot enough to lift skin off paint. Jhace reached the south vent and set his palm to the duct wall. Heat bit through glove. Live and angry. He didn’t say it out loud.

Tiffani stood where the face had buckled, veil cloth dark to the elbow. “Thermal vein tore the splice,” she said. “If it finds the main, we blow the market.”

Brighlee arrived with the board, breath short in the heat. “Work order one-one-five-four. Heat Splice under Alderline. Setter Jhace brast, Glass Tiffani veld. Local hold on foot.” She Tempermarked the slip and stepped back, pen already working.

“Chain it,” Tiffani said.

Jhace clipped his tether to the wall ring. One hand on home. He drew two short wedges from his pouch and drove the first into the duct mouth’s upper seam, the second two spans below. Pitch rose clean. The duct’s noise changed from deep to a tight, held breath.

“Trace wet,” she said.

He thumbed the bladder and set the copper. First line hissed on contact, vapor curling. He kept shoulder over hand and fed the split until white cooled to gray. Tiffani moved the Lume closer, Shade four, then six. “Keep the bead thick.”

He ran a second line parallel, two fingers apart, and held for half-beat Slake. Breath slow. The vein’s pulse dropped.

“Still wants a mouth,” she said.

He gave it one: a short feeder up to the vent slit. The air drew once, then quieted. Heat under his palm softened. She brushed his wrist, not comfort, calibration, and his pulse matched the duct.

“Veil,” Tiffani said.

He stepped aside. She warmed a Shade 2 in her hands and pressed it over the splice. Tap tap tap in sevens. Steam rose and hung, then thinned. The seam went dark and held.

“Window at Nine,” she said for Brighlee’s ink. “Witness after.”

Jhace looked at his glove. Leather gone glossy, almost translucent. Burn through. “Palm later,” he said.

“Clinic if it lifts,” she said, eyes on the seam.

He grunted and stayed on the ring.

The duct gave one deep crack and went still. Tiffani listened with her teeth. “Field even.”

“Pressure five-finger drop,” he said, close enough to feel it. They waited half a count. Silence held the way new fixes lie. It kept holding.

“Test,” she said.

He signaled up the tunnel. Valves opened. Air shouldered through, caught the new splice and turned as if that had always been the plan. Response came back level and measurable. Set.

Mel Quarr came down the shaft with condensation on her lenses. She didn’t waste breath. She crouched, set one ear to the Veil, stayed until both of them forgot to inhale. “Stable,” she said. “Hot vein cooled. No echo. You cleared it before breach.”

“Witness?” Tiffani asked.

“Witness,” Mel said. “Post it.”

Brighlee clipped the slip to the board and pressed the blue seal until the wax took. Ink edged dark and stayed legible in the wet.

“Cost?” Tiffani said.

Jhace flexed. Blister bite starting at the web. “Palm blister. Glove dead. Fray band edge singed.”

“Policy?”

“Clinic if it lifts.”

She dug in her bag and slapped a fresh pair of gloves into his hand. “Use these next time.”

“This city’s always planning a next time,” he said.

They packed without hurry. Fog from the vent mixed with cooler air and turned to a clean smell of burnt dust and new rain. The Veil face stayed matte. No twitch. He ran a thumb along the seam’s edge and found nothing to argue with.

They climbed to street. Alderline’s air showed no heat shimmer now, only flat gray light. He shouldered the dead gloves and let his hand rest a second in the new leather until skin remembered cool.

“If it holds through melt, we buy a season,” Tiffani said, eyes on the run no one on the surface could see.

“One more day that works,” he said.

“Don’t get poetic.”

He didn’t.

They walked the length of the posting wall to make sure the crowd read what needed reading. Brighlee finished her line, snapped the ledger shut, and waited with her palm against the glass for the seal’s heat to settle.

“Filed,” she said.

Jhace stepped back to see the whole. The droplet board sat square. The blue seal lived at lower right. The wax ridge bit under his thumb the way it should. Rain beaded on the pane, slid, and left the print clean inside. The duct face below stayed matte and quiet. The board told a simple story: local hold cleared at Bell Nine, Alderline set.

He let his shoulders drop and left his thumb on the seal a beat longer than necessary, the ridged edge cool and sure.

She reset the corner with care, lifted it clean, and tore along the field so the false ridge couldn’t be repurposed. The tear showed cheap fiber no Conclave stock would own. She held torn and true beside each other one last time and felt both with two thumbs to anchor the lesson in skin.

Kylar stepped in with cord, pinning the safe side of the wall so a gust wouldn’t make a sail of the work. Brighlee laid a dry cloth on the shelf and opened the stamp packet. The Conclave quill square sat in its felt like it had no opinions beyond gravity.

The printer’s nail bed man shifted weight, tried a small smile at a vendor as if to be included in a safer story. The vendor didn’t offer one.

“Posting a warning square,” Tiffani said. She pulled a spare: small field, ridged legend strip space, quill square space, no text. She set it where the counterfeit had lived and ran two ties through while Kylar held tension. The board accepted the new plate with the bored dignity of stone accepting rain.

She inked the quill square and pressed. Wood changed tone under her palm. Lift, check bite. Good. She set the ridger on the legend strip and pressed a fine ridge the width of a thumbprint. Teeth hummed. Raised ink stood like a tiny levee that water admires and walks off. She turned the card and pressed a second bite to crosshatch one corner: an agreed place for the public to test without ruining the field.

Brighlee pinned a torn piece of the counterfeit under the warning square, a small museum of dishonesty. She set a red thread through it, not recall slash—no lot code here—but a cue strangers read as wrong. The Conclave had allowed that thread on Corridor cards for exactly this: not penalty, lesson.

“Why not fine him,” a floor hand asked, eyes ticked toward the printer. The man’s jaw stayed loose, listening for the shape of his name in public air.

“Because we fix the corridor first,” Tiffani said, and let the rest come later. She set the fake’s remaining piece in the burn tray Kylar produced and salted it with drying sand so ink wouldn’t wander. “And because the next one watching will stop if the ridge stops them.”

She lifted the warning square’s edge and set the Conclave serial bottom right. Stamp bit clean again. She did not look up for the steward’s eye. He hadn’t earned it yet today.

The printer twitched toward departure. Brighlee, casual as a gutter, flowed to occupy the air in front of him so his first step would be collision. He reconsidered. The lane watched itself. That is a pleasure when it happens.

Her hand shook once, a fine tremor from bench steadies and this. She flattened it by laying her palm on the warning card’s crosshatch until skin re-learned true. Breath in four. Out six. Tremor listened. Ear ring from the Annex sat low, tolerable.

Valaryn’s return-to-work slips fluttered at the wall’s far end and settled. The lane carried itself for a minute without help. Steam carts hissed. The ring glyph at waist height wore new polish where people touched it to think.

She showed the sequence a final time: thumb to true ridge, thumb to the counterfeit’s flat scrap. Let strangers learn by watching skin behave. Then she tipped the burn tray and fed the torn counterfeit to the clinic’s dish flame. Paper darkened, curled, failed. Ink smoked thin and rain pressed it down.

“Posted,” Brighlee said, tying the warning square so a wet hand could move it later if it needed moving. Kylar checked ties in order because sequence saves him when nerves rise.

The printer adjusted his story and stepped back half a pace. No one followed. The board had more authority than any mouth.

Tiffani pressed a small second bite at the warning card’s lower edge, a signature only fingers read. She rolled her shoulders once. Cost would arrive later as a long line in the hand; heat and rest would flatten it.

A boy with cord at his waist reached out with theatrical caution and touched the crosshatch patch, face serious. He looked up. She nodded. He did it again, lighter, then pressed the cooled ash of the burned scrap to his fingertip and found nothing to feel. He said nothing. He didn’t need to.

The forger-adjacent man made himself small and slid toward the edge where crowds dissolve. He would be spoken to later, not here. This belonged to the board.

Tiffani looked once more along the wall. Map-as-Set. Cadence clause. Clinic square. And now the warning.

She stepped back until rain traced the rim of her hood and opened her palm to the two surfaces that mattered. On the left, the warning square’s ridged patch stood proud, droplets beading and lifting. On the right, between her fingers, the last unburned scrap showed ink that lay flat and dead. Her fingertip crossed both. One surface pushed back like good steel under cloth. The other offered nothing at all.

### Chapter 5 — Tightened (FINAL)
Jhace took the Yard by the anvil line, oil and rain in the air, the board crowded with last-shift claims and one ugly dispute card clipped crooked. The man waiting at the post held his shoulder like it belonged to someone else. Brighlee stood functional at the ledger. Kylar hovered by the brazier with the salt bag. The pin itself, black with old touch, showed a faint X—a birth bite, shallow and proud.

“Countermark dispute,” Brighlee said, temper flat. She clipped the slip under the board edge and left the square open.

Action, then talk. Jhace set his glove, touched the pin’s collar, and felt the thread chatter under skin the way a lie does when it expects to win. He braced on the home ring set into the Yard post. One hand on home. His hammer rode his palm in a way his burn didn’t love.

The claimant stepped close enough to smell tin and wet leather. “Legacy brast,” he said. “I was born into this yard. My pin stands.”

Jhace didn’t answer. He checked the shoulder set at the collar, the bite too shallow for weight. “Who pinned you?”

“Foreman. Old pin. Old ink.”

“Old wrong,” Jhace said, and let his ear find the pin’s tone as he tapped. The note came back too bright. He looked past the man to the ring of hands already watching. Work had paused just enough to hear.

“Let him keep it,” the claimant said, raising his chin toward the old X like it owed him courtesy.

“We match the board,” Tiffani said from the rail, voice level. She’d taken the Lume just long enough to read the serials on the wall and then put it away; this was iron, not glass. A simple case: the Yard had posted new coupling rules last Bell, the Conclave had authenticated them, and this collar didn’t meet depth. If it failed under load, it would be the city’s bones on the floor, not the man’s pride.

Jhace eased the wrench over the shoulder and took half a breath. First bite needed to open the old mark without tearing the collar. Thread, not force. He set the tool and turned until the pitch went from scrape to low. “Shh,” he told the metal, like telling a line to calm. His glove stuck; he changed grip.

The man’s mouth went tight. “You erase me.”

“Not erasing,” Jhace said. “Leveling.” He nodded at Brighlee. “Record the claim.”

“Filed,” she said, ink slow, no commentary.

Jhace set the countermark tool, cooler than birth. He let the hammer’s weight fall a small stroke, shoulder carrying load so the wrist didn’t shake. Tap. The pin listened and gave a fraction. Ear ring. Tap. He worked the bite until the old X lost its pride and became groove. He stopped to feel heat with the back of a knuckle. Warm, not wrong.

“Cool,” he said. Kylar uncapped the bag and poured a clean pinch of salt into the collar. Steam kissed his skin and left a board-soap smell where tin had lived. The fine tremor in Jhace’s hand eased long enough to let him work clean.

He turned the wrench. The thread took then held, a quiet yes traveling into his shoulder. He made the second bite, a cross that would say opened and re-set to anyone with a thumb and a history. The claimant leaned in, jaw clenched. Tiffani didn’t move.

“Countermark reads deeper than birth,” Jhace said for the ring as much as the man. “Any yard can claim you now. Any load can trust this collar.”

The man’s eyes never left the iron. “You erase me.”

“We level you,” Jhace said. “You can work anywhere grip.”

Cost announced itself in Jhace’s shoulder as the hammer sent a last shock up bone. He shifted his stance to keep the line in his elbow honest. He checked the thread again and listened. No chatter. He set the final X mark small, proof the pin had been opened and married again. The hammer said certain, not pretty. The quarrel had nowhere else to go.

Brighlee moved without drama, clipped the countermark slip beside the Tempermark on the Yard board, and inked the serial in the square. “Serial,” she said for the record. “One-three-one.” Ink caught and dulled. She pressed the Conclave seal until the wax sank its teeth.

Kylar took the cooled grain of salt left in his palm and dropped it on the post shelf, a habit like a nod. The claimant breathed out at last, ribs softening. He didn’t say thank you. He didn’t have to.

“Test,” Jhace said. He set his thumb to the new crossbite and felt the ridge push back against skin, as honest as steel under cloth. He stepped aside so the yard hands could feel it too: their thumbs found the bite, then the board’s legend. Same depth, same truth. Shoulders dropped around the circle. Someone coughed up a hoarse “Good.”

“Costs,” Tiffani said quietly.

“Shoulder ache. Glove sticks.” He flexed the burned web; the skin tugged but held. “Clinic if it splits.”

Brighlee finished her line, wiped the stamp clean, and tapped the board with the side of her knuckle. “Posted.”

The Yard returned to itself in small sounds: chain links settling, a cart brake easing, rain ticking off the awning. Jhace stood one more count with his thumb on the crossbite to remind his nerves what correct felt like.

He stepped back into the wet, and the chapter closed itself on the board: the countermark slip pinned beside the Tempermark, serial 131 drying true, and a lace of salt crystals bright against the darkened pin.

# Chapter 6: Oil Purity Check

Tiffani reached the South Annex before the bell, rain ticking off the lintel into a shallow pan. Oil crates sat like squat bricks along the inner wall, water beading on waxed lids. Salee had chalked a neat box on the bench: samples to test, seals to break, seals to replace. The room carried the good metal-cool of racks somewhere else and the neutral smell of clean glass. Ration slips fluttered from a nail near the door, three already torn and gone.

“Hot rack in twenty,” Salee said, not a complaint. “We clear these first.” She tapped the top crate with a knuckle. The sound came back a small wet thud, heavy enough. She thumbed the droplet icon on the lid, then paused. “Seal’s off.”

Action first. Tiffani slid a lamp closer and choked it down until the glass told the truth. Umber year marks should read dull-brown under low flame; this lid leaned two shades light. Under rain, colors lie; under lamp, they confess. Still wrong. She eased the string, lifted the lid, and set it on its side to shed water where it couldn’t lie to anyone later.

Salee set three vials on the bench: two from **L-72**, one from **L-73**. Tiffani rolled one at a time under the lamp and watched the line around each shoulder. “Seal color isn’t the only liar,” Salee said, soft.

Tiffani breathed out once through her nose. “Lot mark?”

“L-72,” Salee said. “Pass blank. No clinic note.”

“Chart,” she said.

A second trainee tried to be useful by sorting seals by shade, lifting lids with unnecessary ceremony. Tiffani nudged the lamp wick down and let the room learn by temperature: under gentler light, the wrong umbers misbehaved worse. Sorting by eye became sorting by outcome. Hands retracted.

Kylar appeared in the doorway and stayed there, reading the board like a person who wants to be useful and knows better than to talk. He took three ration slips out of his pocket, posted them above the chart in a neat V so feet would choose their path without noise. Salee approved with her chin.

“Teach them to feel,” Tiffani said, half to herself. She pressed the ridger across the chart’s lower left field, one thumb-width patch labeled **test here**. Teeth took ink into a low ridge a wet hand could read. She added a small crosshatch beside it to give strangers a sanctioned place to learn, then bit the upper right corner a second time so a thief trying to lift the board would have to lie to texture in two directions.

“Seal it?” Salee asked.

“After burn.” Tiffani lifted the worst L-72 vial, tilted it over the dish, and let three drops go to fire. The oil guttered, then failed to scent the room at all. Proper base carries a note; this carried nothing. She set the vial into the burn dish mouth so no hand would reach for shine later, and the flame traced the shoulder clean, leaving the glass cool and empty. Habit breaker posted in heat, not words.

“Lot and pass at the Annex only,” Salee said, writing it on a small addendum square and tacking it under the chart. “Call outliers to my ledger.” She initialed with tidy care that made paper look competent.

Tiffani lifted a fresh L-73 to the lamp. The line took and held clean. She posted **L-73 — pass** with a straight downstroke, then stepped back to let the room answer if it had learned. Trainees looked at the board before asking out loud. No hands drifted toward the red-slashed crate. Salee’s ledger squared up with the shelf’s new shape.

A messenger stuck a head in the door. “Market wants Annex rations by Bell.” He saw the chart and left without finishing the lecture.

Tiffani wrote **Re-seal: L-72 lids on hold** and pointed to the crate. Kylar tied the lids down with a simple cross that refused casual fingers and yielded to a purposeful hand. He moved to the door and stood where his shadow would send most people around him instead of through.

She felt the cost in her hand as a fine flutter along the thumb where the rod had asked too much yesterday. She laid her palm flat on the chart’s test patch until nerves matched the ridge and quieted. Breath in four, out six. Tremor listened.

Salee looked at the clock face like it owed her a fair count and set the hot rack timer beside the chart. “We’ve got eighteen,” she said. “Plenty if the market doesn’t improvise.”

Tiffani pulled the Conclave stamp, realigned it over the quill square she had just pressed, and seated it lightly again so any lift would show ghost-misalignment. She bit a second **test here** patch lower left in case a wet hand ruined the first. Corners are where lies start. She pressed call marks on three small index squares and clipped them: **L-72 hold**, **L-73 pass**, **return-to-work slips** with the bell glyph so night crews would read without thinking.

The room steadied at a low hum. Outside, a bell note bent and straightened. She wiped the pane, rolled the chalks, and slid them back into leather. The rejected vial in the burn dish cooled to clarity that meant nothing.

“Chart reads,” Salee said finally. She posted ration slips into the V Kylar had set, turning the hallway into a paper corridor that behaved like a physical one. “Lot lives in the open. We’re not short. We’re honest.”

Jall Var’s voice drifted near and faded; even he knew when paper already had the room.

Tiffani took the red thread from her pocket and stitched a small diagonal through the **L-72** mark on the crate’s lid, then tied the ends under the lip so the thread would speak even when tired eyes didn’t. She wrote **internal hold pending three marks** into Salee’s ledger. She would not post a public recall from a bench.

The lamp ticked as the wick ate air. She set two vials side by side on the bench ledge where hands can be taught: L-73 left, L-72 right. She rolled them together under the same light. One line looped smooth around shoulder and joined itself. The other hesitated, flattened a breath early, and left a faint ring that memory would learn to catch in a day.

She leaned her shoulder into cool stone for three breaths. Heat left her hands and went into the wall where it could be someone else’s problem. Then she stepped back to make the last small fix.

With the ridger, she pressed a second crosshatch bite into the chart’s **test here** square, then ran her thumb across both patches to leave oil-slick memory on the high spots. She stamped the Conclave quill again, aligning edges to show any thief’s lift. Ink darkened and sat still.

The Annex felt correct. Stock updated. Chart posted. Crate red-slashed. She lifted a clear vial from **L-73** and held it at the lamp. The liquid inside drew a clean loop around the shoulder. Beside it on the lid of **L-72**, the red-slashed square flagged the wrong seal without a speech. Two vials, one obedient and dull, one refusing to be handled. The chart sat between them, quill square dark, ridged patch proud under a thumb.
