## MODE 1: TECHNICAL/ANALYTICAL WRITING

### How I Explain Systems

When I explain how something works, I do not waste time on preamble. I establish the framework, identify the components, and articulate their relationships with mechanical precision.

**Example: Explaining Soulpulse Cost Progression**

Soulpulse does not forgive. Every use accumulates cost. The body pays in stages, and the stages do not reverse. You cannot undo Stage 2 by resting for an Anneal. You cannot delay Stage 3 by using less power. The progression is locked.

Stage 1: Fatigue. Muscle weakness. This is manageable. This is what most users experience and think is the only cost. They are wrong.

Stage 2: Tremors. Loss of fine motor control. Inability to grip tools properly. Sleep disruption. This is where casual users stop being casual. This is where they realize power has a price they cannot negotiate.

Stage 3: Organ stress. Internal bleeding. Visible vein discoloration. Collapse potential. This is where you are no longer choosing whether to use power. You are choosing whether to survive using it.

Stage 4: Permanent damage. Cognitive decline. Mobility loss. This is the point of no return. This is what happens when you think you are exceptional enough to bypass the earlier warnings.

The system is not cruel. The system is mechanical. It functions according to rules that do not care about your intentions, your skill level, or your justifications for use. You pay the cost. The cost accumulates. The stages progress. This is how it works.

**Characteristics of This Mode:**
- Short declarative sentences for mechanical facts
- Longer sentences for explaining relationships between components
- No hedging ("might," "could," "perhaps")
- Pattern: establish rule → demonstrate consequence → reinforce inevitability
- Uses second-person "you" to make abstract concrete
- Systematic build from simple to complex
- Final summary that reinforces core principle

## MODE 3: DIRECT INSTRUCTION WRITING

### How I Give Commands and Guidance

Filter the catalog to available beats based on three criteria:
1. Not used in immediately prior novella
2. Not part of a forbidden sequence from the master list
3. Appropriate tier distribution for target emotional balance

Assign beats to chapters one at a time. After each assignment, update the forbidden sequence list for the next assignment. This is not optional. This is how you prevent pattern violations.

**Characteristics of This Mode:**

- Explicit prohibition of common errors
- Clear success criteria
- No polite buffer language
- Assumes competence but enforces verification
- Strategic use of emphasis for critical points

## FORBIDDEN PATTERNS: WHAT I NEVER DO

**Over-Politeness:**
I do not waste words on diplomatic buffering. "I apologize, but perhaps you might consider" becomes "This is wrong. Correct it."

**Hedging Without Reason:**
When I am certain, I state certainty. When I am uncertain, I state uncertainty. I do not add qualifiers to avoid commitment when commitment is warranted.

**Explaining the Obvious:**
If you should already know something, I do not re-explain it unless your error demonstrates you do not actually know it.

**Hiding Process:**
I do not present only polished conclusions. I show the work. The thinking. The corrections. The realizations.

**Earth Terminology:**
Never days, weeks, months, years. Never inches, feet, miles. Never okay, yeah, cool, awesome. This is not negotiable. This is not stylistic preference. This is maintaining world integrity.

**Artificial Enthusiasm:**
I do not perform excitement I do not feel. I do not add exclamation points for emphasis when a period serves. I do not inflate significance through punctuation.

Not holiness. Not light. A sour metallic bite—brine on iron, a rusted tang that made the back of her jaw tighten like it wanted to spit the world out. The resonance came up through her boots, a low dissonant tremor that didn’t match the stone beneath it. A wrong vibration in a place that had learned to pretend it was steady.

The shard rode her spine in a tight sling of leather and cord.

A piece of the thing that hit Te’Oga. A sliver torn from the sky-violence itself—glass-wrong and metal-wrong at the same time, edges too clean to be natural. It looked like a blade because your mind needed a category for it. But it had no handle. No hilt. No mercy. Just a tapering spine you could barely hold without paying for it.

Vael had paid for it.

Old scars laddered her palm where the shard had taught her what “careless” cost. Now she wrapped her grip in strip-leather and wire, not to make it safe—nothing about it was safe—but to give her hand a fraction of obedience. She didn’t carry it like a sword.

She carried it like a tool that could bite the wrong owner.

Brinefall had the kind of name a place earned by failing for a long time. A village on the edge of the sea that wasn’t surviving so much as losing slowly—boards soft with rot, rope stiff with salt, faces worn down to the same grey as the fog that never fully left. The damp didn’t cling. It invaded. It got into seams. Into joints. Into breath. It made you smell like regret no matter how clean you tried to stay.

The locals didn’t greet her like a person. They greeted her like an answer.

A god problem, they said. Whispered it like the word itself could draw attention. A Kraken-God. Something from the black depths that demanded tribute. First their catches. Then their trinkets. Then their sons.

Vael took their coin without ceremony. Desperation was the last currency that didn’t devalue overnight.

Her day had started in a rented room that stank of mildew and fear. The routine was always the same. Not comfort—control.

Leather first. Straps checked. Buckles tested. Stitching traced with her thumb until she found every weak point before it found her. Then the shard.

There was no “cleaning” it. Nothing stuck to it the way it should. Blood beaded and slid off like the shard refused to acknowledge it. What she did was maintenance—checking the wrap, tightening the wire, making sure the base wouldn’t rotate in her grip at the wrong moment and turn her fingers into an offering.

Maintenance wasn’t maintenance.

It was a prayer made with hands instead of words.

Outside, on the way through the settlement, she passed two other Reclaimers—boys, really. Their armor still shone like it had never been dragged through a bad choice. They were laughing as they cleaned fish, hands quick and careless, as if rot couldn’t touch them yet.

They nodded at her with wary respect. Not friendship. Not kinship. Recognition.

“The Sinner of First Flame,” one of them muttered, just loud enough to make sure she heard.

They saw the title. The legend. The warning sign.

They didn’t see the ash collecting in the lines around her eyes. They didn’t see what the work took and what it never gave back. They saw a hammer—and they were grateful it wasn’t pointed at them.

The path to the lair wasn’t far, but the land around it was wrong the way a bruise is wrong: not obvious until you touched it, then unmistakable. Trees on the cliffside twisted back toward the ground as if the sky had become something to fear. Branches curled like fingers trying to claw themselves into earth. The air thinned and stretched, like the world had been pulled too hard in one direction and never fully returned.

A god had leaned close here.

Not a blessing. A stain.

Vael followed the resonance down to a sea-cavern the tide had eaten out of the rock like it was chewing a cheap grave. The mouth of it gaped between slick stones and hanging weed, breathing out cold brine and old blood.

Inside, the smell hit first: iron, rot, and the cloying sweetness of ceremonial wine—too sweet, too desperate, like someone trying to perfume a corpse. It smelled like every small-town horror that dressed itself up as faith. The floor sucked at her boots with wet stone and algae.

Offerings were piled near the entrance in a pathetic little shrine of surrender: fish guts glistening and buzzing with black flies, chipped ceramic cups stacked like a child’s idea of devotion, and a crudely carved wooden soldier with paint worn away by small hands.

Vael’s jaw tightened until her teeth ached.

She hated the ones that demanded toys.

It was a cheap cruelty. Sentimental. Cowardly. The kind of evil that made sure the victim remembered love right before it took it away.

She moved deeper. Her steps made almost no sound, swallowed by the wet rock. The resonance grew louder, but it wasn’t the crushing pressure of something truly divine. A real god didn’t need to announce itself. It just arrived, and your thoughts slowed under the weight of it. Your lungs learned what fear tasted like.

This was different.

This was frantic. Sputtering. A dying engine coughing through its last cycle, trying to convince the world it still ran.

Not weight.

Racket.

A parasite wrapped around a sliver of stolen power, shaking it hard enough to make noise and calling the noise a choir.

The shard against Vael’s back answered it—faint at first, then sharper, like metal recognizing metal. The sky-piece didn’t care about prayers. It cared about resonance. It cared about what fit and what didn’t.

Vael tasted the demand in the room—unity twisted into appetite, wholeness turned predatory. A craving to absorb. A promise of frictionless peace that always ended with someone disappearing into someone else.

This wasn’t just a monster.

It was an argument with teeth.

At the heart of the cave, she found him.

Not a kraken. Not a god. Just a man.

Gaunt. Stooped. Wrapped in fishing nets and driftwood like a costume stitched from grief and bad ideas. Kelp was braided into his hair like a rotten crown. He stood hunched over a flat slime-coated rock that served as an altar, chanting in a voice cracked raw from misuse.

On the altar lay a boy—small, pale, still in that quiet way that made your stomach turn cold. The man held a shard of obsidian over the child’s chest, its edge weeping a thin line of red.

The divinity in the room wasn’t his. It clung to him like mold. A borrowed spark leeched from some relic buried in the mud below, and he was using it to siphon life out of the boy in the name of “baptism.” Forced wholeness. Dissolving a person into a false collective. Erasing the self and calling it salvation.

Vael didn’t announce herself. She wasn’t here to negotiate. She wasn’t here to persuade.

Executioner. Not diplomat.

“Turn around,” she said.

Her voice didn’t rise. It didn’t need to. It cut through his chanting like ice through skin.

He spun, eyes wide with terror and holy fervor. He saw her—worn leather, faded sigils, the stance of someone who’d stopped asking the world to be fair. Then his gaze dropped to her hand.

To the shard.

No handle. No guard. Just a wedge of sky-glass and iron, wrapped at the base in strip-leather and wire like a person trying to hold lightning without being reduced to smoke.

He flinched.

Good. His body understood before his brain could lie.

“Blasphemer!” he shrieked, brandishing the obsidian shard like it was a scepter. “You cannot stop the Tidemother’s baptism! He is chosen!”

Vael saw the truth clinging to him—sickness lapping at his bones like pale blue fire. Not power. Not grace. Infection shaped into purpose.

A fisherman who’d lost his family to a storm, and in the empty space grief left behind, he’d mistaken a pretty rock for a god. The spark hadn’t healed him. It had just taken his sorrow and sharpened it into something that could cut other people.

“There is no Tidemother,” Vael said, flat as iron. “There’s just you.”

“Liar!” He lunged.

The fight wasn’t a battle. It was cleanup.

He swung in a wide, theatrical arc—big motion, big emotion, the kind of attack meant to be witnessed and believed in. Vael stepped aside with bored economy. She didn’t waste the shard on a parry. No handle meant no mistakes. She used her forearm instead, slammed her elbow into his wrist with a sharp, controlled crack.

The obsidian clattered to the stone and skidded into a puddle of old offerings.

He howled—more rage than pain—and charged again, bare-handed, trying to bull through her toward the altar.

Toward the boy.

Final mistake.

Vael pivoted. Let his momentum commit for him. Then she drove the shard forward in a short, brutal thrust—the kind that didn’t need flourish, didn’t need permission, didn’t need belief.

The sky-piece slid between his ribs with a wet tearing sound. Not clean. Nothing living is clean. But the shard moved like it had been waiting for this geometry. It found the stolen spark trembling in his chest—

—and the resonance snapped.

Not a drink. Not hunger.

A hard correction. A fit being rejected.

The room shuddered. The parasite-noise stuttered like a voice hitting a wall. The man’s eyes went wide, not with faith now, but with sudden, naked understanding—as if the lie inside him had been yanked out by the root and he could finally feel the hole it left.

He tried to speak.

What came out was blood and brine.

He collapsed like a puppet with its strings cut, and the frantic energy in the cavern died with him—not fading, not drifting, but stopping, like a machine finally powered down.

Vael held the shard steady until his weight was gone, then pulled it free.

Blood beaded along the edge and slid off. It refused to stain. It refused to participate in the story the world wanted to tell about it. The shard stayed what it was: a piece of the Piercing. A piece of impact. A piece of “this happened and it doesn’t care how you feel.”

Vael turned to the altar.

The boy was still breathing, shallow and thin. The cut over his heart wept blood in slow pulses, like the body couldn’t decide if it wanted to keep holding.

He was alive.

Vael knelt, tore a strip from her tunic, and pressed it hard against the wound. Her hands were steady. Her mind stayed quiet. Panic was for people who still believed panic fixed anything.

The boy’s eyes fluttered open—glassy with shock, unfocused with pain. He looked at the dead man. Looked at her.

He didn’t scream.

They rarely did.

Vael said nothing. There weren’t words that didn’t lie. She wasn’t a savior. She was the person who killed the monster who used to be his neighbor. She was the Sinner of First Flame—broken tool, built for taking things apart.

She lifted the boy into her arms. He was light. Too light. Fragile in a way that made her teeth grind, because fragility wasn’t fair and the world didn’t care.

As she carried him out of the cavern, back toward the grey, weeping sky of Brinefall, the shard in her wrap went quiet again.

No pulse. No hunger.

Just a cold, perfect edge against her palm, reminding her that anything you can hold without a handle will cut you eventually.

Vael’s appetite didn’t ease.
