# Chapter 1 — Lateral Stress Detected

The crack runs three meters up Rib 23, frost-patterned and wrong.

Jhace sees it at dawn, before the crew assembles. He's standing on the market floor, alone except for the heat pipes humming beneath rusted grating. The dome ribs curve overhead like a cage made of glass and iron, forty-three of them reinforced five weeks ago. This one wasn't supposed to fail.

He climbs the eastern scaffold, left hand gripping cold rungs while his right trembles against his thigh. The tremor's worse in the mornings. Three fingers numb, two barely responsive. He doesn't think about it. Just moves.

At the third tier, he presses his palm flat against Rib 23. The vein thrashes beneath the glass—stress channels rupturing in microscopic cascades, spreading like roots through frozen soil. Not sabotage. Physics. Consequence.

The rib held through the stabilization because it had to. Now it's paying the price.

Jhace traces the crack with numb fingers, mapping failure vectors. Lateral stress-riser. Foundation architecture destabilized. Five days, maybe less, before the fracture reaches the crown and the whole section comes down.

He hears boots on scaffold below. Tiffani climbs fast, toolbelt rattling, her eyes finding the crack before she reaches him.

"How bad?" she asks.

"Five days," Jhace says. "If we start now."

She touches the glass beside his hand. Her fingers don't shake. They never do. But he sees the calculation behind her eyes—load vectors, crew capacity, cost per hour, what breaks if they push too hard.

"Distributed model," Tiffani says. "You coordinate ribs 21 through 25. I'll handle foundation stabilization. Kylar takes crew rotation."

Jhace nods. He knew she'd say that. They've been working this way for five weeks—partnership formalized, mandatory, the only way the work holds.

His left hand starts to shake.

It's subtle at first. A tremor in his wrist, then his fingers, then his whole forearm locking tight against the scaffold rail. He grips harder, trying to force it steady. The tremor doesn't care. It just announces itself, louder than before.

Tiffani sees it. Doesn't look away.

Below, the crew is gathering. Kylar, Bram, Selenthe, thirteen others. Eighteen total. They're watching him. Watching his hand shake against cold iron, visible from thirty meters down.

Jhace exhales through his teeth. Five weeks, he's hidden this. Bandaged his hands before dawn, worked through the pain, pulled tools with his left when his right failed. Now there's no hiding it.

"Jhace," Tiffani says quietly.

He lets go of the rail. His hand trembles in open air—three fingers numb, two barely holding tension, the whole structure compromised.

"They need to know," Tiffani says.

Jhace climbs down. One rung at a time, left hand leading, right hand gripping wherever it can. By the time he reaches the market floor, his left palm burns where he compensated for his right.

When he reaches the market floor, the crew doesn't scatter. They wait.

Kylar steps forward first. He's younger than Jhace by eight years, pragmatic where Jhace is stubborn, good at asking questions Jhace doesn't want to answer.

"How long?" Kylar asks.

"Since the scaffold collapse," Jhace says. "Five weeks."

Kylar glances at Tiffani. She nods once—confirmation, not permission.

"Sixty percent loss, right hand," Jhace continues. "Forty percent, left. Tremor's chronic. Won't improve."

Bram crosses his arms, jaw tight. Selenthe just stares. Mrs. Kettner, standing at the edge of the crowd with bread wrapped in cloth, looks like she already knew.

"Can you still work?" Kylar asks.

"Yes," Jhace says. "But not alone."

Tiffani moves beside him, close enough their shoulders almost touch. "Distributed model," she says to the crew. "Jhace coordinates. I stabilize. Kylar rotates teams. We hold the rib in five days, or we evacuate the market quarter."

No one moves. The crew watches Jhace's trembling hand, watches Tiffani standing beside him, watches Kylar step into the gap where leadership used to be singular.

Then Kylar turns to the crew. He doesn't look at Jhace for confirmation. "Who's got rigging experience on foundation work?"

Three hands go up. Bram, Selenthe, one of the Halrick refugees.

"You're with Tiffani," Kylar says. "Everyone else, with me. Jhace, you're mapping vectors and calling adjustments. No climbing above second tier."

Jhace opens his mouth to argue. Tiffani touches his wrist—just two fingers, light pressure, finding the tremor's rhythm, the kind of contact that says *let them work*.

He closes his mouth.

The crew disperses. Tools rattle, scaffolds creak, boots scatter across grating. Jhace stays on the market floor, right hand still shaking, watching eighteen people move like a single organism—distributed, functional, holding what he can't hold alone anymore.

Tiffani doesn't leave. She stands beside him, looking up at Rib 23, at the frost-patterned crack spreading like a scar through glass.

"Five days," she says.

"Five days," Jhace repeats.

The tremor doesn't stop. But the work begins.

# Chapter 2 — Tiffani Charts Vessels

The storage alcove smells like oil and rust. Tiffani sits on an overturned crate, refugee file spread across her knees—names, origins, routes. Twelve families. Eight from Halrick, three from Mourne, one from Odern. Integration's been smooth so far. No conflicts, no resource fights. Just exhaustion and gratitude.

Elsa from Halrick sits across from her, hands folded, eyes tired. Mid-forties, scaffold worker before the fire. She answers Tiffani's questions without hesitation. Where she came from. What she left behind. Why she's here.

"The banners went up overnight," Elsa says. "Fire-doctrine. Mercy through burning. They said it cleanses contamination."

Tiffani writes that down. "Did anyone believe it?"

"Some." Elsa's voice goes flat. "My neighbor—friend for fifteen years—she walked into the flames. Smiled while she burned."

Tiffani's pen stops. She looks up.

Elsa meets her eyes. "She was sick. Had been for months. The doctrine said suffering was contamination. Fire was mercy. She believed it."

Tiffani sets the file aside. "And you didn't."

"I ran." Elsa's hands tighten in her lap. "Watched the district burn. Grabbed my daughter and ran west."

Tiffani nods slowly. She doesn't write that down. Just remembers. Fire-doctrine. Mercy as release. Conversion through belief, not force.

She asks three more questions—travel routes, other refugees, what messages Elsa heard on the road. The answers confirm what Tiffani already suspected. Mobel's moving. Not through military action. Through exhaustion. Through people too tired to resist certainty.

"Thank you," Tiffani says. "You're cleared for crew integration. Kylar will assign you tomorrow."

Elsa stands, nods once, leaves.

Tiffani sits alone in the alcove. She pulls a second file from her belt—not crew documents. Her own notes. Names, cities, doctrines. Corlexi in Vieln. Mobel in Halrick. Salee in Mourne. Eries spreading through Odern's refugee network. Four patterns. Four ideologies. Four threats.

She's been tracking them for five weeks. Since the stabilization ended. Since she realized the work wasn't over.

The alcove door opens. Jhace steps in, toolbelt rattling, dust in his hair. He sees the file in her hands and stops.

"What's that?" he asks.

Tiffani closes it. "Refugee data."

Jhace crosses his arms. His left hand trembles against his ribs. "You've been interviewing them alone for three weeks. That's not just integration work."

She doesn't answer immediately. Just looks at him—scarred hands, exhausted eyes, the kind of trust that doesn't flinch when she tells hard truths.

"I'm tracking Vessels," she says finally.

Jhace's expression doesn't change. "Why?"

Tiffani exhales. She sets the file on the crate beside her, opens it so he can see. Names. Routes. Doctrine summaries. "Because I lost someone to this before."

Jhace steps closer. Doesn't touch the file. Just waits.

"Her name was Senna," Tiffani says. "Partner. Seven years. She was tracking stress-pattern anomalies in Mourne—vein work, like ours. Then Salee's doctrine arrived. Binding through wholeness. Coercive compassion. Senna converted in three days."

She stops. Jhace doesn't interrupt.

"I tried to pull her out," Tiffani continues. "She didn't recognize me. Said binding was love. Said I was contaminated because I resisted connection. She walked away like I was a stranger."

The alcove's quiet except for heat pipes humming below. Jhace's tremor is visible in his wrist now, steady rhythm against his belt.

"That's why you're here," he says. Not a question.

"I came to Grindheim because it's far from Mourne," Tiffani says. "Because no one here knew me. Because I thought I could outrun it."

"But you didn't."

"No." Tiffani looks at the file, at the names spreading across cities like cracks through glass. "Mobel's doctrine is different from Salee's. Fire instead of binding. But the structure's the same. Exhaustion. Certainty. Conversion."

Jhace crouches in front of her, close enough she can see the scar tissue crossing his right palm. "You're tracking them so you can stop it."

"I'm tracking them so I know when to run," Tiffani says. "Or fight. Or hold."

Jhace's left hand is shaking harder now. He doesn't hide it. Just lets the tremor announce itself between them, visible proof of cost already paid.

"Does the crew know?" he asks.

"No. Just you."

He nods once. Then he reaches for her hand—not to comfort, just to ground. Two fingers against her wrist, finding her pulse, the way she found his tremor this morning. Diagnostic. Intimate.

"We keep tracking," Jhace says. "But you don't do it alone."

Tiffani's throat tightens. She threads her fingers through his—slow, deliberate, three seconds of contact where their scars align. Then she lets go.

"Refugee integration continues," she says. "We don't change the work. We just know what we're facing."

"Agreed."

Jhace stands. His hand still trembles, but he doesn't favor it. Just adjusts his toolbelt and turns toward the door.

"Jhace," Tiffani says.

He stops.

"The vein burn on my ribs—it's three centimeters now. Visible if you look close. Every Form lock costs me. I'm still functional. But you should know."

Jhace looks at her for a long moment. Then he nods. "I know."

He leaves.

Tiffani sits alone with the file open on her knees. Twelve refugee families. Four doctrines spreading west. One partner lost to binding seven years ago. One crew holding against collapse.

She closes the file. Returns it to her belt. Outside, the crew's moving scaffold sections, voices calling load calculations across the market floor. The work continues.

Tiffani stands, adjusts her toolbelt, walks back into the light. The hidden weight doesn't leave. She just carries it forward.

# Chapter 3 — Supply, Sanitists, Warning

The supply cart arrives three hours late. Jhace waits by the market checkpoint, toolbelt heavy against his hips, watching civic enforcers check every crate. They're thorough today. More thorough than usual.

Brighlee approaches from the east gate, clipboard clutched tight, her face set in the careful neutrality she wears when she's carrying bad news. She's worked civic administration for twelve years—long enough to know when to speak and when to stay silent.

"Jhace," she says quietly.

He nods. "Supply's late."

"Supply's compromised." She glances at the enforcers, then back at him. "Twelve blocks added to watch zones last night. Yours included."

Jhace's left hand tightens against his belt. The tremor starts in his wrist, works its way to his fingers. He doesn't hide it anymore. "Which blocks?"

Brighlee shows him the map. Red ink marks the expansion—twelve new sectors, all Dregs tenements, all surrounding the market dome. The pattern's clear. Civic containment. Slow encirclement.

"They're calling it preventative monitoring," Brighlee says. "Refugee integration review. But the watch patrols doubled overnight."

Jhace studies the map. His crew's scattered across eight of those blocks. Mrs. Kettner's tenement sits dead center in one. Kylar's safe house is inside another.

"When do enforcement quotas start?" he asks.

"Two weeks. Maybe less." Brighlee folds the map, hands it to him. "Evictions first. Then labor reassignments. Then detainment if anyone resists."

The supply cart finally clears inspection. Crates unload—fewer than expected. Jhace counts them. Eight crates short. Steel brackets, glass panels, two spools of bracing wire. Everything they need to finish Rib 23.

"Where's the rest?" he asks the supplier.

The man—Harrold, he's made this run for three years—just shakes his head. "Civic requisition. Took half the shipment at the northwest checkpoint. Said infrastructure priorities shifted."

Jhace's jaw tightens. "They took our materials."

"They took everyone's materials." Harrold lowers his voice. "Word on the route is they're redirecting supplies to upper districts. Fortification work. Glass tower reinforcements."

Brighlee touches Jhace's arm—two fingers, light pressure. A warning.

Jhace exhales slowly. His right hand trembles against his thigh, numb fingers useless. He forces his voice level. "Can you make another run?"

"Not for two weeks," Harrold says. "And no guarantees they won't requisition again."

"Understood." Jhace signs the delivery slip with his left hand. The signature's shaky, but legible. "Thanks for getting here."

Harrold nods once, climbs back onto his cart, leaves.

Jhace stands alone with Brighlee and eight crates of insufficient materials. The market dome looms overhead, frost-patterned crack spreading through Rib 23 like a wound that won't close.

"You can't finish the repair with this," Brighlee says.

"No."

"You need to decide, Jhace." Her voice drops lower. "Proceed with what you have and risk failure. Or retreat. Move operations underground. Abandon the dome."

Jhace looks up at Rib 23. Five days to hold it. Now they're two days in, supply-starved, watch zones expanding, crew scattered across monitored blocks.

"Proceeding means risking the crew," Brighlee continues. "Civic enforcers are watching for vein-workers. Resonance use is flagged as destabilizing activity. They'll detain anyone they catch channeling."

"And retreating means abandoning the market quarter," Jhace says. "Two hundred families. Mrs. Kettner. The refugees we just integrated."

"Yes."

The heat pipes hum beneath rusted grating. Distant voices call load calculations across the dome floor. Somewhere above, Tiffani's coordinating foundation work with Bram and Selenthe, unaware that half their materials vanished at a checkpoint.

Jhace's left hand shakes harder. He grips his belt, steadies it through pressure.

"I need to talk to Tiffani," he says finally.

"She'll say proceed," Brighlee says. "You know she will."

"I know."

"And Kylar?"

"Kylar will calculate risk and give me three options I don't want to choose between."

Brighlee almost smiles. Not quite. "That's fair."

Jhace picks up the supply manifest, folds it into his toolbelt. Eight crates. Twelve watch zones. Two weeks until enforcement quotas. Five days to hold a rib that's already failing.

"Tell the crew to meet at second bell," Jhace says. "Market floor. Everyone."

"You're making the decision public."

"We hold what we have," Jhace says. "Or we don't. Either way, the crew decides together."

Brighlee nods once. She leaves through the east gate, disappearing into the Dregs network of alleys and tenements.

Jhace stays by the supply crates. His right hand won't close around tools anymore, but his left can still count. Eight crates. Not enough to finish. Enough to try.

Tiffani appears at the scaffold edge, twenty meters up, her silhouette sharp against glass and iron. She sees him standing alone with the crates. Sees the insufficient pile. Doesn't call down. Just watches.

Jhace lifts his left hand—two fingers raised. The signal they established five weeks ago. *We need to talk.*

Tiffani climbs down. Fast. By the time she reaches the market floor, Kylar's already approaching from the west corridor, Bram close behind. They saw the signal. They came.

"Materials?" Tiffani asks.

"Half requisitioned," Jhace says. "Watch zones expanded. Twelve blocks. Ours included."

Kylar crosses his arms. "Options?"

"Proceed with what we have. Or retreat."

Bram's jaw tightens. "Retreat means abandoning the quarter."

"Yes," Jhace says.

No one speaks. The heat pipes hum. The crew works above, unaware the choice is already narrowing.

Tiffani steps closer. She doesn't touch him. Just stands near enough he can hear her breathing—steady, deliberate, the way she breathes before channeling into unstable veins.

"We proceed," she says.

Kylar looks at Jhace. "You agree?"

Jhace's left hand trembles. He lets it. "We hold what we have."

"Even if enforcement comes?" Kylar asks.

"Especially then."

Kylar nods slowly. "Then we redistribute the materials. Prioritize Rib 23. Defer secondary work. Stretch eight crates across five days."

"Can it hold?" Bram asks.

"It has to," Tiffani says.

The decision settles between them—not relief, just acknowledgment. The work continues. The risk compounds. The choice is made.

Jhace watches the crew above, eighteen people moving across scaffolds and ribs, unaware their materials vanished at a checkpoint, unaware watch zones expanded overnight, unaware the decision to hold was just made for them by four people standing on rusted grating.

"Second bell," Jhace says. "We tell them together."

Tiffani nods. Kylar nods. Bram nods.

They disperse. Work resumes. The market dome holds.

For now.

# Chapter 4 — Night Repair, Hand Breakthrough

The vein work starts at third bell. Tiffani and Jhace alone on the eastern scaffold, thirty meters up, no crew below. This kind of work requires complete trust—one hand holding the vein open, the other channeling stabilization through the stress points. If either hesitates, the glass shatters.

Jhace grips the scaffold rail with his left hand. His right rests against the rib, numb fingers splayed flat. Tiffani touches the vein beneath the glass—invisible stress channels threading through crystal like cracks waiting to happen.

"Ready?" she asks.

"Always."

She doesn't smile. Just nods once and channels Form into the vein. The glass shifts beneath Jhace's palm—microscopic movement, enough to feel. He pours Wholeness into the fracture points, holding the structure steady while she redistributes load.

His left hand shakes against the rail. The tremor's worse tonight. Three days of insufficient sleep, two days of material scarcity, one day of watching watch zones expand while civic enforcers counted their crew.

Tiffani doesn't comment on the tremor. Just adjusts her angle, compensates for his grip instability, continues the work.

They finish the vein unlock at fourth bell. The glass holds. The rib stabilizes. Jhace's hands burn—not pain, just exhaustion announcing itself through overworked tissue.

"Down," Tiffani says.

They climb in silence. By the time they reach the workshop floor, Jhace's left palm is slick with sweat. His right hand won't close into a fist. Three fingers completely numb. Two barely responsive.

The workshop's empty. Just scattered tools, oil-stained workbenches, one lantern burning low in the corner. Tiffani sits on the bench edge, pulls off her gloves. Her hands are trembling too—not tremor, just fatigue.

Jhace sits beside her. Doesn't speak. Just breathes.

Tiffani looks at his hands. "Let me see."

He hesitates. Then extends his right hand, palm up. The scar tissue crosses his palm like bad welds—thick, uneven, permanent. Three fingers motionless. The tremor in his wrist visible even at rest.

Tiffani doesn't ask permission. Just takes his hand between both of hers and presses her thumbs into the base of his palm. Slow pressure. Working the tension out of muscles that haven't relaxed in weeks.

Jhace exhales through his teeth. It doesn't hurt. But it's intimate in a way that has nothing to do with desire—just vulnerability. Letting someone see the limitation. Letting them touch the failure.

Tiffani works in silence. Thumbs tracing the scar lines, fingers pressing into the spaces between tendons, palms warming the cold tissue that won't heal. Five minutes. No words. Just her hands on his, methodical and careful.

When she finishes his right hand, she moves to his left. The tremor doesn't stop under her touch. But it slows. Becomes predictable. She tracks the rhythm with her thumbs, finding the pattern, grounding him through pressure.

Jhace watches her face. She's not looking at him. Just focused on the work—diagnosing through touch, mapping damage she can't fix but can ease.

"Thank you," he says quietly.

"Don't." She doesn't let go. Just holds his left hand steady between hers, fingers threading through his. Three seconds. Deliberate. Then she releases and stands.

"Get some sleep," Tiffani says. "We start again at dawn."

She leaves.

Jhace sits alone in the workshop. His hands still warm where she touched them. The tremor returns to full strength, but the tension's gone. He flexes his left hand—manageable. Flexes his right—nothing. Three fingers dead weight.

He stares at his right hand. Numb. Useless. Permanent.

Then he does something he hasn't tried before.

He channels Wholeness into his own tissue.

Not into glass. Not into veins. Into his palm. Into the scar tissue. Into the ruined tendons that won't respond.

The finger had moved.

Once.

That was enough.

# Interlude 1 — Corlexi (Build-Up)

**Vieln. Glass-punk city. Dawn.**

The correction begins with geometry.

Corlexi stands at the center of the tower plaza, arms extended, palms facing the distorted glass spire thirty meters up. The building leans three degrees off vertical—fractional deviation, catastrophic in aggregate. Twelve years of wind pressure, thermal expansion, foundation settlement. The tower bent. Slowly. Inevitably.

Now it straightens.

She channels Form into the stress veins threading through crystal—invisible architecture beneath visible structure. The glass shifts. One millimeter per second. Controlled collapse backward. Load redistributed through reinforced anchors she installed last week.

Around her, sixteen followers watch in silence. They've learned not to speak during corrections. Speech is distortion. Breath is interference. Only geometry persists.

The tower reaches vertical. Holds. The veins lock.

Corlexi lowers her hands. No tremor. No exhaustion. Just precision confirmed through measurement.

"Mark it," she says.

Therin—her second, engineer before conversion—steps forward with measuring tools. He confirms alignment. Three-degree correction achieved. Zero structural compromise. The work is perfect.

"Logged," Therin says.

Corlexi nods once. Turns to the gathered followers. Sixteen faces. Twelve refugees from collapsed districts. Four engineers who recognized the pattern early. All exhausted. All certain.

"Next site," she says.

They move east. Through glass streets where light refracts in calculated angles. Past mirror-towers that reflect without distortion. Every surface clean. Every edge sharp. Vieln is becoming what it was meant to be—geometry manifest.

The correction sites are marked with chalk. Seventeen buildings scheduled for today. Corlexi works through them methodically. North tower: two-degree lean, corrected. Market dome: stress-riser in western rib, stabilized. Residential complex: foundation fracture, sealed.

By third bell, eight buildings stand vertical. No collapses. No failures. Just correction.

At the ninth site—a tenement block leaning four degrees, occupants evacuated—a man approaches from the refugee quarter. Mid-thirties, carpenter by trade, exhausted by the way he moves. He stops ten meters from Corlexi's working radius.

"My daughter's inside," he says.

Corlexi doesn't stop channeling. The building's already shifting. "Evacuation was mandatory."

"She's sick. Couldn't walk. I couldn't carry her fast enough."

Therin steps between them. "The correction continues. Occupants who remained accepted the risk."

The man's face goes white. "She's eight years old."

Corlexi adjusts her angle. The building shifts faster—three degrees left, foundation stress redistributing through anchors she can't see but knows are there. The structure groans. Glass cracks. Then settles.

The man runs inside.

Corlexi doesn't follow. Just continues the work. Fourth degree. Third. Second. The building straightens. The veins lock. She lowers her hands.

The man emerges carrying a child—unconscious, breathing shallow. He doesn't thank Corlexi. Just runs toward the medical quarter.

Therin watches him go. "Acceptable loss?"

"No loss occurred," Corlexi says. "The building stands. The child lives. The correction succeeded."

Therin nods. But his expression flickers—brief, suppressed. Doubt, maybe. Or exhaustion mistaken for doubt.

"Next site," Corlexi says.

They move on.

By nightfall, seventeen corrections complete. Vieln's eastern district stands vertical. Stress-risers stabilized. Geometry restored. The followers disperse to rest. Corlexi doesn't rest. She walks the corrected streets alone, measuring angles with her eyes, confirming alignments through touch.

At the corrected tenement, she finds chalk marks on the doorframe. Small handprints. A child's game—uneven, distorted, wrong. She erases them with her palm. The doorframe is clean now. Perfect.

Inside, she hears voices. A woman crying. The man's voice, low and steady. The child coughing.

Corlexi leaves. Returns to the tower plaza. Sits on the central bench—smoothed glass, ergonomic curve calculated for spinal alignment. She doesn't sleep. Just watches the city straighten under moonlight.

Therin finds her at dawn.

"Eighteen new requests," he says. "Western district. Foundation fractures. They're asking for corrections."

"Schedule them," Corlexi says.

"Some are occupied. Evacuation timelines conflict with correction schedules."

"Evacuate them."

"If they refuse?"

Corlexi looks at him. Her eyes are glass-clear. No anger. No compassion. Just geometry. "Then they accept the risk."

Therin hesitates. The doubt surfaces again—not in his words, in the quarter-second pause before he nods.

"Scheduled," he says finally.

Corlexi stands. The city stretches around her—vertical towers, stable domes, corrected angles. Seventeen sites today. Eighteen tomorrow. Fifty by week's end. Vieln will stand straight. The distortions will be erased. The structure will hold.

And if some refuse correction, if some cling to bent buildings and fractured foundations, if some would rather collapse than straighten—

That's acceptable variance.

Geometry persists. Humans adapt or break.

The work continues.

# Chapter 5 — Refugee Integration, Training

Mrs. Kettner's bread arrives at dawn.

Kylar watches her walk across the market floor, basket wrapped in cloth, her steps careful on the rusted grating. She's seventy-two, been baking in the Dregs for forty years, survived three eviction waves and two Sanitist sweeps. Her bread ritual started during the stabilization—feeding crew before work, herbs for Jhace's hands, warmth against cold mornings.

Elsa's daughter is sixteen. Name's Jorin. She's thin, hands calloused from factory work, eyes sharp with the kind of alertness that comes from surviving fire-doctrine districts. The two from Mourne—Raelle and Korvin—look older, mid-twenties, both riggers before Salee's binding rituals forced them west.

"Load calculations," Kylar says, no introduction. "Rib supports three thousand kilograms at vertical. At three-degree lean, what's the load shift?"

Jorin answers first. "Twenty percent redistribution. Stress concentrates at base anchors."

"Close," Kylar says. "Twenty-two percent. Fractional deviations compound. Try again."

She does. Gets it right the second time.

Raelle and Korvin run through stress-pattern reading—tracing veins through glass, identifying failure vectors, calculating repair timelines. They're competent. Not precise like Tiffani, but functional. Good enough.

By third bell, all three are working scaffold sections under supervision. Jorin climbs fast, no hesitation. Raelle and Korvin move cautiously—muscle memory fighting against Mourne's binding rituals, where individual action was discouraged, collective movement enforced.

Kylar watches from below. Jhace appears beside him, left hand gripping the scaffold rail. His right hand rests against his toolbelt, tremor visible but manageable.

"How are they?" Jhace asks.

"Competent," Kylar says. "Jorin's fast. The Mourne pair needs time. They keep waiting for permission to move."

Jhace nods. "Binding does that. Takes weeks to unlearn."

"You think they'll stay?"

"Depends." Jhace watches Raelle adjust a bracket—hesitant, then decisive. "Depends if we give them something worth staying for."

At fourth bell, the crew gathers on the market floor. Eighteen riggers. Twelve veterans, three new apprentices, three refugees integrated last week. Mrs. Kettner brings the bread basket again, sets it on the workbench. Someone produces mugs. Water, not ale—too early, too much work left.

Bram raises his mug first. "To competence."

"To distribution," Selenthe adds.

Jhace doesn't raise his mug. Just lifts his right hand—slow, deliberate. Three fingers motionless. Two barely responsive. But the hand rises. Holds. Ten seconds.

The crew watches in silence.

Then Kylar raises his mug. "To knowing when to hold on."

Laughter. Brief. Real.

Jhace's hand trembles, then drops. The tremor returns full strength. But the ten seconds happened. The crew saw it. Mrs. Kettner saw it. The apprentices saw it.

Tiffani stands at the edge of the group, toolbelt rattling, vein burn hidden beneath her shirt. She's not looking at Jhace's hand. Just watching the crew—eighteen people who chose to stay, who chose to work, who chose uncertainty over certainty.

Jhace catches her eye across the crowd. She doesn't smile. Just nods once.

The bread passes hand to hand. Herbal wraps distribute. Someone asks Kylar about tomorrow's rotation. Jorin asks Bram about scaffold rigging. Raelle and Korvin stand together, not yet integrated but not isolated either.

The found-family holds.

For now.

# Chapter 6 — List Expands, Delegation Decision

The updated list arrives before dawn.

Jhace sits in his quarters—single room, cot against the wall, workbench littered with tools he can barely hold. Brighlee stands in the doorway, clipboard clutched tight, her face set in the careful neutrality that means the news is worse than last time.

"Twelve names added," she says.

Jhace takes the list with his left hand. His right rests against his thigh, tremor visible in the pre-dawn light. He scans the names. Recognizes eight. Crew members, refugees, Mrs. Kettner's neighbor. And—

"Kylar," Jhace says quietly.

Brighlee nods. "Civic designation: 'Resonance coordination, public visibility, ideological risk.'"

Jhace stares at Kylar's name. Inked beside it: *Priority monitor. Labor reassignment authorized if non-compliance confirmed.*

"When does enforcement start?" Jhace asks.

"Two weeks. Maybe less." Brighlee glances at the corridor behind her, then back at Jhace. "They're not just watching anymore. They're building cases."

Jhace folds the list, tucks it into his toolbelt. His left hand shakes as he releases it. "Does Kylar know?"

"Not yet."

"I'll tell him."

Brighlee leaves. Jhace sits alone in the dark room, staring at the wall. His right hand won't close. His left won't stop shaking. The list burns against his hip like a brand.

He thinks about the stabilization. Five weeks ago. When he climbed scaffolds with ruined hands, when Tiffani held veins open while he channeled Wholeness through fractures, when the crew trusted him to carry weight he couldn't distribute.

He can't do that anymore.

He doesn't have to.

Jhace stands, adjusts his toolbelt, walks to the market floor. Dawn's breaking over the dome—frost-patterned crack still visible in Rib 23, stress-riser spreading slower now but not stopped. The crew's already working. Kylar's coordinating scaffold rotations, calling adjustments across the floor, moving with the kind of precision Jhace used to own.

Jhace watches from below. Doesn't interrupt. Just observes.

Kylar sees him. Climbs down fast, boots rattling on rusted grating. "Problem?"

"Your name's on the list," Jhace says.

Kylar's expression doesn't change. Just nods once. "Expected."

"Civic designation says 'ideological risk.'"

"Also expected."

Jhace exhales slowly. His left hand grips his belt, steadies the tremor through pressure. "I need to delegate authority to you. Permanently."

Kylar crosses his arms. "You already did."

"Not formally." Jhace glances at the crew above—eighteen people working without his oversight, moving like a distributed organism, functional without singular control. "I keep watching. Correcting. Micromanaging. That stops now."

"Why?"

"Because if civic enforcers take you, the crew needs to keep working. And they won't if they think leadership's singular."

Kylar's jaw tightens. "You're preparing for my arrest."

"I'm preparing for distribution to hold under pressure." Jhace meets his eyes. "You make decisions independently. I don't review them. I don't override them. You coordinate. I anchor. We split load."

Kylar studies him for a long moment. Then nods. "Agreed."

"No guarantees you won't make errors," Jhace says.

"No guarantees you wouldn't either."

"Fair."

They stand together on the market floor, eighteen people working above, heat pipes humming below. Jhace's right hand trembles. Kylar's stance is solid.

"Tell the crew," Kylar says. "Make it formal."

"At second bell."

Kylar climbs back to the scaffolds. Jhace stays below, watching the distributed work continue without his intervention. The tremor doesn't stop. But the weight shifts.

By second bell, the crew gathers. Jhace stands at the center, Tiffani beside him, Kylar to his left. Bram, Selenthe, the apprentices, the refugees—eighteen faces, exhausted but attentive.

"Kylar's on the civic list," Jhace says. No preamble. "Labor reassignment authorized. Arrest possible within two weeks."

No one speaks. The crew just watches.

"Distributed model becomes permanent," Jhace continues. "Kylar coordinates independently. I anchor strategic work. Tiffani stabilizes critical veins. No singular leader. No single point of failure."

Bram shifts his weight. "What happens if they take Kylar?"

"Selenthe steps into coordination," Jhace says. "Rotation continues. Work holds."

Selenthe's eyes widen. She didn't expect that.

"And if they take you?" Raelle asks.

"Same model," Tiffani says quietly. "Work distributes. Crew adapts."

The silence stretches. Then Kylar raises his mug—water, not ale, too early for celebration. "To distribution."

The crew echoes. "To distribution."

Jhace doesn't raise his mug. His right hand won't grip it. But his left lifts—slow, deliberate, tremor visible to everyone. The crew sees it. Doesn't look away.

Work resumes. The dome holds. The list expands.

That night, Jhace finds Tiffani on the rooftop. She's sitting on the edge, legs dangling over rusted grating, eyes fixed on the heat pipes glowing below. Her toolbelt's discarded beside her. No tools. No work. Just sitting.

Jhace sits beside her. Doesn't speak. Just breathes.

"Kylar's good," Tiffani says finally.

"He is."

"Better than you were at his age."

Jhace almost smiles. "That's not hard."

Tiffani glances at him. Her eyes are tired—not exhaustion, something deeper. Grief, maybe. Or acceptance. "You're learning to let go."

"I don't have a choice."

"You do." She looks back at the pipes. "You're choosing to trust them. That's different."

Jhace's left hand rests on the grating between them. Tiffani reaches over, threads two fingers through his—brief contact, deliberate, the kind of touch that says *I see you holding on.*

Then she lets go.

They sit in silence. The heat pipes hum. The dome holds. The city breathes around them—broken, functional, uncertain.

"I don't want to hold this alone," Jhace says quietly.

Tiffani doesn't answer. Just shifts closer—shoulders touching, warmth shared, no words needed.

Below, the crew disperses. Above, the stars are invisible behind industrial smoke. Between them, two people sit on a rooftop, holding what they can, distributing what they can't.

The work continues.
