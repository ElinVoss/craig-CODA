## PART 1: CORE VOICE SIGNATURES

### Universal Traits (Present Across All Modes)

#### 1. **Transactional Emotional Language**
- Feelings are budgeted, priced, accounted for
- Pain = cost. Care = expense. Guilt = debt.
- **Examples from samples:**
  - "Tears are expensive. He couldn't afford tears."
  - "A cost worth paying. Theres no bargain."
  - "variable she didnt account for"

#### 2. **Fragments as Functional Tools**
- Not decorative. Each fragment does work:
  - **Anchor:** "It just wouldn't stop. This tremor."
  - **Correction:** "she smiled. a determined smile."
  - **Punctuation:** "Then."
  - **Command:** "Back to work."

#### 3. **Profanity = Rhythm + Honesty**
- Never filler. Always purpose:
  - **Internal authenticity:** "This is Bullshit, Jhace thought"
  - **Self-frustration:** "For fucks sakes, lets stay on task Tiffani"
  - **Punctuation of dominance:** "that was a dick move"
  - **Setup for swerve:** Innuendo → pivot to competence

#### 4. **Syntax Performs Emotion**
- When control breaks, sentence structure breaks:
  - Capitals for emphasis
  - Multiple punctuation marks
  - Fragments shrink to single words
  - Repetition loops tighten into silence

#### 5. **Physical Detail Over Abstraction**
- Body state = emotional state
- Concrete sensory data (tremor, breath, posture, temperature)
- Environment = minimal functional placement
- **Never:** "He felt guilty." **Always:** "He wiped the tears away with a work cloth."

#### 6. **Lowercase Bleed-Through**
- Occasional lowercase after periods = stream-of-consciousness override
- Grammar serves voice, not rules
- "or rag or shirt. whatever he decided"
- "but its wasnt much longer"

### UNDERFACE HEAT‑GRID TUNNELS

**Purpose:** Maintenance access for the city’s heating infrastructure.

**Physical Layout:**

* A network of narrow passages beneath the working‑class districts of Saint Merro.
* Copper pipes of varying diameters carry geothermal heat; smaller lines (thimble‑width) snake over large main lines (pole‑width).
* Vertical risers connect deeper levels via ladders; some spaces require squeezing through tight shaft openings.
* The tunnels are single file in most places, with slightly wider junctions for maintenance crews and tool caches.
* Multiple access points: surface grates, doors at forge‑level basements, emergency exits into the Dregs and Underface districts.

**Environmental:**

* Heat: Temperature rises with depth – warm near the surface, becoming uncomfortable near deep mains.
* Humidity: Condensation drips down walls and pipes; occasional puddles gather on the stone floor.
* Sound: Constant low rumble from heat flow, clangs as pipes shift; conversations echo unpredictably and aren’t private.
* Light: Limited to carried lamps or fixed lanterns at junctions; shadows dance on curved walls.

**Signature Visuals:**

* Copper pipes shimmering with heat distortion.
* Chalk marks noting maintenance or structural warnings.
* Tool racks (wrenches, hammers, spare flanges) at intersections.
* Steam vents hissing gently along some runs.

**Functional Details:**

* The tunnels supply heat to homes, guild halls and industrial sites. Failures can mean freezing residents or catastrophic pressure releases.
* Jhace’s inspection routes run through these tunnels; he feels for vibration changes and listens for subtle hum shifts to detect problems.
* Post‑Piercing, the heat‑grid is under unusual strain – more leaks, more pressure spikes, more points of imminent failure.

**Typical Scenes:**

* Chapter 1 opens here with Jhace inspecting the grid and feeling something wrong.
* Chapter 3 shows the tunnels rattling and rumbling during the Piercing.
* Subsequent chapters (4, 6, 12) use them as shelters, shortcuts and sites of quiet reflection or crisis navigation.

**Travel Time:**

* From street level to inspection depth: a few spans of climbing/descending, depending on access point.
* Cross‑district travel underground can be faster than surface routes during crises but is disorienting without experience.

### BEAT_01 — Observation → Pattern Recognition

**Function:**  
Use when the chapter is about **noticing something wrong** and understanding it.

**Structure:**

1. **Baseline Observation**  
   - POV engages in normal work/routine.  
   - Small detail feels off (vibration, light behavior, behavior pattern).

2. **Focused Attention**  
   - POV chooses to pay attention instead of ignoring.  
   - Compares against prior experience.

3. **Testing / Probing**  
   - Small actions to test the anomaly.  
   - Additional observations confirm the anomaly is real.

4. **Pattern Recognition**  
   - Click moment: “This is not random.”  
   - POV understands the pattern (or understands that a pattern exists and is dangerous).

5. **Decision / Micro-Shift**  
   - POV changes course or plan based on the new understanding.

**Emotional Arc:**  
Curiosity → Unease → Focus → Understanding → Resolve

**Best Used When:**
- Introducing a core problem.  
- Showing a character’s core mode of thinking (Jhace sensing structures, Tiffani reading patterns).

**Forbidden Sequences:**
- Cannot immediately follow another BEAT_01.  
- Cannot immediately precede BEAT_06 (both are recognition/synthesis-heavy).

**N01 Examples (intended):**
- Ch 1 (Jhace: “Something Wrong” under the heat-grid).  
- Ch 2 (Tiffani: observing upper atmosphere anomalies).

---

### BEAT_02 — Trial → Failure → Compounding → Success/Cost

**Function:**  
Use when you want a **hard failure** that escalates, followed by a victory that hurts.

**Structure:**

1. **Trial Setup**  
   - Goal is defined (save someone, stabilize structure, train Soulpulse).  
   - Plan seems plausible but not guaranteed.

2. **Initial Attempt → Failure**  
   - First attempt fails clearly.  
   - Stakes increase.

3. **Compounding Problem**  
   - Failure makes situation worse (more damage, less time, more people at risk).

4. **Reframe / New Approach**  
   - POV changes approach, often with help from another character.

5. **Success with Explicit Cost**  
   - Goal is achieved or partially achieved.  
   - Cost is visible: physical, emotional, moral, or relational.

**Emotional Arc:**  
Hope → Frustration → Panic → Determination → Relief + Dread

**Best Used When:**
- Training episodes that go wrong.  
- Major rescue that almost fails.  
- Moments where price of success must be undeniable.

**Forbidden Sequences:**
- Cannot follow another BEAT_02.  
- Should not directly precede BEAT_03 in most cases (risk of fatigue).

**N01 Examples (intended):**
- Kylar’s first training attempt + follow-up.  
- A rescue where Jhace’s first plan fails, and Tiffani’s input salvages it.

### BEAT_03 — Crisis Front-Loaded → Rolling Complications → Partial Resolution

**Function:**  
Use for big chaos chapters, especially disasters in progress.

1. **Drop-In Crisis**  
   - Chapter opens mid-disaster: building already collapsing, Piercing underway, etc.

   - Every attempt to stabilize reveals new problems.

3. **Local Focus Choice**

Not a smear. A line.

Four seconds.

That was not a drift. That was a shove.

She stilled.

No one else on the platform moved. The apprentices at the lower bench kept grinding lens edges. Their stones hissed. Their hands didn’t pause.

# CHAPTER 1 — Something Wrong
*Tide 0, Kindling phase*

Jhace reached Junction Seven before the runner lamps were fully warmed. The Underface held the night cold longer than the streets did. Condensation beaded on the outer skin of the heat-pipes and gathered at the collar seams.

He stopped under the plaque they had bolted to the stone after the dead were carried out.

Twenty-three names. Cut into slate. The cuts still held dust.

He did not touch the plaque. He did not bow. He stood with his lamp held steady and read each line in order. His throat tightened at the seventh name. It tightened again at the last.

He took his slate out anyway.

**UNDERFACE LOG — INSPECTION**  
**Tide:** 0, Kindling  
**Site:** Junction Seven  
**Status:** memorial plaque present; collar line replaced; sealant intact.

He had written “monitor, non-urgent” on this junction once. The memory stayed in his hands. He could feel it in the way his fingers wanted to hesitate on every latch.

He forced the work to begin.

He set his palm against the junction pipe where it met the collar. Metal vibrated through skin and bone. The grid-hum carried its normal low pitch. A second pitch rode under it, close enough to be mistaken for resonance from a nearby line. It stayed constant. It did not fade when he shifted pressure of his hand. It did not change when he stepped half a span to the side.

He pressed his ear to the pipe skin. The cold metal pulled heat from his cheek.

The doubled harmonic persisted.

He wrote it down before his mind could start arguing with it.

**Finding:** doubled harmonic present under baseline hum. Persistent across contact positions.

He pulled his hand away and watched the pipe sweat. Condensation slid in thin tracks. Dust clung to the wet and darkened.

Dust fell again from the ceiling seam. Not a collapse. A small shower in a narrow strip. The particles tapped his lamp hood. The fall repeated after two breaths. Then again.

He looked up. The seam line above Junction Seven ran straight through the stone and crossed an older support beam. The beam sat stable. No sag. No fresh shear.

The dust fall did not match local settlement patterns. It arrived on a rhythm.

He walked.

The Underface tunnels narrowed and widened in planned sections. Heat-pipes ran in pairs, then in bundles, then split into branching spines that fed districts above. Junction markers were stamped into iron plates: numbers, phase tags, maintenance dates. The plates held a thin sheen of copper-gold dust now. Everyone did. It had arrived with the Piercing’s first breath, even before the exit was seen by most people.

Jhace’s tongue tasted copper and iron when he inhaled through his mouth. The taste persisted in the back of his throat. He swallowed and it stayed.

He stopped at Junction Nine. Palm to pipe. Ear to metal.

Two pitches. Same as before.

**Site:** Junction Nine  
**Finding:** doubled harmonic persists. No local leak detected by touch. No heat spike at collar.

He moved to Junction Ten. Then Eleven.

Every stop repeated the same facts.

At Junction Twelve, he saw the worker light first. A wider glow in the tunnel bend. Then voices.

Two guild workers stood over an exposed collar assembly with their sleeves rolled up. One held a wrench in both hands, knuckles white. The other had a wedge kit open, wedges laid out in size order on a cloth. Their mouths moved fast. Their eyes stayed on the metal.

They both saw Jhace’s marker badge at the same time. Their voices did not soften. The tension shifted direction and pointed at him.

“Line’s running,” the wrench-holder said. “It stays running.”

Jhace stopped at the edge of their work space. He did not step onto their cloth. “What’s the collar doing.”

“Shifting,” the wedge-worker said. “Not enough to matter.”

Jhace looked at the collar seam. Sealant had expressed in a thin bead on one side and receded on the other. The bead was fresh. Wet. A small drip hung under the lower lip and trembled with the vibration.

He smelled hot metal and old oil. No strong steam scent. That meant the leak was internal pressure, not a breach to air yet.

He knelt and set two fingers against the collar edge.

The vibration carried the doubled harmonic. Stronger here. Cleaner.

He looked at the wrench-holder. “Why are you bracing the collar instead of isolating the line.”

The wrench-holder’s jaw flexed. “Because isolating the line drops heat for three blocks. Because the kiln schedule doesn’t care about your badge.”

“The schedule won’t matter if this joint fails,” Jhace said.

“It’s not failing,” the wrench-holder said. “It’s settling.”

Jhace did not raise his voice. He didn’t need to. The tunnel carried sound. He kept his words flat and exact.

“This collar is shifting against load. The harmonic is doubled across junctions. This isn’t local settlement. There’s pressure under foundations.”

The wedge-worker hesitated. Their eyes flicked to the seam bead. “We heard the note too.”

“Then you know it’s wrong,” Jhace said.

The wrench-holder spat into the drain channel. “Everything’s wrong. The whole city’s wrong. We still have to keep it warm.”

Jhace stood and reached for his own kit. “Open your wedge kit. Hand me the narrow wedge. Then stand back from the collar.”

The wrench-holder stepped into his space. Close enough that Jhace could smell sweat and kiln smoke embedded in the fabric. “You don’t shut my line.”

Jhace met his eyes. He did not blink.

“This is not your line,” Jhace said. “This is the grid.”

The worker’s face flushed. “You’re the inspector who writes notes and walks away.”

Jhace felt the words land where the names lived. He kept his hands steady anyway.

He lifted his slate and showed the Junction Seven entry. He tapped the line where he had written “doubled harmonic” in hard strokes.

“I write notes and I act,” Jhace said. “I acted too late once. I won’t repeat it.”

The wedge-worker held out the narrow wedge without being asked again. Jhace took it.

He crouched and examined the collar seam. He traced the bead of sealant with his eyes. The shift was biased downslope. The collar wanted to rotate under load, not slide straight. That meant the force was not only vertical. Something was pushing from below and slightly off-axis, feeding torque into the line.

He tasted copper again. Stronger. He realized he was clenching his teeth. He forced his jaw loose.

“Isolation valve,” Jhace said.

The wrench-holder barked a laugh that carried no humor. “You don’t have authority to—”

Jhace cut him off. “I do. And if you make me write your name in a casualty slate, I will.”

The wedge-worker moved first. They went to the side channel and spun the isolation wheel with both hands. The wheel resisted, then yielded. The pipe vibration changed. The baseline hum dropped a fraction. The doubled harmonic stayed.

Jhace logged it immediately.

**Action:** Junction Twelve isolated at side channel valve.  
**Result:** baseline hum reduced; doubled harmonic persists.

The wrench-holder’s hands shook on his wrench. Anger, not tremor. He still looked afraid.

“You just dropped heat to an anneal house,” he said.

“I prevented an uncontrolled failure,” Jhace said.

Dust fell again from the ceiling seam. A narrow shower. It landed on the collar and stuck to the wet sealant bead.

Then the tunnel floor gave a small shudder.

Not enough to knock anyone down. Enough to make the lamp flames twitch and the tool cloth edge creep on stone. Enough to make the workers’ breath catch.

Jhace felt his weight reduce for a fraction of a heartbeat. His boots lost full pressure against the floor. Then weight returned and his knees flexed to absorb it.

No one spoke for one breath. Then two.

The wrench-holder whispered, “What was that.”

Jhace kept his gaze on the collar seam. The bead trembled. The doubled harmonic stayed constant.

“Not settlement,” Jhace said. “Not a local shift.”

The wedge-worker swallowed. “Then what.”

Jhace did not answer with guesses. He answered with the next step.

“Show me the junction map to Sector Fourteen,” he said.

The wrench-holder’s eyes narrowed. “Why.”

“Because the harmonic is stronger deeper,” Jhace said. “I need the source direction.”

“There isn’t a source,” the wrench-holder said, then stopped, because his own ears told him the same thing Jhace’s did.

The wedge-worker pulled a slate map from a wall pocket. Lines marked heat-pipe routes and junction plates. Junction Twelve sat on a trunk line that fed three districts. Sector Fourteen was a deeper junction cluster where the trunk line split and rejoined around an older stone pocket.

Jhace marked the map with a charcoal nib. “This route. I take it.”

The wrench-holder leaned in, voice low now. “If you’re wrong, you cost people heat.”

“If I’m right,” Jhace said, “we cost fewer people lives.”

The words came out harsher than he intended. He let them stand.

He closed his kit and re-secured the strap. He looked once more at the collar.

“Leave it isolated,” he said. “Brace it if you must, but don’t re-open flow until I return with a full assessment.”

The wrench-holder’s throat moved. “And if you don’t return.”

Jhace did not like the question. He answered it anyway.

“Then send a runner to Bram,” Jhace said. “Tell him the trunk line at Twelve is isolated under inspector order. He’ll know what to do with wedges and manpower.”

The name steadied the workers. Bram’s workshop sat above, half broken, still useful. Bram had a way of making the impossible into a list of tasks.

Jhace turned and started down the tunnel that led toward Sector Fourteen.

The Underface air changed as he went deeper. Less lamp smoke. More mineral damp. The copper taste sharpened again. The doubled harmonic grew cleaner, less buried under the baseline.

He stopped at Junction Thirteen. Palm to pipe.

The vibration hit his skin with immediate clarity. Two pitches. The second one carried a narrow edge that made his teeth buzz.

He wrote it.

**Site:** Junction Thirteen  
**Finding:** doubled harmonic stronger. Baseline stable. No heat spike.

Dust fell on rhythm again. He counted the interval with his breath and wrote the count in the margin. Timed pulses. Not random.

His lamp beam caught copper-gold particulate in the air. It floated slow. It settled in the seam corners. It stayed on his skin where sweat had started under his collar.

The city above would be running, screaming, rebuilding, dying. Here, in the Underface, the grid spoke first. It spoke in vibration and dust fall and pressure.

He followed it.

At the bend where the tunnel dropped another half level, he paused and pressed his palm flat to the stone wall itself. Not pipe. Stone.

The shudder came up through his hand. A pulse. Then nothing. Then another.

His stomach tightened. His throat tightened. His hands stayed steady.

He lifted his slate and wrote the line he needed to see later, when panic and fatigue would try to rewrite memory.

**Assessment:** pressure under foundation. Off-local. Persistent. Escalating.

He put the slate away and moved deeper into Sector Fourteen, following the doubled harmonic into the dark.

### Tide 3, Quench phase — Underface kiln (Saint Merro)

It just wouldn’t stop. The tremor.

Jhace’s right hand shook in short bursts that made his fingernails tick against his palm when he tried to close it. He opened it again. He watched the skin across his knuckles jump.

The Underface kiln corridor sat quiet around him. Unfired furnaces lined the walls. Their mouths were dark. Old soot clung to the brick lip where heat used to run. The air was not cold, but it pulled damp off the stone and left it on his forearms anyway.

This is bullshit, Jhace thought.

He paced the length of the kiln bay and back, boots scraping grit, heel catching on a seam in the floor where a plate had been replaced wrong. He stepped over it the next pass. He stepped over it again. He made the same correction twice because the first time hadn’t counted.

His left hand stayed steady. His right refused.

That was the cost. Late. Useless. Interest on a debt he didn’t remember agreeing to.

He stopped at a low work bench that used to hold molds. Tonight it held nothing except a folded work cloth and a thin slate he’d dragged down here because he couldn’t keep the numbers in his head anymore.

Twenty-three.

He didn’t need the slate to remember that part. He needed it to keep the rest from moving.

Jhace set the slate down flat and tried to write the first name with a stub of chalk. His fingers clenched. The chalk snapped. White dust fell onto his boot.

He stared at the broken piece on the floor.

It was my fault, he thought.

He picked up the longer half with his left hand and shifted it to his right. The tremor knocked it against the slate, leaving a streak that was too thick and angled wrong.

I should have been able to help.

He pressed harder, like force would solve a problem of control. The chalk ground into the slate and squealed. The sound cut into his teeth. He pulled back.

He wiped his face with the work cloth without thinking about it. The cloth came away damp. He looked at it. He didn’t allow himself to name what it was.

Not today, he thought.

Tears were expensive. He couldn’t afford tears.

The kiln bay stayed silent. No one answered him. No one corrected him. No one did the normal thing of telling him to sleep and stop making it worse. That was why he’d come here.

He lifted his right hand and held it out in front of the nearest furnace mouth. He tried to steady it by locking his elbow. The tremor shifted into his forearm anyway. It climbed.

“Why now!!?!?” he yelled, voice cracking on the last word. It hit the brick and came back thinner.

He stepped closer to the unlit forge mouth, as if distance was the problem.

“You could have given me this when I needed it!” His breath came hard. “You could have—”

His right hand twitched and the chalk fell again. It hit the stone and rolled until it stopped against a drain channel.

Battling with himself showed on his face because he wasn’t built to hide it. He didn’t have the practice. He had practice at other kinds of hiding.

“i could have saved them,” he said, quieter. “then.”

He swallowed. His throat refused the next breath for a second.

i could have saved them.

His mind ran the loop again because it didn’t know what else to do with a number that didn’t change.

Full sentence.

Shorter version.

Single word.

Then.

Footsteps entered the corridor behind him. Measured. Not trying to be quiet. Not trying to be loud.

Jhace didn’t turn. His shoulders lifted once and dropped. He kept his eyes on the furnace mouth because if he turned he’d have to manage his face, and that was another expense.

Tiffani stopped at the threshold of the kiln bay. She let her eyes take the room in three passes: floor seams, furnace mouths, bench, slate. Then she let her eyes take him. Hands. Posture. Breathing rate.

Jhace’s right hand still shook, even at rest.

He heard the scratch of her boot shifting to a stable stance. He heard the soft clack of glass plates in her satchel as she set it down.

“Your tremor’s frequency changed,” she said.

Jhace laughed once. It came out dry. “Fuckin’ congratulations.”

Tiffani did not react to the profanity. She crouched and picked up the snapped chalk from the drain channel, not because she cared about the chalk but because it showed her where his hand had lost it. She held the piece between two fingers, inspected the break face, then set it on the bench beside the slate.

“You came to a kiln,” she said. “Unfired. Away from people. Away from load. That implies you were either avoiding work or testing something without admitting it.”

“Don’t do that,” Jhace said.

“Do what.”

“Turn me into a problem you can solve.”

Tiffani’s mouth tightened, then released. She looked at the slate again. The thick angled streak. The snapped chalk. The blank space where names should have been.

“A ledger is a trigger object,” she thought, and immediately hated the neatness of the phrase. She heard herself thinking in labels. She heard the part of her that wanted to hide behind that.

Back to work, she told herself.

She reached into her satchel and pulled out her spare slate. Clean. She set it down beside his. Then she opened her logbook on the bench with careful hands, as if care was only about not smudging ink.

Ink was expensive too.

She drew a header line and wrote without looking up.

**Log: Tide 3 — Quench phase**  
**Location:** Underface kiln corridor, south bank, Furnace Row 6  
**Subject:** Jhace Merrow — post-hold cost persistence  
**Observation:** Right-hand tremor present at rest; increases with attempted fine motor tasks (writing); decreases with left-hand substitution; breathing irregular following verbal outburst.

She paused. The pause had a function. It let her listen.

The grid hum reached this deep as a low vibration in the floor. It was not loud, but it existed. It always existed. Tonight it was slightly off, a fraction in pitch that made her molars feel it.

Jhace’s tremor hit a sharper cadence when that pitch shifted.

Tiffani’s pen hovered above the page.

This is data, she thought. Keep it that way.

But she looked at him anyway.

Jhace stood with his head angled down. His jaw worked once as he swallowed. He kept his right hand pinned to his thigh like he could hold it still by force.

He was losing a fight with his own body. He was losing it in a room where no one could see him lose. That choice mattered.

A cost worth paying, she thought.

There’s no bargain.

The thought landed in her chest and tried to become something else. She cut it off.

“For fuck’s sake, stay on task, Tiffani,” she whispered, low enough that if he heard it she could pretend it was about ink.

Jhace turned his head slightly. Not fully. Enough to show he’d heard something.

“What do you want,” he said.

Tiffani kept writing.

“I want you alive,” she did not say.

Instead, she said, “I want a controlled test.”

Jhace’s laugh came again, shorter. “In a kiln. With no heat. Sure.”

Tiffani closed her logbook halfway, leaving her thumb as a marker. She slid her satchel closer and pulled out three thin glass plates wrapped in cloth. Spare lens plates, cut clean, edges ground. She set them on the bench in a line.

Jhace’s eyes tracked them despite himself.

“You carry glass into a kiln corridor,” he said. “That’s either confidence or a death wish.”

“It’s a field kit,” she said. “Chalk, cord, plates, spare slate. You can mock it later.”

She took the cord out next. She anchored one end around a bench leg and stretched it across the floor seam he’d been stepping over, marking a straight reference line. She did it fast and correct, hands efficient.

Jhace watched her hands. He didn’t want to. He did.

“Here’s the test,” Tiffani said. “You’re going to pick up the plate with your right hand.”

Jhace stared at her.

“No,” he said.

“That is not an answer,” Tiffani said. “That is avoidance.”

He stepped forward. The tremor made his boot scuff. “I’m not gonna do your little fuckin’ study so you can write me down and hand it to the RRO.”

Tiffani’s eyes went flat at the acronym. “Harlen already asked. I declined.”

Jhace blinked. That hit him somewhere he didn’t guard.

Tiffani continued, “This isn’t for oversight. This is for efficiency. You spent yourself holding a beam and a support when you didn’t have to spend that much. If we can reduce the cost, we do. That is the entire argument.”

Jhace looked away. His right hand shook harder.

Tiffani waited. Three heartbeats. Then four. She watched his breathing settle into something closer to steady because he was fighting for control again. He liked control. That was not judgment. That was a fact.

Jhace stepped to the bench. He reached out with his right hand.

It jerked sideways an inch before his fingers even touched the plate. He clenched his jaw and tried again. The tremor made his fingertips tap the glass twice. The plate slid a fraction across the bench.

He snatched it with his left hand instead and held it up like a trophy. “See? Problem solved.”

“That is a different test,” Tiffani said.

Jhace’s eyes flashed. “I don’t have time to play—”

“You’re in an unfired kiln at Tide 3,” Tiffani said. Her voice stayed level. “Time is not the scarce resource here. Control is.”

Jhace’s mouth opened. Closed. He looked at the slate again. The blank space. The broken chalk.

That was the real reason he was here. Not the tremor. Not the test. The blank space.

Tiffani saw it. She wrote it down in her head and did not put it on paper.

Trigger object.

Self-prosecution.

Loop.

She had read the pattern in herself too many times to miss it in him.

Jhace set the glass plate down with his left hand. He put his right hand flat on the bench, palm down, spreading his fingers. The tremor made each finger twitch in its own rhythm.

He stared at it, then up at her.

“This is bullshit,” he said again, but softer now, less a curse and more an admission that he didn’t have another word.

Tiffani nodded once.

“Yes,” she said. “Now do it anyway.”

Jhace’s throat worked. He lifted his right hand and curled his fingers around the glass plate.

It rattled in his grip. A thin sound. The plate did not crack. His grip tightened.

Tiffani watched the angle of his wrist. The tension in his forearm. The way his shoulder rose to compensate. She saw the cost building in the wrong place.

“Stop,” she said.

Jhace froze, plate still in hand, shaking.

“That’s not a stop,” he snapped. “I can hold it—”

“You can hold it with muscle,” Tiffani said. “You cannot hold it with precision. That difference matters.”

He stared at her as if she’d insulted him. She didn’t move. She kept her eyes on his wrist.

“Set it down,” she said.

Jhace set it down. The plate clicked against the bench. His hand lifted away and shook empty now, as if it didn’t know what to do without an object to fight.

Tiffani picked up her chalk and drew a small mark on the bench surface. Then another. Three marks in a tight triangle.

“Those are reference points,” she said. “You will touch the plate only at the edges aligned to these marks. No squeezing. No compensation through your shoulder. If you need to brace, you brace with your left hand on the bench leg. Not your right.”

Jhace stared at the chalk marks. He stared at her mouth as she spoke, as if he could catch the trick there.

He didn’t trust tricks.

He trusted load.

He trusted failure.

Tiffani’s pen was back in her hand. She wrote one line in the logbook without looking down, because her hand knew the motion.

**Hypothesis:** Tremor increases when force disperses through non-target joints; precision constraints may reduce cost.

Jhace watched her write. His eyes snagged on the words he couldn’t read from that angle. That bothered him.

“Are you gonna tell me what you’re writing,” he said.

“Yes,” Tiffani said. “When it’s accurate.”

Jhace’s mouth twitched. It was not a smile. It was the shape of a smile that didn’t get paid.

He reached again, right hand hovering over the plate, lining his fingers up with the marks.

His tremor hit. He corrected. He corrected again.

He got his fingertips on the glass edge in the right places. He lifted.

The plate rose off the bench without the rattle this time. His hand still shook, but less. The movement stayed in the fingers instead of climbing.

Tiffani watched his forearm. The vein-lines at his wrist were faint but present, copper-dark under skin. She did not use the word that wanted to form in her mind. She used measurement instead.

“How hot are your palms,” she asked.

Jhace blinked, caught by the directness. “What.”

“Answer,” Tiffani said.

Jhace swallowed. “Warm.”

“Warm is not a number,” Tiffani said.

He exhaled hard through his nose. “Hotter than normal. Not burning.”

Tiffani nodded. “Good. Set it down.”

He set it down. His hand jerked once after release, as if it expected resistance and found none.

Jhace stared at his right hand. Then at her chalk marks. Then back at his hand.

“That worked,” he said. He sounded angry about it.

Tiffani’s chest tightened. She kept her face still.

“Yes,” she said. “Not enough. But yes.”

Jhace’s jaw flexed. “So what, you’re gonna draw on everything I touch now.”

“If you want to keep using it,” Tiffani said, “then yes. I will mark. You will hold. We will reduce waste.”

Jhace laughed once, sharp. “Waste.”

Tiffani looked at the blank slate again, then at him.

“Waste,” she repeated. “That is what uncontrolled cost becomes. You can call it suffering if you want to feel noble. I am calling it what it is.”

Jhace’s right hand shook again, slower now, fatigue creeping in.

His eyes went wet. He wiped them with the work cloth fast, brutal.

Not today.

Tiffani watched the wipe. She logged it in her head. She did not log it on paper.

“You were yelling at the forge,” she said.

Jhace’s shoulders rose. “Yeah.”

“Tell me why,” Tiffani said.

Jhace’s mouth opened. He shut it. He tried again.

“Because it didn’t give me the tremor when it would’ve mattered,” he said. “It waited. It waited until after.”

Tiffani’s pen moved. One line.

**Note:** Subject experiences cost as delayed and therefore unjust; resentment focused on timing.

Jhace saw the pen move and snapped, “Stop writing.”

Tiffani stopped. She set the pen down with care.

She stepped closer. Not into his space. Close enough that her voice did not have to travel.

“I am not writing to punish you,” she said.

Jhace stared at her.

“I am writing,” she continued, “because your memory is not reliable when you’re in a loop. Mine isn’t either. Paper is.”

Jhace’s breathing hitched. The tremor in his hand spiked once.

“The number was twenty-three,” he said, voice low. “And I didn’t—”

He stopped. He couldn’t get the rest out without turning it into a verdict.

Tiffani waited. She let the silence do its work.

Jhace tried again.

“I didn’t hold,” he said. “Long enough.”

Full sentence.

Shorter version.

Single word tried to form.

Then.

Tiffani felt her throat tighten. She swallowed it down.

Precision first.

“I can’t change the number,” she said. “But I can change what happens the next time you’re asked to hold something that would kill people if you fail.”

Jhace’s eyes flicked up. Fast. Hungry. Terrified. All at once.

Tiffani’s voice stayed even.

“I need you to let me mark your work,” she said. “Not because you’re incompetent. Because you’re strong, and strength without geometry burns fuel for no reason.”

Jhace’s laugh came out broken. “You really know how to talk to a man while he’s bleeding.”

“I am not good at softness,” Tiffani said. It was the closest she got to confession. “I am good at results.”

Jhace looked at the chalk marks. He looked at the glass plate.

He nodded once.

One step completed.

Tiffani’s chest released a fraction. She hated that it felt like relief.

Fucking perfect, she thought. Now don’t ruin it.

She picked up her pen again and wrote the last line for the entry.

**Decision:** Establish marking protocol for holds. Subject compliance obtained. Next step: field trial on live load under supervision.

Tiffani closed the logbook. She tapped it once with her fingertips as if sealing the decision into paper.

Jhace stood still for a moment, right hand shaking at his side, but his eyes were on the marks now instead of the blank slate.

He wasn’t fixed. He wasn’t forgiven. He wasn’t done.

But the loop had stopped because it hit a wall of procedure.

That was enough for this Tide.
