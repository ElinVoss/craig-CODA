# Novella 1: The Friction Testament

## Chapter 1

So, this story here, is one that grinds the cogs into steam that drive the gears of ghosts you wished didn't exist. It is personal, detached and existential. Among the Glass Peaks of Te'Oga, the first structures from Grindheim have surfaced the ridges. A contrasting scene when civilization beyond those mountains peaks was never explored. I never seen anything like it in my years traveling the belts across the oceans and plains and industrial risings though history. Many of us on Te'Oga were too busy with keeping the world going. Keeping the argument consistent. Me personally, would rather quiet. It's a shame that ones most desired action requires nothing at all, yet not a single one can abide by it. Therefore, we live with the noise. Over time, and eras and revolutions transforming this world into what it is now, was fucking utterly wasted precious moments that could have been used for just a little silence. Grindheim would be the loudest of all the noise. Not just in terms of what can be heard. But what was felt. Harmonized. True. 

Tiffani stepped off the belt onto the receiving platform. Stretched. Tired. She found her bags and proceeded to the main terminal. It was an old station, newly reopened. You could see the rust forming from the moisture from the conveyor that stretched out farther than any gear tech could see.

Her boots struck the wooden deck with a thud that traveled up through bone, jarring her from three days of numbing rhythm. The air here bit sharp—pine resin and damp earth, nothing like the sterile hum she'd left behind. She adjusted her pack, straps digging into shoulders still tender from harness inspections, and swept her gaze across the sparse setup: raw timber cantilevered over sheer drop, cable incline anchored to granite, a solitary bell mast swaying in the wind. No diagnostic panels. No manifests. Just a weathered ledger box chained to the rail, clasp tarnished but holding.

The irregular rasp emanated from uphill—gears meshing imperfectly, not quite mechanical fault but close enough to trigger her training's caution. She pressed a hand to her chest, felt the thump beneath ribs, attributed the constricting squeeze to altitude rather than anything unsettling.

"You the outsider?"

She turned. A man crested the narrow path, rag clutched in one hand, wiping dark grease from corded forearms. Lean, honed by labor. Shirt sleeves rolled to elbows, trousers patched at knees. No badges. No tools dangling. Just the rag, twisting restlessly between fingers incapable of stillness.

His eyes met hers.

The Pull was a tide, threatening to drown my better judgment. The squeeze in her chest intensified into a vise—metal plates grinding under strain, building with relentless force. Beneath it, a deeper tug stirred low in her gut, warm and unyielding, drawing her toward him like a chain catching on an unseen snag. Then came a sharp prick behind her eyes, cool and precise, honing the world into crystalline edges. Threaded through was a subtle looseness, a release of tension built over days, offering deceptive relief.

Four forces tangled like overworked cables, vying for dominance as her stance shifted involuntarily.

"Tiffani," she replied, voice even despite the internal turmoil. "From Coastal Authority. I'm here for the span consult on the incline systems."

He gave a single nod, rag still working across skin, jerked his chin toward the uphill path. "Come on, then."

Not phrased as invitation, but she followed anyway, legs protesting the immediate bite after flat, unyielding belt motion had left muscles dulled. The switchbacks clawed at her thighs, pack grinding deeper into shoulders, yet the rasping sound grew louder, drawing her onward like a lodestone. Those sensations accompanied every movement—pressure coiling tighter, tug warming her core with unfamiliar insistence, prick needling focus to a razor's edge, looseness tempting surrender to the ascent's rhythm.

I've seen this before, you understand. The universe's argument made flesh—two bodies moving toward collision not because they chose it, but because Friction demands it. The cost of this meeting? Probably more wasted time that could've been spent in silence. But here we are, grinding forward anyway.

As she crested the rise, the full extent unfolded: towering belts looping around massive drums and clutches, tension screws catching morning light with a glint of oiled metal, a heat fin protruding at an awkward angle, scarred by exposure. The space open to elements, unshielded and raw. Two figures on deck observed the belt's cycle, postures tense as the irregular rasp rose and fell in cadence that felt almost alive.

"Misalignment in the lower pulley," she stated automatically, training kicking in. "You need to halt the operation and run a full inspection—"

"It's seated," he interrupted, already stepping toward tension screws, rag tucked into pocket, movements economical and assured.

"It's grinding," she countered, edge sharper than intended.

"Breathing," he said simply, flattening his hand against the passing belt, eyes half-closing as he attuned to vibration humming through material, body holding perfectly still amid mechanical pulse.

If you want to braid our hands and silence the tremor, the geometry of our hearts has to be true first, or that scar will never fade. The pressure in her chest spiked sharply, then redistributed and steadied, mirroring the assembly's subtle shift. The tug surged forward, linking her core to the point of his touch as if an invisible connection had formed. The prick flared, illuminating the rasp's underlying pitch with newfound clarity, while looseness deepened, easing the persistent ache in her legs.

He crouched beside the belt, selected a forked tool from a nearby hook, applied it to a tension screw—a precise half-turn outward that allowed the belt's path to ease just a fraction. The grind transformed, not eliminated but reshaped into something clearer, more honest. Tiffani felt her own chest echo the change: pressure receding slightly, tug settling into comfortable anchor, prick honing perception further, looseness flowing through like oil smoothing a seized joint.

Next, he moved to the heat fin, fingertips delicately adjusting vanes with feather-light touch. The prick blazed—world snapping into vivid detail. Flecks of grease suspended in air, intricate grain of surrounding wood, faint scars on his knuckles from countless similar tasks. Returning to the clutch, he slid a thin shim beneath the mounting plate, secured it with a tap. The tug took deeper root, infusing her frame with warm belonging she couldn't quite place.

Three moves. Minutes. The belt's song evened out, the rasp now carrying raw authenticity rather than discord. Tiffani shook her head, knees locking to steady against unexpected resonance.

"Jhace," he introduced himself as he stood, pocketing the shim with casual flick, gaze returning to the now-smoother operation.

"That's not standard protocol," she pressed, voice thinner than she'd like, laced with frustration of ingrained methods. "Making adjustments on the run—you can't isolate the variables like that. How do you even know if it's truly fixed or just temporarily shifted?"

"You seat it first," he replied, tone matter-of-fact, gesturing broadly across the assembly. "Then you know for sure. It belongs in place, or it doesn't. You hear it. You feel it. The graphs and readings—they don't tell the whole story."

*Feel it.* The words hung, and her chest pulsed once more, four sensations aligning in sharp, unified rhythm that left her momentarily off-balance.

See, this is where the Noise gets interesting. Not the sound—the existence. The argument between what should be still and what insists on moving. Jhace here, he's the universe's advocate for Friction. Tiffani's world sealed it away, traded the rasp for smoothness, and now she's standing here feeling what was amputated.

Her vision blurred at edges. "In my world, we have sensors on everything—tension monitors, thermal scans, predictive algorithms. There haven't been failures in cycles because we—"

"How do you stop it?" Jhace interrupted, gaze steady and unyielding. "Your belts, the ones you rode here. If something goes wrong mid-run, how do you stop them?"

Her throat went dry, sensations falling eerily still.

"You signal for assistance," she said finally, words measured. "There are stations positioned every fifty meters along the line. Response time is under two minutes—"

"But you can't stop it yourself," he pointed out, voice calm but pointed.

The pressure evaporated in a rush, leaving hollow void. The tug chilled to uncomfortable distance. The prick turned accusatory, sharpening awareness of the flaw in her certainty. Looseness soured into unsettling emptiness, as if all tension holding her together had simply unraveled.

Her knees buckled slightly, and she caught herself on the rail, heat flooding her face as the two deck workers shifted attention elsewhere, politely averting eyes. "It's safe that way," she rasped, grasping for composure. "One person's override could strand thousands—disrupt the entire network—"

"For who?" Jhace asked, question simple yet piercing.

"For everyone," she shot back, words tasting hollow.

"Everyone can't stop it either."

The words lingered, unanswerable.

Her chest felt empty now, echoing the silence of three days on a flawless belt run, devoid of interruption. Those thirty seconds of raw friction—of feeling the machine's pulse through his hands—had vanished, and their absence gnawed worse than any persistent grind.

This is the cost I'm talking about. Thirty seconds of Friction versus three days of perfect Silence. Which one's alive? Which one's just death pretending to be efficiency?

Jhace framed the assembly with his hands once more, performing final visual check, focus returning to work. "You're tired from the ride. There's a deck down that way for rest. The ledger's on the rail—put your name and hours. The clerk will find you a bunk."

She wanted to mount a defense, to recite her certifications, the efficiencies of the system that had carried her here without falter, unbidden and precise. Instead, her eyes drifted back to the belt, now humming its breathing song, alive with subtle imperfections.

Pressure flickered back to life, tentative. Tug warmed once more. Prick refocused her gaze. Looseness tempted her to listen closer.

Stillness had always meant safety. Smoothness had been the hallmark of rightness, sealing out the chaos of friction.

Three days of perfect, unyielding stillness.

And thirty seconds of friction made incarnate.

Was perfection just another form of stillness? Of death?

"Thanks," she murmured, word slipping out quieter than intended.

He nodded, turning back to his tasks without another word.

The rail ledger lay open: a cloth-bound volume, pages thumbed and grease-stained from countless entries. She scrawled her name, origin, time of arrival, hand steady despite lingering churn.

But when it came to the departure field, her pen hovered, refusing to commit.

Sensations cycled faintly, refusing to settle into quietude.

She left it blank.

The belt rasped on, testament to reality's stubborn refusal to remain perfectly still.

Friction was supposed to hurt, after all.

Being alive was supposed to hurt.

And so she listened, exhaustion tugging but curiosity hooking deeper still.

Her chest pulsed again: pressure building boundaries, tug drawing toward wholeness, prick clarifying pure notes amid noise, looseness offering entropy's quiet relief.

It was waking her, this place—this man—this song.

Jhace glanced back over his shoulder, catching her lingering stare as she closed the ledger with soft snap. His hand paused on the assembly's frame, rag forgotten in pocket. The tug within her deepened, not just toward the work but toward him specifically—warm and insistent, like precise slotting of chain links into proper grooves. He stepped closer without a word, closing distance until proximity alone began to ease internal churn, their signals harmonizing in subtle waves that steadied her breathing. His fingers brushed against hers where she gripped the rail, a touch that started accidental but lingered tactile, transferring faint grit of grease and sparking prick-sharp awareness of shared skin, synchronized pulses, and underlying rasp that now felt like their own shared rhythm.

She didn't pull away, and neither did he; the contact held, grounding four sensations into balanced hum, relief loosening deeper than any bunk or rest could provide.

"We start tomorrow," he said in low voice, thumb grazing her knuckle once more—intentional this time, quiet test of the fit they both seemed to sense.

Her chest settled fully, tug rooting firm. Consent passed unspoken between them, but it was there, solid as the incline's cables.

Tomorrow.

## Chapter 3

Tiffani's boots crunched over packed earth of eastern yard, gravel shifting underfoot like reluctant bearings in dry socket, as she made her way toward low sheds framing the work area. Midday sun had crested valley rim, casting long shadows from scattered parts and half-assembled frames that dotted space, metal edges glinting with residue of recent oiling. She could still feel faint warmth from Jhace's thumb on her wrist, tactile echo that lingered in skin, harmonized signals from their proximity easing constant churn in chest to manageable hum. But now, with springcar staging behind and deck hands' distant clinks fading, pressure rebuilt subtly, reminder of consult's demands pressing against her like unadjusted load.

Voices reached her before figures did, carrying over faint whine of tensioner being tested—sharp, measured tones laced with undercurrent of debate she recognized from coastal briefings, though here it felt rawer, less buffered by protocol.

"—not about efficiency alone; it's about the feel under hand. Your numbers might say it holds, but the operator won't trust a wind-down they can't hear settling."

That voice belonged to a man, younger than Jhace by timbre, edged with precision of someone who measured twice before committing to a turn.

"Trust comes with training," came the reply, smooth and assured, carrying polish of someone accustomed to boardrooms rather than decks. "Give them a week of silent operation, and the old rasp becomes unthinkable. Progress proves itself—show them better, and they adapt without question."

Listen to this. This is the voice of amputation dressed as advancement. I've heard it in every era, every civilization that decided Friction was the enemy instead of the proof of life.

Tiffani rounded corner of nearest shed, scent of heated metal and fresh-turned soil rising to meet her, grounding abstract tensions in core. There, kneeling beside partially assembled springcar, was source of first voice: a man in mid-twenties, dark-skinned with close-cropped hair, canvas apron streaked with oil as he wielded calipers against exposed tensioner assembly. His movements exact, no excess motion, each measurement deliberate as if tolerances were sacred lines not to be crossed.

Korv, she recalled from Jhace's mention earlier—rival fitter, edges and measures over feel and seat.

Standing over him was another figure, mid-thirties, clad in traveling clothes that seemed out of place amid yard's grit: pressed linen jacket, leather satchel at side, boots polished to sheen untouched by valley dust. "Ah, you must be the Authority consultant," latter said, extending hand. "Tiffani, isn't it? Drell, at your service—independent contractor, bridging vendors to sites like this. Just outlining how sealed tech from the network could elevate their local rigs."

Tiffani shook his hand briefly, grip firm but cool, then turned to springcar. It was compact, four wheels on open frame, bench for two, spring drum visible under propped hood—coiled mass of tempered steel, wound tight enough to hum with latent force. No seals marred its housing; heat shimmered faintly from metal, and she could hear subtle tick of residual cooling, alive in way coastal lines never were.

"Understood." She nodded, positioning herself at respectful distance, slate tucked under arm for notes.

Drell observed exchange with faint amusement curling lips. "Charming, this procedural dance. In the wider world, we'd call it redundant—needing permission to observe. But I suppose in tighter communities, such rituals knit the fabric."

Sting in her chest sharpened, accusatory. "It's not ritual," she said evenly. "It's respect for the work. And the worker."

Drell's smile thinned fractionally, but he recovered smoothly, turning back to Korv. "Be that as it may, the offer stands: three sealed bearing kits, sensors pre-tuned, full docs for install. Trial's free—three months. Satisfied? We talk terms. Not? Return, no strings."

Korv's expression remained steady, hands wiping on apron. "I'll consider."

"Can't ask fairer." Drell snapped case shut with click that echoed like latch engaging. "I'll be at the station house next few days. Maelis has my reach." He nodded to Tiffani, smile practiced once more. "Pleasure meeting network blood. Dinner to trade notes on integration? Your take could sway things."

This is what I mean about Noise. Not just the sound of mechanisms—the Friction between what wants to be and what insists on being otherwise. Drell's selling silence like it's salvation, but silence is just death with better marketing.
