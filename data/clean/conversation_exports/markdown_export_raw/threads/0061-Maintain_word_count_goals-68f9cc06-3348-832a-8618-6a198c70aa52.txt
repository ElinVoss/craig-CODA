## **﻿Prologue — The Year the World Took a Breath**

The world ended once and decided it was too much trouble to stay dead. It didn’t break apart; it flexed, like a lung taking air after being punched. A pull went through the crust—straight, fast, absolute—and stopped just short of tearing clean through. What it left was a tunnel with no opposite mouth. People called it the Bore, because naming the wound makes it sound finished.  
From the Upper Crown, it looks like an even circle of shadow on maps. From the Underface, it’s a black horizon that presses in your teeth when the air gets heavy. Nobody pretends it’s a mystery anymore. We live around it, not above it. Every crack, every bridge, every load-bearing inch answers to what the Bore changed.  
It began with water taking new routes. Rivers turned, cliffs sagged, docks listed without warning. For a year the wind came up from the ground instead of the sky, and you could smell iron on your skin no matter how often you washed. People called it divine; the rest of us called it gravity. When the air stopped shaking, a handful of us were still standing, and we started measuring before we started praying.  
We found the veins by accident. They ran under the ground like nerves under skin, thin lines where force preferred to travel. When the veins slipped out of their old paths, they bled heat and pressure and made Harrow—the kind of rupture that doesn’t care if you’re watching. A few people, tired of being erased, learned to touch the veins and guide them. Those hands became Veinwrights. Their work became the city’s language: Trace. Set. Bridge. Veil. Slake. Tempermark.  
The Bore taught one rule that never stopped mattering: everything carries stress, and stress remembers. You can’t erase a fault; you can only give it a safer way to move. That’s what the craft is—negotiation between material and will. Every building stands because someone convinced it not to fall.  
Six months after the pull, the first bridges started breathing. At night you could see vapor lifting from the joints like ghost breath. No one remembers laying the mains that kept them warm, but the warmth stayed. It rose through basements and clinics, through pipes that had no valves, through the Conclave halls where the Veinwrights kept their ledgers. The city learned to live with the heat as a fact, not a gift. It kept people alive in winter, and it kept them honest—because if a line failed, everyone nearby heard it.  
When the air cooled, the Bells came. Not temple chimes—work signals. Long clear rings for reopen, two short for brace, fast beats for Harrow risk. Each Bell came with a board stamped in copper and sealed in glass. You could tell a city’s health by how clean its Tempermarks were.  
The first posted rule was ugly and perfect: One hand on the home ring. Hold something that won’t move while you touch what might. It was less philosophy, more survival.  
Then came the rest, written by people who’d already bled once for the knowledge: — Trace wet. Dry lines please eyes and lie to Veins. — Wedge before Veil. Cool the draft, then the glass. — Half-beat Slake. — Countermark cooler than birth. Pride bends things wrong. — No White. — Brace taps only in Bells Two and Three. You are not special in Four. — If it isn’t on the board, it didn’t happen.  
They’re not prayers. They’re methods. Watch a street when a blue HALT goes up and every hammer stops midair. Watch the crowd hold breath until the setter witness signs the clear. That’s how cities stay alive—by honoring pause more than noise.  
We forbid Overbind, because a clean disaster still kills you. We forbid Whitequench, because a lie in glass cuts deeper than heat. We measure by what doesn’t fail, not by what shines.  
After the Bore, Bells multiplied. Every trade learned its rhythm. The Couriers’ Bell marked motion; the Conclave’s marked repair. You could walk a full day and never be out of hearing range. It was noise that meant survival—call, answer, repeat.  
Six hundred and twelve rounds later, Te’Oga still leans a fraction toward the Bore. Maps mark the tilt with a single diagonal line through the city’s name. Children in the Underface draw circles in ash to guess the next drift. Crown engineers add decimal places to comfort themselves. Neither side is wrong.  
The Bore doesn’t threaten anymore. It just exists. We’ve made a kind of peace with the wound: keep it measured, keep it known, keep it held.  
Work here isn’t sacred. It’s necessary. The Conclave says “Trace wet.” The Couriers answer, “One hand on home.” Together, they make the city stay where it is.  
If you need a prayer, you use your hands: Trace wet. Wedge before Veil. Slake on the half-beat. Tempermark what you promise. Post it. The world isn’t healed. It’s held. That’s enough.

## **Chapter 1: Wet Trace at Blackgate**

Bell Two rang short across the Underface. Jhace climbed the ladder out of the low heat, hand closing on rung, the metal sweating under his palm. He came up into Blackgate’s choke where the carts were backed nose to nose, and the Posting board glared white with its Tempermark pin and droplet icon.  
Tiffani had already chalked a half-circle behind them so the crowd couldn’t press too close. She stood in the ring’s gap, one hand on her Veil case, the other tracing light against the stone seam. “Hold your tongue out of the way,” she said without looking. “It’s a quick one.”  
Jhace set his pack down, rolled his sleeves, and touched the home ring bolted to the post. “One hand on home.”  
She nodded, voice gone flat with routine. “One hand on home.”  
Brighlee arrived from the Conclave, still breathing through paper. “Work order nine-three-two. Wet Trace, Blackgate span. Setter Jhace brast, glass Tiffani veld, no hold.” She Tempermarked the droplet card, clipped it to the board, and stepped back. Tiffani’s eyes lingered on the ink longer than Brighlee’s hand—counting strokes, weighing silence. Names posted, Bell recorded. The carts quieted.  
The note from the Chain said quick Vein under the gate plate—heat moving wrong, a shallow slip that could cut the cart path clean through the Underface if ignored. Persuasion before force. Always persuasion first.  
Jhace unpacked what he needed: two wedges, seven-degree cut; copper-nib trace stick; bladder of wet. Nothing else. He checked his fray bands and the dull brass ring on his left wrist for crack. Sound.  
Tiffani tilted the Lume rod, spreading its beam along the seam. The light came back too bright—bad depth. She adjusted the Shade one mark darker until the glare steadied. “Fault’s running shallow,” she said. “Quick vein wants the short path.”  
“Short path’s under their wheels,” he answered. “We give it a long one.”  
He took his wedges, set the first two spans north of the gate mouth. A low angle, shallow drive. The second he planted at the shoulder where the seam ducked under the bridge stone. Two measured taps. Wood met resistance, then held.  
“Half-beat Slake,” Tiffani said.  
He thumbed the bladder cap, wet the copper nib, flicked the bead clear. He placed it to the face and drew the line from north to shoulder, pressure through the shoulder, not the wrist. Wet Trace moved slow, steam rising in thin threads that curled before dying in the air. The smell was clean—mineral, no burn.  
Tiffani adjusted her light. “Shade three to four. Keep it honest.”  
“No white,” he said. He traced through the curve, kept the bead unbroken. A cart axle squealed behind them, then stopped when the driver saw the droplet card. A brake eased. The lane held.  
The copper scratched across grit. Jhace froze, steadied, smoothed on the second pass. The bead almost broke. He held his breath until it rejoined. Tiffani’s knuckles brushed his sleeve, steadied his wrist like truing a wheel.  
“Stay,” she said.  
He did. Her hand rested against his wrist, steadying the tremor. His muscles quieted.  
At the gate mouth, the Vein flicked toward the wheel groove. Veins liked edges; they remembered where stone met air. Jhace flattened the nib, widened the wet into a shallow bow across the lip. The Vein answered—a tight pulse running north. “Turning,” he said.  
“Counter it,” she said.  
He drew a narrow correction line upstream, heat balancing through stone. The trace shimmered, found the longer route, settled. The vein stopped tugging. Pressure equalized. Even breathing again.  
“Veil small,” she said. She warmed a palm-wide Shade skin, stretched it over the new trace, and tapped the edges in sevens, each strike exact. “Annealshift?”  
“Zero. Let the wall hold its own heat.”  
She nodded. “Veil in. Trace held.”  
Brighlee wrote the line as it cooled. “Wedge set, trace complete, Veil sealed. Bell two and three.”  
“Sign it,” Jhace said.  
She passed him the stamp. He pressed the setter mark into the metal plate beside the droplet symbol. The ring’s bite echoed down the stone.  
Tiffani leaned back, listening. “Gate’s steady. Field calm.”  
He touched the plate with his bare hand. Warm, not burning. “Field even,” he said. The heat moved out and down like a slow breath. “Cost: fray band worn, thirst.”  
She passed the bottle. Veld water always tasted faintly of glass. He drank and gave it back.  
“Policy,” she said. “Clinic if the ring hisses past four.”  
He grunted agreement. Brighlee clipped the finished work order under the droplet icon, lifted her ledger so the crowd could see the names. Tiffani’s chalk mark still hung half‑erased on the stone, a line left unfinished. The carts shifted. Law to motion again.  
Jhace crouched once more, checking the seam. Wet line dull and even. No bubbles under the veil, wedges seated clean. The air above the trace no longer shimmered. “Blackgate’s quiet.”  
Tiffani smiled without teeth, the kind of smile that left the ledger unbalanced. “It remembers when we make it look good.”  
They packed in silence. Tools wiped, copper cooled, wedges tapped free. Brighlee closed her ledger with a single snap. The sound meant done.  
The first cart eased forward, front wheel passing the seam. The rim rolled the shallow bow and stayed silent. The driver glanced down, then up, gave the small nod that meant you did the thing; I saw you.  
They stayed until the third cart cleared, then unhooked from the home ring. Tiffani closed her case. Brighlee walked off, already writing the next line of work that would prove they’d lived this day correctly. Jhace lingered, eyes on the wet mark as it cooled. The steam had gone to thin breath, rising straight and vanishing against the stone.

## **Chapter 2: Thread Draw at Strathmoor Span**

Bell Four came late, smothered by fog off the river. The air sat wet enough to drink. Jhace crossed the east truss walkway, his boots scraping the rust film that always rose before thaw. Strathmoor Span was a good bridge when it wanted to be—broad face, six weight lines—but it carried a habit of slow leaks under the mid-beam couplers. Every thaw, someone found the problem too late.  
Tiffani was already halfway down the central run, kneeling over a length of open face, one palm on the metal, one arm buried to the elbow in a coil pit. She didn’t look up. “Thread slipped. South coupler. Field’s off-center. You feel it?”  
He knelt beside her and laid a hand flat on the deck. The vibration came up through his glove—a low push that staggered every third pulse. Not a crack, just uneven tension. “Field pulling north. Thread’s walked a quarter.”  
“Quarter?”  
“Quarter and a hair.”  
She made a mark on the plate with chalk, short horizontal dash. “That’s enough to make a vein forget itself.”  
Brighlee clanged down the stair, ledger box bumping her hip. “Work order seven-five-six. Thread Draw, Strathmoor. Setter Jhace brast, Glass Tiffani veld, no hold. Bell Four.” She Tempermarked the form, clipped it to the post, and stood aside to let them breathe.  
“Keep the carts back two lengths,” Tiffani said. “And watch for echo if the draw pulls too fast.”  
Jhace unpacked from his belt: two threaded rods, one brace clamp, half bladder of wet. Nothing else. He slipped the rods through the anchor flanges and tightened both heads finger-firm. “Ready on brace.”  
“Home ring?” she asked.  
He set his hand to the anchor post. “One hand on home.”  
“Half-beat Slake,” she said, and took position above him with the Lume angled down. The fog caught the light and turned the air to milk.  
He drew the first line of wet from the north coupler down into the seam. The water hissed where metal met stress. The thread—a slender vein of heat beneath the plate—shifted once and hesitated. He guided it slow, not pushing, not chasing, just offering a cleaner route.  
“Pull steady,” Tiffani said.  
“I’m not a cart driver.”  
“You’d crash one anyway.”  
The words sat warm between them, a scrap of talk to keep the hands honest. He pressed down, tracing the second run parallel to the first, copper nib close enough to taste heat through the glove. Steam rolled up his wrist.  
“The draw’s taking,” she said. “Shade three to five.”  
He adjusted the Lume’s glare with his shoulder, watching the water line bead, then flatten as it met its opposite pressure. The field steadied; the vibration under his knees leveled.  
“Coupler read?” she asked.  
“Pulse every eight. No skip.”  
“Then it’s breathing again.”  
She reached for her Veil case, drew out two small Shade 1 skins, warmed them over the hand brazier, and sealed the coupler mouth. The new skin caught the damp fog and turned it to bright sheen.  
Brighlee, back at the board, scratched ink slow enough that the strokes sounded like distant rain. “Thread draw complete,” she said. “Coupler sealed. Bell Four.”  
Tiffani straightened, back cracking once. “Test lift.”  
Jhace gave the signal. The chain crew took the leaf one finger up. The line ran even through the plates. He pressed his thumb to the coupler. Warm, no chatter. “Set.”  
She exhaled, the sound tight through her teeth. “We’ll call it half-day work if it doesn’t drift before dusk.”  
He packed his tools: copper nib cleaned on his sleeve, bladder recapped, rods released. “Costs?”  
“Palm burn, two Shade skins, no ring.”  
He nodded. “Fray band notch from clamp.”  
“Clinic if it splits,” she said.  
He flexed his hand, skin tight but whole. “Policy met.”  
The fog thinned enough to show the river moving below, dark and quick. A bell buoy swung under the span, knocking once, then going quiet.  
Mel Quarr arrived before they could finish stowing gear. Her coat carried old rain. “Strathmoor. What did it try this time?”  
“Thread drifted north,” Tiffani said. “Quarter and a hair. Reset, Slaked, Veiled. No hold.”  
Mel bent to the coupler, touched the new seam, and closed her eyes. The city’s best witness never looked; she felt. “You gave it a longer walk,” she said. “Good. The old route was too proud.”  
“Witness?” Tiffani asked.  
“Witness,” Mel said, straightening. She wiped her fingers on her coat and signed Brighlee’s ledger. “Post it.”  
Brighlee clipped the cleared slip under the droplet. The ink still wet, she waited for the air to fix it. “Law in effect.”  
The carts began to move again, a single wheel squealing, then catching rhythm. Jhace watched the strain lines across the span ease back into place—steel carrying itself again, the bridge alive but content.  
Tiffani leaned on the railing, eyes on the water. “You ever think it remembers the first one who traced it?”  
“Bridges don’t remember people. They remember treatment.”  
She gave the smallest shrug. “That’s people.”  
He didn’t argue. The fog folded them both into the same shape for a while, and that was enough.  
They gathered tools, slung packs, and crossed back toward the Underface stairs. Brighlee stayed behind to finish her ink. She always waited for it to dry before moving, superstition or craft—same thing down here.  
At the far end, Jhace looked back once. The Veil skins had cooled, light catching on them like dull glass. The seam between plates looked born that way, not repaired. That was the goal: leave no sign of having been needed.

## **Chapter 3: Counterbend at Lowcourt Arch**

Bell Six came sharp and bright through the Upper shafts, ringing against every pipe that held the morning fog. Lowcourt’s Arch never liked daylight—it showed what the night had hidden. A hairline split ran between the arch stones where the third rib met the walkway bracket, a clean crack widening by breath.  
Jhace was halfway up the scaffold before Brighlee finished reading the notice. “Work order eight-eight-one. Counterbend, Lowcourt Arch. Setter Jhace brast, Glass Tiffani veld. Local hold on half-lane until witness clear.” The last word echoed in the hollow under the span.  
Tiffani was already on the inner run, hand to the seam. “It’s new,” she said. “Two fingers deep. Warm on one edge.”  
Jhace crouched, running his glove along the joint. The air pushed out, steady pulse. “Thermal vein undercut the rib. We bend it back or lose the bracket by Bell Nine.”  
She tapped her chalk against the stone, marking the stress lines. “We’ll go double wedge, five-degree counter, and Slake at the midpoint. No Veil until it cools.”  
He unpacked two metal wedges, dull-edged from the last job, and a trace stick cut short for tight space. The arch smelled of lime dust and wet iron—old labor and new problem.  
“Clamp the east strut,” Tiffani said.  
He hooked his belt to the ring set in the beam. “One hand on home,” he said, not ceremony, just habit.  
She smiled with half her mouth. “One hand on home.”  
They moved like practiced hinge—she setting the chalk arc, he setting the wedges, rhythm falling into the old count. “First at angle, second at base,” she said.  
He slid the first wedge into the gap, shoulder against stone. Tap, tap. The wedge slipped.  
Tiffani’s eyes sharpened. “Angle?”  
“Too proud,” he said, pulling it free. He cut the angle flatter with his knife, tried again. This time it bit. The crack hissed. He set the second wedge opposite, tapping in counter rhythm. The arch groaned low.  
“Hold there,” she said. “Trace wet.”  
He thumbed the bladder, wetting the copper tip until the bead grew heavy. He ran the line between the wedges, two hands wide, guiding water into heat. Steam came off sharp and white; he worked through it.  
“Half-beat Slake.”  
He matched her voice to the work. Two fingers on the plate, slow breath, pulse steady. “It’s listening,” he said.  
“Let it answer,” she said. She lowered the Lume, blue light cutting the mist. The trace glowed dull red, not bright—a good sign.  
He traced the counterline across the top of the split, met the first run clean. The gap closed by half, a dark seam going gray as it cooled.  
“Drop wedges a count,” she said.  
He lifted his hammer, gave each head one measured strike. The arch gave back a sound like teeth setting. The air around them steadied.  
“Vein’s settled,” he said.  
“Still wants a leash,” she said, drawing a small loop with chalk over the join. “We’ll set a band.”  
He unwound a thin steel fray band, wrapped it once around the rib, drew it tight, and pinned it with the small rivet she handed him. The band clicked into place. “Hold?”  
“Hold,” she confirmed. “Shade it.”  
She warmed a small strip of Shade 2 skin between her palms and laid it over the scar, tapping in sets of seven. The smell of curing resin drifted up.  
Brighlee wrote from the scaffold base, voice low but steady. “Counterbend executed, Slake at midpoint, band set, Veil applied.”  
Tiffani looked down. “Bell six confirmed?”  
“Confirmed,” Brighlee said. “Witness on route.”  
The air shifted before Mel Quarr arrived—everyone knew her step, the way the Bells seemed to pause a fraction when she was near. She climbed the last rung without taking the offered hand. “You bent it clean?”  
“Quarter-gap closed to eighth, heat discharged through counterline,” Tiffani said.  
Mel touched the Veil with the back of her knuckle, then with the flat of her palm. She frowned.  
“Problem?” Tiffani asked.  
Mel pressed harder. “Just wanted to be sure.” She listened a count longer. “Witness. Post it.”  
Brighlee Tempermarked the slip, ink catching the light. “Counterbend, Lowcourt Arch, local hold cleared. Bell six.”  
Jhace exhaled, rolling his shoulder. “Cost: heat bite on right palm, one fray band spent.”  
Tiffani brushed her thumb across his wrist once. “Clinic if it blisters.”  
“Policy met.”  
Mel nodded toward the city. “You’ve got another seam at West Court before dusk. This one holds.”  
“Thanks,” Tiffani said.  
The crew packed down. Jhace wiped the wedges clean, brushed stone dust off the lip, checked the band for tightness. The arch held still.  
Below, the carts started again. A teamster looked up once, tipped his chin, and moved on. The bridge carried weight like it meant to.  
They left the scaffold, walking down into the tunnel of cooled air beneath the arch. The smell of stone after steam clung to them both.

## **Chapter 4: Heat Splice under Alderline**

Bell Eight hit in dull echoes under Alderline’s underbelly, the sound rolling slow through stone and steam. It was late afternoon, gray and thick, the kind of day that made metal taste of air instead of earth. The lower conduits had been running hot for two Bells past limit—enough to burn shade off a wall skin. By the time Jhace reached the site, sweat had already stripped the chalk from his collar.  
Tiffani stood by the south vent where the duct wall buckled. Her veil cloth was half soaked, but her voice stayed level. “Thermal vein tore through the splice. You feel the air?”  
He crouched, palm to conduit. The heat bit through the glove fast. “Alive and angry. Vein’s running blind.”  
“Blind means fast,” she said. “And if it finds the main, we’ll have a blow under the market.”  
“Field direction?”  
“East to west, downhill. No counter. Just run.”  
Brighlee appeared, collar up against the heat. “Work order one-one-five-four. Heat Splice under Alderline. Setter Jhace brast, Glass Tiffani veld. Local hold to foot traffic until clear.” She Tempermarked the droplet board with shaking fingers; the wax nearly ran before it set.  
“Chain it,” Tiffani said.  
Jhace fastened his tether to the wall ring, one hand gripping it. “One hand on home.” The metal burned even through his sleeve. “We wedge the run first.”  
“Use the short wedges,” she said. “The long ones will swell.”  
He pulled two half-lengths from his pouch, jammed the first at the upper seam of the duct mouth, the second two spans below. He hammered them in until the pitch rose high and sharp.  
The duct noise shifted—deep to shallow, like breath caught in the chest.  
“Trace wet,” she said.  
He thumbed the bladder cap, drew water into the copper nib, and began the run. The first line hissed as it met the heat; vapor curled into ribbons. He kept the nib steady, shoulder over hand, driving water into the split vein until the color under the metal dulled from white to gray.  
Tiffani moved her Lume rod closer. “Shade four to six,” she said. “Keep the bead thick.”  
He adjusted his wrist, laying the second line parallel, two fingers apart. Heat surged under his hand—vein testing him—but the wet held. Steam rolled off the metal in heavy breaths.  
“Half-beat Slake,” she said.  
He let the wet rest, pressure through shoulder, breath slow. The line steadied. The vein’s pulse dropped.  
“Still wants a mouth,” she said.  
“I’ll give it one.”  
He ran a short feeder line from the end of his first trace to a small vent slit above the duct. The air drew through it, hissing once, then falling quiet.  
The heat at his glove softened; the metal under his palm stopped twitching. Tiffani’s hand brushed his wrist once, not comfort but calibration, steadying his wrist until his pulse matched the duct.  
“Slake complete,” she said. “Now Veil.”  
He stepped aside. She drew a Shade 2 skin from her case, warmed it between her hands, and pressed it over the splice. Steam rose and hung there. Her fingers moved quick—tap tap tap in sets of seven. The seam went dark, settled.  
Brighlee’s pen scratched. “Heat Splice contained, Veil applied, local hold remains until witness.”  
“Tell them we’ll have it cleared by Bell Nine,” Tiffani said.  
Brighlee nodded and climbed back up the tunnel steps to keep the onlookers from drifting too close.  
Jhace checked his gloves. The leather had gone glossy, almost translucent. “Burn through,” he muttered.  
“Palm?” she asked.  
“Blister later. Not yet.”  
“Clinic if it lifts,” she said, without looking away from the seam.  
He grunted.  
The air shifted again—cooler this time. Steam thinned. The duct gave a single deep crack, then went still.  
“Field even,” Tiffani said.  
He leaned close, listening with his teeth. “Pressure five-finger drop. The vein found its line.”  
They waited half a count, because silence after a heat job lies half the time. When nothing moved, she nodded. “Test run.”  
He signaled up the tunnel. The vent valves opened. Air rushed through, caught in the new splice, turned direction like it had never meant to fight them. The response that came back was mechanical peace: even pulse, measurable.  
“Set,” he said.  
Mel Quarr came down through the vent shaft, wiping condensation from her glasses. “Alderline again,” she said, and crouched without ceremony. “Let me hear it.”  
Jhace shifted aside. She put one ear to the Veil and stayed still long enough to make them both hold breath. Then she nodded. “Stable. Hot vein cooled, no echo. You cleared it before the breach.”  
“Witness?” Tiffani asked.  
“Witness,” Mel said. “Post it.”  
Brighlee reappeared, ink already drying at the pen’s edge. “Heat Splice contained under Alderline. Local hold cleared. Bell Nine mark.” She clipped the slip, pressed the blue seal, held until it took.  
“Cost?” Tiffani asked.  
“Palm blister, glove dead. Fray band edge singed.”  
“Policy?”  
“Clinic if it lifts,” he said again.  
She handed him a new pair from her bag. “Use these next time.”  
He took them, thick leather still cool. “You planning another heat job?”  
“This city’s always planning another heat job,” she said.  
They gathered tools while the fog from the duct mixed with the cooler tunnel air. It smelled of burnt dust and new rain. The metal face they’d fixed stayed matte, clean.  
Brighlee finished her last mark and snapped the ledger shut. “Filed.”  
They climbed back to the street. The air above Alderline carried no heat shimmer now, only slow gray light.  
Tiffani looked down the length of the duct run, eyes tracing where the line bent invisible under stone. “If it holds through melt, we buy ourselves a season.”  
Jhace slung the dead gloves over his shoulder. “One more day of a world that works.”  
“Don’t get poetic,” she said.  
He didn’t.

## **Chapter 5: Lockfall at Greymantle Cut**

Jhace hit the pavement running, Bell Ten already shuddering through the pipes. The street pitched like a breathing lung. A lock had failed in the Cut, lower east face, the kind that took carts whole if left arguing.  
Tiffani was kneeling in runoff halfway down the slope, gloves black with silt, hair damp with steam. “Second lock’s sheared,” she said. “First’s holding by prayer. Get down here.”  
He slid in beside her. The air stank of boiled metal and canal gas. Water climbed over the curb in gray fingers. “How deep?”  
“Too deep.” She shoved a wrench into his hand. “Counterbrace’s stuck. I can’t see the pin.”  
He put his shoulder into it, turned once, twice. Nothing. The wrench teeth jumped, bit air.  
“Strip it and we’re drowned,” she said.  
“Already halfway.” He jammed the tip again, braced one knee against stone, and pulled. The wrench gave a sound like bone cracking. The pin twitched free—then stuck again.  
“Angle’s wrong,” Tiffani said.  
“No time for right.”  
He leaned harder. Metal shrieked. The lock gate shifted, not enough. Water surged up his chest, warm, rising fast.  
“Cut the feed line\!” she shouted.  
Brighlee’s voice echoed from the walkway above. “Valve’s jammed\!”  
“Then bleed it\!” Tiffani snapped.  
Jhace drew his hammer and drove it into the pin head. The gate lurched an inch. Water punched him in the throat. He couldn’t breathe. He struck again, blind. The pin jumped. The lock slid another inch.  
Tiffani grabbed his collar and hauled him back. The water line dropped half a hand.  
“Hold\!” she shouted.  
The gate creaked, uncertain. He pressed both palms to the metal, felt it trembling. “Vein’s live under it\!”  
“Trace wet\!”  
He fumbled the bladder, hands shaking, found the copper nib, scrawled a crooked line across the seam. The water hissed, turned steam, hissed again. The vibration smoothed. The gate took weight.  
She lowered her Lume, breath cutting short. “Pulse?”  
“Even. For now.”  
“Brace it anyway.”  
He reached for the short wedges. The first slipped from his hand, vanished in the runoff. The second stuck, skewed.  
“Wrong cut,” she said.  
“I know.” He sliced the edge flat with his knife, jammed it again. This time it bit.  
The low vibration dropped into the stone. The water stopped climbing.  
Brighlee clattered down the stair, half-drenched, ledger bag thumping. “Bell Ten and a half\! Setter Jhace brast, Glass Tiffani veld—Lockfall emergency\!”  
“Post later\!” Tiffani said. “Witness first\!”  
Mel Quarr stumbled in from the north end, coat dripping. “You’re lucky I was on the bridge,” she said.  
“Get down here,” Jhace said. “We need your ear.”  
Mel crouched, pressed her head to the lock seam. Her jaw tightened. “Still movement.”  
“We bled it\!”  
“Not enough.” She moved her hand lower, searching. “There’s another line.”  
Tiffani cursed. “The feeder vein.”  
Jhace turned, searching the wall through steam. “Where?”  
“Under you.”  
He knelt, groped through the flood, found the hairline crack near his boot. Warm water bubbled through. He shoved the copper nib into it, let the bladder feed until the hiss died.  
“Now?” he said.  
Mel stayed still for a long count. Then she looked up, doubtful. “Maybe.”  
“Maybe doesn’t clear it,” Tiffani said.  
Mel pressed her ear again, eyes shut. Silence. “Now.”  
Jhace slumped against the wall. The water around his knees had gone still, the kind of stillness that made sound travel.  
Brighlee wiped water from her ledger with the side of her sleeve. “Lockfall at Greymantle Cut contained. Local hold to remain partial until tomorrow inspection.”  
“Post it,” Tiffani said.  
Brighlee clipped the slip to the drenched board. The ink ran but stayed legible.  
Jhace peeled his glove. His palm was split across the web between thumb and forefinger, blood mixing with gray water.  
“Cost?” she asked.  
He flexed the hand; skin pulled. “Grip’s gone for a day.”  
“Clinic,” she said.  
“I said a day, not a week.”  
“Policy doesn’t care what you said.”  
He laughed once.  
The lock groaned, then quieted. Mel stood, brushing grit from her coat. “You held it,” she said. “Don’t expect thanks.”  
“Wouldn’t know what to do with it,” Jhace said.  
She almost smiled. “Good. You’ll get another call before dawn.”  
She left up the wet stairs, boots leaving perfect prints.  
Tiffani leaned on the gate, breathing hard. “You almost drowned.”  
He nodded. “Almost fixed it, too.”  
Her hand found his wrist, still wet. “Stay,” she said.  
He did.

## **Chapter 6: Corlexi’s Fire (Interlude)**

Bell Twelve rolled flat over the roofs, muffled by mist and smoke. Corlexi stood in the market square with both hands in the ash bin, pulling placards out one by one. Each card bore the old single‑brace symbol: two wedges facing inward, out of date by one Bell. Every lock failure that week had carried that mark. Policy changed this morning. Someone had to burn the proof.  
He held the first card by its corner. The wax Tempermark bled blue when the heat touched it, curling into itself like muscle recoiling. The stamp read Greymantle Cut—Work Order 2043\. He remembered the red handprint rumor before he saw the stain burned into the ink edge. “Lucky he didn’t drown,” he muttered, and dropped it into the flame.  
A group of apprentices watched from the steps, carrying fresh boards. They whispered to each other, eyes on the burn box. “Double brace rule?” one asked.  
Corlexi didn’t look up. “Double brace rule. Within ten rounds.”  
The fire spat once. Wax smell thickened. The second placard carried a name he knew—Maren hilt, a good setter who’d cracked a knee when a brace failed two months ago. He held it longer before letting it fall.  
On the far side of the square, the Couriers’ Bell office was already laying out the new boards. Ink crews worked off a folding table, stamping wedge glyphs two‑by‑two, copper heads hot enough to hiss when they touched wood. The pattern had changed overnight: old inward braces replaced by paired diagonals, more metal, more safety.  
“Quicker this way?” an apprentice asked.  
“No,” Corlexi said. “Slower, safer.”  
He fed the third placard into the bin. The crowd near the food stalls had gone quiet, watching the smoke. It wasn’t ceremony; it was relief disguised as boredom.  
A Conclave clerk crossed the square with a basket of new seals. “Posting priority corners first,” he said, voice careful. “The Conclave wants public compliance before dusk.”  
Corlexi wiped sweat from his cheek with the back of his arm. “They always do.”  
The clerk hesitated, holding a placard halfway. “You were there for Lowcourt, right?”  
“Doesn’t matter where I was.” He took the placard and set it into the fire. “Now I’m here.”  
The last of the old signs turned to smoke. He raked the ashes with a long iron rod until the glyphs broke apart. Behind him, the apprentices lifted the new boards, carried them toward the market posts, the stairwells, the corners by the canal. Every new mark would show two wedges instead of one.  
A teamster paused his cart to watch. “Another rule already?”  
Corlexi turned, voice even. “One that keeps your wheels on the ground.”  
The man tipped his cap and moved on.  
Corlexi knelt by the ashes, scooped a handful into a small cloth bag, tied it tight. He labeled the bag in careful script: Witness residue — old policy burned clean.  
By the time the Bell rang half, the square had changed color. The old blue of single braces had gone gray in the soot, replaced by fresh copper and paint that hadn’t yet dulled. Children traced the new glyphs with their fingers as they passed.  
From the east alley came a gust of steam—pipe vent clearing from Alderline’s repair. Corlexi looked up through it, saw the faint shadow of the Conclave tower against the fog. The world was always patching itself faster than people could keep up.  
An apprentice returned from the posting run, breath quick. “Every corner’s marked. Last one’s drying by the pump road.”  
Corlexi nodded. “Good. When it rains, the wax will tell us which ones hold.”  
“What if one doesn’t?”  
“Then we bury someone,” he said. “And trace it again.”  
The clerk from the Conclave sealed the record page with the last of his ink. “Policy change filed: all Cut locks to carry double brace before Bell Twenty‑two. Witness Corlexi, Market Station.”  
He signed his name small, a single curl at the end. “Filed,” he said.  
The clerk hesitated. “You burn them clean.”  
“Everything burns clean if you give it enough air.”  
The clerk left with the ledger. The apprentices lingered, unsure if they were dismissed. Corlexi pointed at the fresh boards stacked by the rail. “Post two more at the bridge ends before Bell Thirteen. People forget fast.”  
They moved, boots thudding.  
When the square cleared, Corlexi sat on the edge of the ash bin. The fire had gone to coals, orange and low. He held one unburned corner of an old placard, tracing the single‑wedge mark with his thumb. It wasn’t wrong when they made it; it was just small. The city had outgrown small.  
He tossed it into the coals and waited for the wax to hiss.

## **Chapter 7: Double Fault at Rindle Yard**

The Thread was already broken when they got there, hanging like split rope from the upper seam. Bell Two had barely cleared; the air smelled of burnt iron and shame.  
Jhace dropped to one knee beside the fault. The brace plates were wrong—the old single set, bent inward, paint flaked. Someone had patched over the crack with tar and hope.  
“Double rule went out last Bell,” Tiffani said, standing over him. “This is still single.”

“Doesn’t make it safe.”

“Ghosts kill slower, but they still kill.”

Tiffani exhaled. “That’s better.”

The seam snapped open a hair. Water hissed.  
Mel shouted, “Step back\!”

“Now?” Tiffani asked.

The carts started rolling again, wheels creaking, drivers staring down at the fresh seal as they passed. Nobody spoke.  
Jhace cleaned his tools, blood streaking the wet cloth. His glove was torn; skin raw at the palm.  
“Cost?” Tiffani asked.  
“Grip’s gone again.”  
“Clinic.”  
“Policy.”  
Mel turned away, already writing. “Habit saves cities,” she said over her shoulder.  
They packed up. Fog lifted just enough to show the new double‑brace gleaming dull under water sheen.

## **Chapter 8: The HALT at Narrowvale Rise**

Bell Five hit mid‑echo across the Crown. The air changed pitch. Blue banners flared at every post from Bridgegate to the Vale, the signal that stopped carts and silenced every forge at once. HALT ORDER: STRUCTURAL RISK.  
Jhace reached the rise at a run, lungs raw, coat half‑soaked from the night before. The flag snapped above the street, light cutting through fog. A ten‑span arch in the rise had cracked. Half the Upper Market’s weight ran through that bridge.

“They said consultation complete.”  
“We’re it, then.”

“Dead and live next to each other,” he said. “It’ll shear itself trying to breathe.”

“It’ll climb if we don’t split the feed.”  
“Brighlee\! Drop flow from the mid‑valve.”  
“Flagged.”

“Pulse even?” she asked.  
“Barely.”  
“If it jumps again, we run.”  
“Noted.”  
She set a second wedge lower. The ground under them thudded twice. Distant—pipes reacting.  
“Feed shift,” he said. “Upstream.”  
“Then finish before it notices.”  
He wet the bridge line, smaller run to join both veins. The bead trembled. Sweat stung his eyes.  
“Shade three,” she said. “Keep it honest.”

“Joined feed,” Jhace said. “Trace holding.”  
She crouched by the seam. “Both lines live?”  
“Equalized.”  
She pressed her ear to the plate. Long pause. “Steady,” she said. “For now.”  
“For now gets carts moving.”  
“Not until I sign.” She pulled her pen.  
The ground rumbled again, small but rising. Everyone froze. The seam under her hand pulsed once, hard.  
“That wasn’t feed,” Mel said. “Expansion.”  
“Backflow,” Tiffani said. “Pressure from the Crown line.”  
Jhace grabbed the release wrench. “We vent.”

“Then we brace.” He set another wedge, deep, metal ringing like struck Bells.  
Tiffani cut light to zero, then reopened to full. “Still rising.”

“Half‑turn only.”

Mel pressed her palm to the plate. “Pressure’s gone. Breathing normal.”  
“We clear?” he asked.  
“HALT lifts at Bell Nine.”

He nodded.

Brighlee wrote. “Logged.”  
Carts began to move again, careful. The crowd thinned with the kind of silence near‑misses earn.  
Jhace watched the line. “We nearly lost it.”  
Tiffani sat beside him, shoulder to shoulder. “We didn’t.” Her hand found his burned wrist. His breathing evened.

## **Chapter 9: Slow Bell at Mirecourt**

## **Chapter 10: Mobel at the Table (Interlude)**

Bell Two chimed through the Mercers’ Quarter, each note sliding through the lattice rafters above the weighing floor. Mobel had been there since dawn, coat off, sleeves rolled, the kind of tired that doesn’t show until it stops moving.  
The scales in front of him were old Crown brass, polished to dull gold. They didn’t lie often, but they could be coaxed. The night auditors had caught three weight sets marked light in the middle coin. Not enough to hang a merchant, enough to make the Conclave twitch.  
He placed one suspect weight on the pan, thumbed the notch along its rim. “Filed too deep,” he said to his clerk. “Half‑grain shave.”  
The clerk squinted. “Still legal?”  
Mobel didn’t answer. He flicked the weight off the pan. It hit the floor with a hollow ring. “Not when it starts breeding.”  
The smell of old oil filled the air as apprentices cleaned their scales. Every trade table in the room carried its own seal—the droplet for Couriers, the hammer for Forgewrights, the two crossed wedges newly added for Locks and Loads. That mark had cost Jhace a burn and half the city a rulebook rewrite.  
He checked the next weight. The brass surface bore faint scrape lines, deliberate. He turned it over and read the faint initials carved into the base. “Harnet.”  
The clerk’s eyes widened. “He’s senior.”  
“Was,” Mobel said. “Post the charge.”  
The clerk hesitated. “Sir, it’s only half a grain—”  
Mobel’s voice stayed even. “Half a grain turns into famine when multiplied. Post it.”  
The clerk went pale but moved, writing the notice on a strip of wax paper. Mobel pulled the ink stamp from his belt, Tempermarked the sheet, and clipped it to the board above the scales. The room stilled. Every merchant in sight turned toward the sound.  
“Official?” one called.  
“Filed under Bell Two,” Mobel said.  
Someone muttered, “Conclave doesn’t usually bother with half‑grains.”  
“They do now.”  
He reached for the next weight, turned it, sniffed the faint tang of fresh polish. Too clean. He scraped a knife edge across it. Bright line. “Fresh file,” he said.  
A Forgewright approached, face dark. “We recalibrate weekly. No harm in keeping tools smooth.”  
“Smooth isn’t light,” Mobel said. He balanced the weight against a verified measure. The pan dipped slow, traitorous. “See?”  
The forgewright looked away. “Barely a whisper.”  
“That whisper just cost you your table.”  
He wrote the notice himself: Forgery of official weights, Harnet Forge Cooperative. License revoked pending audit. Seals to be melted public. He signed, Tempermarked, and handed it to the clerk. “Fire the furnace.”  
The apprentices dragged a small crucible from the side wall, set it on the coals, pumped the bellows until the air shimmered. The smell of heating metal cut through the damp.  
Mobel took the forged weights one by one, dropped them into the pot. They sizzled as moisture burned off, then sagged into liquid. The room went quiet except for the hiss of betrayal cooking.  
The Forgewright spoke again, voice thin. “You’re making a show of this.”  
“No,” Mobel said. “A record.” He stirred the molten brass with a rod, watching the color shift from yellow to red to dull silver. “Shows fade. Records stay on the board.”  
When the last of the fake weights had gone under, he tilted the crucible, poured the liquid into a flat mold shaped like a wedge. “New symbol,” he said. “Half‑grain truth.”  
The clerk stared. “What do we do with it?”  
“Post it on the Table.”  
They nailed the cooled wedge to the central board between the trade seals, the scarred metal still faintly warm. It read: Mass must match mark. Deviance voids claim.  
Mobel looked around the room. No one met his eyes. “Back to work,” he said.  
The merchants dispersed. The clatter of weights returned, but softer, careful now.  
At the Bell of mid‑morning, the clerk handed him the ledger. “Three confessions filed already,” she said. “Two from vendors who weren’t questioned.”  
Mobel nodded, too tired for satisfaction. “Guilt travels faster than ink.”  
He sat a while after the room cleared, listening to the slow tick of cooling metal. He wasn’t proud. Just done. He wiped his hands on his coat and left the burned smell for the next shift.  
Outside, a new placard hung on the Mercers’ archway—approved by existing rule. It carried Corlexi’s twin‑wedge symbol above the words DOUBLE CHECK. DOUBLE PAY. He smiled once, small, then vanished into the crowd before anyone could mistake it for approval.

# Chapter 1 — Tiffani

She unlocked the corridor box with cold fingers and a brass key that still smelled faintly of solvent. The lid stuck. She wedged two knuckles under and pried, felt the paint give like thin ice. Inside: stamps in their tin bed, ink two shades from dry, a tray of seals in counted rows, the folded Standard marked with the last shift’s initials. Nothing missing. Nothing generous either.

The corridor itself breathed around her. The long spine of the east run carried air with the hush of a river. Steel ribs, glass panes, heat ghosts rising where the Thermal Mains bled residual warmth through the floorplate joins. She bent and set her gauge frame where the chalk had left pale smudges, a rectangle of metal teeth to test the width callout. It sat square. She didn’t.

There was a curl in the left-hand floor seam. Someone had skimmed the patch last night, left it proud. Not unsafe, not posted. Just the kind of softness that grows moss on a standard.

Tiffani knelt, set her palm to the seam to feel the heat signature. Warm for this hour. She slid the thin feeler blade into the joint and rapped it with the heel of her hand. The blade sang a high note. Her low-stack wrench gave her three turns before the fastener bit again. She liked the way metal answered. No gossip, just torque.

The clock on the wall read six and a few. First convoy from the river gate had been moved forward by a half hour, according to the last entry in the shift ledger. She read it again, lips tight. The ledger ink had bled at the downstroke, a tell she’d learned to distrust. She bent to the Standard and unfolded it. The seal on the back had last shift’s stamp, but no continuance mark. Sloppy. She would fix it and then tell no one.

Action before chatter. She mounted the gauge frame, spun the tension pins snug, then rolled the test cart to the line. The cart’s wheels were newly chalked from the mill. They left clean round kisses on the concrete. She pushed. The frame’s teeth grazed the cart’s side. Good. She pushed harder. The left tooth squealed. Not good.

She set the cart’s brake, knee to metal, shoulder to glass. The glass door gave back a hollow tune from the sealed space beyond, like water under ice. The corridor breathed again. She measured. Two millimeters narrow at the tooth. She checked the posted board. Transit width callout: 1.25 meters, tolerance minus two, plus three. The tooth was right. The corridor was not.

She could have filed the tooth and called it a day. She didn’t. She took the feeler blade to the wall mount and found the culprit: the left bracket had settled into the cure unevenly. Somebody had put heat on it too fast. Not Whitequench, but the cousin sin. She loosened, shimmed with the thin brass set, retightened until the bracket sat flush, skin to skin with the wall’s anchored plate.

The cart went through without complaint, the sound now dull, respectable. She stood, rolled her shoulders. Heat crawled up her neck and cooled quick. She liked that too.

At six-twenty, the first watchers showed. They came in pairs, because people do. Boots lined to the yellow. She didn’t look at faces. It was faster to look at hands and the way they carried their bags. Two hands on a bag meant worry. One hand meant habit. No hands meant carelessness. She put careless to the back of the queue with the chalk block and a raised eyebrow. That was all the dialogue she needed.

A junior from the night shift hovered by the post box. He had the elastic look of someone waiting for permission he hadn’t earned. He pointed at the Standard sheet like it might bite.

“You want me to post?” he asked, voice soft enough not to scratch glass.

“You want you to post,” she said, and that was the end of it.

He fumbled the stamp and aimed the ink at its center. She slid the tin closer.

“Edge,” she said. “Load the edge. You’re not painting a barn.”

He pressed, lifted, left a bruised rectangle. She took the sheet back. Her name and the date went where they belonged in the ledger, neat, legible, without flourish. She signed for continuance as if signing a debt.

At six-thirty, a scrapper pair shoved a dolly piled with broken glass behind a tarp stitched with blue thread. Their inventory slip had someone’s blessing scrawled in the corner that looked like a bird died there. She didn’t trust that either. Glass is patient. It waits for doubt.

“Hold,” she said.

They held. One of them exhaled like a bad valve.

She pulled the tarp back and found clean edges, not scavenged, not dirty. New glass pays high. New glass means someone skipped the order book and went straight to the dock. That was a problem she couldn’t prove in three minutes. So she made a smaller problem she could.

“Weight check,” she said. She rolled the platform scale out from beneath the bench, set it on level, tamped the shims under its feet until the bubble sat between the glass lines, dead center. She input the serial of the dolly, spun the knob, waited for the old instrument to settle. The dial called out twenty-two kilos more than the slip claimed.

She needed a visible mark people could walk around without thinking. That’s what a corridor standard is for.

Her junior returned with an empty cart and too-fast hands. “I can fix the strap,” he said.

“It’s not broken,” she said. “It’s wrong.”

He looked at her and then at his shoes. She liked that he looked at the shoes. The shoes confessed: scuffed fresh, wet, not careless. He would learn if the world didn’t kill him first.

“Post the continuance. Then bring me the narrow-gauge mirror and a light.”

He ran. Good.

In the mirror, the inside of the crate turned to glass and air. She put the light to it and saw what she needed: a stack of plates had drifted to the bruise and nosed against the crate’s wall. The straps held them like a parent’s too-loose hand. She could have pushed and called it safe. She didn’t. She made them reset at the gate, outside the corridor boundaries, so if anything fell it wouldn’t fall on the standard.

That cost twenty minutes. It paid in the watchers’ silence. Silence is the cheapest currency if you know how to earn it.

While the gate untied and retied, she adjusted the sideboard: moved the “Transit Width Callout” one bracket over so someone slightly shorter could read it without leaning. She raised the ledge for the chalk and cleaned the chalk block with glass paper until it wrote square again. She took down the stale notice about a water boil from three weeks ago and replaced it with a “Greyline Flush Schedule,” clear, stamped, with its serial. Policy is only policy if someone can see it without hunting.

A woman at the back of the queue raised a hand. “We’ll be late,” she said, no whine, just fact.

“Then you’ll be late together,” Tiffani said. “Inside the standard.”

Air moved. The Mains sighed. The loaders finished their reset under watch and brought the crate to the line again. This time the strap grabbed properly. No bulge. She tapped metal. Metal answered without notes.

She set the test cart ahead of the crate and ran it through one last time. Teeth cleared. Wheels sang low. Heat on her wrists, cool on her sternum. She lifted her chin.

“Proceed,” she said, and the crate entered the corridor’s glass throat.

After the first roll, the rest learned. That was the cost going cheap into the morning.

She returned to the post box, set the seals in their tray, counted again. One fewer. Accounted for. Ink pad closed. Stamps turned face-down to keep dust off the letters. She slid the Continuance Notice under the glass of the wall frame, the rust-free screws already waiting, and set the witness line for the first person to touch it with their eyes.

The junior came back with a bandage on a finger and the circuit breaker map unrolled in his other hand. He thought he was helping. She took the map, folded it, handed it back.

“We don’t need the Mains,” she said. “We needed the mirror.”

He nodded. A little shame is a kind of heat. It moves.

She stepped back and looked at the wall. The new posted sat level, catching light. The serial looked crisp. The stamp had bled a tiny amount along the top of the C; it would dry sharp. She cleaned the frame’s glass with a square of cloth until the light spread evenly and the corridor saw itself reflected with no distortion. People will obey a thing that looks like it knows what it’s doing.

She placed the chalk block dead center on the sideboard shelf, wrote the day’s code on the tin tag, and hooked the tag next to the frame, so even the careless could bluff competence. The code would be changed at noon. Noon would come either way. Standards are just a way to barter with time without losing your shirt.

Her last act was small: she re-seated the floor seam plate where she had tightened it and pressed her palm flat until the heat from her hand matched the metal’s. The seam lay down. She stood, wiped the sweat from her temple with the back of her wrist, and let the corridor tuck itself into the day.
