Do this by adding one file that everything routes through:

### What makes it actually work (anti-“make up my own shit”)
1. **Every non-obvious detail in prose must trace to a repo citation** (file path + heading or line range).
2. **Hard bans**: no new proper nouns, factions, gods, mechanics, dates, measurements, tech, geography, etc.
3. **Quarantine lane** for ideas: anything invented goes into `QUARANTINE:` blocks, not into canon/prose.
4. **Pre-draft “Fact Table”**: before writing, the model must compile a short table of facts it will use, each with a repo citation. If it can’t cite it, it can’t use it.

Below is a one-document template you can copy as-is.

This document is the single source of truth for generating N01 prose.
All prose must be derived from repo canon and chapter packets.

- Allowed: N01-only events, knowledge, vocabulary, rules.
- Forbidden: any knowledge outside N01, later reveals, meta orchestration, “because future book says so.”

1) N01 Chapter Packets (most specific)
2) N01 Story Bible
3) Lexicon / Time System / Systems docs

Do NOT create:
- new character names, factions, gods, districts, tools, materials, measurements, institutions
- new magic rules/costs/stages
- new geography, history, timelines, “how the world works”
- new terminology that competes with lexicon

### 3.2 Traceability requirement
Any prose detail beyond basic sensory description MUST cite a repo source in the *drafting notes*:
- format: [src: path/to/file.md#Heading]

No meters/feet/miles; no minutes/hours/days/weeks/months/years.
Use Te’Oga units only (Tide/Anneal/Crucible/Casting etc.) per lexicon doc.

- write it ONLY under a block labeled:

- never blend it into prose.

## 4) Prose Style Constraints
- Deep POV discipline for the POV character.
- Forge/Glass-punk sensory language.

- **Pressure Wall:** Chest compression felt before audible roar arrives
- **Copper-Gold Particulate:** Metallic dust coating everything, sticks to sweat/surfaces

## CH01 — “Something Wrong” (prose)

The plaque waited at Junction Seven like a bruise that never finished healing.

Jhace stopped with one boot on the iron grate and one on bare stone. Lamp up. Light angled. Names caught the oil-glow and threw it back in dull relief—cut too deep for a memorial meant to be polite.

Twenty-three.

He read them in order. Not fast. Not soft. Mouth moving just enough to make it real. Jaw tight enough to ache. No hand to heart. No bowed head. No ceremony. If he made it a ritual, it became a comfort. Comfort made people sloppy.

When he hit the last name, he exhaled through his nose and let the air go thin.

Work.

He checked his kit by touch the way he checked a joint: without looking, without trusting luck. Slate—edge chipped where he’d dropped it and hated himself for it. Chalk—two sticks, one cracked. Cord—coiled tight, no fray. Collar keys—cold iron teeth biting his palm. Wedge slips—thin, sharp, mean little pieces that saved walls when men wouldn’t. Lamp oil—stopper seated, no leak. He thumbed each piece and put it back where his hand expected it.

Slate out. He wrote the marker at the top: **Tide 0, Kindling.** Under it: **start of route.** Then he slid the slate back into his belt like it weighed more than stone.

The Underface took him the way it always did—swallowing sound, stealing distance, turning every breath into a measured expense. Damp lived in the mortar seams. The heat-grid ran along the walls in thick iron veins, collars clamping branch lines into place, junction housings squared off like knuckles.

Baseline grid-hum rode through it all. Constant. Familiar. A thing most people stopped hearing after their first Anneal in the tunnels.

Jhace never stopped.

He put his palm to the main line, leather and callus bridging iron. Cold at the surface. Warmth bleeding through underneath. The hum met his bones the way a hammer met an anvil—clean, expected, honest.

One. Two. Three.

The count held.

He moved a hand-span downslope and pressed again.

The hum changed. Not loud. Not dramatic. Just… wrong. A second note riding the first, tight enough to rattle his teeth if he let his jaw loosen. A doubled harmonic where there should have been one clean line.

He lifted his hand and pressed again, like repeating a measurement made it less true.

Wrong.

He wrote on the slate without looking at it: **double present.** Then he marked the pipe with chalk—one short line at the seam, one longer line at the collar. A warning only another inspector would read.

He walked on.

Junction housings passed in a rhythm: iron plate, bolt heads, collar clamp, branch feed. Each one had its own voice. Each one told the truth if you listened long enough and didn’t let the truth inconvenience you.

The doubled harmonic followed him.

It wasn’t just one collar singing off-key. It was the line itself. The whole damn run carrying an extra note like someone had tightened a string too far and pretended it wasn’t going to snap.

He stopped at the next junction and pressed his ear to the iron casing. Sound vibrated straight into his skull. The doubled note sharpened there—thin, insistent. He tasted copper on the back of his tongue and paused hard, sudden suspicion cutting through habit.

He ran his thumb along his gum line and checked his teeth with the edge of his tongue.

No blood.

Copper anyway.

He hated that more than blood. Blood had a cause. Copper without cause meant the system was talking in a language he didn’t like.

He moved toward Junction Twelve with his lamp held low, the light cutting along the floor where grit gathered. The access corridor narrowed and then widened again into the junction chamber—old stone, newer braces welded in crooked desperation, collar banks feeding side branches into numbered sectors.

**Sector Fourteen** sat stenciled on the branch plate in faded paint: kiln-quarter feed. The branch line ran out of sight, carrying heat and pressure to people who would scream if you shut it down and never think about what kept their hands warm.

Jhace pressed his palm to the collar at Twelve and felt the doubled harmonic flare under his skin.

Then he saw it.

Not a crack. Not a blowout. The collar wasn’t seated right—just a fraction, a finger’s worth of misalignment that would pass every lazy glance and still kill someone when load shifted.

Mis-seated.

He chalked the collar seam with a hard circle and drew an arrow toward the branch plate: **Fourteen—third.** He didn’t need to write more. If you knew, you knew.

Bootsteps came fast behind him. Not careful. Not tunnel-trained. A pair of workers—maintenance muscle, not inspectors—rounded into the chamber with a coil of cord and a shoulder brace between them.

One saw the chalk and slowed. The other didn’t.

“What’s that?” the second demanded, like chalk belonged to him.

Jhace didn’t turn right away. He kept his hand on the collar. Let them feel how serious he was through his stillness.

“Mis-seat,” he said. One word. Enough.

The one who’d slowed set his load down with a careful grunt. The other barked a laugh that tried to turn worry into offense.

“This feed stays open,” he said. “We’re already behind. You gonna tell Sector Fourteen their kilns go cold because you got a feeling?”

Jhace finally looked at him. Lamp light picked out sweat shine on the man’s upper lip and dust packed into his nail beds. The kind of worker who lived by throughput and lived just long enough to brag about it.

“It isn’t a feeling.” Jhace tapped the collar with a knuckle—light, controlled. The doubled harmonic answered, thin and ugly. “Hear that?”

The man’s eyes flicked to the pipe like it might suddenly confess.

“It always hums.”

“It always hums *one*,” Jhace said. “This is two.”

The first worker—quiet one—leaned in and pressed his ear to the iron. He flinched back, face tightening like he’d bitten a bad seed.

“That’s… not right,” he muttered.

The loud one squared up anyway. “We don’t have the authority—”

Jhace cut him off with his slate, held up like a badge he hadn’t earned from any committee. “I’m the Underface authority when the line is about to shear.”

“About to,” the man repeated, clinging to that word like it was a rope. “Always about to. Never does.”

Jhace’s jaw ticked once. He thought of Junction Seven. Twenty-three names cut into iron because someone had said *about to* and then gone upslope to keep people happy.

He set the collar keys into the clamp and tested the seat with a careful twist. Resistance came too early—wrong. The collar caught like a tooth on a thread.

He backed it off a hair and reseated, pushing with the heel of his palm until iron kissed iron the way it was meant to. The doubled harmonic softened—just a fraction. Not fixed. But less screaming.

The loud worker stepped closer, crowding him in the narrow chamber. “You shut this branch and you’re going to have men on you in a span. You ready for that?”

Jhace didn’t look away from the collar. “They can get in line behind the dead.”

“Don’t talk like—”

A pulse rolled through the tunnel.

Not a settling creak. Not a localized shiver from one wall deciding it had had enough. This was a clean, heavy push—like the whole Underface took a single breath and let it go wrong.

Dust lifted off the floor in a thin sheet and slapped back down. The lamp flame snapped sideways, then steadied. The doubled harmonic deepened, dropping lower into something you didn’t hear so much as feel in your molars.

All three of them went still.

Jhace pressed his palm to the main line again. The vibration rode up his arm and into his shoulder. Tunnel-wide. System-wide. Not Junction Twelve being dramatic. Not Sector Fourteen overloading its branch.

Something bigger.

The quiet worker swallowed hard. “That… that was the whole run.”

Jhace tasted copper again, sharper, like he’d sucked a coin.

He wrote on the slate with a fast, hard scrape: **pulse—wide. repeat?** Then he listened. Counted breaths. Waited for a second.

The second pulse came.

Same weight. Same clean shove. Same dust lift. Same deepened wrongness in the hum.

The loud worker’s face lost its argument. “What is that?”

Jhace didn’t answer because he didn’t know, and pretending you knew was how you got plaques.

He glanced at the inspection shaft ladder set into the wall—rungs disappearing upslope toward street access. His route should have taken him up. Report it. Let foremen fight about whether “wrong” deserved a shutdown.

The doubled harmonic answered for him, steady and ugly. The pulses came like a warning bell without a hand to ring it.

Deeper first.

He tightened the collar clamp as far as it would honestly go, chalked a second mark to show it had been touched, and shoved the collar keys back into his belt.

To the workers: “Keep hands off this joint. If it shifts again, you run. You don’t argue.”

The loud one opened his mouth.

Jhace’s stare shut it for him.

He took his lamp, turned away from the ladder, and walked deeper into the Underface at a controlled pace. No sprint. No panic. Panic wasted breath and broke ankles.

Behind him, Junction Twelve kept singing two notes where there should have been one, and the tunnel-wide pulse repeated like the city was testing its own bones.

Jhace didn’t look back.

He listened. He counted. He went deeper.
