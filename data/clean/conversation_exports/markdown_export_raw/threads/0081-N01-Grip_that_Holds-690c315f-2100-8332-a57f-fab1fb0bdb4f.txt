**Why sealed:**
- Innocent-frame belief: Ancient seal from God War (the Severing); magic or craft-law locked it

**Pre-Piercing consequences of sealed Bore:**

- Curtain zone unstable (pressure differential trying to equalize)

# INTERLUDE PLACEMENT LOGIC

**Document Version**: 1.0  
**Created**: October 31, 2025  
**Purpose**: Define interlude placement rules and build-up/break-down methodology  
**Usage**: Reference for Perplexity Space when determining interlude positioning in novella bibles  

---

## INTERLUDE OVERVIEW

**Interludes** are god-vessel POV chapters (800 words each) that provide glimpses into faction activities. Each novella contains **2 interludes** featuring **2 different god-vessels**.

**Purpose**:
- Show god-vessel perspective (doctrine, followers, operations)
- Provide horror/surveillance contrast to Jhace's heroic struggle
- Build world beyond Jhace's immediate awareness
- Create unpredictability (readers cannot predict when interludes occur)

---

## PLACEMENT VARIATION

Interludes vary placement across novellas to prevent predictability:

**N1**: After Ch.3, After Ch.11  
**N2**: After Ch.4, After Ch.12  
**N3**: After Ch.5, After Ch.10  
**N4**: After Ch.2, After Ch.14  
**N5**: After Ch.11 (only one, Auditor Epilogue absorbs second)  

**Pattern**: Early/mid, mid/late, early/late, very early/very late, late only

**Principle**: Never place interludes at same chapter positions across consecutive novellas.

---

## BUILD-UP VS. BREAK-DOWN

Each interlude uses either **build-up** (pre-crisis setup) or **break-down** (post-crisis consequences), never both in same novella.

### Build-Up Methodology
**Definition**: A-plot events before interlude setup what interlude reveals.

**Example**:
- **A-plot (Ch.4)**: Jhace notices dome scars, geometric patterns appearing
- **Interlude 1A (after Ch.4)**: Xilcore's perspective — obsessive geometric "corrections"
- **Effect**: Reader understands what Jhace witnessed

**Usage**: When god-vessel activity is mysterious, interlude explains

---

### Break-Down Methodology
**Definition**: Interlude explains what A-plot characters witness after.

**Example**:
- **Interlude 2A (after Ch.11)**: Blemo's purge operations shown
- **A-plot (Ch.12)**: Refugees (Halrick) describe fires and purges
- **Effect**: Reader knows truth before A-plot characters do

**Usage**: When god-vessel activity has consequences, interlude provides context

---

### The Crafted Calendar System

Time flows like molten glass through the sacred mathematics of craft—five tides to shape, seven anneals to perfect, nine crucibles to complete.

---

## **Tide** (Day)

A single rotation of the world, named for the flowing rhythm of molten glass and the inexorable passage of heat through the forge.

**Phrasing:** "Three tides hence" = three days from now

**Daily divisions:**
- **Kindling** (Dawn) — First Tide; call to work; heat-grid activates to daily minimum
- **Pouring** (Mid-morning) — Second Tide; full work begins; peak energy
- **Shaping** (Noon) — Third Tide; peak heat; maximum output
- **Striking** (Afternoon) — Fourth Tide; refinement; precision work
- **Cooling Hour** (Dusk) — Fifth Tide; The Quench; work winds down; heat-grid begins daily rest

**Example usage in prose:**
- "By Kindling, the scaffold was down." (by dawn)
- "They worked through Shaping and into Striking." (through the hottest part of day)
- "The Quench brought reprieve." (evening came)

---

## **Anneal** (Week - 5 days)

The five-day cycle required for proper annealing of significant glassworks. Each day has a craft-name and thematic purpose:

| Order | Name | Meaning | Crafting Stage |
|-------|------|---------|---|
| 1 | **Heattide** | Preparation, gathering materials | Material assembly |
| 2 | **Pourtide** | Beginning work, molten state | Activation/ignition |
| 3 | **Shapetide** | Formation, the peak of creation | Maximum effort |
| 4 | **Striketide** | Refinement, hammer and tool | Precision work |
| 5 | **Cooltide** | Settling, hardening, rest | Integration/completion |

**Phrasing:** "Before the anneal cools" = within the week

**Example usage in prose:**
- "On Striketide, Jhace realized the crack ran deeper." (fourth day of week; refinement work day)
- "They had until Cooltide to finish." (by end of week)
- "Meet me at Pourtide." (second day of week; transition to active work)

---

## **Crucible** (Month - 7 anneals / 35 tides)

Seven anneals, representing the seven stages of a master crucible's use before it must be relined or replaced. The seven anneals within a Crucible are named for the crucible's lifecycle:

| Order | Name | Metaphor | Purpose |
|-------|------|----------|---------|
| 1 | **First Heat** | Foundation setting | Organizational/planning month |
| 2 | **Rising Glow** | Momentum building | Active work month |
| 3 | **Full Blaze** | Peak intensity | Crisis/climax month |
| 4 | **Steady Burn** | Sustainable rhythm | Recovery/stabilization month |
| 5 | **Ember Anneal** | Maintained heat | Consolidation month |
| 6 | **Fading Coals** | Waning intensity | Transition month |
| 7 | **Final Temper** | Hardening/locking | Resolution month |

## 1. VEIN SUBSTRATE: THE INVISIBLE STRESS ARCHITECTURE

### What Are Veins?

**Veins** are invisible channels of structural stress that exist within all materials (objects, living tissue, the planetary substrate itself).

**Pre-Piercing state:** Veins exist but are **inert**—stress channels locked in place, unresponsive to will or intention.

**Post-Piercing state:** The projectile's alien materials cause **vein-infection**—veins throughout Te'Oga become **responsive to conviction and Soulpulse manipulation**.

### Vein States: Three Conditions

Every vein exists in one of three states:

#### 1. Stable

**Definition:** Veins in equilibrium; stress distributed normally; material/body functioning as intended.

**Indicators:** No sensations; object holds; body healthy; no tremor or pain.

**Manipulation:** Stable veins are difficult to deliberately activate; require high conviction and precise application.

**Narrative use:** Characters describe stable objects as "holding" or "settled"; bodies feel "intact" or "whole".

#### 2. Stressed

**Definition:** Veins under unusual pressure; stress accumulating; material/body approaching failure point.

**Indicators:** Humming sensation; pressure-feeling (like water-weight); localized pain; tremor in proximate tissue.

**Manipulation:** Stressed veins are easiest to manipulate; they respond to conviction quickly; cost is moderate.

**Narrative use:** Characters describe stressed objects as "creaking" or "singing" (forbidden: "humming like a tuning fork"); bodies feel "stretched" or "tight".

#### 3. Rupturing

**Definition:** Veins in cascading failure; stress exceeding maximum threshold; material/body approaching structural collapse.

**Indicators:** Intense pain; visible cracks appearing; explosive sensation (pressure-release imminent); nosebleeds if in living tissue.

**Manipulation:** Rupturing veins cannot be stabilized through normal Soulpulse (cost too high; would kill user); can only be managed through Wholeness-braiding (accepting the failure and integrating around it) or accelerated through Entropy (allowing graceful collapse).

**Narrative use:** Characters describe rupturing objects as "screaming" (forbidden: "singing like a broken instrument"); bodies feel "shattering" or "dissolving from inside".

### Vein Density: Geographic Distribution

Vein density (responsive veins per cubic meter) varies by location post-Piercing:

| Location | Vein Density | Responsiveness | Cost Modifier |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Grindheim (Underface, near entry) | 95%+ | Extreme (veins respond instantly) | x1.5 cost (veins too sensitive) |
| Mid-Underface cities | 70–80% | High (veins respond reliably) | x1.0 cost (baseline) |
| Underface periphery | 40–50% | Moderate (veins respond inconsistently) | x0.8 cost (easier manipulation) |
| Curtain zone | Fluctuates wildly | Chaotic (veins respond unpredictably) | x2.0+ cost (dangerous) |
| Outer Crown entry | 60–70% | High (veins respond reliably) | x1.0 cost (baseline) |
| Mid-Outer Crown | 30–50% | Moderate (veins respond inconsistently) | x0.8 cost (easier manipulation) |
| Outer Crown periphery | 10–20% | Low (veins barely respond) | x0.5 cost (very difficult to activate) |

**Narrative consequence:** Magic is hardest (costliest) in Grindheim; easiest in periphery; fluctuates unpredictably in Curtain zone.

## 2. WHAT SOULPULSE IS (HARD DEFINITION)

### Core Mechanism

**Soulpulse** is the deliberate manipulation of vein state through focused conviction and directed internal energy.

**Process:**
1. User senses vein(s) in a material or living tissue
2. User focuses conviction on specific vein target
3. User channels internal energy (metabolic, emotional, or both) into the target vein
4. Vein responds by shifting state (stable → stressed, stressed → rupturing, or reversed)
5. Material/body responds to vein state change (object strengthens/weakens; wound heals/tears; structure adjusts)

### Prerequisites for Successful Soulpulse

**User must possess:**

1. **Vein sensitivity** — ability to sense veins within materials/bodies
   - Post-Piercing baseline: 70% of population develops some sensitivity
   - Trainable: with practice, sensitivity increases
   - Genetic component: some people naturally gifted; others require years of practice

2. **Conviction** — psychological state of absolute certainty + grounding + acceptance of cost
   - **Certainty:** User must know exactly what they intend to accomplish (vague intentions fail)
   - **Grounding:** User must be emotionally anchored (panic/fear causes manipulation to fail)
   - **Cost acceptance:** User must consciously accept the physical toll (hesitation causes incomplete manipulation or backlash)

3. **Energy reserves** — metabolic and emotional capacity to fuel the manipulation
   - Soulpulse draws on internal energy (blood sugar, emotional intensity, willpower)
   - Depletion causes fatigue, tremor, nosebleeds
   - Different principles draw on different energy types (Form = willpower; Wholeness = emotional; Purity = rage; Entropy = acceptance)

### What Soulpulse CANNOT Do

❌ **Cross significant distances** (must sense veins directly; range ~5 meters maximum with training)  
❌ **Work on dead matter** (veins become inert; no responsiveness)  
❌ **Affect non-biological material** (metals, stone respond weakly; biological substrate responds more readily)  
❌ **Reverse permanent damage** (scarred tissue has dead veins; cannot restore)  
❌ **Save someone at the moment of death** (veins rupture completely; no recovery point)

## 3. ACCESS REQUIREMENT: CONVICTION

### What Is Conviction?

**Conviction** is the triple-state psychological condition required to activate Soulpulse:

#### Element 1: Certainty

**Definition:** Absolute clarity about intended outcome; no ambiguity or hesitation.

**Examples of sufficient certainty:**
- "I will stabilize this child's fractured ribs" (specific, measurable)
- "I will heal the poison in this person's blood" (specific target, clear method)
- "I will strengthen this scaffold until it holds for one hour" (time-bound, specific)

**Examples of insufficient certainty:**
- "I hope I can fix this" (hope is not certainty)
- "Maybe I can stop the bleeding" (doubt present)
- "I'll try to make it better" (vague intention)

**Consequence of insufficient certainty:** Manipulation fails; veins do not respond; no cost incurred but no benefit either.

#### Element 2: Grounding

**Definition:** Emotional stability and presence; ability to remain focused despite pain, fear, or external crisis.

**Grounding sources:**
- Love/attachment (protection of someone you love grounds you in purpose)
- Competence confidence (mastery in your craft grounds you in capability)
- Physical anchoring (feet on ground, breath controlled, body present)
- Community/crew (feeling part of something larger grounds you in collective purpose)

**Threats to grounding:**
- Panic (disassociates you from body/intention)
- Grief (overwhelms focus with emotion)
- Physical injury (pain pulls attention away)
- Doubt about worthiness (moral questioning of whether action is right)

**Consequence of insufficient grounding:** Manipulation fails or causes **backlash**—veins respond incorrectly; user experiences intense pain or temporary paralysis.

#### Element 3: Cost Acceptance

**Definition:** Conscious psychological acceptance of the permanent toll the manipulation will exact.

**What acceptance means:**
- Not intellectually knowing the cost, but *emotionally accepting* it
- Willingness to trade your physical health for the outcome
- Pre-commitment to living with the scar (literal tremor, chronic pain, sensory loss)

**How acceptance fails:**
- User hesitates mid-manipulation (thinking "this will hurt too much")
- User second-guesses the worthiness of outcome (thinking "am I really willing to sacrifice for this?")
- User tries to minimize cost through technique (thinking "I can do this efficiently"; magic doesn't work that way)

**Consequence of insufficient acceptance:** Manipulation fails at critical moment; partial vein activation causes **Hollowing cascade** (see §7).

# Chapter 1: Grip Work

Kindling rose slow and copper through the vault, a thin warmth that kissed the skin without softening the bones. Jhace was already on the ladder when the bell-notes rolled through the craft-hold. Third rung. Right hand on the rail. Left hand carrying the weight of his kit, tools nested and clacking, each with its polite hunger for use. He counted the clacks under his breath—habit, not superstition. Predictable things should be counted. Counting is how you make them yours.

Bram stood on the grating below, halfway between amusement and warning. “Fine day to be above ground,” he called, which in Grindheim meant: above the main walkways, up where the heat-lines sang and the frames were unkind.

“It’s always a fine day when someone forgets to bleed a coil,” Jhace said. He reached the junction chamber and wedged his shoulder against the access. Hinges gave a stubborn sigh. “You got me?”

Bram laid one big hand on the ladder’s side rail. “I have you.”

The junction smelled of old heat and oil. The line was a velding era antique, glass-lined iron wrapped with braided sand-thread, a compromise from when the grid had outgrown its own bones but not yet learned how to shed them. Someone had misread a pressure gauge—no, not misread, Jhace corrected, because they didn’t use gauges. Someone had mistaken sound for holding. That thin whine? Not holding. That’s a complaint. The work teaches you the difference.

He touched the conduit with the back of his fingers. Warmth; not dangerous. He loosened the clasp-bands and peeled the shell. Steam whispered at him, just enough to fog his wrist hairs and lay down a mint of caution. The bleed-valve was stuck open a whisper. That explained the starving branch downline and the angry slaps from the cooks in Northface Market. Heat wants a path and a home. Give it neither and it gnaws the first thing it can reach.

“Valve’s flirting,” Jhace said. “I’ll tame it.”

“You’ll replace it,” Bram said, voice light but spine iron. “We don’t coax bad habits to live another anneal.”

“That’s six hours shut at least.”

“Then we shut six. Grip that holds or don’t bother.”

Jhace grunted acknowledgment and worked the threads with the short hook, easing the valve from its seat. The threads squealed, then surrendered. He could feel Bram’s gaze the way you feel a brace under your feet—comfort not praise. Bram didn’t praise. He did the rarer thing: he trusted you with the part of the grid that could kill you if you told yourself a story instead of the truth.

They traded the valve for a new one. He applied paste, not too thick, not too thin, thumb smoothing to a clean spiral. Replace, align, seat. He tightened until the resistance sang low. He bled the line to taste the heat, a small exhale of steam touching his cheek. Good. Not perfect; perfect is for sermons and funerals. Good holds through Striking.

Bram’s arm tapped the ladder. “Down. Next bleed.”

Jhace descended, shoulders burning in the useful way. He wiped his hands on the rag cable-tied to his belt and looked past Bram into the web of the hold. The craft-hold was a layered hive of frames and conduit runs, stairs and catwalks hung like notes on a staff. People moved with purpose. Voices carried in the narrow ways. Everything here had weight and name. The grid fed the vault neighborhoods their measured warmth. Without it, breath would fog and then freeze. Without it, children would go to bed and wake as glass.

“Chloen didn’t report?” Jhace asked. Not because he needed her—the work would move in any case—but because she watched sharp and learned fast, and watching sharp spared someone a scar later.

“Runner’s sick,” Bram said. “Thin season in Southcut. Send soup if you pass their way.”

“I’ll pass,” Jhace said, not meaning it as virtue. He lived in Southcut. The soup would be his. He would carry two bowls and pretend one had been meant for the steps.

They crossed to the next branch. A whistle stitched itself through the noise of the hold, higher than the usual complaint, and then cut short. Jhace and Bram both turned toward it, same moment, same angle. You spend long enough with a man and your attention learns his routes. The whistle had lived in the east run. He took the lead without asking.

The east run hung a level higher, goat path narrow and guardless. Jhace’s body knew the turn of each rivet head under his palm. He jogged. At the bend, heat slapped his face—dry, old heat, the kind that has crawled through too many lengths of pipe and picked up every argument on the way. The sound that followed was worse than the whistle: a stutter-pop like someone cracking knuckles all at once the entire length of a hand.

“Brace,” Bram said behind him, already unslinging the patch roll. “Rupture-grade?” A question that was not a question, a measure for Jhace’s mind to take.

“Three, maybe four,” Jhace said, feeling the pulse through the grating. “No followshock.”

“Good,” Bram said. “Patch or cut?”

“Patch first. If it sings again, we cut.”

They reached the leak. A band seam had failed along a palm-width, vapor fogging the air. It hadn’t found a victim yet—blessing of Kindling, few people this high. Jhace cursed the seam, the cheap band, the hand that had settled for almost. He unrolled the patch, set it in place, and drove the clamp teeth home, teeth biting through wet to the honest surface beneath. His fingers went numb in the mist; the skin would complain all Striking.

“Hold for count,” Bram said. “One, two—”

The patch held. The fog thinned from fury to breath.

They stood quiet to listen. No song, no stutter. The line spoke its relief in soft rumbling.

“Cut anyway,” Bram said after a long count. “I don’t argue with your ear. But relief is not repair.”

Jhace nodded. “Quench me a window. We don’t starve Northface.”

Bram turned and barked orders down through the web. Valves spun three holds away, voices passing confirmations along the lattice. Jhace thought about the way the grid mapped onto people—old truth, that. If a valve sticks, it’s because someone is tired. If a seam fails, it’s because someone’s overseer counted days wrong. If a branch runs hungry, it’s because someone lied to themselves about enough. The grid is truth and cost. It remembers.

“Your shoulder,” Bram said, nodding to the right one. “You’re favoring it.”

Jhace rolled it. “Work sits there. It’s fine.”

Bram made a face that meant: fix it before it fixes you. Then the old man crouched to look through the mist at the band. “When I was your age,” he said, “men tried to vield these with brute force and a prayer. Now we’ve got proper bands and still we treat them like wishes.” He snorted. “We know better. Do better.”

“We will.” Jhace didn’t put honey on it. He meant it the way a clamp means its bite.

The window came. He cut the band free, slipped the new one on, set it, tightened until the strain moved from scream to tenor. He sealed the seam with a line of resin that ran like new glass, then heated it with the torch until it hardened to the right shine. He bled the line to taste the heat again. Good. A little too good. Someone would steal warmth tonight and swear it was just what the meter read.

“Done,” he said.

Bram grunted, the sound he used when a student had finally learned to see the invisible thing. “Next,” he said, but not to rush. There isn’t rushing in the hold. There’s pace. There’s the rhythm of a tide.

They worked through Pouring and into Shaping. Sweat salted their mouths. Jhace drank and ran the back of his hand over his lips, then refilled from the can Bram insisted he carry, not the wall spigots with their metallic aftertaste. He had bruises that would take on color by Cooltide. He liked knowing where his hurts would flower before they did. It kept the world in the family of the known.

“Eat,” Bram said at last. He handed Jhace a wrapped pocket of bread and spiced mash. “Before you go soft-headed and marry a leak by accident.”

They ate sitting on the grating, boots through the grid, looking out over the hold’s ribcage. Jhace chewed until his jaw ached—a good ache—and let the silence do the talking. Bram didn’t crowd quiet. That was one of the first things that made Jhace stay.

“You still living alone?” Bram asked after a while, casual as checking a seal.

“I sleep,” Jhace said.

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the one I have.”

Bram folded the bread wrap flat, corners squared. “Work is a poor blanket. Keeps out the cold a few tides and then you’re shaking again.”

Jhace didn’t look at him. “It holds me upright.”

“So does a ladder,” Bram said. “You don’t sleep on it.”

There was nothing for that but a grunt. Jhace watched a boy three levels down carry a coil twice his weight. The boy’s back was bent wrong, hands white with the lie of grip. He filed the boy’s face into the drawer labeled “Soon to need a word.” He would give the word when it would land. He had learned that from Bram.

The bell for Striking sang. They rose. Work called, and they answered the way a tool answers a hand—without flourish, without complaint. The day made itself into a chain of small decisions and paid-for corrections. A misaligned hanger here, a cracked catch there, a hiss that hinted at a branch starving. They fixed. They listened. They sweated. They swore. They thanked. Grindheim breathed because they did.

Near the Cooling Hour, a runner found them—little Marri from Southcut, hair tied in a sharp knot and eyes like struck glass. “Master Bram,” she said, trying to fold her voice into properness.

“Don’t master me,” Bram said. “You’ll have people asking me for sermons.”

She flushed. “Bram, then. The market posted two complaints. Northface says their heat came high then flat. A fishmonger is yelling at the tiles. Also a baker says his oven fought him.”

Jhace and Bram exchanged a glance. Flat after high meant someone had learned to fiddle a valve long enough to roll out loaves and then left the branch to cough.

“Name?” Jhace asked.

Marri hesitated. “He wouldn’t give it. Threw a cloth. Threw another. Called me a pipe-girl.”

Bram’s mouth went thin. “All right. Jhace, you take the baker. I’ll take the fishmonger. Marri, you go home. Tell your mother your errands earn meat tonight.”

Marri slipped away at the speed pride likes—unhurried, spine straight—and Jhace climbed back to the grating edge. The baker’s hall lived along the east rim, a walk of ten minutes if you didn’t stop to untangle the lattice of bodies and carts funneling through end-of-tide tasks.

“Careful,” Bram said, resting a hand on his shoulder as they parted. “Men who bark at tile put teeth on people if you let them. Don’t let them.”

“I’ll bring a meter,” Jhace said.

Bram’s eyes crinkled. “We don’t use meters.”

“I meant my face.”

Bram chuckled, a rumble that felt like held stone. “Grip that holds,” he said by way of blessing.

Jhace took that blessing and carried it. The east rim smelled of yeast and burned sugar and the sharp ghost of last tide’s fish. He knocked once on the baker’s post and didn’t wait for permission; the work had already given it. The man inside turned, flour on his forearms like snow that had forgotten where it belonged.

“You the boy who thinks the grid owes you a crown?” the baker said.

“I’m the man who keeps your ovens from killing you,” Jhace said. He kept his voice flat as a leveled frame. “Point me to your branch valve.”

“There,” the baker said, a jerk of chin toward a wall spigot that was not a valve and that told Jhace more in one second than another man’s hour of words. The baker did not know the grid. He knew convenience.

Jhace followed the line with his eyes, found the hidden latch, and lifted the panel that revealed the true valve. The handle was warm and smug. “You set this high,” he said. “Then you left it to starve your neighbors.”

“I pay my taxes,” the baker said. “My heat is mine.”

“Your heat is borrowed and returned,” Jhace said. “The grid is a circle. Break it and it breaks you back.”

He turned the valve down to honest. The baker’s face went a color that didn’t suit the room. “I’ll have a word with your master.”

“Bram will enjoy that,” Jhace said. He didn’t smile. He didn’t need to. He set the seal with a wax mark that told the next worker this was the place to watch and the man to ask about. He left the room without looking back.

Walking back through the rim, the vault felt a shade warmer—imagined, likely, but the mind has its own grid and some lines run belief. He breathed the end of Striking like a man who had earned it.

At the stair, he paused and looked up through the metal lace at the high vault. There were no fissures there, only old soot and the careful pattern of lights that told the eye, no, nothing has changed, not in any way that will steal your sleep. He let the pattern calm him and then wiped the sweat from his brow with his wrist and started down.

Bram waited on the grating, a small fish in a paper twist in his hand, grease blooming a circle into the wrap. “Payment for our sermon,” he said, handing half over.

“Grid work and theology,” Jhace said, taking the fish. “We should charge double.”

“We do,” Bram said. “We call it truth.”

They ate in companionable silence, the kind that has space for a life inside it. When the last bell of the tide tolled and the Cooling Hour’s breath washed the hold, Jhace slung his kit and felt the good ache map itself across his shoulders and hands. He could sleep inside that ache. He could dream the measured hum of lines that held because he’d told them to and paid the price they asked.

“Tomorrow?” Bram said.

“The grid will forget we loved it today,” Jhace said. “We’ll remind it.”

Bram nodded, satisfied. “Grip that holds,” he repeated, as if saying it twice set a seam.

Jhace stepped into the stairwell, the vault air cooling against his neck, all the day’s work behind him like a frame he could trust. He didn’t know that two tides from now, truth would cost more than bruises. He didn’t know that he would soon place his hands on a child and feel the world answer. He knew this: the hold, the heat, the hand that steadies the ladder. For now, that was enough.

# Chapter 2: Lattice and Lung

Jhace walked Southcut in the Cooling Hour, steam lifting in gray veils from the narrow runnels where condensate bled back toward the mains. The air down here kept a copper edge, sharp enough to remind you you were alive without making a project of it. He carried two bowls nested in one hand, the smell of bone-simmer and garlic doing half the work of warmth.

Marri’s door hung uneven on its hinge. He nudged it with the toe of his boot. “Brought your meat,” he said—not loud, not soft—so the room could decide whether to be grateful or proud without feeling seen.

Her mother answered with a wipe of her forearm across her face, flour streaks turning her into someone drawn. She took the bowls with quick fingers, eyes avoiding his. “For running,” she said.

“For work done,” Jhace said. “Tell Marri to keep her feet under her when men talk loud. They like to make a show of stepping on what moves.”

A ghost of a smile cracked the woman’s mouth. “She’ll outrun their show.” Then, as if the words were a window that needed opening, she added, “Thank you.”

He tipped his chin and left before the thanks turned the room fragile. Southcut’s halls made their own river of voices, little eddies where gossip stuck and spun. He let it wash around him, counted steps and handholds like beads. Home was a single room off a side corridor, a hook for his kit and a pallet that could forgive a body and a mind at once. He unbuckled his boots and eased onto the pallet with a groan that set satisfaction in his ribs.

Sleep came straight, without dream.

Anneal bell pulled him out of it. He rolled his shoulder and found the bruise he’d predicted starting to color, a bloom under the skin the size of Bram’s thumb. He liked being right about his hurts. It meant the world was still bargaining fairly.

The craft-hold woke like a lung. First an inhale—the low swell of voices greeting tools, valves moving in their seats—and then the work-exhale, purposeful and hot. Bram was already at the map-board, chalking a line from the east rim down through a tangle that looked like a mess to anyone who hadn’t spent years learning the grid’s grammar. To Jhace, it read clean as a set of ribs.

“Morning,” Bram said, not looking up. “I took a walk last tide. Found a stress-riser on a hanger near the west manifold.”

Jhace stepped in close and followed the chalk. A hanger drilled off true, weight dragging where it oughtn’t, vibration feeding a seam a story it would someday believe. “How loud?”

“Only whisper,” Bram said. “But I don’t sleep to whispers.”

“Shut window?”

Bram nodded. “We’ll take Westbath cold for two hours. They can curse me and then thank me next Casting when they still have pipes.”

“The bathing hall won’t like it,” Jhace said. Not disagreement. Inventory.

“That’s what the hall is for,” Bram said. “Taking a load. Better we choose it than the grid chooses for us.”

They pulled the tools they’d need: brace clamps, a short chain jack, resin bars, bands of good make with teeth that knew the difference between a patch and a promise. Jhace slung the coil of line with the ease of someone who’d been taught early that the body endures best when the load is honest. He stowed an extra rag at his hip. Resin always found a way to argue with skin.

On the walk to Westbath, they passed the food court off Main Span—steam buns stacked in triple baskets, a woman singing an old Velding song while her son twisted dough into knot-shapes. Bram slowed half a step, enough for the song to press in and latch on. He didn’t hum, didn’t comment. He let himself be human and then he kept walking.

Westbath lay under a ceiling painted to look like sky from a world none of them had ever seen: blue and soft and full of fat white drifts. The paint had flaked in places, and the drifts looked like something less friendly now. The keeper met them with the square jaw of a man who’d built his authority from denial and clean floors. “We’re early for our cold,” he said, defensive already.

“You’re getting an extra,” Bram said. “Two hours. A hanger’s going bad at your manifold. We keep it and it keeps you.”

The keeper’s mouth set wrong. “Tide’s wrong for—”

“No tide asks permission,” Bram said. “You can choose fury or you can choose flood. Pick one.”

Jhace stepped past them to the service hatch. He unclipped the grate with a practiced wrist flick and dropped into the crawlspace, the world narrowing to pipe and bolt and the rasp of his breath. Heat gathered under his chin, a hand that knew his name. He found the hanger with a glance—the bolt hole egg-shaped from work, the strap dragged out of line, a hairline creeping at the seam. He touched it with the back of his fingers out of habit. Warm, not scalding. Too much vibration in the song.

“Whisper’s true,” he called up. “Cut the window.”

Valves latched in the next hold over, shouts thrown down the line. The song of the manifold shifted from crowded to lean. Jhace slid the jack into place to take the load off the strap, feeling the shift in his wrists and forearms as the weight moved from pipe to tool to him. “Up,” he breathed to the jack, and Bram’s hand on the lever made the pipe rise a whisper, metal sighing its relief.

Resin does what you ask if you tell it the truth. He shaved the seam clean with the flat edge of the hook, wiped it dry, and set a new band, teeth biting into the fresh. He let the heat from the line speak back to the resin, softening it just enough to seat, then cooled it with a rag dipped in the stand bucket. Steam rose, kissed his face, and left a constellation of sting he accepted like penance. “Hold,” he said. He doesn’t know why he said it. The work likes quiet words.

He drilled a new hang point higher in the web, true this time, the bitt humming steady in his hand. New bolt. New strap. He loaded the weight slow back onto the hanger, listening. The whisper faded. The line’s song settled into its proper throat.

“Back,” he called. “Bring it back.”

Valves turned. The grid took its breath. The manifold’s exhale tasted right again.

He slid out and blinked up at Westbath’s false sky. Bram’s face was a mean joy of someone who had stolen an accident and put it in a box. The keeper stood with his arms folded, working himself up to a complaint. “We had a posted heat-slot,” he said.

“And now you have pipes,” Jhace said, wiping resin from his fingers and then giving up and wiping it on his pants. “You can post about that too.”

Bram fought a smile and lost. “Cold another hour,” he told the keeper. “Resin needs to set. You open too soon and you’ll bathe in our work. It doesn’t suit anyone.”

The keeper swore with academic precision and stalked away. Bram watched him go, then laid a hand on the newly seated hanger and patted it like a shoulder. “You know who thanks you on time?” he said.

Jhace lifted the coil onto his shoulder. “Nobody.”

“That’s right,” Bram said, almost merry. “And we get to do it anyway.”

They climbed back to Main Span. Chatter stitched across the frames. A boy with a coil once again drug it wrong. Jhace caught his eye. “You’ll pull your shoulders forward like that,” he said, demonstrating the right lay down the back, over the hip. “You want lines, not knots.”

“Yes, master,” the boy said, the word not a flattery but a habit of this place. Jhace wanted to shrug the word off and keep it. He settled for adjusting the coil and sending the boy on.

At the craft-hold’s edge, a thought had been bothering Bram enough to shake out of him. He turned, leaned his weight against the rail, and looked at Jhace with the kind of assessing calm that made a man feel seen and weighed without being diminished. “You’re strong on patching. On listening. You choose cuts late.”

“Cuts bleed,” Jhace said.

“They also end a lie,” Bram said. “There’s a leak in Northwalk that’s lived three Castings because no one wanted to move three families for a half-tide. You and I are going to cut it today.”

Jhace let himself think about the families. He’d seen them: the old man with his blanket of a beard, the twins who ran one step faster than their shame. He let himself think about the leak like a hand over a mouth. “All right,” he said. “We move them.”

They walked Northwalk. The leak had written its story in soot and damp: a darkening along the seam, scratches where someone had pretended attention. Bram knocked at the first door. The old man answered with a stare sharp enough to vector birds. “What’s broken now?”

“The lie under your floor,” Bram said mildly. “Pack a bag. You’ll spend two hours in the assembly hall and come back to a pipe that doesn’t poison you slow.”

The old man held his gaze a long breath. Then his shoulders loosened the way they do when a spine decides it can be humble without being defeated. “Two hours,” he said. “And this bag is heavy.”

“We’ve carried worse,” Jhace said, and took it without making a sermon of it.

Moving the twins and their mother took another five, and then convincing a woman who wanted to argue that her tea would go cold took ten. Jhace listened, let the words spin out until they tired themselves. He was careful to keep his tone in the range between “I understand” and “I will do this anyway.”

They set the cut. The hiss when the line emptied had a relief edge, like a fever broken. The pipe cooled under their hands, skin grateful for the chance to be useful instead of brave. Jhace set the saw to the seam and felt every tooth argue and then agree. He cut clean. Bram pulled the length free and set it down like bone. New pipe. New bands. No patch, no compromise. They seated it with the satisfaction that comes from not letting a future you deal with a today you didn’t have the courage to face.

“Listen,” Bram said when the line sang again, and Jhace did. The note was truer, the heat carried evenly through the branch. He pictured the twins sleeping later with their mouths closed.

The assembly hall smelled like bread and tiredness. People sat with their bags at their feet and their hands clasped in ways that looked like prayer to people who liked that word. Jhace brought the old man his bag and a cup of broth. The old man took it and said, “What do you call the thing you do?”

“Work,” Jhace said.

“That makes it sound small.”

“It’s heavy. That’s enough.”

The old man considered that, then nodded as if his opinion mattered to anyone but him, and maybe it did. “When I was a boy, they said the world ended once and started over. Now they call that a story. I call it a seam. You keep it closed, we live. You don’t, we see what’s beneath.”

Jhace didn’t wince at talk of endings. Endings belonged to other rooms. He finished his broth and put the cup on a table where it wouldn’t tip.

By Casting bell, their bodies had that clean line between ache and pride. They walked the Spans and listened for complaints. A few came. A few were honest. The baker had kept his hands to himself, Marri reported with derision, and the fishmonger had learned Bram’s name and was misusing it with respect. The grid held like a chest might in sleep, steady and sure.

Back in the hold, Bram set his kit down with a sound that meant he was going to say something he hadn’t planned to. “I’m not forever,” he said. “You know that.”

Jhace wiped resin flakes from his fingers and said nothing because the wrong words would make it a worse truth.

“I want you on the master list next Casting,” Bram said. “Title doesn’t matter. It’s the authority—people listen early instead of late. You like late, Jhace. You treat the world as if it deserves the full trial before you pass sentence. I admire that. But the grid doesn’t wait for deliberations. Sometimes you have to bang the gavel with the leak still talking.”

Jhace swallowed the first answer because it was the shape of No made polite. He turned it over, tried to see if it had more angles than fear and habit. He saw the grid in his head not as pipes but as a set of obligations, and the title as a kind of lever that could move a load you couldn’t muscle. He didn’t have to like it. Liking was for pastries and cool water. “All right,” he said finally, the words feeling like a clamp biting down, clean and permanent. “We’ll post me.”

Bram’s face didn’t shift, but something in him set easier. “We,” he repeated, and it meant its simple thing: not alone.

They finished the tide with small honest tasks that accumulate like a floor. A bracket readjusted here, a hand laid on a pipe to feel if the heat-gradient was walking left when it should take even steps. Jhace marked a hanger for future work and didn’t promise the future more than he could pay.

When the last bell said the day was a story finished, Bram reached for his fish wrap and found nothing there and shook his head, the tragedy of a man who is always half hungry by nature. Jhace laughed, brief and low, and pressed a coin to Marri’s palm as she materialized like competent fate. “For the fish,” he said. “And for the next man who calls you a name, buy him the smallest fish and make him say thank you.”

Marri grinned in a way that made the hall brighter. “Grip that holds,” she said, the words casual as a handshake and heavy as an oath.

Jhace walked home through Southcut, counting steps and lit alcoves, a little off balance from Bram’s ask but not in a way that felt dangerous. The grid breathed behind his ears, steady. He slept like a man who knew the lattice and trusted the lung.

# Chapter 3: Through-Core

Shaping slid toward Striking, the vault as hot as it ever got, when the world let go of its old agreements. Jhace felt it before he saw it—a weight change, like the floor leaning a hair toward something that didn’t exist yet. Then air shoved at his chest and pulled at his back in the same breath, a wrongness that made the hairs at the base of his skull stand sharp. Somewhere below, a rack spilled tools; above, loose clamps ticked against rails.

He turned to Bram. The old man had already set his hand to the ladder, jaw squared, eyes narrowed toward the east run. They both listened the way workers listen—with bodies—so when the first low pressure-pulse went through the frames, it was as clear as a bell you could feel. The second pulse had teeth. The third broke things.

Light ripped across the vault’s roof. Not a beam. A seam. A clean, impossible cut that ran jagged-straight along the high stone and showed not darkness but a smeared red-orange glow through fused rock, as if the roof had become glass in a single breath and the world behind it were burning slow forever. Dust lifted; the whole craft-hold quivered; hanging spanners trembled in their hooks, the metal answering a new, constant pressure underfoot fileciteturn2file17. Wind hit from below—no, not wind as he understood it. Draft drawn through a hollow. It carried the taste of old heat and wet stone, and it didn’t stop. It found every gap and made a whistle of it, high and thin, the sound of blades at distance. The vault’s old amber turned to a pale, flickering gold that climbed and faded along the seam, as if the light itself had learned to breathe wrong fileciteturn1file1.

“Down,” Bram said, even as half the hold began to scream. “We go down and out.” His voice cut clean, a tool set true.

They moved. The ladder shook once hard under Jhace’s feet; he rode it, knees loose, and dropped the last two rungs to the grating. Above them, a hanger sheared with a bright crack and a branch line swung mad and snapped a man’s shoulder clean; the man went down wordless. Air kept pulling. The hum underneath everything—no, the pressure-vibration that lived in this new wound—settled into the metal like fever, and tools quivered to it without caring what hands they belonged to fileciteturn1file6.

“Rupture-grade?” Bram asked, not because he needed the number but because numbers put a wall between you and panic.

“Seven, maybe eight,” Jhace said, feeling the rail bite his palm as the next shift rolled through the frames. “Followshocks will come.”

“Hold routes,” Bram said. He grabbed a runner by the collar. “You take Northwalk and you tell them to move. Don’t ask, you tell.” The runner nodded, face gone to chalk, and vanished into bodies.

The market level below the craft-hold was already a mess of overturned stalls and rolling baskets, heat-smoke cutting into the dust. The east rim bakery had lost its outer rail; loaves lay like bricks scattered across the stone. A child cried somewhere underneath the clamor, the brittle sound a thin thread you could follow if you had a hand free to do it. Jhace did. He went toward it.

He found the child under a collapsed spice table, the air thick with pepper and shattered clay. The table’s crossbeam had come down square on a little chest. The boy had that gray mouth that means breath has been confused into leaving. His ribs moved wrong, a stutter that was more wish than motion, bubbles of red at the corner of his lips. The mother had both hands on the beam in that hopeless grip people try when physics has already made its choice.

“Don’t move,” Jhace said. He slid a knee under the beam and wedged his shoulder to take some of the load. It was more a promise than relief. He looked for Bram—no Bram; Bram was already hauling bodies clear, voice everywhere at once. Jhace checked the boy with two fingers at the throat, counting. A pulse like thread. Time measured itself differently under this light.

He had a choice tree he knew by heart: lever the beam, drag the boy, risk ripping something that still held; or cut the beam fast and pray the pieces didn’t shift worse. Behind that, the grid-worker part of his head kept listing other emergencies—valves starving, hungers pulling heat down the wrong lines, gradient inversion already cracking the wrong pipes as cold air sank into places it didn't belong fileciteturn1file6. Another followshock ran under him like a growl.

“Help me lift,” the mother said through her teeth.

“It will crush him on the drop,” Jhace said. He put both palms on the beam. The wood vibrated the way over-stressed lines do, a deep insect shiver under skin. He imagined the table as a frame he knew, the boy as a branch that had to hold long enough for a clamp to bite. The thought wasn’t noble. It was useful.

The next part came without permission. He decided, simple as setting a valve: The beam will hold steady. The chest will be whole. Not perfect—useable. No speeches. He accepted the cost before he knew what it was. He had lived long enough with the grid to understand that all the good work comes with a bill.

Heat rose along the veins in his forearms like someone had poured warm water into his bones. His hands went hot, then hotter, then steady. He felt the beam in a way that wasn’t touch—the structure, the stress lines, the places where the fibers had already started to tear; and at the same time he felt the boy’s chest as an arrangement of strained parts: bone ends not quite meeting, flesh swollen against itself, a tiny crescendo of panic in living tissue. He knew what he was asking. He asked anyway, and the asking was a kind of command because he did not leave room for doubt. Something in him clicked into an order he had never learned.

The beam stilled—no, it didn’t stop being heavy; it just stopped wanting to wander. Under his left hand, the table’s crosspiece behaved as if a proper hanger had been set, a firm geometry imposed. Under his right, the child’s chest rose a fraction deeper, and the wrong stutter of the ribs smoothed. The bubbles at his mouth slowed, then ceased. Warmth ran up his wrists into his elbows, and a shiver started in his fingers he couldn’t stop. He held both intentions like you might hold two lines under tension, carefully, aware that if either slipped the whole would lash back. He did not think in principles. He thought like a man with a clamp in one hand and a failing seam in the other.

The boy coughed once and pulled a breath that had weight in it. The gray left his mouth. He started to cry properly, noisy and obscene and perfect. Jhace wanted to laugh and found he couldn’t because his throat had turned to glass lodged in heat. The mother’s palms fell to the boy’s hair. “How?” she asked, not looking at him.

“Move him,” Jhace said. His voice came out level only because he had spent years making it so. “Away from the beam.” She did, hands sure now, and Jhace let go of the wood the way you let go of a live wire, slow, ready to take it back. The beam held where it was. He had no language for what he had done other than the craft words he always used: held, settled, integrated.

He staggered. A tremor ran through his hands, fine as dust in sunlight. Thirst hit him like he’d been walking the dry runs for a whole tide without water, mouth webbed with need. His wrists showed a faint red tracery under the skin, as if his veins had come forward to see what he was doing and stayed too close to the window. He wiped his mouth and found his sleeve wet; a nosebleed had started without asking. The edge of the world glittered. He breathed past it, counting.

“Jhace!” Bram’s shout. Relief, then warning in the same breath.

Jhace turned. The old man was twenty paces off on the market stairs, and a loaded cart that had been upended by the last pulse had slid into a support post just above him. He was bracing two people with his shoulders to keep them from being crushed, his hands white, the kind of decision you make when you know there isn’t time to pick between lives. Jhace stepped—one, two—the floor shifted. The support post cracked like a tooth.

Jhace ran. He had held a beam a moment ago with the same hands that now shook. He knew what it would cost to try that again right away and he knew he was going to try anyway. He reached for that heat in his veins as if it were a tool on his belt. It answered slower. He shaped the thought—hold—and the post hesitated.

Bram looked at him and did the smallest thing: he shook his head once. Not refusal. Choice. He moved his shoulder a fraction and turned his body to take the angle. It spared the two he pinned. It did not spare him.

The post let go. Stone doesn’t care who you are. The cart slipped and the corner caught Bram under the ribs, a blunt force that had no drama in it, just conclusion. He sat down hard as if he had decided to rest. His mouth made a shape that might have been a word. Jhace took the last two steps in a stumble and caught him by the shoulders, slid him back from the fall’s mess, put his palm under Bram’s neck the way a worker cradles a delicate piece not to drop it, not to drop it.

The old man’s eyes were fogging into that look the very old and the very tired get when they choose to let go. “Grip that holds,” Bram said, and if it had been a benediction before, it was a joke now, and a true one. He tried to get enough breath to say more and couldn’t. The blood under the shirt spread quick. Jhace could feel the veins there failing, not as magic yet but as a technician: stutter, stop.

“I can—” Jhace said, which was a lie; he didn’t know what he could do, not really, only that heat was still moving under his skin and there was a new ability in him that might be asked to work. He wanted to make Bram whole. He reached in his head toward that certainty and felt it slip, felt the way the body under his hands had crossed a line from stressed into rupturing, and the channels there were not answering, not holding, not available to any hand. He could push—he could try—and in that second he understood what it would cost and that it would still fail. Bram’s eyes told him a thing he had taught Jhace first: do not make promises to physics.

“Don’t,” Bram whispered, finding enough air to push the word. A ghost of humor tugged his mouth. “Patch lie. Cut truth.”

Jhace bent and put his forehead to Bram’s for a breath, not in prayer but in respect. The old man’s chest moved once, a long release. He was gone.

The world stayed wrong. That was the part Jhace would never forgive it for: not stopping to notice. A gust cut through the hall from the new seam above, sending banners snapping; the draft made a high keen through the broken tiles. People ran like water finding slope. A followshock rattled tins in a pattern he would hear in sleep for years, a low pressure pulse that reset the body’s idea of balance toward the Core-line that had opened like a throat through the stone fileciteturn1file6.

A woman knelt beside Jhace without asking permission and put her fingers to Bram’s eyelids, closing them. She had ash in her hair and a set to her mouth that suggested she had already done this twice today and would do it twice again. Her hands were clean in a way that work-hands rarely are—more a surgeon’s precision than a butcher’s strength. She looked at the red film drying at Jhace’s upper lip, then at his shaking hands.

“What did you intend,” she said, voice even, “when you made the table hold and the child breathe?”

Jhace stared. “I didn’t—” he began, then realized that was a useless sentence. “I decided the beam would not move. I decided the boy would be whole enough to live. It felt like heat. It hurt after.”

Her eyes went narrow, interested and unkind only in their honesty. “Two separate decisions,” she said. “At the same time?”

“Yes.”

She exhaled. Not surprise. Confirmation. “You’re going to need water in a minute,” she said. “And your hands will shake for hours. It will get worse if you do it again today.” Her gaze flicked to Bram. “You did not try here?”

“I wanted to,” Jhace said. It scraped to say it. “I felt—” The word he wanted was wrong. Not a song. Not beauty. “I felt the channels there failing. There wasn’t anything to catch.”

“Good,” she said, and the word didn’t mean approval; it meant comprehension. She reached under Bram’s shoulders. “Help me move him.”

They carried him to the edge of the hall where the bodies were being laid with the kind of efficiency that comes when the living have made a contract with necessity. Jhace’s hands shook so badly he had to go slow to keep from dropping his mentor. He set Bram down with care he couldn’t find words for and put the fish-wrapper coin in the old man’s vest pocket because it had no other home now.

“Name,” the woman said.

“Jhace,” he said. “You?”

“Tiffani,” she said. “Who taught you to do what you just did?”

“No one,” he said. “It taught itself.”

“Then this is going to be difficult,” she said, and the way she said it made him want to argue and also to listen. She scanned the hall, counting problems like a ledger: three people pinned under a collapsed rail; a line spraying steam against the far wall; a cluster of children coughing in dust-shock. “We stabilize what we can, we grieve later. Can you walk?”

“Yes.”

“Can you decide something and hold to it for a full minute without doubt?”

“Yes,” he said again, and in the saying discovered it was true.

“Good,” she said. “We’re going to work. When you feel the heat rise in your arms, you will tell me, and when it becomes pain, you will stop unless I tell you not to. Do you understand what that will cost?”

He did not, not fully. He understood only the tremor and the thirst and the way his wrists felt scorched. He looked past her at the seam of light above, steady now, a burning scar that made the vault look smaller and older. He thought of Bram’s simple face of trust in him, and the cut truth he had just made by not trying to fix the unfixable. “Yes,” he said. “I understand enough.”

“Then come,” Tiffani said. She did not take his arm. She did not give him a command. She turned and moved with the careful speed of someone who had done this in other cities under other lights. He followed. The draft from the new wound lifted the edge of her coat. The hall’s air tasted of metal and pepper and grief.

They crossed to the spraying line. Steam was fogging the air, faces peeling away from it with animal revulsion. Tiffani put her palm on the pipe and shut her eyes like someone listening to a distant conversation. “Stressed here. If we clamp, it will hold long enough to move the trapped. You, Jhace. Decide the clamp is already biting. Open, don’t force.”

He did as told. Heat ran his veins again, and his breath caught at the first bite of pain as his hands tried to shape two things at once—the clamp’s seated teeth, the line’s pressure narrowing. He held as long as he could stand and then a little longer because people were still dragging the last trapped man clear. The tremor in his fingers sharpened to an ache. He let go only when she said, “Release.”

The clamp stayed. The line steadied. He blinked at the world, which had gone a little bright at the edges.

“You’re doing two again,” Tiffani said, an observation, not an accusation. “You’re not supposed to be able to.”

“Then I won’t,” he said, fierce from pain.

“You will,” she said. “Because you already did.” She wet a cloth from a bucket someone shoved toward them and wiped the blood from his lip with a precision that wasn’t tenderness but did the same work. “We have a lot to do before the next followshock. And the next after that.”

They worked until Striking vanished into a kind of night whose name no one had agreed on yet. The seam of light held, pouring its wrong glow into every corner. The air never stopped moving. Somewhere in the noise a child laughed once, a stray resourceful sound. Jhace heard it and didn’t trust it but took it into inventory with a kind of stubborn gratitude. He carried water when his thirst made his thoughts go sludgy. He set clamps. He held breaths. He watched people live and people not. # Interlude 1A: Angle That Wakes

# Chapter 3: Open, Don’t Force

By morning, the vault had learned the new draft and made a habit of whistling. Where two stones didn’t quite meet, air made a thin high note; where vents bowed, breath pushed and tugged as if trying to pull the whole city through the seams. The core-light along the vaulted roof—not a sky, a wound—threw a steady red-orange that turned faces foxed and made blood look already dried. The heat-grid had been built for a sealed world. It was now an open one, and everything designed for consent was being asked for endurance instead.

They slept where they fell for a little while. Jhace woke to someone setting a cup in his hand. Water. It shocked his tongue, almost sour. His mouth had a paper feel, and when he licked his lips he found the ghost-salt of an old nosebleed. The tremor was there too, a fine busying in his fingers, small enough to ignore when standing still, sharp enough to make buckling a band take two tries. He logged it without self-pity: five becoming six, costs accruing like any ledger that tells the truth.

Tiffani was kneeling on the hall’s edge, hair caught back in a clean knot, coat sleeves rolled. People moved when she pointed, but she rarely pointed. She asked.

“Can you keep the children behind the chalk line?” she said to a woman whose eyes were taking in too much. “Can you carry water with him?” she asked a man gripping a useless broom. “Can you hold a clamp for a count of twenty?” to a boy who wanted a job that would make his sister stop crying. Questions anchored people in their own strength; commands slid off panic and left shame. She knew that. Jhace recognized the technique because he recognized any tool that worked.

She looked over when he stood. “Walk,” she said, and that was the whole of it.

They crossed the rim to a failed junction where a branch had lost its temper and chewed through a soft section of duct. Heat smeared the air there; the pipe had a stressed groan like a promise about to be broken. Civilians had tied cloths over their mouths and were doing the brave useless thing of staring at it as if disapproval could tighten a seam.

Tiffani put her palm to the pipe and shut her eyes. A long breath. “Stressed, not rupturing,” she said. “We can work it.” She opened her eyes and considered Jhace’s hands. “What did you attempt yesterday? Tell it plain.”

“Decided the beam would hold,” he said. “Decided a child would breathe. It felt like heat under the skin. It hurt after. I bled.”

“Two decisions.” She didn’t say impossible. She said, “We will not do two today. Form only.” She set a clamp on the pipe and adjusted it a hair, then another. “This will seem like the opposite of what you think—gentle is stronger. Open, don’t force.” She guided his fingertips to the clamp’s teeth. “Ask them to bite where they belong. Don’t push them into it.” Her voice had procedure to it; there was comfort in that. He’d worked under masters who thought rules were a prison. Bram had taught him rules were the only reason a prison wasn’t what you lived in every day.

Jhace set his breath. He chose simple. The clamp is true. The teeth seat. The pipe accepts the load. He didn’t reach for breath or blood or soul; he reached for the way a line behaves when the hanger is finally drilled true. Heat rose in his forearms the way it had before—contained, deliberate—and he rode the edge of it rather than leaning into it. The clamp settled with a tiny tick he could feel more than hear. The pipe’s groan dropped a register.

Pain edged in after four, five heartbeats, a scrape in his wrists like wire pulled through raw skin. He flinched, almost, then held a count more because people were still too close and the line hadn’t yet found its new path. “Release,” Tiffani said. He did. The clamp stayed. The pipe steadied.

His hands shook harder. The thirst returned immediately, a body-level cry for water and salt. He took a sip and waited for the world to sharpen back to workable. “Good,” Tiffani said, without honey. “You opened correctly. No brute forcing.” She tapped the clamp with a knuckle. “You can’t bully geometry. It will lie to your face and then collapse when you’ve turned away.”

He nodded once. “Next,” he said.

“Next,” she agreed.

They made a circuit of harm. Not heroics—procedures. Valves closed where they would spare a branch; vents opened where pressure needed somewhere to go besides an old woman’s kitchen. At a collapsed rail, Tiffani had the crowd take three steps back with a raised hand and a question to one man: “Can you be the line I need?” The man straightened as if given a rank. At a split seam high on a wall, Jhace and two others stacked crates while Tiffani watched their footing like a hawk. She asked Jhace to set a Form-lock—only that—to hold a hung bracket stable while she drove a bolt through with a real tool. “This is velding,” she said, as if naming a simple craft, not whatever it was that ran heat into his arms. “Persuasion, not force.” Her words fit with the work he knew; it made the work less suspicious inside him.

Where there were bodies, they moved them with clean attention. Where there were lives to keep hold of, they kept hold. Jhace avoided the spot on the market stairs where Bram had lost his breath. Avoidance was not his habit. He allowed himself one tide’s worth and filed the debt.

By the second bell, Chloen found them, hair in a knot so tight it had stung to make. Her cheek was marked with soot and the stubborn line of someone who had decided she would not be useless. “I’m here,” she said, and then, because she was learning, “Where do you need me.”

Jhace wanted to tell her to find a safe corner and hold it down with her back. He counted the work instead. “We have a bracket to pin.” He pointed. “Stand there. If it shifts, you shout for me, not for courage. You don’t lie to me about how your arm is doing.”

Chloen glanced at Tiffani with the quick flick of someone assessing the weather at sea. Tiffani nodded to her as if they’d already worked together for seasons. “Open, don’t force,” Tiffani said to the girl, same tone she’d used with Jhace. “If it sticks, ask it to stop sticking.”

Chloen frowned. “You want me to…ask it.”

“Yes,” Tiffani said. “Words help bones remember.”

Jhace bit back a protest he couldn’t articulate without admitting he’d just done the same. He shifted the bracket; Chloen held like a post, breath steady. The bracket stayed, not because of magic—the weight was well-set—but because the girl did what she was told the way a good hanger does: true, without wobble. He felt a satisfaction that tasted like hunger.

“Can I learn what you’re doing?” Chloen asked in a quiet window between tasks.

“You’re learning,” Jhace said, and meant it. He didn’t want to use the word magic near her. It would make things too light and then too heavy. “You watch. You hold lines true. That’s the first half.”

“And the second?”

“You pay for what you ask,” he said. “And you don’t pretend otherwise.” He flexed his fingers to show the tremor. She watched, serious as a ledger.

Tiffani’s ribs were starting to telegraph their own costs. She masked a wince once and failed to mask it the second time. When she bent to lift a fallen panel, she paused mid-motion and put a hand just under her breastbone as if something inside had demanded a receipt. Jhace took her side of the panel without comment. She let him without comment. The exchange felt like a declaration disguised as sensible labor.

A civic clerk found them—Brighlee, who ran posts and permits and never lost a slate. Her neat hand had smudged, but her voice still carried the calm of someone who liked rules because rules prevent fistfights. “The Hall is assigning routes,” she said. “We’re short in Southcut by six. Northwalk’s valves want a second pass. The Assembly wants a head for the craft crews. I wrote Jhace. They stamped it.”

Jhace blinked at her. “Now.”

“Now,” Brighlee said, and her look at his hands was not pity but math. “You’ll need runners.”

Chloen’s chin lifted. “Here,” she said.

“Good,” Brighlee said, as if she’d expected as much. To Tiffani: “You two are a set. I stacked your names. You’re paired.” It wasn’t policy; it was an observation about what would happen even if no one called it that.

They set their routes. Chloen ran ahead, light-footed, her braid cutting a line through dust. Jhace found that people listened at his first words now. Title is a lever; he didn’t like it, and he used it, and both truths could be held at once. He learned quickly not to ask for more than the materials present could provide. He learned quicker that if he tried to do two things with that heat in his veins—the way he’d done yesterday—pain ballooned past usefulness. Tiffani could read his face and called release at the right breath. She didn’t praise him for stopping; she expected it. That helped.

By midtide, they faced a thing that would not be persuaded: a severed main above a stair where people had to pass or sleep in the hall with their lives held in the open. The main spat scald like anger, and the draft from the new wound caught the steam and drove it where it could do harm. Jhace wanted to close the valve that would solve it. The valve lived behind a wall now three tons heavier than yesterday. He put his hand on the stone.

Tiffani watched him. “What’s the least you can ask?” she said.

“Stall the spray to a curtain,” he said. “Enough to pass.”

“Do that,” she said. “Then we figure how to close what you can’t reach.”

He tried. Heat rose along his arms, filling the veins like they were inspected channels. He shaped the spray into a sheet with his will and a little help from a board scavenged from a shattered stall. It held for a few breaths. The cost sharpened from ache to pain, then to the kind of hurt that makes the jaw tighten and the vision grainy. He let go. The spray burst back into fury.

“Good,” Tiffani said. “You did not make a lie last longer. You let it fail right.” She was always like that: knowledge as kindness. “We’ll pull people through in pulses. Curtain between. You hold only long enough for five at a time.”

“Five,” he said, and made it true twice, three times. On the fourth, his hands missed the count, and he felt the edge of something bad—like the start of a split in a hanger you thought you could trust. “Enough,” Tiffani said sharp, and he stopped. The curtain collapsed into steam and scalded no one, because she had timed the pull to his strength instead of his pride.

When it was done, he leaned back against stone and tried to keep the world in one piece. The tremor had climbed. His wrists were raw under the skin, a faint map of red lines that had not been there before and now were. He covered them with his sleeves and didn’t pretend.

Tiffani took his forearm and turned it palm up, clinical and careful. “This will mark,” she said. “You’ll wear it for a day. Maybe two.” She looked at his mouth. “Drink.” He did. She sipped too and did not hide her own flinch when it hit her ribs wrong. “We’re not gods,” she said in a tone that warned off arguments.

“Good,” Jhace said, and meant it.

The last bell of the tide found them in Southcut, pinning a stair that had tired of pretending to be straight. Chloen held the bracket while Jhace persuaded a bolt to accept the world as it currently existed and Tiffani made a tiny, precise correction with the heel of her hand that made the difference between true and almost. The light through the fissure had settled into a steady stare. The draft had become part of how people breathed. None of it looked like it would ever stop.

“Tomorrow,” Tiffani said, as they walked back toward the hall.

“Tomorrow,” Jhace said.

“Shared work,” she added, as if listing a tool to bring. “I’ll show you a way to lower cost. Not free. Less alone.” A beat. “If you consent.”

“I consent,” he said, before he knew what it would be. He trusted her enough to say it without a speech. He knew that if she asked him to stop, he would stop. He knew that if he asked her to rest, she would ignore him until her hands told her otherwise.

# Chapter 4: Shared Load

Tide turned without ceremony. The vault breathed wrong but regular—the new hum under the stone steady as a heartbeat that had decided no one got to vote anymore. The core-light made a long scar across the vault’s roof, red-orange and patient. Dust hung in the air and learned to be part of lungs. People had run out of fresh ways to be afraid and settled into work.

Jhace’s fingers shook before he even picked up a clamp. Small, fussy movement, the kind that turns threading a nut into a meditation. He didn’t hate it. Hate wastes heat. He logged it as a cost and moved.

Tiffani met him at the Assembly steps with a slate Brighlee had issued and lines drawn in clean ink. Her braid had loosened—one strand escaped, a bright line against her temple—but everything else about her looked engineered. “Two priorities,” she said. “Breath and flow. We keep people breathing. We keep heat where heat belongs.”

“Flow first at Southcut Junction,” Brighlee said, appearing like a new task. “Breath at Hall Three. Dust took the old ones.”

“Then we split,” Tiffani said, and didn’t. She looked at Jhace’s hands. “We stay paired. Shared work, lower cost. If you consent.”

“I do,” Jhace said.

They took Southcut first. A branch had gone sulky where it coughed into a tangle of home vents—someone’s clever workaround that had worked fine in a sealed world. Now the draft from below pulled at it, made a whistle out of the whole knot, and the whistle had a rhythm that made babies cry. The pipe groaned its complaint with a pitch that told Jhace the hanger above was lying by a finger’s width.

Tiffani brushed the knot with her fingertips, as if asking a child to tell her where it hurt. “Three teeth out of place left. Clamp here,” she said, but made it a question: “Can you set three?”

“Yes,” Jhace said, and then she did the next strange thing.

“Let me share the sentence,” she said, standing beside him close enough that he could smell the clean mineral of water on her skin. “I’ll carry Form, you keep to it. I’ll hold the grammar; you make the statement.”

He didn’t understand the words and the work made the words unnecessary. She put her palm just above the clamp as if feeling the shape the metal wanted, and Jhace felt the geometry go quiet in his head the way a line quiets under a true hanger. He didn’t have to find the angle; she presented it. He set his intention inside it like a rivet hammered into a drilled hole. Heat rose along his veins, familiar now, but it came up softer, less like an edge dragged across raw skin. He imagined the three teeth biting, not everywhere, just where they should, a sentence that didn’t waste syllables. The clamp clicked. The pipe’s groan shifted.

Pain arrived slower. Not absent, slower. Tiffani’s breath hitched once beside him, a private cost she didn’t make a face for. He might have missed it if he hadn’t been leaning inside the same sentence. “Release,” she said at the right breath; he let the hold go. The clamp stayed. His hands trembled, but not at the same hum as before. Lower, steadier.

“That’s shared load,” she said, workmanlike. “It won’t make you a god. It will keep you from debt you can’t pay by noon.”

“What’s your cost,” he asked, because truth divides evenly when you make it.

“Bruising,” she said, frank. She tapped below her ribs. “Inside. Not for you to borrow. Look at the hanger.”

He looked up. The hanger had chewed at the seam. “We can hold all morning,” he said, “or cut once and move three families.”

Tiffani turned her head, assessing with that not-quite-kind honesty she carried. “Cut,” she said. “Brighlee?”

“Already posted,” Brighlee said, raising a slate. “Hall Three first, then the move.”

They made Hall Three in four minutes of hard motion. Dust had been the worst teacher overnight. It had sat in a corner of the hall until a followshock lifted it like a thrown rug. Now six people coughed in the shallow, panicked way that spends breath and buys little. One woman lay with her eyes open and her hands on her ribs like she could hold the ache shut. A child’s cough hit the same spot in Jhace’s chest every time—same frequency as a leaking seam.

“Breath first,” Tiffani said, and people who had been waiting for a command brought water and cloth and moved benches without needing to be told which direction.

“Wholeness?” Jhace asked, low. They hadn’t said the word. He didn’t like the word, too clean for what it took.

Tiffani did not soften. “Form around it,” she said. “You don’t knit; you keep shape so the body can do its job. You set ribs true for a minute; the lungs handle the gas. Open; don’t force.”

He knelt beside the woman holding her own ribs. “Put your hands on mine,” he said, showing her the place—lower cage, front and sides. “Breathe with me. Slow. You’ll take it as far as you can and then more.”

She would have laughed at him yesterday. Today she followed. He set his attention as Tiffani had shown him—found the geometry of the ribcage, the expanding ovals, the tiny shiver where pain had taught the muscles to flinch early—and he decided those flinches would wait. Heat crawled into his forearms, more intimate now, a presence that felt like the body had decided to inform him of its designs. It hurt at four heartbeats. He held to six. The woman took a deeper breath than she had managed since the dust had lifted, and her eyes flooded with tears the body makes without asking permission. He let go. The ache stayed, a clean ache this time. He moved to the next.

“Curtain,” Tiffani said to a pair of men standing useless. They snapped to, stretched wet cloth across the hall’s open arch to keep the worst of the dust from walking back in. “You,” she told a girl near the chalk line. “Count breaths out loud for me.” The girl did, voice steady and kind in its small way.

On the third patient, Jhace overreached. Too much ask, too fast. He went to hold the ribs and to ease the throat at the same time, the way you do when you don’t trust a single fix to be enough. Pain bit hard, a white flicker behind his eyes, and his hands lost their fine. The woman choked. “No two,” Tiffani said, hand quick on his wrist, not a chastisement, a trim. “One line. The body will do the rest.”

“One line,” he repeated, and set it. The woman coughed up a clot of dust and old blood, spat, then breathed shallow but steady. He sat back on his heels and wiped sweat that had dried before it reached his jaw.

“Drink,” Tiffani said. She was lighter in the face now, the way you get when the work finally obeys. Then she put a fist to her sternum the way a person tests a bruise and said nothing.

They cut Northwalk’s liar pipe an hour later. Chloen ran ahead to knock doors. Jhace carried the old man’s bag down the stair again and the old man saw the tremor and didn’t pretend not to. “You shake,” he said. Not pity. Measurement.

“Yes,” Jhace said.

“Still hold,” the old man said, and the simplicity of it fitted in Jhace’s chest like a wedge that had finally found its slot.

The cut was clean. New band. New true. While the resin cooled, Jhace stood with his hand on the wall and felt the draft as a set of smaller currents, like a complicated vent. “We need a baffle,” he said. “Not pretty. Hung cloth and frame. Take the bite out.”

“Show me,” Tiffani said, because she asked people to show as much as she asked them to do. He sketched it with a finger in dust: two posts wedged into the stair seam, a crossbar, blankets from Hall stocks doubled and soaked, weight them with chain so they lay obediently. “Open, don’t seal,” he said. “Let it exhale.”

They hung it. The whistle softened from a blade to a voice you could share space with. People stepped through without instinctively ducking. A girl coming up the stair put a hand out to test the cloth and looked at Jhace like she had never expected the world to get quieter again and was grateful it had managed a little. He looked away before the look made promises out of him.

Eating took too long and tasted like nothing. He did it anyway. Tiffani finished before him and stood, then paused, hand to that same place beneath her ribs. Her throat worked once. He set his bowl aside unfinished and reached, not to help—a reaching that would insult—but to be there if help failed to be a choice.

“I’m all right,” she said. It wasn’t a lie; it was a decision. “Shared work cuts you first and me later. That’s the arithmetic. I accept it.”

“We can rotate call,” he said. “You hold Form; I go Wholeness. Then trade.”

“We will,” she said, and the future in her voice made the stair feel less narrow.

By Casting, they had a rhythm: shared holds that bit clean and released on time; physical tools doing all the work that should be left to metal and resin; the least magic possible because the bill always came due. They moved three families, then four more, then mediated a fight between a baker who had learned nothing and an old woman who had learned to make a weapon of her tongue. Brighlee posted a revision to the route map and highlighted corridors that would become vents whether the city wanted them or not. “We name them,” she said. “We own them.”

Late, finally late, they came to a boy holding his own arm like it would fall off if he let it. A scald had painted his forearm raw and white in a cuff above the wrist. He tried bravado and failed at it. “It’s nothing,” he said. “I can—”

“You can’t,” Tiffani said, kind and exact. “Show me,” she added, and the boy turned his arm, jaw clamped so hard it had to hurt twice.

Jhace heard Bram in his own mouth before the words came. “We don’t promise what physics won’t give,” he said. “We can give you breath over a rise. We can stop the panic that makes it worse. We can keep the swelling from crushing what’s beneath.” He looked to Tiffani. She nodded once: do it.

He set Form around the swelling—gentle ring, not prison—and asked it to stop growing for a count. Heat rose. Pain came like an honest visitor. The boy’s shoulders dropped a fraction. “Now you,” Tiffani told the boy. “Breathe wide. In the back.” He did, surprised at his own competence. Jhace released when the ring wanted to become a fist. The skin pinked a little. The white corners softened.

“Tomorrow we’ll see a proper cutter,” Tiffani said to the boy. “Today you keep it clean. Don’t be brave about dirt.”

“Noted,” the boy said, fierce as if he’d been given an honor rather than a warning.

They walked home at the line between work and collapse. Jhace’s wrists carried a new tenderness above the old tracery, as if the heat had left a map under the skin it meant to consult again. His tremor sat at the edge of usefulness but not past it. Tiffani’s breath ran shallow when she forgot to boss it; when she remembered, it steadied. They passed the place Bram had died and did not pause; payment was made, ledger noted.

At the hall, Chloen was waiting with notes written in a neat, stubborn hand: where people were hoarding, where pipes sounded wrong, where children took to chalking lines on walls because they had watched Tiffani and thought they could make the world obey too. “We’ll let them,” Tiffani said softly when she saw it. “We’ll correct them, but we’ll let them start.”

“Tomorrow,” Jhace said.

“Together,” Tiffani replied.

He lay down where he could see a slice of the core-light through a hall arch. It did not defy him. It did not explain itself. He held his hands up and watched them shake, then turned them palm-down and let them rest. The new hum under the stone kept time. His body tried to settle and found a way. The world was not forgiving; the work did not ask it to be. He slept.

Her braid had loosened—one strand escaped, a bright line against her temple—but everything else about her looked engineered. “Two priorities,” she said. “Breath and flow. We keep people breathing. We keep heat where heat belongs.”

“Flow first at Southcut Junction,” Brighlee said, appearing like a new task. “Breath at Hall Three. Dust took the old ones.”

“Then we split,” Tiffani said, and didn’t. She looked at Jhace’s hands. “We stay paired. Shared work, lower cost. If you consent.”

“I do,” Jhace said.

They took Southcut first. A branch had gone sulky where it coughed into a tangle of home vents—someone’s clever workaround that had worked fine in a sealed world. Now the draft from below pulled at it, made a whistle out of the whole knot, and the whistle had a rhythm that made babies cry. The pipe groaned its complaint with a pitch that told Jhace the hanger above was lying by a finger’s width.

Tiffani brushed the knot with her fingertips, as if asking a child to tell her where it hurt. “Three teeth out of place left. Clamp here,” she said, but made it a question: “Can you set three?”

“Yes,” Jhace said, and then she did the next strange thing.

“Let me share the sentence,” she said, standing beside him close enough that he could smell the clean mineral of water on her skin. “I’ll carry Form, you keep to it. I’ll hold the grammar; you make the statement.”

He didn’t understand the words and the work made the words unnecessary. She put her palm just above the clamp as if feeling the shape the metal wanted, and Jhace felt the geometry go quiet in his head the way a line quiets under a true hanger. He didn’t have to find the angle; she presented it. He set his intention inside it like a rivet hammered into a drilled hole. Heat rose along his veins, familiar now, but it came up softer, less like an edge dragged across raw skin. He imagined the three teeth biting, not everywhere, just where they should, a sentence that didn’t waste syllables. The clamp clicked. The pipe’s groan shifted.

Pain arrived slower. Not absent, slower. Tiffani’s breath hitched once beside him, a private cost she didn’t make a face for. He might have missed it if he hadn’t been leaning inside the same sentence. “Release,” she said at the right breath; he let the hold go. The clamp stayed. His hands trembled, but not at the same hum as before. Lower, steadier.

“That’s shared load,” she said, workmanlike. “It won’t make you a god. It will keep you from debt you can’t pay by noon.”

“What’s your cost,” he asked, because truth divides evenly when you make it.

“Bruising,” she said, frank. She tapped below her ribs. “Inside. Not for you to borrow. Look at the hanger.”

He looked up. The hanger had chewed at the seam. “We can hold all morning,” he said, “or cut once and move three families.”

Tiffani turned her head, assessing with that not-quite-kind honesty she carried. “Cut,” she said. “Brighlee?”

“Already posted,” Brighlee said, raising a slate. “Hall Three first, then the move.”

They made Hall Three in four minutes of hard motion. Dust had been the worst teacher overnight. It had sat in a corner of the hall until a followshock lifted it like a thrown rug. Now six people coughed in the shallow, panicked way that spends breath and buys little. One woman lay with her eyes open and her hands on her ribs like she could hold the ache shut. A child’s cough hit the same spot in Jhace’s chest every time—same frequency as a leaking seam.

“Breath first,” Tiffani said, and people who had been waiting for a command brought water and cloth and moved benches without needing to be told which direction.

“Wholeness?” Jhace asked, low. They hadn’t said the word. He didn’t like the word, too clean for what it took.

Tiffani did not soften. “Form around it,” she said. “You don’t knit; you keep shape so the body can do its job. You set ribs true for a minute; the lungs handle the gas. Open; don’t force.”

He knelt beside the woman holding her own ribs. “Put your hands on mine,” he said, showing her the place—lower cage, front and sides. “Breathe with me. Slow. You’ll take it as far as you can and then more.”

She would have laughed at him yesterday. Today she followed. He set his attention as Tiffani had shown him—found the geometry of the ribcage, the expanding ovals, the tiny shiver where pain had taught the muscles to flinch early—and he decided those flinches would wait. Heat crawled into his forearms, more intimate now, a presence that felt like the body had decided to inform him of its designs. It hurt at four heartbeats. He held to six. The woman took a deeper breath than she had managed since the dust had lifted, and her eyes flooded with tears the body makes without asking permission. He let go. The ache stayed, a clean ache this time. He moved to the next.

“Curtain,” Tiffani said to a pair of men standing useless. They snapped to, stretched wet cloth across the hall’s open arch to keep the worst of the dust from walking back in. “You,” she told a girl near the chalk line. “Count breaths out loud for me.” The girl did, voice steady and kind in its small way.

On the third patient, Jhace overreached. Too much ask, too fast. He went to hold the ribs and to ease the throat at the same time, the way you do when you don’t trust a single fix to be enough. Pain bit hard, a white flicker behind his eyes, and his hands lost their fine. The woman choked. “No two,” Tiffani said, hand quick on his wrist, not a chastisement, a trim. “One line. The body will do the rest.”

“One line,” he repeated, and set it. The woman coughed up a clot of dust and old blood, spat, then breathed shallow but steady. He sat back on his heels and wiped sweat that had dried before it reached his jaw.

“Drink,” Tiffani said. She was lighter in the face now, the way you get when the work finally obeys. Then she put a fist to her sternum the way a person tests a bruise and said nothing.

They cut Northwalk’s liar pipe an hour later. Chloen ran ahead to knock doors. Jhace carried the old man’s bag down the stair again and the old man saw the tremor and didn’t pretend not to. “You shake,” he said. Not pity. Measurement.

“Yes,” Jhace said.

“Still hold,” the old man said, and the simplicity of it fitted in Jhace’s chest like a wedge that had finally found its slot.

The cut was clean. New band. New true. While the resin cooled, Jhace stood with his hand on the wall and felt the draft as a set of smaller currents, like a complicated vent. “We need a baffle,” he said. “Not pretty. Hung cloth and frame. Take the bite out.”

“Show me,” Tiffani said, because she asked people to show as much as she asked them to do. He sketched it with a finger in dust: two posts wedged into the stair seam, a crossbar, blankets from Hall stocks doubled and soaked, weight them with chain so they lay obediently. “Open, don’t seal,” he said. “Let it exhale.”

They hung it. The whistle softened from a blade to a voice you could share space with. People stepped through without instinctively ducking. A girl coming up the stair put a hand out to test the cloth and looked at Jhace like she had never expected the world to get quieter again and was grateful it had managed a little. He looked away before the look made promises out of him.

Eating took too long and tasted like nothing. He did it anyway. Tiffani finished before him and stood, then paused, hand to that same place beneath her ribs. Her throat worked once. He set his bowl aside unfinished and reached, not to help—a reaching that would insult—but to be there if help failed to be a choice.

“I’m all right,” she said. It wasn’t a lie; it was a decision. “Shared work cuts you first and me later. That’s the arithmetic. I accept it.”

“We can rotate call,” he said. “You hold Form; I go Wholeness. Then trade.”

“We will,” she said, and the future in her voice made the stair feel less narrow.

By Casting, they had a rhythm: shared holds that bit clean and released on time; physical tools doing all the work that should be left to metal and resin; the least magic possible because the bill always came due. They moved three families, then four more, then mediated a fight between a baker who had learned nothing and an old woman who had learned to make a weapon of her tongue. Brighlee posted a revision to the route map and highlighted corridors that would become vents whether the city wanted them or not. “We name them,” she said. “We own them.”

Late, finally late, they came to a boy holding his own arm like it would fall off if he let it. A scald had painted his forearm raw and white in a cuff above the wrist. He tried bravado and failed at it. “It’s nothing,” he said. “I can—”

“You can’t,” Tiffani said, kind and exact. “Show me,” she added, and the boy turned his arm, jaw clamped so hard it had to hurt twice.

Jhace heard Bram in his own mouth before the words came. “We don’t promise what physics won’t give,” he said. “We can give you breath over a rise. We can stop the panic that makes it worse. We can keep the swelling from crushing what’s beneath.” He looked to Tiffani. She nodded once: do it.

He set Form around the swelling—gentle ring, not prison—and asked it to stop growing for a count. Heat rose. Pain came like an honest visitor. The boy’s shoulders dropped a fraction. “Now you,” Tiffani told the boy. “Breathe wide. In the back.” He did, surprised at his own competence. Jhace released when the ring wanted to become a fist. The skin pinked a little. The white corners softened.

“Tomorrow we’ll see a proper cutter,” Tiffani said to the boy. “Today you keep it clean. Don’t be brave about dirt.”

“Noted,” the boy said, fierce as if he’d been given an honor rather than a warning.

They walked home at the line between work and collapse. Jhace’s wrists carried a new tenderness above the old tracery, as if the heat had left a map under the skin it meant to consult again. His tremor sat at the edge of usefulness but not past it. Tiffani’s breath ran shallow when she forgot to boss it; when she remembered, it steadied. They passed the place Bram had died and did not pause; payment was made, ledger noted.

At the hall, Chloen was waiting with notes written in a neat, stubborn hand: where people were hoarding, where pipes sounded wrong, where children took to chalking lines on walls because they had watched Tiffani and thought they could make the world obey too. “We’ll let them,” Tiffani said softly when she saw it. “We’ll correct them, but we’ll let them start.”

“Tomorrow,” Jhace said.

“Together,” Tiffani replied.

He lay down where he could see a slice of the core-light through a hall arch. It did not defy him. It did not explain itself. He held his hands up and watched them shake, then turned them palm-down and let them rest. The new hum under the stone kept time. His body tried to settle and found a way. The world was not forgiving; the work did not ask it to be. He slept.

# Chapter 6: Baffle Law

Anneal bell found the vault already hot. The draft had decided on its new habits—steady pull up through every crack, high whistle where stone lied to stone, a low hum you felt in the teeth. Core-light smeared along the vault roof, red-orange and impartial. Dust had learned people’s names and sat at their doorways like a stray that refused to leave.

Jhace’s hands shook when he knotted his rag to his belt. The tremor wasn’t theatrical; it just made every fine motion cost a thought. He rolled his shoulders and counted the useful aches: forearms tender, wrists hot where that faint red map lived under the skin, throat dry on waking. He called it fair.

Brighlee met them on the Assembly steps with a slate that had new ink and fingerprints. “Two reds,” she said, meaning rupture-grade. “Westbath manifold again, and a main at Petrel Span. Twenty ambers. Pick your starting sin.”

“Westbath,” Tiffani said. “Bathing halls lie beautifully. They kill you honest.”

Jhace nodded and they moved. Chloen, punctual as a bell, fell in with a runner’s bounce that didn’t waste breath. She’d cut her hair shorter; strands had singed yesterday and she’d taken a knife to the rest without ceremony. She carried a coil right. He noticed because noticing kept people alive.

Westbath had adapted. The keeper had hung a sign that said “COLD BY DECREE” and had got ahead of the complaints by posting it large enough to be read from a grievance away. The false sky on the ceiling had flaked more, and the drift-painting looked like it was finally telling the truth about storms. Steam crouched in the corners, mean.

The manifold sang a note Jhace didn’t like. He felt the pitch in his wrists—too thin, too high. He pointed without asking what was allowed. “Clamp there, half-step. Hanger’s lying at the seam.”

Tiffani put her palm to the pipe, eyes closing as if to invite a second sense into the room. “Three teeth,” she said, and then, to Jhace: “Shared sentence?”

He set himself beside her, shoulders almost touching. The closeness made the work easier in a way that didn’t feel mystical—just accurate. She held Form’s grammar open—forty-five, true; seat, don’t shove—and he placed his intention inside it. Heat came up along his veins, a manageable swell. The clamp accepted the bite like it had been waiting to be asked properly. The groan dropped a register. Pain followed late, less sharp. Tiffani’s breath snagged once. He released on her word.

“Band’s chewed,” he said, listening with his hand. “We can hold all tide, but it’s an apology if we do.”

“Cut,” Tiffani said. “Chloen, clear the hall. You’re good at being obeyed.”

Chloen made a face like praise had brushed her the wrong way and went to work. She didn’t shout. She told three people each and made them into deputies without letting them notice. The room emptied to the edges in a disciplined wave. Jhace did the arithmetic: with the clamp holding, they could pull the band, seat a new one, and bring flow back up before the bathers turned mutiny into policy.

They shared the hold while Jhace torched the resin and set the new band. His hands made clean work; his wrists objected, a bright complaint under the skin. Tiffani took weight when his attention had to go to heat. He felt the shift in her breath, the small quiet cost she never announced. When it was time to load the line again, he gave her a nod just bigger than nothing. She returned it with the same economy. They let the clamp go. The song settled into its proper chest.

“Baffle law,” Jhace said, half to himself.

“What law,” Tiffani asked, wiping steam from her cheek with the back of her hand.

“We keep trying to seal the world and it’s not sealed anymore,” he said. “So we baffle. Guide the draft instead of losing fights with it. Westbath needs a frame and cloth at each arch, hung loose. We’ll never keep it out; we can teach it manners.”

Tiffani’s mouth tilted in that way that meant approval without sugar. “Write it,” she said to Brighlee. “Baffle law at bathing halls. Materials: posts, chain, soaked cloth. Guidance: open, don’t seal.”

“Already inked,” Brighlee said, stylus scratching. “You two name a thing, I put it on paper. The city learns by reading.”

Petrel Span was less cooperative. A main had split along a seam, steam jetting with a mean periodicity that matched the followshocks—like the city’s new heartbeat had found a personal vent. The span was a choke point; people had to cross or sleep on the wrong side of their lives.

“We can curtain in pulses,” Tiffani said, her eye measuring the flow. “Five at a time.”

Jhace shook his head a fraction. “Not today. The jet bites harder than yesterday. It’ll peel someone if we miss the count.”

“What’s your ask, then?”

“Form frame the valve box,” he said, pointing to the wall where the access sat under three tons of toppled stone. “Hold it just enough. I punch through and close.”

“You can’t hold that and your arm,” she said.

“We share,” he said. “You keep the frame true. I turn. If it slips, we let it go and do pulses. We don’t hero our way into a funeral.”

“Consent,” he returned.

Heat rose in his arms, matched in her breath. He felt the stutter of the toppled stones quiet into a temporary obedience, a grace period carved from disaster. “Now,” she said.

The valve moved. Not all the way, but enough. The jet dropped to a spit. People crossed, heads down, hands on shoulders. He let go. The frame failed in a controlled slump. The panel fell, harmless now. He had to put his hand against the wall to keep the world correctly vertical.

“Drink,” Tiffani said. She was pale in the cheeks in a way that translated to bruising later. He handed her the cup first. She swished, swallowed, pressed a knuckle under her ribs, and said, “Next.”

Next was a training line. They hadn’t called it that, but that’s what it turned into. Brighlee posted a chalk-drawn brief in the market hall—clamp etiquette, baffle law, pulse curtain protocol—and Tiffani began asking the right people the right questions. “Can you count out loud for thirty?” to a woman who could. “Can you hold this bracket without turning it into a fist?” to a man with careful hands. “Can you run and not get heroic?” to Chloen. The hall became a class without anyone calling it one. Jhace watched and learned how to teach without making speeches.

“Stop,” Tiffani said, calm and iron. She took his wrist, turned it, and looked. “You were brave at the wrong angle.” No scold, just a diagnosis. She set Form like a ring around the new swelling and nodded at Jhace. “One line. Keep the bite from marching.”

Heat rose. Pain followed. The swelling backed off a fraction, enough. The boy’s mouth softened from a clench. “It’s going to scar,” Jhace said, voice even. “It will not own your grip.”

The youth blinked hard. “Teach me the right angle.”

“We are,” Tiffani said, and meant it.

They ate standing up, which is a kind of worship in a city that needs you more than you need a chair. Jhace chewed without taste and felt the tremor run steady rather than sharp. Shared work had shaved down the peaks. The valleys, though—they were deeper. Fatigue came in honest, a weight at the base of the neck.

“Who taught you,” he asked Tiffani in a small, earned quiet between crises. Not challenge. Inventory.

She watched the core-light along the vault’s seam for a beat too long and answered with the truth she was willing to offer. “Someone who learned early and alone,” she said. “Who paid poorly at first, then better. Who taught me to ask, not order. That’s enough for today.”

He let that be the wall for now. You don’t cut access panels where there isn’t a valve behind them.

In the second half of tide, a followshock ran under the floor with a longer stride. The market tiles clacked like teeth. A corner pole at the Assembly plaza leaned, and you could feel the city decide to pretend it hadn’t. Jhace put his hand on the pole and listened. The grain had split at the base like a bad promise. “We pull it,” he said. “Replace with angle iron. Baffle the arch so the draft doesn’t set it singing again.”

“Do it,” Brighlee said, already sending runners for iron and chain. “I’m posting a team to chalk every pole with an age older than your grandmother.”

“Which grandmother,” Chloen asked, too sharp to be funny and exactly funny anyway.

Jhace cut the base with care. The pole came free and wanted to fall into the path of people who hadn’t learned the habit of looking up yet. He decided it would not. Heat rose. Pain bit. He set its path with his will—there, not here—and Tiffani’s hand touched his elbow, taking a sliver of the cost in that quiet way that did not feel like mercy so much as good craft. The pole obeyed. He hated that the work now had seconds where it depended on that kind of decision. He loved that it did not make a liar out of him when he made it.

By Casting, the plaza had angle iron where it needed it and baffle frames at three arches. Brighlee had handfuls of notes with people’s names next to them, which is how cities actually live: not by laws but by lists. Chloen brought two new runners into their orbit, terrible at knots, wonderful at not panicking. The keeper at Westbath sent a fish pocket as penance for yesterday’s words. They ate it and called it forgiveness.

They finished at Hall Three, because the day had decided to be a circle. Dust still tried to make a church of people’s lungs. Jhace set Form around three ribs. The heat came, the ache followed, he released on time. Tiffani took a longer breath than she had in hours and pressed her fist once under her sternum and then stopped pressing because watching was not helping.

“Tomorrow,” she said.

“Baffle law teaches itself,” Jhace said. “We need a crew that only builds frames.”

“We make one,” Brighlee said, overhearing like a talent. “I’ll grab carpenters out of Northface. Somebody’s work wants to be useful again.”

Chloen scratched it onto a slate with a seriousness that made the idea real. “I’ll route them,” she said, forehead smudged, eyes bright with the kind of tired that earns sleep.

At the hall’s edge, the core-light did its patient burning. The draft played the arch cloths like a low instrument. Jhace flexed his fingers. The tremor was there. The red map under his skin had climbed a finger higher. He rolled his wrists and accepted the ache.

“Tomorrow we figure the cost curve,” Tiffani said as they parted. “Where it breaks you. Where it breaks me.”

“Not free,” he said.

“Less alone,” she answered. It had become their doctrine.

He slept with his hands under his cheek like a worker guarding tools and dreamed the city finally breathing steady. The dream didn’t lie—only invented a later tide to set it in. He took the mercy and didn’t argue.

# Chapter 7: Cut Truth

Casting slid over the city like a hand smoothing a wrinkled cloth—order attempted, not guaranteed. The hum under the stone kept its patient tempo. Baffle cloths at the arches learned to answer the draft with civility instead of fear. Even so, every time a followshock padded through the frames you could feel a fraction of the city brace.

He tightened the ask, felt the river of heat narrow to a blade. Pain went from honest to bright. Tiffani’s breath cut short; she rode it without sound. The valve moved. It stuck. He felt the slack come into his hands, the way a hanger tells you it’s had enough.

“Release,” Tiffani said.

“Nearly—”

“Release,” she said again, and because he trusted her, he obeyed. The stone settled back an inch. The valve held at seventy percent. The leak slowed to a weeping.

“That’s enough to buy a cut,” she said, sweat bright on her upper lip. “We don’t spend our hands to be heroes.”

He wanted to argue that heroes aren’t what this is. He swallowed the old boy’s romance and picked up the saw. They cut the main clean, shamelessly. Steam took the lane, rolled it, cleared it. People stepped back a step and then another, because evolution loves a second chance.

The band needed more heat than he had patience for. He kept his hands visible to himself—red map, tremor now past steady, a new tiny tick in the thumbs—and made himself go slow. Tiffani kept the frame true with touch instead of will—real braces, real chain, real craft. He loved her for that in a way that had no speech and no room right now.

On the third, a child’s cry cut through the steam—the thin edged sound of scald. A boy had slipped the net and taken the spray across his calf. He went down hard, bite of scream, hands fumbling for a hold on air. Jhace was there before he felt how he’d moved.

“Don’t promise,” Bram had said, and Jhace didn’t. “Breathe wide,” he told the boy. “Look at me.” The boy did—the crisis gift children give when they decide to live because you have told them it is possible. Jhace set a Form ring around the swelling rising under the scald—gentle, not prison. Heat hurt. He held for a count that made his vision grain. The swelling eased back from the first crush. “You’ll limp,” he said. “You’ll keep the leg.”

The boy’s face twisted into an ugly brave and then into a smaller real. “Okay,” he breathed. He let himself be carried. Jhace let go and the ache in his wrists went from ache to pain to the edge of something worse—the white goodnight that eats tools out of your fingers. He shook his hands once, hard, then laid them still to keep the tremor from learning a habit.

Tiffani had already stepped into the slot he’d left, band setting true under her hands. “Another minute,” she said.

“Then water,” Jhace said.

“And bread,” she added, because even the competent are kinder than they pretend.

The band seated; the resin cooled; the seam took truth and held. When they opened the feeder to find the new flow, it settled without complaint. The market exhaled. People looked for someone to thank and decided to thank bread instead. That was fine. Bread does not blush.

They ate standing with their backs to a post, both of them counting the tiny sounds that mean settled instead of settling. Chloen appeared with the boy’s name written on slate in case names got lost in all the moving. “Rion,” she said. “His aunt will keep him from being heroic. I threatened her with you.”

“Wise,” Tiffani said, dry. She finished the last bite like fuel, not joy. Then she set the heel of her hand lightly under her sternum and held it there as if to feel the bruise back into obedience.

“How deep,” Jhace asked.

“Like a thumb’s worth of stone sitting inside,” she said. “It will pay me late tonight. Nothing you can fix without me paying more.”

“I wasn’t going to offer,” he said. “I was going to demand you sit for one count longer than your pride likes.”

She considered the luxury of resistance and chose not to. She sat for two counts. Then she stood. “Spoke Six again by cool,” she said. “If the band sings, we hear it.”

They walked the rest of the route with that specific end-of-tide competence—fewer words, every gesture clean. Brighlee’s new baffle crew had grown a spine; you could tell because they bickered over inches. Jhace corrected once—open, don’t seal—and the baffles took the draft from bite to breath. A woman at Westface pressed a jar of preserved plum into Tiffani’s hands and then pretended she’d meant to hand it to Jhace so she wouldn’t have to make a speech. He tucked it in his kit like a tool.

Near last bell, a valve wheel at Spoke Two resisted a quarter turn and then lurched, the lie in the threads finally rounding out. The handle kicked, smashed hard into Jhace’s forearm. Nerves lit, a bright burn laid along the red map. He swore—quiet, efficient. Tiffani’s hand was there, holding his elbow, taking a sliver of the cost again with that without-language that made the work feel less like a fight against a patient god and more like velding. He breathed, regripped, finished the turn. The line settled.

“Pay later,” she said.

“Always,” he said.

They made the hall as the core-light dimmed a fraction—which is to say: the city’s eyes adjusted and pretended that meant dark. Chloen laid out slates: where baffles were wanted, where clamps sang a note she didn’t like, where hoarding stopped looking like prudence and started looking like theft. Brighlee posted a notice: “Cut Truth Schedule,” with times and corners and a line at the bottom that said, in her neat straight hand, “We will make mistakes and then corrections. We will choose corrections.”

Jhace sat. The tremor had found a new hum—higher, insistent—but it hadn’t stolen his grip. The red under his skin had climbed to just below the elbow, a painted gauntlet he hadn’t asked for and couldn’t remove. He flexed and let the pain write its small signature. He drank water until his stomach protested and then stopped. Tiffani didn’t take his cup; she went to the bucket and poured her own because sharing has rules, too.

“You’re at twelve,” she said, voice low. “Maybe twelve and a half.”

He considered the number like a bolt he’d need to fit tomorrow. “Thirteen is where hands lie and say they’re fine,” he said.

“Then we stay at twelve this tide,” she said. “We don’t push into hollowing for a clean seam.”

“Agreed.”

She sat beside him, not touching, close enough that if he listed he could pretend gravity had done it. “Tomorrow I show you a way to put Form around Wholeness without letting them eat each other,” she said. “It’s physics, not romance. If we can do it, people breathe easier and pipes don’t pay for it.”

He nodded slowly, because even the idea was a heat in the veins. “Open, don’t force.”

“Cut truth,” she answered, and he felt Bram’s ghost in it like a hand on a ladder rail.

They slept beneath a baffle cloth that moved with the draft like a lazy tide. Jhace dreamed of valves that turned when asked, of breath that came without a cost ledger, of hands that did not shake. He woke before dawn and counted his fingers steady by stubborn choice. The count came true on the third try. He called it progress and stood into another day.
