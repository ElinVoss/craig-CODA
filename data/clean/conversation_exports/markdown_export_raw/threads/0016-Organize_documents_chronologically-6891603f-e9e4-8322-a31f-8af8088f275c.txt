Chapter 1: Vael: The Ash in the Soul 

The first god I ever tasted was a low, dissonant hum that vibrated up through the soles of my worn-out boots. It was a flavor like a rusty nail under your tongue for too long, a sharp, metallic tang more a memory than a physical sensation. This god, this goddamn infection on the world, was a permanent stain on the air, and my weapon, the Boneblade, pulsed with a quiet, hungry energy against my back. The thing only ever wanted one thing: more. It was a perfect, terrible, and utterly honest companion. 

I wasn’t a god-killer back then. I was a child. I was eight years old. I remember the sound of my little brother's laughter, a bright, happy thing, until it twisted and soured into a discordant shriek, a note on a broken string, before his small body just… dissolved. Turned to a cloud of shimmering green dust that caught the afternoon light in a way that was so beautiful, so tragically perfect, it was horrifying. The stench of that Divine Sickness, a miasma that smelled of overripe fruit and burnt hair, is the first thing I remember. It was my baptism, a raw, gut-level, "I AM" that ripped through the silence of my childhood. A moment, I've come to realize, that was both the end of my life and the true beginning of it. 

A week later, the Order came. They were a line of black ink drawn across the crumpled page of my ruined village, their polished armor an accusation. Father Morr, their leader, a man whose face looked like it was carved from granite and regret, spoke of purity and cleansing. He was a promise of order in a world of screaming chaos. I didn't want prayers. I wanted a cure. I wanted a goddamn weapon. I joined the Order a year later, a furious twelve-year-old with eyes that had seen too much to ever be young again. I was a beautiful monster in the making, and I was thrilled to get started. 

My training was a special kind of hell, designed to burn away the girl and forge a tool. Kora, my mentor, was a grim-faced woman whose hands were calloused from prayer and violence in equal measure. She taught me the "flavors" of the Sickness, the different ways the divine could curdle a soul. The smooth river rock that hummed with a placid quiet? "That's Leesa's poison," she'd grunt. "A Siren's song promising a frictionless peace. It'll absorb you, smooth you out, until there's nothin' left of you but a quiet echo." The jagged obsidian that radiated a sterile, calculating silence? "And that's Blemo's logic-blight. The Surgeon's promise of perfection. It'll carve away every flaw, every feeling, every memory, until you're a perfect, clean, and utterly empty machine." 

Kora looked me dead in the eye, her own gaze a flat, hard thing. "Your job isn’t to understand them, little sinner. Your job is to recognize them. And to hate them. That hate will keep you sharp. It's the only thing that will." I never forgot that. The hate was a beautiful, terrible gift. My first purge was a minor "grief-storm." A farmer’s sorrow had festered, turning his fields to a black, bubbling ichor. We found him wreathed in a visible aura of despair. "He is a symptom," Kora said, her voice devoid of pity. "The sickness is the disease. The man is just the fever." I didn't hesitate. With a grim, cold satisfaction, I buried the Boneblade in his chest. A capacity for the necessary sin." She called me a "great Sinner." I embraced the title, of course. I was a magnificent tool, a glorious instrument of the world's inevitable, and necessary, ruin. For years, I was the "Sinner of First Flame," an efficient, remorseless purger who never questioned her purpose. I was a hammer, and the world was just full of nails I had to pound flat. 

Until Brinefall.

The job was a footnote in a long list of purges. A damp, rotting village on the coast, suffering under some local superstition they called a "Kraken-God." I took their coin, a collection of salt-crusted silver and worn-down copper, and prepared for another day at the office. On my way out of the village, I passed two young Reclaimers, their armor still shiny, their faces still full of a belief I'd long since traded for efficiency. They muttered my title with a wary respect, seeing the legend but not the ash that was starting to collect in the lines around my eyes. 

The landscape itself felt sick. The trees clawed at the earth, their branches twisted into agonized shapes, as if some bored god had leaned in too close and left a stain. I found the lair in a stinking sea cave, the entrance choked with the smell of blood, cheap ceremonial wine, and rotting fish guts. Offerings were piled at the entrance, and among them, a crudely carved wooden soldier, its paint worn away by a child's hands. My jaw clenched. His final mistake was charging me bare-handed, a scream of pure, impotent rage on his lips. I pivoted, a simple, economical movement, and thrust the Boneblade into his chest. I wasn't a savior. I was the Sinner of First Flame, a broken tool who only knew how to take things apart. As I lifted the boy into my arms and carried him out of that stinking, sad excuse for a church, the Boneblade was silent against my back, its hunger sated for now. My hunger, I was beginning to realize, was a whole different kind of animal. And it was just getting started.
