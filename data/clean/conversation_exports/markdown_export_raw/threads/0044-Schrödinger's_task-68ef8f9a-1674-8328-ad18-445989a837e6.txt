**2. Middleware (the translator)**

- **Node.js / Python service** running on-prem or in cloud  
- Connects to SAP via REST or RFC calls  
- Talks to the AI (e.g. GPT or similar LLM) through an API  
This layer handles authentication, message parsing, and prevents the AI from accidentally booking a truckload of corrugate to Mars.  

**3. The AI layer (the chatty operator)**  
This is the natural language front-end. You could feed it your SAP data model so it learns:  
- Material master codes  
- Plant and storage locations  
- Movement types (101, 261, etc.)  
Then the AI can reason through “Hey, scanned material 123456 is GR’d for PO 7789, it should go to bin 42-B.”  

**4. Hardware Integration**  
Your scanners can send data to the middleware through:  
- API hooks or MQTT (IoT-style message broker)  
- Bluetooth or local network triggers when an item’s barcode is scanned  

Voice can be added through something like **Azure Cognitive Services Speech**, **OpenAI Whisper**, or **Google Speech-to-Text**, layered on top of the chat UI — so you can literally say, “Move roll 2305 to slitter 4,” and it executes the SAP transaction behind the scenes.

A lightweight app on a tablet or Zebra scanner could host the chat UI. Think of it as “AI warehouse copilot.” It listens, looks up, confirms, then books the movement.

- Fine-tune or instruct the AI on your warehouse logic

## 4. In‑House vs Vendor Solution

- **Third‑Party Voice Systems:**  Commercial voice-directed warehousing solutions (e.g. Lucas Systems, Honeywell) typically involve purchasing specialized headsets, software licenses (often per user/year), and vendor implementation services.  They boast big productivity gains and a fast ROI (some report **50% error reduction and <1 year ROI**【2†L142-L150】).  But they also carry high upfront and ongoing costs.  For instance, using a cloud voice API at scale can reach **hundreds of thousands of dollars per year**【26†L123-L131】 (e.g. ~$750/employee-year for 1,000 users【26†L123-L131】).  Even proprietary on-prem systems charge license fees (often $500–$1,000+/user-year) and require dedicated hardware.  

- **In‑House with Open Tools:**  By contrast, an internal solution leverages existing tablets and networks, plus open-source AI and speech tools.  There are no per-user license fees.  Example: Meta’s Llama 3 or Llama 2 models are free to use (with an appropriate GPU), and Picovoice’s offline speech engine is free for development【26†L115-L122】.  Thus, you save recurring costs.  Even if an implementation partner were used, comparing ~\$100K–200K in dev costs vs vendor licensing/hardware easily into similar range, in-house recoups value via flexibility and no markup.  

- **Savings Example:**  If a vendor solution required, say, 20 operators with $500/user/yr license (a total $10K/yr) plus custom integration fees ($50K+), over 5 years that’s $60K+ before hardware.  An in-house system might cost \$150K up front (dev+hardware) but minimal annual fees, paying back in 2–3 years.  (Actual vendor quotes vary; the key point is open models/voice cut software fees.)  As one voice-AI provider notes, building on-device (“embedded”) can cut dev time from months to days and avoid hefty cloud bills【26†L115-L122】【26†L123-L131】.

Ongoing costs will be primarily internal support and incremental compute (a few hundred dollars/year), vs vendor solutions that carry fixed license fees.  The improved efficiency (e.g. reduced errors) is in line with industry findings (e.g. 50% fewer picking mistakes, sub-1-year ROI【2†L142-L150】), meaning an internal solution can achieve similar gains without the hefty price of third-party tools.
