**Te’Oga Underface, Heat-Grid 7**

Chalk dust felt like a second skin. Jhace Torrins wiped a stripe of it from his jaw, the white powder mixing with the soot and sweat of the Underface. Jhace worked alone.

He knelt by saddle pin 4-Alpha. His fingers, stained and scarred, brushed the hex-head. He didn't need the gauge. He could feel the tension in the metal, a faint vibration that told him it was seated right, bearing the load as designed. This was his language. This was the work. This was all he had.

He set the torque key, braced his core, and pulled. Not a jerk, but a steady, practiced flow of force. The key clicked, the bolt groaned a fraction, and the riser’s hum sweetened, shifting half a note higher into perfect resonance.

*There*. A line of tension eased in his own shoulders. Metal remembered how it was hurt. You just had to listen to what it needed.

“See? Like that.” Bram’s shadow fell over him. Jhace hadn’t heard him approach.

Chloen was wiping her hands on a rag, smiling. “Jhace makes it look like the bolt *wants* to turn.”

“He’s good with metal,” Bram said, his voice kind, which always made it heavier. “Less good with people.”

Jhace stood, racking the key on his hip belt. He didn't face Bram, keeping his eyes on the next saddle pin, already reading the chalk marks from the last inspection. “Metal’s predictable.”

“People are too, if you listen right,” Bram said.

“Metal doesn’t leave,” Jhace said, the words flat and final.

Bram’s sigh was a familiar sound, like steam easing from a gasket. He knew about Jhace’s parents, about the collapse five castings ago. He’d been there. He knew why Jhace preferred the company of a heat-grid that would either hold or break, but would never just walk away.

Before Bram could find another platitude, Jhace felt it.

A shift.

He froze. The riser’s hum, so sweet a moment ago, soured. It went flat, dropping a full note, like a plucked string suddenly gone slack.

“What did you do?” Chloen asked, looking at her wrench.

“Not me.” Jhace’s eyes scanned the grid. Every part of him went tight. This was *wrong*.

The fine hairs on his forearms stood straight up.

“Jhace?” Bram’s voice lost its warmth.

A taste bloomed on Jhace’s tongue. Sharp and bright, like sucking on a copper shard. He looked at his hand, half-expecting to see it bleeding. Nothing. Just chalk and soot.

“Pressure,” he whispered.

It wasn't in the pipes. It was in the air. In his skull. A deep, seismic *press* that made his ears pop. The shadows in the Underface, usually just an absence of light, suddenly deepened, pooling in the corners with a tangible, oily quality.

“Bram…” Chloen’s voice was thin. She was pointing up, past the grid, toward the sliver of sky visible through the district’s light-well.

The pressure peaked, becoming a physical weight. It felt like the whole world was inhaling, drawing one massive, shuddering breath.

Jhace looked up.

The sliver of sky was gone. Something was descending. Something vast and dark, big enough to blot out the Rising Glow, trailing fire as it fell.

- **Weirdness escalation.**  
  Hum souring, copper taste, pressure in the skull, shadows thickening, then the falling thing. Good rising line from “routine” → “what the fuck.”

  “Something vast and dark, big enough to blot out the Rising Glow, trailing fire as it fell.” It’s a clean cut-to-black moment.

### Things to tighten / sharpen

1. **On-the-nose emotional lines.**  
   - “This was his language. This was the work. This was all he had.”  
   - “Metal doesn’t leave.”  
   These aren’t *bad*, but they’re very “underline the theme twice.” You can often get the same impact with one of those beats, plus implication.

2. **Bram’s internal explanation about Jhace.**  
   > He knew about Jhace’s parents, about the collapse five castings ago…  
   This drifts out of Jhace’s close POV into explanatory narrator and starts smelling like backstory injection. You can hint at this without the mini-infodump: a single image of the collapse or a gut reaction in Jhace would carry more weight.

3. **Repetitive sentence rhythm.**  
   You lean on short, declarative fragments a lot:  
   - “Jhace worked alone.”  
   - “This was his language. This was the work.”  
   - “This was wrong.”  
   Used often, they lose punch. Keep some, but let others merge into a more flowing line.

4. **The “shift” moment could lean even harder into Jhace’s attunement.**  
   Right now, the riser hum goes off, and then it’s almost immediately big cosmic pressure. You *do* have a nice build (taste, pressure, shadows), but you could sharpen the connection between the grid resonance (his comfort zone) and the incoming phenomenon. Make it feel like the world is “detuning” around him.

5. **Last line is strong but a bit generic in diction.**

The horizon was gone.

Bram's Forge, Underface

The world had narrowed to the cot, the low ceiling of Bram's forge, and two kinds of pain.

The first was the burn. His right hand was a club of bandages, heavy with cooling grease that Bram dabbed on every few rings. The fire was banked, not gone, pulsing in dull, distant waves.

The second pain was the itch.

It wasn’t on his skin. It was in his right forearm, a crawling, electric itch beneath the new copper-gold line that had seared itself along his veins.

The vein-burn.

He didn’t understand it. He didn’t understand how Chloen was alive—walking, breathing, moving through the forge with a fresh helmet and a haunted look in her eyes. He didn’t understand the light, the heat, or the sudden, absolute emptiness that had followed.

"You can't just keep staring at the ceiling, lad," Bram’s voice rumbled from across the forge. "You did a good thing. A strange thing, aye, but a good one."

Jhace swallowed. His throat felt scraped raw. "I don't know what I did."

"You saved her. That's what you did." Bram spooned stew from a pot simmering near the forge-mouth. The smell of it turned Jhace's stomach. "Now eat. You're no good to anyone hollowed out."

Before Jhace could refuse, the forge door slid open, cutting a bright, cold rectangle of Cooltide light into the dim, warm space.

A figure stood silhouetted in it.

"I'm looking for the man who was at the Impact Verge," a woman's voice said. Clean. Precise. "The one with the burned hand."

Jhace tried to sit up too fast. The room tilted, and the itch in his forearm spiked.

It was her. The woman from the fallen glass-house. Cleaned up, mostly. Her auburn hair was tied back. Practical work trousers instead of ruined observatory gear. She moved with a stiff, careful grace—the leg he’d seen pinned still not right.

Bram set his spoon down, his big frame tightening. "Who's asking?"

"My name is Tiffani Merrow." She stepped inside, eyes sweeping the forge—not with curiosity, but with assessment. She ticked off tools, layout, structural beams, as if cataloguing them. Then her gaze locked onto Jhace. "I traveled fifty kilometers to find you."

"Why?" Jhace croaked.

"Because I think I know what you did," she said. "And I’m certain you have no idea how you did it."

Jhace bristled. Lying on Bram’s cot, wrapped in bandages, he felt exposed, reduced. Her cool stare made it worse, turning his confusion into something that felt like failure.

"I don't need any glass-guild theories, Mistress," he said.

Tiffani ignored the title and the edge. She crossed to a workbench piled with scrap from the collapse—a tangle of warped flanges and sheared bolts. She picked up a heavy, twisted flange, its ninety-degree angle mangled into something unrecognizable.

"What you did for your apprentice… that was instinct," she said, voice quiet, almost detached. "You flooded her with heat. With connection."

"I…" Jhace had nothing that made sense even to himself.

"You forced it," she said, gaze flicking to his bandaged hand, then to the vein-burn on his arm. "And it cost you. Keep doing it that way, it will kill you."

"And you know a better way?" Bram asked, folding his arms across his chest.

Tiffani didn’t answer him.

She held the warped flange in both hands. "You see a break," she said to Jhace. "I see a structural failure. A loss of geometric integrity."

She closed her eyes.

Jhace felt it before he saw it: a shift in the air, nothing like the furnace-surge that had torn through him at the Impact Verge. This was cooler. Sharper. As if the space between them had snapped onto an invisible grid.

A faint blue-silver light flickered between her hands.

It wasn’t a flood. It was a line—thin, exact.

Her hands moved over the metal, not quite touching. The light traced the flange, mapping its original, intended shape. Jhace heard a thin, clear ping, like a tuning fork hit just once. The metal didn’t so much heal as remember. With a groan, it straightened, warped edges flowing back into a clean, precise right angle.

Tiffani set the restored flange on the bench. Her hands were steady, but a tremor rippled down her arm when she let go. She exhaled a long, controlled breath and blinked once, slow, like someone surfacing.

"You force," she said, turning back to him. "I guide. You mend. I reinforce."

She stepped closer. Her measuring gaze softened, just a fraction.

"You nearly burned yourself to ash doing something I don’t understand," she said. She nodded toward the flange. "And I just did something you don’t understand."

She held up her hand—not offering it, simply showing it. The same precise fingers, now with the faintest shake. "You have the power, Jhace Torrins. But you have no structure. No control."

The anger drained out of him, leaving only exhaustion and the restless itch in his arm.

She's not trying to control me, he realized. She’s trying to keep me from burning out.

His way was a wildfire. Hers was a blueprint.

"You forced it," she said again, softer now. "I can teach you to guide it."

Jhace looked at his bandaged, ruined hand. At the perfect flange on the bench. At Bram, who was watching them both with a look of stunned, slow-dawning respect.

"Alright," Jhace said. The word scraped his throat on the way out. "Teach me."

Bram let out a heavy breath, something like a laugh buried in it, and broke into a broad, relieved smile. "About time you accepted some help, lad."

**Junction 7, Heat-Grid**

Junction 7 was a mess. A primary heat-riser, as thick as Bram’s torso, had a spiderweb of fractures near its main seating. The pressure gauge was deep in the red, the riser humming a strained, discordant note that set Jhace’s teeth on edge.

“This is the one,” Tiffani said, her voice crisp in the hot, dry air of the Heattide. “If this seating fails, the whole sub-grid cascades.”

“I see it.” Jhace’s right hand, still bandaged but usable, gripped his torque key. His left, the "good" one, flexed, the vein-burn on his forearm itching. He’d spent the last two tides in the forge with her, practicing. *Guiding*, not *forcing*. He thought he had it.

“Your approach first,” he said, trying to sound more confident than he felt. “Structure.”

Tiffani nodded, all business. “The geometry is compromised. We need to reinforce the molecular lattice before you try to seal it. Watch my trace.”

She placed her hands on the riser, a few spans from the damage. The blue-silver light bloomed, cool and precise. “Feel the grid? The lines? You have to map the integrity first, then…”

Jhace tried. He put his good hand on the metal, closed his eyes, and *reached*. He didn't feel a grid. He felt a *wound*. He felt the chaos, the pain of the metal, the pressure trying to tear it apart. Her systematic approach felt cold, wrong, like trying to fix a broken bone by describing it.

“I don’t…” he began.
“Don’t talk. *Feel* the lines,” she insisted.

He pushed past his instinct, trying to force his will into her pattern. He tried to "see" the geometry. The blue-silver light he was trying to copy sputtered and turned a sickly, greenish color. The riser’s hum spiked into a shriek.

*Feedback.*

“Stop!” Tiffani yelled, yanking her hands back as if burned. “You’re fighting it! You’re just pushing heat into the lines. You’re making it *worse*.”

Jhace stumbled back, his arm throbbing. “I can’t feel lines, Tiffani! It’s broken! It needs to be *mended*.”

“You can’t mend something that’s lost its shape!” she shot back, her face flushed with frustration. “It’s not *alive*, it’s an *object*.” She shoved him aside, her movements sharp. “Fine. Your way. *Feel* it.”

She put her hands on the cracks, mimicking his stance from the forge. She closed her eyes.
Jhace watched her. Nothing happened.
No warmth. No copper-gold glow.
Her brow furrowed in concentration. She was *thinking* her way through the problem, trying to analyze the sensation, to quantify the "wound."

“I don't… I don't feel anything,” she said, her voice tight. “It’s just hot metal. How do you know where to push?”

“You don’t push, you *listen*,” he said, frustrated.
“Listen to *what*? The pressure? The strain? I need data, not... not *feelings*!”

The riser groaned, a deep, tearing sound. A new fracture, thin as a hair, split the ceramic casing. They were competing. And they were losing.

They both stared at the new crack.
“This isn’t working,” Jhace said.

“No,” she agreed. She looked at him, her analytical gaze scanning him, then the riser, as if finally seeing the two parts of the equation.
“You’re right,” she said, shocking him. “It’s broken. And I’m right. It’s lost its shape.”

He looked at the riser. *Heat. Structure. Warmth. Precision.*
“Wait,” Jhace said, a new idea clicking into place. “I’ll seal the fractures. You reinforce the geometry.”
Tiffani’s eyes widened slightly. “Parallel work. Not sequential.”

It was the most dangerous thing they could attempt. If their different resonances crossed, they could shatter the riser—and themselves.
“Can you do it?” he asked.
“Can you keep your warmth out of my lines?” she retorted.
He nodded.

They took their places.
“On my mark,” she said. “Three. Two. One.”

They touched the riser.
Jhace sank into his instinct, letting the copper-gold warmth flow from his core. He didn't push. He *offered*. The warmth found the micro-fractures, flowing like molten metal into a mold, sealing the tiny, screaming breaks. He felt the riser’s pain ease, its hum steadying.

At the exact same time, Tiffani’s blue-silver light flared, but this time it wasn't a map. It was a *scaffold*. It wrapped around the riser, a cage of hard, geometric light, locking the structure in place. Her hands moved in sharp, precise angles, his in flowing, circular patterns.

She was the blueprint; he was the forge.
She built the container; he poured the metal.
They didn’t speak. They didn't need to. He felt her shift her footing and leaned to counterbalance her. She felt his surge of power to seal the main crack and locked the geometry a breath before it could buckle.

The riser’s discordant shriek resolved, finding its true note. The hum was steady. Deep.
The pressure gauge fell from red, to amber, to green.
It was done.

They pulled their hands back at the same instant, both of them breathing hard.
Jhace looked at his right forearm. The vein-burn was angrier, a little longer. Maybe four-tenths of a centimeter now. A mild tremor buzzed in his fingers.
Tiffani was staring at her own hands, opening and closing her fists. She flexed her fingers, a small, confused frown on her face.
“What is it?” he asked.
“My hands,” she said, still flexing. “They feel… stiff.” She shook them out, as if trying to rid them of a cramp.

“That was…” Jhace started, still reeling from the feeling of their work slotting together.
Tiffani looked up from her hands, meeting his gaze. A slow, genuine smile touched her lips, the first he’d ever seen on her.
“...effective,” she finished.
He couldn't help it. He smiled back.

Two hundred spans away, standing on an overlooking catwalk, Bram lowered a long-lens. Chloen stood beside him, her face a mask of awe.
“Gods below,” Bram murmured, his voice heavy. “Those two are going to change everything.” He ran a hand over his jaw. “Or destroy themselves trying.”

She set her hands on the riser, a few spans from the damage. The blue-silver light bloomed, cool and precise.

“Feel the grid?” she asked. “The lines? You have to map the integrity first, then—”

Jhace tried. He laid his good hand on the metal, closed his eyes, and *reached*.

He didn’t feel a grid.

He felt a *wound*. A tearing, a raw edge. He felt the pressure trying to rip the riser apart, the metal screaming under the strain. Her neat, geometric overlay felt wrong—cold, distant, like trying to fix a broken bone by describing it.

“I don’t…” he began.

“Don’t talk. *Feel* the lines,” she insisted.

He pushed past his instinct, tried to jam his will into her pattern, to “see” the geometry the way she did.

The blue-silver light he was trying to copy sputtered and went a sickly green. The riser’s hum spiked into a shriek.

*Feedback.*

“Stop!” Tiffani snapped, yanking her hands back as if burned. “You’re fighting it! You’re just pushing heat into the lines. You’re making it *worse*.”

Jhace stumbled back, his arm throbbing. “I can’t feel lines, Tiffani! It’s broken. It needs to be *mended*.”

“You can’t mend something that’s lost its shape!” she shot back, face flushed. “It’s not *alive*, it’s an *object*.”

She shouldered past him, movements sharp. “Fine. Your way. *Feel* it.”

She planted her hands on the cracked section, mimicking his stance from the forge, eyes closing.

Jhace watched.

Nothing happened.

No warmth. No copper-gold glow. Just the riser’s ragged hum and the faint tick of the pressure gauge.

Her brow furrowed. She was trying to think her way through it, to quantify the “wound,” to turn his instinct into numbers.

“I don't… I don't feel anything,” she said through gritted teeth. “It’s just hot metal. How do you know where to push?”

“You don’t push, you *listen*,” he snapped.

“Listen to *what*? The pressure? The strain? I need data, not… not *feelings*.”

The riser groaned, a deep, tearing sound. A new fracture, thin as a hair, split the ceramic casing.

They stared at it.

“This isn’t working,” Jhace said.

“No,” she agreed. Her gaze flicked between him and the riser, as if finally admitting they were parts of the same problem.

“You’re right,” she said at last, surprising him. “It’s broken.” She nodded at the fractures. “And I’m right. It’s lost its shape.”

He looked at the riser.

*Heat. Structure. Warmth. Precision.*

“Wait,” Jhace said, something clicking into place. “I’ll seal the fractures. You reinforce the geometry.”

Tiffani’s eyes widened a fraction. “Parallel work. Not sequential.”

The idea hung between them, bright and dangerous. If their resonances crossed the wrong way, they wouldn’t just crack the riser—they could blow it apart and take themselves with it.

“Can you do it?” he asked.

“Can you keep your warmth out of my lines?” she countered.

He nodded.

They took their positions on opposite sides of the riser.

“On my mark,” she said quietly. “Three. Two. One.”

They touched the metal.

Jhace sank into his instinct, letting the copper-gold warmth flow from his core. He didn’t force it; he *offered* it. The heat slipped into the micro-fractures, flowing like molten metal into a mold, sealing the tiny, screaming breaks. He felt the riser’s pain ease, its hum deepening.

At the same time, Tiffani’s blue-silver light flared—but this time it wasn’t a flat map. It was a *scaffold*. It wrapped the riser in a cage of hard, geometric light, locking the structure in place. Her hands moved in sharp, precise angles; his traced slow, circular paths.

She was the blueprint. He was the forge.  
She built the container. He poured the metal.

They didn’t speak. They didn’t need to. He felt her shift her stance and leaned without thinking to counterbalance her. She felt his surge of power as he sealed the main crack and snapped the geometry tight a heartbeat before the riser could buckle.

The riser’s discordant shriek slid down through a rough chord and settled into a clean, steady hum. The pressure gauge crept from red to amber, then dropped into green.

It was done.

They pulled their hands away in the same instant, both of them breathing hard.

Jhace glanced at his forearm. The vein-burn glowed a little brighter, a little longer now—about a fingernail more than before. A faint tremor buzzed through his fingers.

Tiffani stared at her own hands, opening and closing them. She flexed her fingers, a small, puzzled frown creasing her brow.

“What is it?” he asked.

“My hands,” she said, still flexing. “They feel… stiff.” She shook them out, as if trying to fling off a cramp.

“That was…” Jhace started, still reeling from the way their work had slotted together.

Tiffani looked up, meeting his gaze. A slow, genuine smile touched her mouth—the first he’d seen on her.

“…effective,” she finished.

He couldn’t help it. He smiled back.

***

Two hundred spans away, on an overlooking catwalk, Bram lowered a long-lens. Chloen stood beside him, her face a mask of awe.

“Gods below,” Bram murmured, voice heavy. “Those two are going to change everything.”

He rubbed a hand over his jaw, eyes never leaving the riser.

“Or destroy themselves trying.”
