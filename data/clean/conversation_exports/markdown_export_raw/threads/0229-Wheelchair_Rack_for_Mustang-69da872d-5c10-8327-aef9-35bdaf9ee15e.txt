[ASSISTANT]
You’re basically building a hitch-mounted carrier—but customized for a non-folding wheelchair. Good news: this is very doable. Bad news: if you half-ass it, it’ll either drag, shake itself apart, or block half the car.

Here’s the straight, practical way to approach it:

---

## 1. First constraint: the car (this matters more than the chair)

Most Mustangs aren’t meant to carry stuff like this, but you *can* make it work if you stay realistic.

- You’ll need a **Class I or II hitch (1.25" receiver)**  
- Typical tongue weight limit: **100–200 lbs max**
- If the chair is heavy (power chair = problem), this may not work safely

👉 If it’s a **manual wheelchair**, you’re fine.  
👉 If it’s **motorized**, stop here unless you reinforce or switch vehicles.

---

## 2. The simplest design (don’t reinvent the wheel)

Build a **platform carrier**, not a hanging rack.

Think:
> mini flatbed that sticks out from the hitch

### Core structure:
- **2" steel square tube** (main spine into hitch adapter)
- **Expanded steel mesh or aluminum plate** for the platform
- **Side rails (low, 2–4")** to keep the chair from sliding

### Size it like this:
- Width: just wider than chair (usually ~26–30")
- Depth: 30–40" depending on wheelbase
- Keep it tight—don’t make a picnic table

---

## 3. Critical detail: ground clearance

Mustangs sit low. If you ignore this, you’ll scrape everything.

Fix it:
- Add a **rise in the hitch adapter (4–6")**
- Or weld the platform slightly **above** the receiver centerline

---

## 4. Loading (this is where most builds suck)

You need a **ramp**. No ramp = useless.

Options:
- Folding aluminum ramp (buy one)
- Or build:
  - 2 lightweight channels
  - hinge them to the platform

Keep angle manageable or you’ll fight it every time.

---

## 5. Securing the chair

Do NOT rely on “it sits in there pretty snug.”

Use:
- 4 tie-down points (D-rings welded to frame)
- Ratchet straps (2 front, 2 rear)
- Optional: wheel chocks

If it moves at highway speed, it’s going airborne or eating your trunk.

---

## 6. Stabilization (the wobble problem)

Hitch carriers love to rattle like hell.

Fix:
- Anti-wobble hitch clamp
- Or drill and bolt through receiver (old-school but solid)

---

## 7. Lights & legality (don’t skip this)

If the chair blocks:
- tail lights
- brake lights
- license plate

You need:
- Trailer light kit wired in
- Plate relocation bracket

Otherwise you’re asking to get pulled over constantly.

---

## 8. Weight reality check

Before you build:

- Weigh the chair
- Estimate rack weight (usually 30–60 lbs)

**Total must stay under hitch tongue rating.**

---

## 9. If you want a cleaner version (upgrade idea)

Instead of a fixed tray:

- Make it **tilt-down or fold-up**
- Add **gas struts** if you’re feeling fancy
- Or build a **receiver swing-out arm** (more complex but way nicer access)

---

## 10. Alternative (honest answer)

If the chair is even remotely heavy or used daily:

- A small hatchback or SUV solves this instantly  
- Or a **hitch-mounted powered lift** (expensive but zero effort)

---

- 2" steel spine into receiver
- 30"x30–40" platform
- Raised height for clearance
- Fold or attach ramp
- 4-point strap system
- Anti-wobble clamp
- Add lights if blocked

[ASSISTANT]
You *can* build something without a hitch—but now you’re not making an “accessory,” you’re building a **structural extension of the car**. That’s where people get into trouble fast.

A Mustang’s rear isn’t designed to carry load off the bumper skin or thin sheet metal. If you just weld rails to whatever looks solid, it’ll tear metal, twist the trunk floor, or worse—drop the chair in traffic.

So here’s the only way to do this that isn’t sketchy:

---

## 1. Where you actually anchor (non-negotiable)

You need to tie into **frame-level structure**, not body panels.

On a Mustang, that means:
- Rear **frame rails** (run under the trunk)
- Or **rear crash bar / bumper reinforcement**

👉 Do NOT attach to:
- Trunk floor sheet metal  
- Spare tire well  
- Rear bumper cover (obviously useless)

---

## 2. Your bed rails = your main arms

Use those bed rails as your **outriggers**:

- Run **two rails straight back** from under the car
- They should sit parallel, about the width of the wheelchair
- Length: enough to clear bumper + give platform space (~36–48” total)

Mounting them:
- Bolt (preferred) or weld to **plates** that are bolted to frame rails  
- Use **thick plate (at least 1/4")** as your interface

If you weld directly to the car, you better know what you’re doing—modern cars aren’t friendly to random welding.

---

## 3. Build a subframe (don’t just hang a tray)

Between the rails:

- Weld crossmembers (front + rear + middle)
- Add diagonal bracing (this stops flex and bouncing)

Think:
> ladder frame hanging off the back

Then mount your platform on top of that.

---

## 4. Platform

Same idea as before, just mounted to your rails:

- Expanded steel or slotted angle
- Keep it tight to chair size
- Add small lips/rails on edges

---

## 5. Support struts (this is what saves your build)

Without a hitch, you need **triangulation**.

Add:
- Two angled supports from the outer ends of the rack  
- Running forward/up into the chassis or bumper reinforcement

This turns it from:
> “lever trying to rip your car open”

into:
> “load distributed into structure”

Skip this and it *will* flex like crazy.

---

## 6. Loading ramp

Same deal:
- Hinged ramp off the back  
- Or removable ramps

Keep it light or you’ll hate using it.

---

## 7. Securing the chair

- Weld in 4 tie-down points
- Use ratchet straps
- Add wheel stops if possible

---

## 8. Big problems you need to think about

### ⚠️ Suspension sag
Mustangs are soft in the rear.

Fix:
- Stiffer springs OR
- Air bags inside coils (cheap and effective)

---

### ⚠️ Leverage (this is the killer)
The farther back the load is, the heavier it *acts*.

Example:
- 80 lb chair can feel like 150+ lbs on the structure

Keep it:
- As close to the bumper as possible  
- As high as practical

---

### ⚠️ Exhaust heat
Wheelchair tires + exhaust = bad combo

- Reroute tips or add heat shielding

---

### ⚠️ Legal visibility
Same as before:
- Lights visible
- Plate visible

---

## 9. Reality check (no sugar coating)

This setup:
- Takes real fabrication skill  
- Can permanently mess up the car if done wrong  
- Will never be as clean or safe as a hitch-mounted carrier  

But…
If you build it like a **bolted-on subframe with triangulation**, it’ll work.

If you build it like:
> “lemme just weld these rails to the back real quick”

…it’ll fail. Probably at the worst time.

---

## 10. Smarter hybrid option (strongly recommended)

Since you weld:

**Build your own hitch instead.**

- Buy a cheap receiver tube (~$20–40)
- Weld it to plates that bolt to frame rails
- Now you’ve got a legit mounting point

Then build your carrier like normal.

👉 This gives you:
- Strength  
- Removability  
- Way less stress on the car

[ASSISTANT]
“Hanging it” sounds simpler, but it’s actually *more dangerous* than a platform if you don’t anchor it right. You’re turning the chair into a swinging load behind a low car—so control and structure matter a lot more.

Here’s how to do it without building a death pendulum.

---

## The only viable “hang” design

You don’t hang it from the bumper. You hang it from a **rigid upright frame** that’s tied into the car’s structure.

Think:
> mini crane / goalpost mounted to the rear of the car

---

## 1. Build the upright frame (the backbone)

Use your bed rails + tubing:

- Two vertical posts (left/right of trunk)
- One top crossbar connecting them

Shape = upside-down “U”

Mounting:
- Bottom of posts bolt to **frame rails or bumper reinforcement**
- Use **steel plates (1/4")** as mounting feet
- Spread the load—don’t use tiny contact points

Height:
- Just high enough so the chair clears the ground by a few inches

---

## 2. How the chair actually hangs

You’ve got two solid options:

### Option A — Strap suspension (simpler)
- 2 heavy-duty straps from top bar to chair frame
- Lift chair so wheels are just off the ground

Add:
- A **bottom stabilizer bar** or strap pulling inward to stop swing

---

### Option B — Hook + cradle (better)
- Weld hooks or a clamp system that grabs the wheelchair frame
- Add a **lower support bar** the rear wheels rest against

This turns it from:
> “dangling object”

into:
> “held in place with tension”

Much safer at speed.

---

## 3. Stop the swing (this is the whole game)

If it swings, it’ll:
- slam into your car  
- shift weight unpredictably  
- rip mounts over time  

You need **triangulation**:

- Two straps or bars from bottom of chair → back toward the car  
- Or a rigid lower frame the wheels press against

Goal:
> zero side-to-side movement

---

## 4. Reinforce the base (critical)

All that weight is pulling *backward and downward*.

Add:
- Angled supports from the vertical posts → forward into frame rails

Without this:
- the whole structure flexes backward like a crowbar

---

## 5. Loading method

You’ve got 3 realistic approaches:

### 1. Deadlift it (only if light)
Not ideal unless it’s super light

### 2. Ramp + winch (best DIY)
- Roll it up a short ramp
- Use a cheap hand winch mounted to the top bar
- Crank it up into position

### 3. Pivot arm (advanced but slick)
- Build a swing-down arm
- Load at ground level
- Swing up and lock

---

## 6. Weight reality

Hanging makes weight *feel heavier* to the car because:
- It’s higher  
- It’s farther back  

So:
- Keep it tight to the trunk
- Keep it as low as possible while still clearing ground

---

## 7. Big risks (no fluff)

- **Metal fatigue** at mount points  
- **Rear-end sag** (you will notice it)  
- **Visibility issues** (chair blocks everything)  
- **Rear impact = destroyed chair + structure**

---

## 8. Honest verdict

Hanging works **if**:
- It’s a lightweight manual chair  
- You overbuild the frame  
- You eliminate swing completely  

But compared to a platform:
- Harder to build right  
- Less stable  
- More stress on the car  

---

## 9. The smarter version of “hanging”

Do this instead:

- Build a **short platform**
- Add a **vertical hoop**
- Use straps from the hoop to “hang” the chair *onto the platform*

Now you get:
- weight supported from below  
- stability from above  

Best of both worlds.
