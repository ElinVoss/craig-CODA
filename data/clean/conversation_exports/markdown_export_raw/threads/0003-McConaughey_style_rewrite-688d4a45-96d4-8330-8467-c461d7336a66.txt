He shrieked, a thin, reedy sound, and brandished his obsidian knife. "You cannot stop the Tidemother's baptism! He will be made whole!"

Vael saw the whole sad story in his wild, terrified eyes. A fisherman whose grief for a lost family at sea had curdled into this monstrous, pathetic shape. "There is no Tidemother," Vael said, her voice flat and hard. "There's just you."

His final mistake was charging her bare-handed, a scream of pure, impotent rage on his lips. She pivoted, a simple, economical movement, and thrust the Boneblade into his chest. Vael pulled her blade free, feeling the hollow echo where his power had been. It left a new kind of emptiness inside her, a cold ash in the wound of her own spirit. She turned to the altar. The boy was alive, a thin trickle of blood running from a shallow cut on his arm. Vael knelt, her knees cracking in the sudden silence. She tore a strip from her tunic and gently, almost tenderly, bandaged the boy's thin arm. He looked from the dead man on the floor to her, his eyes wide, but he didn't scream.

Vael said nothing. She wasn't a savior. She was the Sinner of First Flame, a broken tool who only knew how to take things apart. As she lifted the boy into her arms and carried him out of that stinking, sad excuse for a church, the Boneblade was silent against her back, its hunger sated for now.

Vael's hunger, she was beginning to realize, was a whole different kind of animal. And it was just getting started.

Chapter 3: The Architect's Grieving Will
