# The Grip That Holds

## Chapter One: Something Wrong

Junction Seven. The plaque held twenty-three names. Jhace read each one in order—Harren Corl, Bessa Tyne, Mikael Stradd—jaw tight. No ceremony. Just the reading.
    
He'd filed the report four Anneals before the collapse. Eastern support column, stress fractures documented. The report went to Master Hellik. Sat in Crown archives. Cleared him officially when the investigation closed.
    
Twenty-three names stayed on the plaque anyway.
    
The weight sits in your chest and does not leave.

He pulled his inspector kit from his pack. Slate, chalk, cord, collar keys, wedge slips, lamp oil—each item confirmed by touch, fingers moving through the sequence without looking. Everything present.

He logged the time on his slate: Tide 0, Kindling. Wrote below it: start of route.

The grid-hum ran through everything in Saint Merro—pipes, walls, floors. Constant low vibration. Background noise you learned not to hear. Jhace pressed his palm to the main pipe at Junction Seven. The hum arrived through his skin. Normal baseline present. But something else rode with it. A second pitch.

He shifted his hand. Different contact point, different pressure. The second pitch stayed.

He leaned in. Put his ear to the metal. Cheek cooled fast against the pipe. The second pitch carried a narrow edge. He felt it in his teeth.

Dust fell from a ceiling seam. Thin strip, maybe a finger-width across. Jhace watched it fall through his lamp light. Counted his breaths. Three. Four. Five. The dust fell again. Same pattern.

He wrote on his slate: repeat interval.

The collar seam showed no active steam. Sealant bead intact. No obvious leak. He moved to Junction Nine. Pressed his palm to the pipe there. Same second pitch. Same narrow edge.

He tested a side spur valve. Baseline hum changed when he closed it. The second pitch didn't. It kept running underneath.

He wrote: off-local.

Copper taste arrived on his tongue. He checked his gums with his finger. No blood. The taste stayed anyway. He logged it: metal taste without injury.

Twenty-eight Turns of this work had taught him the grid's voice. When it changed pitch, something fundamental had shifted. Not maintenance. Not scheduled work. Something outside normal parameters.

A guild chalk board hung near Junction Twelve. Someone had written: keep flow, meet schedule. Jhace looked at it. Kept walking.

Two guild workers argued over a collar shift at Junction Twelve. One stepped into Jhace's path, wrench held across his chest.

Jhace stopped two strides back. Set his kit down. Kept his hands visible.

He read the collar's torque bias by how the seam pulled. The shift trended Downslope. Dangerous territory if it failed.

"Last tightening sequence?" he asked.

The worker with the wrench shrugged. "By feel."

"No torque marks."

"Didn't need them."

Jhace's shoulders stayed level. His voice stayed flat. "Isolate the collar."

"Not doing that. We're behind schedule."

"Junction Seven's dead. Twenty-three names on that plaque. This collar fails, you add more."

Neither worker moved. One looked at the other. Tools hung quiet in their hands.

Then one reached for the valve.

Jhace used the phrase that held weight in the tunnels. "This is the grid."

The valve turned. Baseline hum dipped. The second pitch stayed.

Jhace wrote: persistence confirmed.

The tunnel shuddered. Brief. Micro-displacement. Dust fell from three seams at once.

Both workers looked up. Jhace stayed focused on the collar. The torque bias had shifted two degrees Downslope in the last ten heartbeats. Active degradation.

"Mark the seam," he said. "File the report. Don't touch it until engineering clears it."

The worker with the wrench lowered it. "You're paranoid."

"I'm accountable. Different thing."

Jhace packed his kit. The second pitch ran through the pipe under his hand. He felt it in his wrist bones now. Spreading.

He walked the Underface route for another two hours. Logged fourteen anomalies. Dust patterns. Harmonic shifts. Micro-displacement events. The grid wasn't failing. Not yet. But something had changed in its geometry.

At Junction Nineteen, he found Chloen sitting on a supply crate. Her hands shook. She held a rivet gun but wasn't using it.

"You alright?" he asked.

She looked at him. Copper on her breath when she spoke. "You feel it?"

"The hum."

"The other thing. Under it."

Jhace nodded. "Logged it. Fourteen junctions."

"It's getting louder."

He checked her hands. The tremor wasn't fatigue. Wrong rhythm for that. "When did it start?"

"Two hours ago. Came on fast."

He wrote on his slate: worker symptom—tremor, copper taste. Added: evaluate causation.

"Go Crownward," he said. "Get checked."

"Can't. Behind schedule."

"Chloen. Go."

She stood. Swayed. Caught herself on the crate. Jhace moved to steady her but she waved him off.

She walked toward the Crown elevator, each step careful. Measured.

Jhace watched until she rounded the corner. Then he pressed his palm to Junction Nineteen's main pipe.

The second pitch hit harder here. Almost loud enough to hear without contact.

He logged the intensity. Wrote: escalation gradient—Downslope to Crown.

His hand started shaking. He pulled it back. Counted to ten. The tremor stopped.

He put his hand back on the pipe. The tremor returned. Five heartbeats. Ten.

He pulled away. The tremor faded over the next twenty heartbeats.

He wrote: contact-dependent physical response.

The grid-hum changed pitch. Sudden drop. Half-tone lower.

Jhace's stomach tightened. That shouldn't happen. The grid ran on constant pressure. Constant resonance. No variance.

He checked three pressure gauges. All reading normal. But the pitch had dropped. He could hear it now without touching anything.

Dust fell from the ceiling in sheets.

Jhace looked up. The seam had opened two millimeters. He could see movement in the stone beyond it. Micro-fractures spreading.

He logged: structural displacement, active propagation.

The tunnel shuddered again. Longer this time. Three full heartbeats. The pressure gauges spiked. Dropped. Stabilized.

Jhace moved toward the Crown elevator. Didn't run. Running caused panic. But he moved with purpose.

The grid-hum rose again. Overtook the dropped pitch. The two frequencies fought each other in the pipe resonance. Jhace felt it in his spine.

He reached the elevator. Pulled the call chain. Waited.

The second pitch grew teeth.

Somewhere Crownward, something cracked. Big sound. Stone-on-stone. Echoed down through the tunnel network.

The elevator arrived. Jhace stepped in. Pulled the gate closed. Started the ascent.

The hum followed him up.

## Chapter Two: Glass That Listens

The theodolite base sat uneven. Tiffani scraped grit from under the rear foot until the bubble centered. She checked the other feet. All seated properly now.

She pulled three lens plates from their case. Inspected each edge under angled light. No micro-chips visible. She swapped the installed plate anyway. Eliminated the variable.

Her logbook opened to a fresh page. She wrote the header in order: Tide 0, Pouring. Target: Seven-Three. Instrument: theodolite, calibrated.

Twenty-six Turns of lens work had made the motion automatic.

The observatory platform perched three terraces above Crown-face. Wind came steady from Downslope. Tiffani set her jacket hem under the documentation case. Kept it from sliding.

She aligned the theodolite on Seven-Three. The offset marker sat visible in her scope—bronze plate mounted at the terrace junction. She logged the angle. Checked it twice. Both readings matched.

The copper taste arrived while she was writing. She stopped. Ran her tongue over her teeth. Metallic. Sharp. No blood when she checked.

She logged it: unexplained copper taste, Tide 0 Pouring.

The grid-hum changed pitch. Tiffani looked up from her logbook. The hum ran through the platform supports. Constant background. She'd stopped hearing it three Turns ago. But now it pushed different. Lower frequency. She felt it in her sternum.

She pressed her palm to the railing. The vibration came through wrong. Two frequencies. One under the other.

She pulled her hand back and logged: harmonic anomaly, platform supports.

The sky looked normal. Clear. Typical pressure gradient for Pouring phase. She checked her instruments. Barometer steady. Thermometer within range. Wind speed normal.

But the copper taste wouldn't leave.

She walked the platform perimeter. Checked each support mount. All showed proper seating. No visible stress fractures. She'd filed the safety inspection two Tides ago. Everything had been sound.

The hum doubled again. This time she heard it without touching anything. Two frequencies running parallel. Not quite in phase. The dissonance sat in her inner ear. Made her jaw tight.

She wrote: dual-frequency grid resonance. Added: source unknown.

Something pulled at her attention. Not thought. Just pressure. She looked Downslope. Underface district. Where the grid originated. Where any harmonic shift should be strongest.

She didn't know why she looked there. Just did.

A scholar from the Architecture sect arrived on the platform. Tiffani didn't know his name. He carried survey equipment. Set it up without speaking.

She returned to her theodolite. Logged three more Seven-Three measurements. The offset had shifted. Not much. Two arc-seconds. But it had moved.

She rechecked her calibration. The theodolite read true. The offset had actually shifted.

She logged: Seven-Three displacement—2 arc-seconds Downslope.

The scholar looked up from his equipment. "You feel that?"

Tiffani kept writing. "The harmonic?"

"The pressure."

She stopped. Checked her barometer again. It had dropped. Three millibars in five minutes. That shouldn't happen. Not during Pouring phase. Not without storm buildup.

She logged the pressure drop. Added: rapid onset, no atmospheric precursor.

The copper taste got stronger. Dust rose from the terrace gaps below. Thin streams. Visible in her scope when she checked Seven-Three again. The offset marker looked hazy through the particulate.

She logged: airborne copper-gold particulate, source unknown.

"Something's wrong," the scholar said.

Tiffani checked her scope. The dust streams had multiplied. Dozens of them now. Rising from terrace seams across the visible grid. The particulate caught light. Copper-gold. Actual metallic sheen.

She'd never seen that before.

The grid-hum spiked. Both frequencies. Her stomach dropped. The platform vibrated hard enough to rattle her documentation case.

She grabbed the theodolite. Held it steady. The scholar braced against his own equipment.

The vibration stopped. The hum stayed doubled. Louder now.

Tiffani logged: major displacement event, duration three heartbeats.

She looked Downslope. The dust streams were visible across half the city. Copper-gold threads rising from every terrace junction she could see. Hundreds of them.

"I need to document this," she said.

The scholar had already left. Running for the stairs.

Tiffani pulled her distance scope from the equipment case. Focused on the densest dust column. It rose from somewhere in the Underface. She couldn't see the source but she could track the column. It bent. Wrong angle. Against the wind.

She logged: particulate movement inconsistent with atmospheric flow. Added: external force suspected.

The copper taste turned sharp. Bit into her sinuses. Made her eyes water. She blinked. Kept the scope steady.

The dust column was spreading. Horizontal propagation. It touched another column. They merged. Both turned brighter.

She zoomed tighter. The merged column showed internal structure. Spiral. Rotation visible in the particulate density.

The platform shuddered. Tiffani grabbed the railing. Her logbook slid toward the edge. She caught it with her foot. Pulled it back.

The grid-hum screamed. The doubled frequencies collapsed into each other. Made a sound that had edges. Physical presence.

Tiffani felt it in her molars.

She looked Downslope.

The sky tore.

Not metaphor. The air split. Vertical line. Maybe two hundred strides tall. She could see through it. See something else on the other side. Not sky. Not TeOga.

Light poured through. Copper-gold. Bright enough to leave afterimages.

Tiffani's hand moved. She was writing before she thought about it: atmospheric breach, Downslope origin, estimated two hundred strides vertical displacement.

The breach spread. Horizontal. Fast. The edges stayed sharp. Clean.

Dust fell from the gap. Not up. Down. Copper-gold particulate streaming into TeOga. Volume increasing.

The streams she'd been tracking reversed. Started flowing toward the breach instead of away from it.

She wrote: directional particulate flow, breach-oriented.

The platform tilted. Tiffani grabbed the theodolite. Kept it from sliding. The tilt increased. Five degrees. Ten.

The documentation case slid past her. She lunged. Caught it.

The railing screamed. Metal stress. Loud enough to cut through the grid-hum.

She looked at the nearest support. The mount had shifted. Bolts pulling free.

The platform was separating from the terrace face. She had maybe ten heartbeats.

The breach widened. Light flooded across the city. Tiffani couldn't look at it directly. Had to squint. Shield her eyes.

Heat arrived. Pushed through stone itself. The platform went from cool to warm to hot in heartbeats. The air thickened. Her lungs burned with each breath.

Pressure slammed her. Wall of force. Acoustic and kinetic. Her ribs compressed. Breath knocked out. She gasped. Couldn't refill. The pressure held.

Then released.

She sucked air. Tasted copper-gold. The contamination was everywhere now. Coating her mouth. Her lungs. Her skin.

The platform lurched. Dropped a full pace. Tiffani went to her knees. The documentation case hit the deck. Slid toward the edge.

She grabbed for it. Caught the strap.

Five strides to the stairs.

The grid-hum cut out. Total silence. Wrong silence. Her ears rang in the absence.

Then something else arrived. Different sound. Lower. Subsonic. She felt it in her chest cavity. In her lungs. Like the air itself was vibrating.

She stood. Ran for the stairs.

The platform tilted steeper. Thirty degrees. Her boots slipped. She went down again. Caught the railing. Pulled herself forward.

Three strides.

A support beam snapped overhead. Tiffani looked up. Saw it falling. Twisted.

The beam clipped her shoulder. Knocked her sideways. Her leg caught on the platform edge. Something tore. Pain shot through her knee and calf.

She rolled. Kept rolling. Got clear of the platform before the final support gave way.

The whole structure collapsed behind her. Metal and glass crashing down where she'd been lying.

Tiffani pressed her back against the terrace wall. Breathed. Counted heartbeats.

Her leg was damaged. Glass shard embedded near the knee. Blood spreading. The joint wouldn't hold full weight.

But she could move.

She pulled her documentation case closer. Opened the primary logbook. Blood on her fingers. Copper-gold dust everywhere.

She wrote: Exit event confirmed. Thermal bloom massive. Gravity shift detected. Personal status: mobile with compromise. Documentation complete.

Then she started crawling toward the stairs.

The breach in the sky spread wider. Light fell like rain.

## Chapter Three: The Piercing

The street cracked open. Jhace stood three strides from the fissure. Watched it spread. Stone splitting along terrace joints. The sound arrived after—low grinding that made his back teeth ache.

People ran. Away from the crack. Toward Crown elevators. Somewhere a child screamed. The sound cut off sudden. Jhace didn't look for the source.

The sky tore. He looked up. Couldn't help it. Vertical split in the air itself. Light poured through. Copper-gold. Bright enough to hurt.
    
The smell arrived with the light. Ozone. Burnt metal. Something else underneath—sweet and wrong, like fruit rotting in copper heat.

He raised his hand to shield his eyes. The light came through his fingers anyway.

The grid-hum died. Wrong silence followed. His ears rang. Pressure built in his skull. He swallowed. Didn't help.

Then the subsonic wave hit. Not sound. Pressure. Physical force in the air. Jhace felt it compress his chest. He exhaled hard. Couldn't get air back in. His lungs fought the pressure. Three heartbeats. Four.

Then it released.

He gasped. Copper taste flooded his mouth. Actual texture to it. Gritty. He spit. The saliva came out stained. Copper-gold tint.

Dust rose from the street. Streams of copper-gold particulate coming up through cracks. Through terrace seams. Through gaps in building facades. Hundreds of streams. Thousands.

The streams merged. Made columns. The columns spiraled.

Jhace watched one rise past his face. The particulate moved against the wind. Moved toward the breach in the sky.

A building collapsed two terraces Downslope. The sound arrived three heartbeats later. Stone-on-stone. Sustained. Wrong harmonics in the resonance. Jhace's molars ached.

Chloen's voice cut through the noise. "Jhace!"

He turned. Found her twenty strides away. She stood at the entrance to a residential block. The facade leaned. Active tilt. Maybe fifteen degrees already.

"The building's coming down!" she shouted.

Jhace could see that. Could see the support wall separating from the terrace face. Could see the hairline fractures spreading through the load-bearing columns.

People were still inside. He could hear them. Voices calling for help. Movement sounds. Someone coughing in the deep interior.

Chloen ran toward the entrance. Jhace ran after her.

"We need to get them out," she said.

The building shuddered. The tilt increased. Twenty degrees. Dust poured from the upper windows. Copper-gold. Dense enough to obscure the interior.

Jhace grabbed Chloen's shoulder. "It's not stable."

"There are children inside."

He looked at the facade. Calculated the collapse vector. Maybe five minutes before total failure. Maybe less.

"Get the ground floor," he said. "I'll take the second terrace apartments."

They split. Chloen went left. Jhace went right. Found the interior stairs. They tilted with the building. He climbed them anyway. Hand on the wall for balance.

The copper-gold dust hung thick. He pulled his shirt over his mouth. Didn't help much. The particulate was too fine. It got through the fabric. Coated his throat. Made him cough.

First apartment: empty.

Second apartment: elderly couple. They moved slow. Jhace got them to the stairs. Pointed them down.

Third apartment: a woman with two children. Young. Maybe six and eight Turns old. They clung to her. Wouldn't let go.

"The building's failing," Jhace said. "You need to move now."

She nodded. Got the children moving. Jhace followed them to the stairs.

The building lurched. Dropped a full pace. Jhace grabbed the doorframe. Held on.

The woman fell. One child caught themselves on the wall. The other one didn't.

The younger child slid toward the stairwell. The tilt had increased. Thirty degrees now. Gravity pulled everything Downslope.

Jhace lunged. Caught the child's arm. Pulled them back.

The woman grabbed both children. Started down the stairs.

Jhace checked the next apartment. Door jammed. He kicked it. The frame splintered but held. He kicked again. Harder. The door broke inward.

Chloen inside. Trapped under a fallen beam.

She'd been trying to reach a girl pinned in the corner. Maybe ten Turns old. The beam had caught Chloen across the shoulders when it fell.

"Can you move?" Jhace asked.

"No. Too heavy."

The girl in the corner wasn't moving. Jhace checked her pulse. Still alive. Unconscious. Blood on her forehead.

He assessed the beam. Solid iron. Load-bearing. Maybe two hundred kilograms. He couldn't lift that. Not alone.

The building shuddered again. The tilt hit forty degrees. The beam shifted. Pressed harder on Chloen.

She screamed.

"I'm stuck," she said. "Get the girl. Leave me."

Jhace looked at the beam. Looked at Chloen. Looked at the girl.

Five minutes had passed. Maybe less. The building had maybe two minutes left before total collapse.

He couldn't carry both of them. Not down tilted stairs. Not in time.

The copper-gold dust swirled around the beam. Dense concentration. It moved wrong. Against physics. The particulate spiraled. Made patterns.

Jhace put his hands on the beam.

The copper-gold dust touched his skin. Warm. Not hot. Just warm. Metal that held heat.

He thought: Need this beam to move. Need it light enough to lift. Need Chloen free.

The dust brightened. Light spread from his hands. Copper-gold. Same color as the dust. Same warmth.

It flowed into the beam. Into the iron.

The metal responded. Started to shift. Not shape. Some other property.

The beam weighed less.

Jhace didn't understand how he knew that. But he did. The beam's mass was changing. Reducing.

The iron stayed solid. Stayed structural. But the weight was leaving.

He lifted. The beam rose. Smooth. Easy. Like it weighed twenty kilograms instead of two hundred.

Chloen scrambled free. Grabbed the unconscious girl. Started for the stairs.

Jhace held the beam. His hands burned. Not pain. Not yet. But heat building. Pressure building. Something in his muscles starting to strain in a way that didn't match the physical effort.

He lowered the beam. Set it down gentle.

Pulled his hands back.

The copper-gold light faded. The beam's weight returned. It crashed into the floor. Broke through. Fell to the apartment below.

Jhace looked at his hands. They shook.

Not fatigue tremor. Something else. His fingers moved wrong. Micro-spasms in the small muscles.

He tried to make a fist. The fingers didn't fully close. Coordination compromised.

The building tilted steeper. Forty-five degrees. Jhace ran for the stairs.

The tremor spread up his wrists. Into his forearms. Each step harder to control than the last.

He found Chloen at the building entrance. She'd gotten the girl outside. Set her down on stable ground. Someone else had already started tending to her.

"Your hands," Chloen said.

Jhace looked down. The tremor was visible. Obvious. His whole hand shaking. Constant. No rhythm to it.

"I'm fine," he said.

The building collapsed behind them. Total structural failure. Three heartbeats from top to bottom. The sound hit like a physical blow.

Dust cloud spread across the terrace. Copper-gold particulate mixing with stone dust. The cloud swallowed everything.

Jhace and Chloen moved away from the collapse zone. Found clear air two terraces Crownward.

His hands still shook.

The breach in the sky spread wider. Light fell in sheets. The copper-gold dust rose to meet it. Columns of particulate connecting sky to ground.

Saint Merro wrapped in threads of light.

The subsonic pressure hit again. Harder. Jhace's knees buckled. He caught himself against a wall. Held on.

The pressure built. Built. Then released.

He vomited. Copper-gold saliva. Actual metallic taste. His stomach clenched. Nothing left to bring up. He dry-heaved twice. Stopped.

Chloen knelt beside him. "What did you do in there?"

"Lifted the beam."

"That's not what I meant."

He looked at his hands. The tremor hadn't stopped. Wouldn't stop. He could feel it now. Something changed. Permanently. The fine motor control was gone. The precision that let him work junction collars. That let him read torque by feel. Gone.

"I don't know what I did," he said.

The sky split wider.

And deeper below, in the Underface, the grid-hum screamed.

## Chapter Four: After the Shudder

The med station smelled like copper and burnt stone. Jhace sat on a supply crate. His hands still shook. Hadn't stopped in two hours.

A medic had checked him. Found no injury. No explanation for the tremor. Told him to rest. Gave him water. Moved on to people with visible wounds.

The station sat in mid-terrace. Temporary structure. Canvas walls. Insufficient light. Too many people.

Jhace counted forty-three injured. More coming in every few minutes.

Chloen sat across from him. Bandage around her ribs where the beam had caught her. She watched his hands.

"Still shaking," she said.

He nodded. Didn't try to hide it. No point.

"What happened in that room?"

"I lifted the beam."

"That beam weighed more than you do."

Jhace looked at his hands. The tremor ran constant. Small amplitude. High frequency. Like his muscles couldn't remember how to be still.

"The copper-gold dust. It changed something."

"Changed what?"

"The beam's weight. Or my strength. I don't know."

Chloen's jaw tightened. "That doesn't make sense."

"I know."

A woman stumbled into the med station. Older. Maybe forty-five Turns. Hair streaked with white.

Jhace recognized her. Corvan. Metalworker from the Underface. They'd crossed paths on inspections. Professional acquaintance. Not friends.

She looked wrong. Way older than Jhace remembered. Her skin hung loose. Liver spots on her hands. Deep lines around her eyes. She moved like her joints hurt.

She saw Jhace. Stopped. Stared at his shaking hands.

"You too," she said.

"What?"

"You manifested."

Jhace stood. The tremor made it awkward. "I don't know what that means."

Corvan moved closer. Lowered her voice. "The copper-gold dust. The light from your hands. You changed something. Made it do what you needed instead of what physics says it should."

"How do you know that?"

"Because I did it too."

She held up her hands. They shook. Same pattern as Jhace's. Same high-frequency tremor. But her hands looked decades older. Thin skin. Prominent veins. Age spots.

"My daughter was dying," Corvan said. "Crush injuries. Internal bleeding. I put my hands on her wounds and thought: stop the bleeding. Close the damage. Fix her."

"Did it work?"

"She's alive. In the back section. Stable."

Corvan's voice stayed flat. Matter-of-fact.

"But look at me."

Jhace looked. Really looked. Corvan's hair had been dark brown three Tides ago when he'd seen her at Junction Fifteen. Now it was white. Her face had been smooth. Now it carried lines that belonged on someone twenty Turns older.

"What happened to you?" he asked.

"I paid the price. Healing isn't magic. It's transaction. I gave years of my life to repair her body. The copper-gold dust just made the exchange possible."

The tremor in Jhace's hands intensified.

"How many years?"

"Ten. Maybe more. I aged a decade in thirty heartbeats."

Chloen stood. "That's not possible."

"Jhace's hands say different."

Corvan looked at him. "What did you do?"

"Made a beam lighter. Lifted it off Chloen."

"And the cost?"

He raised his hands. Showed the tremor. "Fine motor control. Precision work. It's gone."

Corvan nodded. "The cost matches the action. You manipulated metal. Now your hands don't work right. I healed flesh. Now my body's aged."

She paused. "The dust doesn't give. It trades."

A medic approached. Told Corvan her daughter needed her. She left without another word.

Jhace sat back down. Stared at his hands. The tremor wouldn't stop.

He tried to make his fingers still through pure focus. Didn't work. The shake ran deeper than conscious control.

"You believe her?" Chloen asked.

"My hands believe her."

"There has to be another explanation."

"Like what?"

Chloen had no answer.

The med station entrance opened. Someone being carried in on a stretcher.

Jhace caught a glimpse. Woman. Leg injury. Blood-soaked bandage. She was conscious. Writing in a notebook while they moved her.

Something about the focus. The documentation instinct. It registered.

The medics set the stretcher down in the back section. Jhace stood. Moved closer. Got a better look.

He recognized her. The woman from the observatory. The one who'd been tracking Seven-Three.

Her leg was bad. Glass shard wound. Wrapped but still bleeding through. She'd need stitches. Maybe surgery.

But she kept writing. Kept logging data.

A medic tried to take her notebook. She pulled it away. Held it against her chest.

"I need to document this," she said.

Her voice carried. Clear. Precise.

"Exit event. Thermal bloom. Gravity shift. Personal status: mobile with compromise."

The medic tried again. "You need treatment."

"After I finish the entry."

She wrote for another thirty heartbeats. Then closed the notebook. Let the medic examine her leg.

Jhace watched. Recognized the instinct. The need to record. To document. To maintain accountability even when your body's failing.

He understood that.

The tremor in his hands had spread to his wrists. Maybe his forearms. He couldn't tell anymore. The shake felt like part of him now. Background noise. Constant.

He looked at the woman on the stretcher. Looked at Corvan sitting with her daughter. Looked at his own hands.

Three people. Three manifestations. Three costs.

The pattern was there. He just didn't know what it meant yet.

## Chapter Five: Where to Press

Tiffani's leg hurt. Not acute pain. Constant. Deep ache in the joint. The glass shard was out. Medic had stitched the wound. Wrapped it. Told her to stay off it.

She'd nodded. Waited for the medic to move to the next patient. Then stood up.

The leg held. Barely. She limped. Each step sent pain up through her knee. But she could move. That's what mattered.

Her documentation case sat on the cot beside her. She pulled it close. Opened the primary logbook. Found the last entry: Personal status: mobile with compromise.

She added: Treatment complete. Mobility confirmed. Resuming documentation.

The med station had filled. Maybe sixty people now. More arriving.

Something pulled at her attention. Not thought. Pressure. Direction.

She looked toward the entrance. The metalworker with shaking hands stood there. Watching. Cataloging. Same instinct she had.

She didn't know why she noticed him specifically. Just did.

Tiffani limped toward him. Each step measured. Her leg screamed. She ignored it.

"You're the one from the residential collapse," she said.

He looked at her. "You're the one from the observatory."

"Correct." She shifted weight. Tested the joint. It held. "You manifested during the rescue."

His jaw tightened. "How do you know that term?"

"The older metalworker. Corvan. She used it. I heard."

"You manifest too?"

"No. I documented the event. Observatory platform. Full visual on the sky breach."

She pulled her logbook out. Showed him the entry. "Exit event confirmed. Thermal bloom massive. Gravity shift detected."

He read the entry. His hands shook while he held the page. The tremor made the text wobble.

"You measured the breach?"

"Estimated. Two hundred strides vertical. Expanding horizontal at approximately twenty strides per minute during initial phase."

"That's precise."

"That's necessary."

She took the logbook back. "Your hands. The tremor. That's the cost of manifestation?"

"According to Corvan."

"What did you do?"

"Made a beam lighter. Lifted it."

Tiffani wrote: Manifestation type—mass manipulation. Cost—motor control compromise.

She showed him the entry. "This matches Corvan's pattern. Healing manifestation. Life-force cost. Aging."

"You're cataloging this."

"Someone has to."

A commotion at the entrance. Someone carried in on a stretcher. Unconscious. Young man. Maybe twenty Turns. His hands glowed. Faint copper-gold light. Visible even in the med station lamps.

The medics set him down. Checked his vitals. One of them shouted for the senior medic.

Tiffani moved closer. The metalworker followed.

They stood at the edge of the treatment area. Watched.

The young man's breathing was shallow. Rapid. His skin felt cold when the medic checked. But his hands stayed warm. The glow pulsed. Matched his heartbeat.

"What happened to him?" the senior medic asked.

The person who'd brought him answered. "Found him in a collapse zone. He was holding up a wall. Actually holding it. Hands on the stone. The wall wasn't falling. Then he passed out. The wall came down after."

The senior medic examined the glowing hands. "How long was he holding it?"

"Ten minutes. Maybe more."

Tiffani wrote: Prolonged manifestation = sustained cost accrual.

The young man convulsed. Brief. His back arched. Then he went still. Too still.

The medics worked. Tried to revive him. Called for equipment. Tried again.

He didn't come back.

The senior medic called time. Wrote the death record.

The copper-gold glow faded from the young man's hands. Left them looking normal. Unmarked.

Tiffani stood frozen. Watched them cover the body.

Her pen stayed hovering over the page. She needed to write this. Document it. But the words wouldn't come.

The metalworker's voice came quiet. "The cost can be fatal."

She wrote: Manifestation death confirmed. Cause: sustained reality override beyond physiological tolerance.

Her hand shook while she wrote. Not tremor. Just shock. She forced the letters steady. Finished the entry. Closed the logbook.

"We need to warn people," she said.

"Warn them about what? Not to save people?"

"Warn them about the limits. About sustained use. About—"

She stopped. Looked at the covered body. "About the price."

They stood in silence. The med station noise continued around them. Wounded arriving. Medics working.

"You know what I realized?" the metalworker said finally.

"What?"

"We're doing the same thing. You document reality until institutions listen. I worked junctions until the grid broke. Both of us trying to prevent casualties through method."

"Both failing," Tiffani said.

"Both trying anyway."

She looked at him. Really looked. "I'm Tiffani. Observatory specialist. Structural analysis."

"Jhace. Grid inspector. Metalwork."

"We should coordinate documentation," she said.

"Agreed," Jhace said.

## Chapter Six: The Mistake

Three Tides after the Piercing, Jhace made his first mistake.

A young woman approached him at the med station. Maybe nineteen Turns. Burn scars on her forearms. Guild metalworker's marks.

"You made the beam lighter," she said.

Jhace nodded.

"Can you teach me?"

His stomach tightened. "I don't know how I did it."

"But you did it." "That means it can be learned."

"That's not—"

He stopped. Looked at his shaking hands. "It cost me. Look."

She looked. "I see. But people are dying in collapses. If we could stabilize structures. Save them. The cost would be worth it."

"You don't know that."

"Don't I?" She held up her scarred arms. "I work junction collars. Hot metal. Steam pressure. I carry scars every day. This is just another scar."

Jhace wanted to refuse. Walk away. But she was right. People were dying. If manifestation could be controlled. Taught. Used safely.

The cost might be acceptable.

"I'll try," he said. "But I can't promise anything."

They moved to a quieter section. Found a broken support beam. Small. Maybe twenty kilograms. Manageable weight.

Tiffani appeared. Limping. "What are you doing?"

"Teaching," Jhace said.

Her jaw tightened. "You don't know how manifestation works."

"I know it saved Chloen. If others can learn it—"

"You're guessing," she said. "Experimenting on people."

"She volunteered."

"That doesn't make it safe."

The young woman interrupted. "I want to try. My choice."

Tiffani pulled out her logbook. "Then I'm documenting this."

Jhace turned back to the woman. "When I manifested, I needed the beam to move. Needed it light enough to lift. Needed the person trapped to be free."

"Desperate need," Tiffani said.

"Correct," Jhace said.

"And the copper‑gold dust?" the woman asked.

Jhace looked around. The particulate still hung in the air. Thinner now, but present. He scooped some from a window ledge. Held it in his palm.

"I touched it. It touched the beam. The light came after."

The woman copied him. Scooped dust from the ledge. Held it.

"Now what?"

"Put your hands on the beam. Think about making it lighter."

She did. Pressed her palms to the metal. Closed her eyes. Concentrated.

Nothing happened.

She tried again. Harder focus. Visible strain in her face.

Still nothing.

"I don't feel anything," she said.

"Maybe the need has to be real," Jhace said. "Not practice."

"So I need to be in danger?"

Jhace's jaw tightened. This was wrong. Stupid. Reckless.

He started to tell her to stop.

The woman lifted a heavier beam. Sixty kilograms. Held it overhead. Arms shaking with the weight.

"This is danger," she said. "This could crush me if I drop it."

"That's not the same—"

She closed her eyes. Focused.

The copper‑gold dust on her hands started to glow.

Jhace's pulse spiked. "Wait. You need to—"

The light intensified. Spread into the beam. The woman gasped.

"I feel it," she said. "I feel the weight changing."

"Lower the beam," Jhace said. "Slowly."

She didn't. Kept pushing. Kept trying to make it lighter.

The glow brightened. Her hands shook. Not from weight. From something else. Cost starting to pull through her.

"Stop," Jhace said. "You need to stop now."

The woman screamed. Not pain. Something worse. The sound tore out of her throat.

The beam crashed down. Missed her by a hand's width.

She fell. Convulsed. Her whole body seizing.

Jhace grabbed her. Tried to hold her steady. The convulsions intensified. Blood came from her nose. Her eyes rolled back.

Medics ran over. Pushed Jhace aside. Worked on the woman. Tried to stabilize her.

Her convulsions stopped. She went limp. Too limp.

The senior medic checked her pulse. Checked her breathing.

Called time.

Dead.

Jhace stood frozen. Stared at the body. At his shaking hands. At the copper‑gold dust still dull on her palms.

He had told her to try. He had helped her.

He had killed her.

Tiffani appeared beside him. Limping. Her leg barely holding. She looked at the body. Looked at Jhace.

"What happened?" she asked.

"I tried to teach her," he said. "She manifested. It killed her."

Tiffani's expression didn't change. She pulled out her logbook. Wrote: manifestation attempt under instruction = fatal. Added: teaching manifestation not viable. Desperate need cannot be simulated.

"You're documenting this," Jhace said. Voice flat.

"Yes," she said.

"She's dead because I tried to help."

"She's dead because manifestation doesn't work that way," Tiffani said. "Now we know."

Jhace's hands shook harder. "That's all this is to you? Data?"

"That's what keeps more people from dying," she said. "Data. Understanding. Method."

She wrote another line. "Your guilt doesn't help anyone. Your documentation might."

Jhace wanted to hit her. Wanted to scream. Wanted to do something other than stand there with his useless shaking hands and watch them cover another body.

He did none of those things. Just stood. Breathed. Counted heartbeats.

Tiffani kept writing.

## Chapter Seven: Tiffani's Calculation

Four Tides after the Piercing, Tiffani made her mistake.

Crown officials posted shelter locations on a board outside the med station. She read the list, then cross‑referenced each location against her pre‑Piercing structural data. Three shelters marked safe sat on foundations she knew were compromised.

She limped to the posting board. Found the official who had put up the notices.

"These locations are wrong," she said.

The official looked at her leg, at the bloodstained wrappings. "You need to be resting."

"I need you to remove three shelters from the safe list."

"On what authority?"

"On the authority of structural analysis." She opened her logbook. "I have pre‑Piercing stress measurements. These buildings were already at or above threshold. They will not hold under aftershock load."

The official checked his slate. "These buildings passed last Turn's inspection."

"Last Turn's inspection didn't account for harmonic changes in the grid." "I documented dual‑frequency resonance two Tides before the Piercing. That indicates subsurface stress propagation. These shelters sit on compromised foundation."

"Do you have certification to make structural assessments?" he asked.

"I have data."

"That's not the same thing."

Tiffani held up the logbook so he could see. Calculations. Measurement sequences. Projection models.

"This building." She tapped a line on the posting. "Terrace Six administrative block. Foundation stress at one hundred twelve percent of safe threshold before the Piercing. Under aftershocks, it should already be failing. Anyone sheltering there is in direct collapse risk."

The official scanned the numbers. "These could be wrong."

"They're not."

"How do you know?"

"I measured them twice with calibrated instruments."

"You're one person with one notebook." "The inspection guild certified these buildings as safe."

"The inspection guild didn't measure harmonic shifts."

"Because harmonic shifts don't matter for structural integrity."

"They do now."

He closed his slate. "I can't strike official shelters on the word of a single observatory specialist." "People need somewhere to go. These buildings are on the safe list. They stay."

He walked away.

Tiffani stood there, jaw locked. The logbook weighed the same. The count had changed.

Something needled at her. She looked back down at the page. Terrace Six showed extreme stress. Terrace Eight—the shelter she had passed on the way here—logged lower values. Still elevated. Maybe acceptable if aftershocks stayed within current range.

She closed the logbook halfway.

On the next aftershock cycle, people would move. With the official notices fixed, the only variable she could change was direction.

She took position near the board. When families approached to read the shelters list, she pulled them aside.

"Terrace Eight shelter is more stable than Terrace Six," she told them. "Foundations are better. Go there instead."

Some listened. Some didn't. Enough did to matter.

Jhace found her three hours later.

"You're routing people to specific shelters," he said.

"Correct," she said. "Based on structural analysis."

"What if your analysis is wrong?"

"It's not."

"Junction Seven wasn't supposed to fail either." "The engineering report said acceptable parameters. Twenty‑three names ended on a plaque."

Tiffani's fingers tightened around the logbook. "That was different."

"How?"

"I have better data now." "Pre‑Piercing measurements. Harmonic tracking. Stress analysis the inspection guild never did."

"But you're still one person making projections." "If you're wrong—"

The aftershock hit.

Magnitude high enough to rattle the med station cots. Eight heartbeats of sustained shaking.

Tiffani counted. Logged the duration in her head out of habit.

Then the screaming started.

Not from Terrace Six. From Terrace Eight.

The shelter she had called safer. The one she had directed people toward.

The facade had failed.

Jhace was already moving. Tiffani followed as fast as her damaged leg allowed.

They reached the collapse zone. Smoke and dust. Copper‑gold tint in the air. Support columns broken. Roof slab down. Rescue crews pulling bodies free.

Twelve confirmed dead within the first hour. More trapped.

A Crown official pushed through the crowd. The same one from the posting board.

"You told people this building was safer," he said.

"My calculations—" she started.

"Your calculations killed twelve people. Maybe more."

"Terrace Six would have killed more."

"Terrace Six is still standing."

Tiffani opened the logbook with hands that did not feel like hers. The Terrace Six stress readings were clear. The building should have failed before Eight. It hadn't.

Her model had predicted the order of failure wrong.

The official took the logbook from her grip. "You're done making recommendations. Shelter guidance is Crown authority only from now on."

He walked away with her work in his hands.

Tiffani stayed at the edge of the rubble. Watched rescue crews move through broken stone. Every body they carried out passed through the space where her certainty had been.

Jhace came to stand beside her. His hands shook in the air between them.

"Twelve," he said.

"I logged the count," she said.

"You were trying to help."

"So were you," she said. "When you tried to teach manifestation. Your attempt killed her. My attempt killed them."

"Yes."

They watched the rubble. Two people who had used method to prevent casualties and produced different casualties instead.

"We need better data," Tiffani said.

"We need to stop pretending we understand this," Jhace said.

"If we don't try to understand, more people die."

"If we try to understand and are wrong, more people die."

"So what do we do?" she asked.

Jhace watched the rescue crews for a long moment. "Document. Observe. Stop making predictive calls until the model matches reality more often than not."

Tiffani reached for a fresh page in the logbook the official hadn't taken—the secondary one she kept in her case. Her hand shook as she wrote: Shelter miscalculation. Casualties: twelve confirmed. Cause: incomplete structural model and premature application. Recommendation: suspend predictive shelter assessments until data set expands.

She showed it to Jhace.

He read. Nodded once. "That's honest."

"That's necessary," she said.

"They won't listen."

"They will, after enough people die from ignoring accurate data."

"That's a grim calculation."

"That's the only calculation that moves institutions," Tiffani said.

## Chapter Eight: Fragmentation

One Anneal after the Piercing, three Crown factions arrived at the med station within the same Tide.

First came the Religious sect. Elder Vrasten. Copper bands around both wrists. Scroll cases sealed against moisture. He walked the aisles between cots, looking at manifestors like they were scripture made visible.

Second came the economic guild. Representative Chelm. Clipboard. Guild seal pinned sharp at his collar. He asked manifestors what crafts they practiced, what guild marks they carried, whether they had "authorization to alter material properties."

Third came the military. Commander Tallec. Armored escort. She moved through the station with an aide taking down names, manifestation types, locations, and witness statements about "unregulated force deployment in civilian zones."

None of them spoke to each other. None coordinated. Each operated from a different theory about what manifestation was.

Tiffani watched from her cot. She documented all three approaches in the margin of her logbook. Wrote: Institutional fragmentation—no unified response. Three competing frameworks: divine, economic, military.

Near the entrance, Jhace stood where he could see the flow of bodies and uniforms. The girl Corvan had healed—the one who had cost Corvan ten Turns of her life—approached him.

"They're trying to take us," she said. "The military. They say we're weapons. That we have to be controlled."

"Are you a weapon?" Jhace asked.

"I don't know," she said. "I just want to find my family."

Commander Tallec reached them.

"You," she said to the girl. "Manifestor. Name and capability."

The girl froze. Jhace stepped half a stride forward, his useless hands shaking between him and the commander.

"She hasn't broken any grid law," Jhace said.

"Manifestation is unregulated capability," Tallec replied. "Until classified, all manifestors report for evaluation."

"Evaluation for what?" Jhace asked.

"Threat assessment." "We don't know what you people can do. That uncertainty is risk."

"We held up walls," Jhace said. "Pulled people from rubble. Saved lives."

"You also killed that woman trying to learn manifestation," Tallec said. "I have three witness reports. You encouraged her. She died."

Jhace's jaw tightened. "That was my mistake. Not hers."

"Exactly." "You don't understand what you're doing. Ignorant force is the most dangerous kind."

Elder Vrasten stepped in, copper bands catching lamplight.

"Weapons?" he said. "These are the Marked. The Piercing unveiled them. They should be under temple guidance, not military custody."

Representative Chelm arrived on the other side.

"They should be registered with the guild," he said. "Manifestation is material manipulation. That's craft. That's jurisdiction. Unauthorized manipulation of matter destabilizes markets."

The three of them—Tallec, Vrasten, Chelm—formed a triangle around Jhace and the girl. They argued over who had the right to claim her. Over whether she was threat, miracle, or asset.

At no point did anyone ask her what she wanted.

Around them, medics tried to work. The argument pulled eyes. Pulled focus. Patients were left waiting while factions fought over names on lists.

Tiffani slid off her cot, pain spiking through her stitched leg. She limped closer, keeping out of the direct line of fire but close enough to hear.

"This is going to get worse," she said quietly to Jhace when she reached him.

"I know," he said.

"They're splintering," she said. "No unified policy. Each faction will try to control manifestors in its own way. Different requirements. Conflicting orders."

"What do we do?" he asked.

Something in her attention pulled toward the back of the station. Corvan sat there with her daughter, hands aged decades by one act of healing.

"We track what actually happens," Tiffani said. "Under each faction's control. Who lives. Who dies. What costs manifestors pay."

"You want to turn manifestors into a study," Jhace said.

"I want to stop improvising," she said. "Right now policy is theory. We counter theory with evidence. If we can't stop them from taking people, we can at least measure what they do."

"They won't listen if the data contradicts their stories," Jhace said.

"They will when their stories produce enough casualties that denial breaks," she said.

"Cold," he said.

"Method," she answered.

The argument in front of them escalated. Voices sharpened. More uniforms pressed in behind each leader.

Commander Tallec unfastened the strap on her sidearm. Didn't draw, but made the option visible. Assertion of authority.

Elder Vrasten lifted both copper‑banded arms in a ritual gesture.

Representative Chelm called for guild enforcers from the corridor.

The med station had become a pressure test. Not of stone and metal. Of institutions. Of how they behaved when power appeared where it had not existed before.

Tiffani opened a fresh section in her logbook. Titled it: Institutional response patterns—comparative analysis. She began noting everything: recruitment language, threats used, incentives offered, constraints imposed.

She would follow Corvan if the temple took her. Follow the girl if the military seized her. Follow any manifestor the guild pulled into contract. Track outcomes under each framework.

It was not comfort. Not protection. Not justice.

It was a record. A way to force institutions, later, to look at what they had done.

Method was all she had. So she held to it.

## Chapter Nine: Fourth Threshold

Ten Tides after the Piercing, another collapse hit Saint Merro. Terrace Four this time. Residential block. Fifty people trapped by initial reports.

Jhace stood at the edge of the collapse zone. His arms shook in visible waves. Every breath carried a small hitch as his muscles misfired.

First manifestation had taken his hands.
Second had pulled the tremor into wrists and forearms.
Third had reached shoulders and upper back.

Now the shake owned everything above his ribs. He could not hold tools. Could not sign his own name without the letters tangling.

But when he closed his eyes and imagined his hands on stone, he could still feel the dust answer.

Tiffani found him there, near a buckled support column.

"You're thinking about it," she said.

"Fifty people," he said.

"Fourth manifestation pushes you past survivable cost." "You've seen the pattern."

"I know the numbers," he said. "But fifty people die if I stand here and do nothing."

"You're not responsible for every death you could, in theory, prevent," she said.

"Aren't I?" he asked. "I have capability. I know the trade. If I choose not to act, those deaths are a cost I accept. Same as if I act and pay with my body."

Tiffani had no immediate counter. She had run the same equation from the other side, every time she chose to intervene with data.

"Corvan chose," Jhace said. "She knew manifestation would cost her. Paid a decade to keep her daughter breathing. That trade cleared in her mind."

"Her death was her choice," Tiffani said. "This is yours."

"Correct," he said. "And I'm choosing."

He looked down at his trembling frame. "I can't work junctions. Can't hold a collar key. My function is gone. But I can still manifest once more. Turn what's left into saved lives."

"You're rationalizing self‑destruction," she said.

"I'm calculating expected value," he said. "One manifestor whose effective function is already zero versus fifty trapped. If I succeed, the city keeps fifty people who can still work, still hold. Math is clear."

"Math doesn't measure what your loss does to the people who know you," she said.

"Like who?" he asked.

"Like me," she said.

He blinked.

"You barely know me," he said.

"I know you document even when no one listens," she said. "I know you refuse shortcuts on the grid. I know you carry cost openly instead of pretending it isn't there. That matters."

"You can document without me," he said.

"Not the same way," she said. "You're ground truth. First‑hand account of what this does to a body and mind. If you die, I lose that vantage point."

"So I'm data," he said.

"You're also the only person who doesn't call me cold when I pick the necessary line instead of the comforting one," she said.

The block groaned. Stone shifting deeper inside the collapse. Someone screamed from under a slab.

Jhace looked toward the sound.

"People are dying in there now," he said.

"I know," she said.

"I can change that," he said.

"At the cost of your life," she said.

"Or at the cost of everything but," he said. "Either way, the cost is mine to pay."

"It's not acceptable," she said.

"It's not your choice," he answered.

They held there, between theory and stone.

"If you do this," Tiffani said quietly, "I document it. Every second. Every shift in your vitals. Every cost. Your decision becomes part of the record that shapes the next one."

"Good," he said.

"And I use that record to force institutions to face what they ask of manifestors," she said. "So maybe the next person doesn't have to stand at a collapse edge alone and do this calculus."

"Even better," he said.

He walked—more a stagger now—toward the largest intact slab of the ruined block. Rescue crews had marked it as primary load. If it slipped, it would crush the voids where voices still called.

Tiffani followed, logbook open, pen ready. Witness and instrument at once.

Jhace pressed his forehead against the stone. His arms hung useless; the tremor made them worse than dead weight.

"I need this to hold," he whispered. "I need them free. I need this to count."

The copper‑gold light answered.

It did not confine itself to his hands this time. It rose from his chest, from his back, from every place the cost had already marked. It ran into the stone. Into the fracture lines.

The rubble settled. Stopped shifting. The groaning ceased.

Tiffani logged onset time, visible intensity, stabilization markers. Wrote through the shaking in her own hand.

Rescue crews moved in. They cut, lifted, pulled. Voices came out of voids as bodies. Some breathing. Some not.

Eight minutes passed. Jhace did not move from the stone. His whole body shook in increasing amplitude. Sweat ran copper‑streaked down his neck.

By the time crews pulled the last living person clear, they had extracted forty‑three survivors. Seven had died in the initial failure.

"Release," Tiffani said softly. "They're clear."

Jhace let go.

The light left faster than it had come. He dropped with it.

He hit the stone wrong. Limbs at angles that meant nothing now because they did not respond.

Medics were there in heartbeats. Tiffani recorded vitals while they worked.

Heart rate: thirty beats per minute.
Respiration: four breaths per minute.
Body temperature: falling toward hypothermic range despite ambient heat.

They dragged him onto a stretcher. Stabilized his neck. Ran copper‑gold dust tests they knew would tell them nothing.

He did not die. The numbers crawled back from the edge. Heart rate strengthened. Breathing deepened.

The cost came another way.

By the time they had him in long‑term care, his legs did not respond to pinpricks. Feet, calves, thighs—no reaction. Below the waist, his body might as well belong to someone else.

The tremor in his upper body had not eased. Combining both, the medics wrote the word they did not want to say aloud: quadriplegic.

Tiffani stood at the foot of his cot while they finished their assessment. She logged everything. Sensation maps. Range‑of‑motion tests. Failed movement attempts.

Later, when they left, she moved to his side.

"I'm still alive," he said. His voice came out thin but steady.

"Yes," she said.

"Forty‑three people are alive too," he said. "Not bad for someone who was supposed to die on the fourth."

"You can't move," she said.

"I'd noticed," he said.

"This may be worse than dying," she said.

"Is it?" he asked. "I can still think. Still speak. Still tell you what this feels like from the inside. You don't get that from the ones who stopped breathing."

She wrote his words down almost verbatim. Not because they were pretty. Because they were ground truth.

The fourth threshold had not killed him. It had taken everything else.

She underlined the final line of the entry: Fourth manifestation—non‑fatal outcome possible. Cost: total loss of mobility with preserved cognition. Assessment: worse than death for some; essential data point for all.

That was the trade now. That was what the dust had bought and sold.

## Chapter Ten: Institutional Support

Half a Tide after the fourth manifestation, the Crown Council convened an emergency session. All three factions attended: military, religious, economic, plus central administrators.

They summoned Tiffani with an armed escort. Not optional. She limped into the chamber with her primary logbook and supplementary calculations—every manifestation event, cost, and casualty count she had.

The council sat elevated, looking down. Architecture built for intimidation, not acoustics.

"You've been documenting manifestation events," Commander Tallec said.

"Correct."

"Without authorization."

"I am an observatory specialist. Documentation is my function. No authorization required."

"During a crisis, all research requires military clearance."

"Then file for retroactive clearance. The data stands regardless."

She pulled her logbook out. Showed him the entry. "Exit event confirmed. Thermal bloom massive. Gravity shift detected."

He read the entry. His hands shook while he held the page. The tremor made the text wobble.

"You measured the breach?"

"Estimated. Two hundred strides vertical. Expanding horizontal at approximately twenty strides per minute during initial phase."

"That's precise."

"That's necessary."

She took the logbook back. "Your hands. The tremor. That's the cost of manifestation?"

"According to Corvan."

"What did you do?"

"Made a beam lighter. Lifted it."

Tiffani wrote: Manifestation type—mass manipulation. Cost—motor control compromise.

She showed him the entry. "This matches Corvan's pattern. Healing manifestation. Life-force cost. Aging."

"You're cataloging this."

"Someone has to."

A commotion at the entrance. Someone carried in on a stretcher. Unconscious. Young man. Maybe twenty Turns. His hands glowed. Faint copper-gold light. Visible even in the med station lamps.

The medics set him down. Checked his vitals. One of them shouted for the senior medic.

Tiffani moved closer. The metalworker followed.

They stood at the edge of the treatment area. Watched.

The young man's breathing was shallow. Rapid. His skin felt cold when the medic checked. But his hands stayed warm. The glow pulsed. Matched his heartbeat.

"What happened to him?" the senior medic asked.

The person who'd brought him answered. "Found him in a collapse zone. He was holding up a wall. Actually holding it. Hands on the stone. The wall wasn't falling. Then he passed out. The wall came down after."

The senior medic examined the glowing hands. "How long was he holding it?"

"Ten minutes. Maybe more."

Tiffani wrote: Prolonged manifestation = sustained cost accrual.

The young man convulsed. Brief. His back arched. Then he went still. Too still.

The medics worked. Tried to revive him. Called for equipment. Tried again.

He didn't come back.

The senior medic called time. Wrote the death record.

The copper-gold glow faded from the young man's hands. Left them looking normal. Unmarked.

Tiffani stood frozen. Watched them cover the body.

Her pen stayed hovering over the page. She needed to write this. Document it. But the words wouldn't come.

The metalworker's voice came quiet. "The cost can be fatal."

She wrote: Manifestation death confirmed. Cause: sustained reality override beyond physiological tolerance.

Her hand shook while she wrote. Not tremor. Just shock. She forced the letters steady. Finished the entry. Closed the logbook.

"We need to warn people," she said.

"Warn them about what? Not to save people?"

"Warn them about the limits. About sustained use. About—"

She stopped. Looked at the covered body. "About the price."

They stood in silence. The med station noise continued around them. Wounded arriving. Medics working.

"You know what I realized?" the metalworker said finally.

"What?"

"We're doing the same thing. You document reality until institutions listen. I worked junctions until the grid broke. Both of us trying to prevent casualties through method."

"Both failing," Tiffani said.

"Both trying anyway."

She looked at him. Really looked. "I'm Tiffani. Observatory specialist. Structural analysis."

"Jhace. Grid inspector. Metalwork."

"We should coordinate documentation," she said.

"Agreed," Jhace said.

## Chapter Six: The Mistake

Three Tides after the Piercing, Jhace made his first mistake.

A Crown engineer arrived at the med station two hours before that. Older. Structural sect marks. He carried measuring equipment and a face that had stopped hoping.

He found Tiffani at her documentation station. Set a slate on her workspace without asking permission.

"Grid resonance analysis," he said. "Completed this morning."

Tiffani read the data. Foundation integrity compromised across Terraces Four through Eight. The dual-frequency harmonic shift was propagating through substrate stone. Load-bearing columns showing micro-fracture patterns that matched pre-collapse signatures from Junction Seven.

"Time frame?" she asked.

"Three Anneals," he said. "Maybe four if manifestors keep stabilizing active failures. After that, cascading collapse becomes self-sustaining. We lose the Underface. Then mid-terrace. Then everything Downslope of Crown."

"Confidence level?"

"Seventy percent. Could be slower. Could be faster. The grid's behavior doesn't match historical models anymore."

Tiffani logged the projection. "You've reported this to Crown administration?"

"They want confirmation from the Architecture sect before committing resources. The Architecture sect wants the Religious sect's approval because the grid is 'sacred infrastructure.' The Religious sect is waiting for a sign." He picked up his slate. "I'm telling you because you're the only person documenting what's actually happening instead of what people want to believe is happening."

He left.

Tiffani wrote: Structural collapse timeline—three Anneals estimated. Current mitigation: manifestor intervention only. Institutional response: pending theological approval.

She looked across the med station. Jhace sat near the entrance. Hands shaking. Already compromised from two manifestations. The cost ledger said a third would take more. A fourth would likely kill him.

Three Anneals. Fifty collapses projected. Hundreds dead if no intervention.

The numbers were clear. The numbers were always clear.

A young woman approached him at the med station. Maybe nineteen Turns. Burn scars on her forearms. Guild metalworker's marks.

"You made the beam lighter," she said.

Jhace nodded.

"Can you teach me?"

His stomach tightened. "I don't know how I did it."

"But you did it." "That means it can be learned."

"That's not—"

He stopped. Looked at his shaking hands. "It cost me. Look."

She looked. "I see. But people are dying in collapses. If we could stabilize structures. Save them. The cost would be worth it."

"You don't know that."

"Don't I?" She held up her scarred arms. "I work junction collars. Hot metal. Steam pressure. I carry scars every day. This is just another scar."

Jhace wanted to refuse. Walk away. But she was right. People were dying. If manifestation could be controlled. Taught. Used safely.

The cost might be acceptable.

"I'll try," he said. "But I can't promise anything."

They moved to a quieter section. Found a broken support beam. Small. Maybe twenty kilograms. Manageable weight.

Tiffani appeared. Limping. "What are you doing?"

"Teaching," Jhace said.

Her jaw tightened. "You don't know how manifestation works."

"I know it saved Chloen. If others can learn it—"

"You're guessing," she said. "Experimenting on people."

"She volunteered."

"That doesn't make it safe."

The young woman interrupted. "I want to try. My choice."

Tiffani pulled out her logbook. "Then I'm documenting this."

Jhace turned back to the woman. "When I manifested, I needed the beam to move. Needed it light enough to lift. Needed the person trapped to be free."

"Desperate need," Tiffani said.

"Correct," Jhace said.

"And the copper‑gold dust?" the woman asked.

Jhace looked around. The particulate still hung in the air. Thinner now, but present. He scooped some from a window ledge. Held it in his palm.

"I touched it. It touched the beam. The light came after."

The woman copied him. Scooped dust from the ledge. Held it.

"Now what?"

"Put your hands on the beam. Think about making it lighter."

She did. Pressed her palms to the metal. Closed her eyes. Concentrated.

Nothing happened.

She tried again. Harder focus. Visible strain in her face.

Still nothing.

"I don't feel anything," she said.

"Maybe the need has to be real," Jhace said. "Not practice."

"So I need to be in danger?"

Jhace's jaw tightened. This was wrong. Stupid. Reckless.

He started to tell her to stop.

The woman lifted a heavier beam. Sixty kilograms. Held it overhead. Arms shaking with the weight.

"This is danger," she said. "This could crush me if I drop it."

"That's not the same—"

She closed her eyes. Focused.

The copper‑gold dust on her hands started to glow.

Jhace's pulse spiked. "Wait. You need to—"

The light intensified. Spread into the beam. The woman gasped.

"I feel it," she said. "I feel the weight changing."

"Lower the beam," Jhace said. "Slowly."

She didn't. Kept pushing. Kept trying to make it lighter.

The glow brightened. Her hands shook. Not from weight. From something else. Cost starting to pull through her.

"Stop," Jhace said. "You need to stop now."

The woman screamed. Not pain. Something worse. The sound tore out of her throat.

The beam crashed down. Missed her by a hand's width.

She fell. Convulsed. Her whole body seizing.

Jhace grabbed her. Tried to hold her steady. The convulsions intensified. Blood came from her nose. Her eyes rolled back.

Medics ran over. Pushed Jhace aside. Worked on the woman. Tried to stabilize her.

Her convulsions stopped. She went limp. Too limp.

The senior medic checked her pulse. Checked her breathing.

Called time.

Dead.

Jhace stood frozen. Stared at the body. At his shaking hands. At the copper‑gold dust still dull on her palms.

He had told her to try. He had helped her.

He had killed her.

Tiffani appeared beside him. Limping. Her leg barely holding. She looked at the body. Looked at Jhace.

"What happened?" she asked.

"I tried to teach her," he said. "She manifested. It killed her."

Tiffani's expression didn't change. She pulled out her logbook. Wrote: manifestation attempt under instruction = fatal. Added: teaching manifestation not viable. Desperate need cannot be simulated.

"You're documenting this," Jhace said. Voice flat.

"Yes," she said.

"She's dead because I tried to help."

"She's dead because manifestation doesn't work that way," Tiffani said. "Now we know."

Jhace's hands shook harder. "That's all this is to you? Data?"

"That's what keeps more people from dying," she said. "Data. Understanding. Method."

She wrote another line. "Your guilt doesn't help anyone. Your documentation might."

Jhace wanted to hit her. Wanted to scream. Wanted to do something other than stand there with his useless shaking hands and watch them cover another body.

He did none of those things. Just stood. Breathed. Counted heartbeats.

Tiffani kept writing.

## Chapter Seven: Tiffani's Calculation

Four Tides after the Piercing, Tiffani made her mistake.

Crown officials posted shelter locations on a board outside the med station. She read the list, then cross‑referenced each location against her pre‑Piercing structural data. Three shelters marked safe sat on foundations she knew were compromised.

She limped to the posting board. Found the official who had put up the notices.

"These locations are wrong," she said.

The official looked at her leg, at the bloodstained wrappings. "You need to be resting."

"I need you to remove three shelters from the safe list."

"On what authority?"

"On the authority of structural analysis." She opened her logbook. "I have pre‑Piercing stress measurements. These buildings were already at or above threshold. They will not hold under aftershock load."

The official checked his slate. "These buildings passed last Turn's inspection."

"Last Turn's inspection didn't account for harmonic changes in the grid." "I documented dual‑frequency resonance two Tides before the Piercing. That indicates subsurface stress propagation. These shelters sit on compromised foundation."

"Do you have certification to make structural assessments?" he asked.

"I have data."

"That's not the same thing."

Tiffani held up the logbook so he could see. Calculations. Measurement sequences. Projection models.

"This building." She tapped a line on the posting. "Terrace Six administrative block. Foundation stress at one hundred twelve percent of safe threshold before the Piercing. Under aftershocks, it should already be failing. Anyone sheltering there is in direct collapse risk."

The official scanned the numbers. "These could be wrong."

"They're not."

"How do you know?"

"I measured them twice with calibrated instruments."

"You're one person with one notebook." "The inspection guild certified these buildings as safe."

"The inspection guild didn't measure harmonic shifts."

"Because harmonic shifts don't matter for structural integrity."

"They do now."

He closed his slate. "I can't strike official shelters on the word of a single observatory specialist." "People need somewhere to go. These buildings are on the safe list. They stay."

He walked away.

Tiffani stood there, jaw locked. The logbook weighed the same. The count had changed.

Something needled at her. She looked back down at the page. Terrace Six showed extreme stress. Terrace Eight—the shelter she had passed on the way here—logged lower values. Still elevated. Maybe acceptable if aftershocks stayed within current range.

She closed the logbook halfway.

On the next aftershock cycle, people would move. With the official notices fixed, the only variable she could change was direction.

She took position near the board. When families approached to read the shelters list, she pulled them aside.

"Terrace Eight shelter is more stable than Terrace Six," she told them. "Foundations are better. Go there instead."

Some listened. Some didn't. Enough did to matter.

Jhace found her three hours later.

"You're routing people to specific shelters," he said.

"Correct," she said. "Based on structural analysis."

"What if your analysis is wrong?"

"It's not."

"Junction Seven wasn't supposed to fail either." "The engineering report said acceptable parameters. Twenty‑three names ended on a plaque."

Tiffani's fingers tightened around the logbook. "That was different."

"How?"

"I have better data now." "Pre‑Piercing measurements. Harmonic tracking. Stress analysis the inspection guild never did."

"But you're still one person making projections." "If you're wrong—"

The aftershock hit.

Magnitude high enough to rattle the med station cots. Eight heartbeats of sustained shaking.

Tiffani counted. Logged the duration in her head out of habit.

Then the screaming started.

Not from Terrace Six. From Terrace Eight.

The shelter she had called safer. The one she had directed people toward.

The facade had failed.

Jhace was already moving. Tiffani followed as fast as her damaged leg allowed.

They reached the collapse zone. Smoke and dust. Copper‑gold tint in the air. Support columns broken. Roof slab down. Rescue crews pulling bodies free.

Twelve confirmed dead within the first hour. More trapped.

A Crown official pushed through the crowd. The same one from the posting board.

"You told people this building was safer," he said.

"My calculations—" she started.

"Your calculations killed twelve people. Maybe more."

"Terrace Six would have killed more."

"Terrace Six is still standing."

Tiffani opened the logbook with hands that did not feel like hers. The Terrace Six stress readings were clear. The building should have failed before Eight. It hadn't.

Her model had predicted the order of failure wrong.

The official took the logbook from her grip. "You're done making recommendations. Shelter guidance is Crown authority only from now on."

He walked away with her work in his hands.

Tiffani stayed at the edge of the rubble. Watched rescue crews move through broken stone. Every body they carried out passed through the space where her certainty had been.

Jhace came to stand beside her. His hands shook in the air between them.

"Twelve," he said.

"I logged the count," she said.

"You were trying to help."

"So were you," she said. "When you tried to teach manifestation. Your attempt killed her. My attempt killed them."

"Yes."

They watched the rubble. Two people who had used method to prevent casualties and produced different casualties instead.

"We need better data," Tiffani said.

"We need to stop pretending we understand this," Jhace said.

"If we don't try to understand, more people die."

"If we try to understand and are wrong, more people die."

"So what do we do?" she asked.

Jhace watched the rescue crews for a long moment. "Document. Observe. Stop making predictive calls until the model matches reality more often than not."

Tiffani reached for a fresh page in the logbook the official hadn't taken—the secondary one she kept in her case. Her hand shook as she wrote: Shelter miscalculation. Casualties: twelve confirmed. Cause: incomplete structural model and premature application. Recommendation: suspend predictive shelter assessments until data set expands.

She showed it to Jhace.

He read. Nodded once. "That's honest."

"That's necessary," she said.

"They won't listen."

"They will, after enough people die from ignoring accurate data."

"That's a grim calculation."

"That's the only calculation that moves institutions," Tiffani said.

## Chapter Eight: Fragmentation

One Anneal after the Piercing, Junction Fourteen failed in a way that didn't match any documented collapse pattern.

Jhace heard about it from a guild runner. The pressure valves had reversed. Steam flowing backward through the system. Against physics. Against engineering. The gauge needles spun counterclockwise.

A maintenance worker tried to shut it down. The valve wheel turned under his hands. Opened further instead of closing. He pulled harder. The wheel accelerated. Spun so fast it stripped skin from his palms when he finally let go.

The junction sealed itself twenty minutes later. No human intervention. The valves closed in sequence. Pressure normalized. The gauges returned to standard readings.

The worker's hands required medical attention. The junction showed no damage.

Jhace logged it: grid behavior—autonomous valve control. Personnel injury: friction burns, both palms. Cause: infrastructure non-compliance with operator input.

He added: The grid is no longer responding to human intervention.

Tiffani read his entry when he showed her. "You're saying it has intent."

"I'm saying it acted without us," Jhace said. "Whether that's intent or malfunction doesn't change the outcome."

"It changes what we prepare for."

"Does it?" He looked at his shaking hands. "Either way, the grid is something we can't control anymore."

She wrote her own note: Infrastructure autonomy confirmed. Implications: unknown.

One Anneal after the Piercing, three Crown factions arrived at the med station within the same Tide.

First came the Religious sect. Elder Vrasten. Copper bands around both wrists. Scroll cases sealed against moisture. He walked the aisles between cots, looking at manifestors like they were scripture made visible.

Second came the economic guild. Representative Chelm. Clipboard. Guild seal pinned sharp at his collar. He asked manifestors what crafts they practiced, what guild marks they carried, whether they had "authorization to alter material properties."

Third came the military. Commander Tallec. Armored escort. She moved through the station with an aide taking down names, manifestation types, locations, and witness statements about "unregulated force deployment in civilian zones."

None of them spoke to each other. None coordinated. Each operated from a different theory about what manifestation was.

Tiffani watched from her cot. She documented all three approaches in the margin of her logbook. Wrote: Institutional fragmentation—no unified response. Three competing frameworks: divine, economic, military.

Near the entrance, Jhace stood where he could see the flow of bodies and uniforms. The girl Corvan had healed—the one who had cost Corvan ten Turns of her life—approached him.

"They're trying to take us," she said. "The military. They say we're weapons. That we have to be controlled."

"Are you a weapon?" Jhace asked.

"I don't know," she said. "I just want to find my family."

Commander Tallec reached them.

"You," she said to the girl. "Manifestor. Name and capability."

The girl froze. Jhace stepped half a stride forward, his useless hands shaking between him and the commander.

"She hasn't broken any grid law," Jhace said.

"Manifestation is unregulated capability," Tallec replied. "Until classified, all manifestors report for evaluation."

"Evaluation for what?" Jhace asked.

"Threat assessment." "We don't know what you people can do. That uncertainty is risk."

"We held up walls," Jhace said. "Pulled people from rubble. Saved lives."

"You also killed that woman trying to learn manifestation," Tallec said. "I have three witness reports. You encouraged her. She died."

Jhace's jaw tightened. "That was my mistake. Not hers."

"Exactly." "You don't understand what you're doing. Ignorant force is the most dangerous kind."

Elder Vrasten stepped in, copper bands catching lamplight.

"Weapons?" he said. "These are the Marked. The Piercing unveiled them. They should be under temple guidance, not military custody."

Representative Chelm arrived on the other side.

"They should be registered with the guild," he said. "Manifestation is material manipulation. That's craft. That's jurisdiction. Unauthorized manipulation of matter destabilizes markets."

The three of them—Tallec, Vrasten, Chelm—formed a triangle around Jhace and the girl. They argued over who had the right to claim her. Over whether she was threat, miracle, or asset.

At no point did anyone ask her what she wanted.

Around them, medics tried to work. The argument pulled eyes. Pulled focus. Patients were left waiting while factions fought over names on lists.

Tiffani slid off her cot, pain spiking through her stitched leg. She limped closer, keeping out of the direct line of fire but close enough to hear.

"This is going to get worse," she said quietly to Jhace when she reached him.

"I know," he said.

"They're splintering," she said. "No unified policy. Each faction will try to control manifestors in its own way. Different requirements. Conflicting orders."

"What do we do?" he asked.

Something in her attention pulled toward the back of the station. Corvan sat there with her daughter, hands aged decades by one act of healing.

"We track what actually happens," Tiffani said. "Under each faction's control. Who lives. Who dies. What costs manifestors pay."

"You want to turn manifestors into a study," Jhace said.

"I want to stop improvising," she said. "Right now policy is theory. We counter theory with evidence. If we can't stop them from taking people, we can at least measure what they do."

"They won't listen if the data contradicts their stories," Jhace said.

"They will when their stories produce enough casualties that denial breaks," she said.

"Cold," he said.

"Method," she answered.

The argument in front of them escalated. Voices sharpened. More uniforms pressed in behind each leader.

Commander Tallec unfastened the strap on her sidearm. Didn't draw, but made the option visible. Assertion of authority.

Elder Vrasten lifted both copper‑banded arms in a ritual gesture.

Representative Chelm called for guild enforcers from the corridor.

The med station had become a pressure test. Not of stone and metal. Of institutions. Of how they behaved when power appeared where it had not existed before.

Tiffani opened a fresh section in her logbook. Titled it: Institutional response patterns—comparative analysis. She began noting everything: recruitment language, threats used, incentives offered, constraints imposed.

She would follow Corvan if the temple took her. Follow the girl if the military seized her. Follow any manifestor the guild pulled into contract. Track outcomes under each framework.

It was not comfort. Not protection. Not justice.

It was a record. A way to force institutions, later, to look at what they had done.

Method was all she had. So she held to it.

## Chapter Nine: Fourth Threshold

Ten Tides after the Piercing, another collapse hit Saint Merro. Terrace Four this time. Residential block. Fifty people trapped by initial reports.

Jhace stood at the edge of the collapse zone. His arms shook in visible waves. Every breath carried a small hitch as his muscles misfired.

First manifestation had taken his hands.
Second had pulled the tremor into wrists and forearms.
Third had reached shoulders and upper back.

Now the shake owned everything above his ribs. He could not hold tools. Could not sign his own name without the letters tangling.

But when he closed his eyes and imagined his hands on stone, he could still feel the dust answer.

Tiffani found him there, near a buckled support column.

"You're thinking about it," she said.

"Fifty people," he said.

"Fourth manifestation pushes you past survivable cost." "You've seen the pattern."

"I know the numbers," he said. "But fifty people die if I stand here and do nothing."

"You're not responsible for every death you could, in theory, prevent," she said.

"Aren't I?" he asked. "I have capability. I know the trade. If I choose not to act, those deaths are a cost I accept. Same as if I act and pay with my body."

Tiffani had no immediate counter. She had run the same equation from the other side, every time she chose to intervene with data.

"Corvan chose," Jhace said. "She knew manifestation would cost her. Paid a decade to keep her daughter breathing. That trade cleared in her mind."

"Her death was her choice," Tiffani said. "This is yours."

"Correct," he said. "And I'm choosing."

He looked down at his trembling frame. "I can't work junctions. Can't hold a collar key. My function is gone. But I can still manifest once more. Turn what's left into saved lives."

"You're rationalizing self‑destruction," she said.

"I'm calculating expected value," he said. "One manifestor whose effective function is already zero versus fifty trapped. If I succeed, the city keeps fifty people who can still work, still hold. Math is clear."

"Math doesn't measure what your loss does to the people who know you," she said.

"Like who?" he asked.

"Like me," she said.

He blinked.

"You barely know me," he said.

"I know you document even when no one listens," she said. "I know you refuse shortcuts on the grid. I know you carry cost openly instead of pretending it isn't there. That matters."

"You can document without me," he said.

"Not the same way," she said. "You're ground truth. First‑hand account of what this does to a body and mind. If you die, I lose that vantage point."

"So I'm data," he said.

"You're also the only person who doesn't call me cold when I pick the necessary line instead of the comforting one," she said.

The block groaned. Stone shifting deeper inside the collapse. Someone screamed from under a slab.

Jhace looked toward the sound.

"People are dying in there now," he said.

"I know," she said.

"I can change that," he said.

"At the cost of your life," she said.

"Or at the cost of everything but," he said. "Either way, the cost is mine to pay."

"It's not acceptable," she said.

"It's not your choice," he answered.

They held there, between theory and stone.

"If you do this," Tiffani said quietly, "I document it. Every second. Every shift in your vitals. Every cost. Your decision becomes part of the record that shapes the next one."

"Good," he said.

"And I use that record to force institutions to face what they ask of manifestors," she said. "So maybe the next person doesn't have to stand at a collapse edge alone and do this calculus."

"Even better," he said.

He walked—more a stagger now—toward the largest intact slab of the ruined block. Rescue crews had marked it as primary load. If it slipped, it would crush the voids where voices still called.

Tiffani followed, logbook open, pen ready. Witness and instrument at once.

A child's voice came from under the slab. Young. Maybe five Turns. Singing. Not screaming. Just singing the counting rhyme they taught in Crown schools.

"One stride, two stride, three stride, four—"

The song stopped mid-count.

Jhace pressed his forehead against the stone. His arms hung useless; the tremor made them worse than dead weight.

He didn't move until the count ended.

"I need this to hold," he whispered. "I need them free. I need this to count."

The copper‑gold light answered.

It did not confine itself to his hands this time. It rose from his chest, from his back, from every place the cost had already marked. It ran into the stone. Into the fracture lines.

The rubble settled. Stopped shifting. The groaning ceased.

Tiffani logged onset time, visible intensity, stabilization markers. Wrote through the shaking in her own hand.

Rescue crews moved in. They cut, lifted, pulled. Voices came out of voids as bodies. Some breathing. Some not.

Eight minutes passed. Jhace did not move from the stone. His whole body shook in increasing amplitude. Sweat ran copper‑streaked down his neck.

By the time crews pulled the last living person clear, they had extracted forty‑three survivors. Seven had died in the initial failure.

"Release," Tiffani said softly. "They're clear."

Jhace let go.

The light left faster than it had come. He dropped with it.

He hit the stone wrong. Limbs at angles that meant nothing now because they did not respond.

Medics were there in heartbeats. Tiffani recorded vitals while they worked.

Heart rate: thirty beats per minute.
Respiration: four breaths per minute.
Body temperature: falling toward hypothermic range despite ambient heat.

They dragged him onto a stretcher. Stabilized his neck. Ran copper‑gold dust tests they knew would tell them nothing.

He did not die. The numbers crawled back from the edge. Heart rate strengthened. Breathing deepened.

The cost came another way.

By the time they had him in long‑term care, his legs did not respond to pinpricks. Feet, calves, thighs—no reaction. Below the waist, his body might as well belong to someone else.

The tremor in his upper body had not eased. Combining both, the medics wrote the word they did not want to say aloud: quadriplegic.

Tiffani stood at the foot of his cot while they finished their assessment. She logged everything. Sensation maps. Range‑of‑motion tests. Failed movement attempts.

Later, when they left, she moved to his side.

"I'm still alive," he said. His voice came out thin but steady.

"Yes," she said.

"Forty‑three people are alive too," he said. "Not bad for someone who was supposed to die on the fourth."

"You can't move," she said.

"I'd noticed," he said.

"This may be worse than dying," she said.

"Is it?" he asked. "I can still think. Still speak. Still tell you what this feels like from the inside. You don't get that from the ones who stopped breathing."

She wrote his words down almost verbatim. Not because they were pretty. Because they were ground truth.

The fourth threshold had not killed him. It had taken everything else.

She underlined the final line of the entry: Fourth manifestation—non‑fatal outcome possible. Cost: total loss of mobility with preserved cognition. Assessment: worse than death for some; essential data point for all.

That was the trade now. That was what the dust had bought and sold.

## Chapter Ten: Institutional Support

Half a Tide after the fourth manifestation, the Crown Council convened an emergency session. All three factions attended: military, religious, economic, plus central administrators.

They summoned Tiffani with an armed escort. Not optional. She limped into the chamber with her primary logbook and supplementary calculations—every manifestation event, cost, and casualty count she had.

The council sat elevated, looking down. Architecture built for intimidation, not acoustics.

"You've been documenting manifestation events," Commander Tallec said.

"Correct."

"Without authorization."

"I am an observatory specialist. Documentation is my function. No authorization required."

"During a crisis, all research requires military clearance."

"Then file for retroactive clearance. The data stands regardless."

Elder Vrasten leaned forward, copper bands catching light.

"These manifestations are divine gifts," he said. "Your writing treats them as medical phenomena. That is sacrilege."

"My documentation treats them as observable reality," Tiffani said. "Whether that reality is divine is outside my scope."

The sky tore. He looked up. Couldn't help it. Vertical split in the air itself. Light poured through. Copper-gold. Bright enough to hurt.
    
The smell arrived with the light. Ozone. Burnt metal. Something else underneath—sweet and wrong, like fruit rotting in copper heat.

He raised his hand to shield his eyes. The light came through his fingers anyway.

The grid-hum died. Wrong silence followed. His ears rang. Pressure built in his skull. He swallowed. Didn't help.

Then the subsonic wave hit. Not sound. Pressure. Physical force in the air. Jhace felt it compress his chest. He exhaled hard. Couldn't get air back in. His lungs fought the pressure. Three heartbeats. Four.

Then it released.

He gasped. Copper taste flooded his mouth. Actual texture to it. Gritty. He spit. The saliva came out stained. Copper-gold tint.

Dust rose from the street. Streams of copper-gold particulate coming up through cracks. Through terrace seams. Through gaps in building facades. Hundreds of streams. Thousands.

The streams merged. Made columns. The columns spiraled.

Jhace watched one rise past his face. The particulate moved against the wind. Moved toward the breach in the sky.

A building collapsed two terraces Downslope. The sound arrived three heartbeats later. Stone-on-stone. Sustained. Wrong harmonics in the resonance. Jhace's molars ached.

Chloen's voice cut through the noise. "Jhace!"

He turned. Found her twenty strides away. She stood at the entrance to a residential block. The facade leaned. Active tilt. Maybe fifteen degrees already.

"The building's coming down!" she shouted.

Jhace could see that. Could see the support wall separating from the terrace face. Could see the hairline fractures spreading through the load-bearing columns.

People were still inside. He could hear them. Voices calling for help. Movement sounds. Someone coughing in the deep interior.

Chloen ran toward the entrance. Jhace ran after her.

"We need to get them out," she said.

The building shuddered. The tilt increased. Twenty degrees. Dust poured from the upper windows. Copper-gold. Dense enough to obscure the interior.

Jhace grabbed Chloen's shoulder. "It's not stable."

"There are children inside."

He looked at the facade. Calculated the collapse vector. Maybe five minutes before total failure. Maybe less.

"Get the ground floor," he said. "I'll take the second terrace apartments."

They split. Chloen went left. Jhace went right. Found the interior stairs. They tilted with the building. He climbed them anyway. Hand on the wall for balance.

The copper-gold dust hung thick. He pulled his shirt over his mouth. Didn't help much. The particulate was too fine. It got through the fabric. Coated his throat. Made him cough.

First apartment: empty.

Second apartment: elderly couple. They moved slow. Jhace got them to the stairs. Pointed them down.

Third apartment: a woman with two children. Young. Maybe six and eight Turns old. They clung to her. Wouldn't let go.

"The building's failing," Jhace said. "You need to move now."

She nodded. Got the children moving. Jhace followed them to the stairs.

The building lurched. Dropped a full pace. Jhace grabbed the doorframe. Held on.

The woman fell. One child caught themselves on the wall. The other one didn't.

The younger child slid toward the stairwell. The tilt had increased. Thirty degrees now. Gravity pulled everything Downslope.

Jhace lunged. Caught the child's arm. Pulled them back.

The woman grabbed both children. Started down the stairs.

Jhace checked the next apartment. Door jammed. He kicked it. The frame splintered but held. He kicked again. Harder. The door broke inward.

Chloen inside. Trapped under a fallen beam.

She'd been trying to reach a girl pinned in the corner. Maybe ten Turns old. The beam had caught Chloen across the shoulders when it fell.

"Can you move?" Jhace asked.

"No. Too heavy."

The girl in the corner wasn't moving. Jhace checked her pulse. Still alive. Unconscious. Blood on her forehead.

He assessed the beam. Solid iron. Load-bearing. Maybe two hundred kilograms. He couldn't lift that. Not alone.

The building shuddered again. The tilt hit forty degrees. The beam shifted. Pressed harder on Chloen.

She screamed.

"I'm stuck," she said. "Get the girl. Leave me."

Jhace looked at the beam. Looked at Chloen. Looked at the girl.

Five minutes had passed. Maybe less. The building had maybe two minutes left before total collapse.

He couldn't carry both of them. Not down tilted stairs. Not in time.

The copper-gold dust swirled around the beam. Dense concentration. It moved wrong. Against physics. The particulate spiraled. Made patterns.

Jhace put his hands on the beam.

The copper-gold dust touched his skin. Warm. Not hot. Just warm. Metal that held heat.

He thought: Need this beam to move. Need it light enough to lift. Need Chloen free.

The dust brightened. Light spread from his hands. Copper-gold. Same color as the dust. Same warmth.

It flowed into the beam. Into the iron.

The metal responded. Started to shift. Not shape. Some other property.

The beam weighed less.

Jhace didn't understand how he knew that. But he did. The beam's mass was changing. Reducing.

The iron stayed solid. Stayed structural. But the weight was leaving.

He lifted. The beam rose. Smooth. Easy. Like it weighed twenty kilograms instead of two hundred.

Chloen scrambled free. Grabbed the unconscious girl. Started for the stairs.

Jhace held the beam. His hands burned. Not pain. Not yet. But heat building. Pressure building. Something in his muscles starting to strain in a way that didn't match the physical effort.

He lowered the beam. Set it down gentle.

Pulled his hands back.

The copper-gold light faded. The beam's weight returned. It crashed into the floor. Broke through. Fell to the apartment below.

Jhace looked at his hands. They shook.

Not fatigue tremor. Something else. His fingers moved wrong. Micro-spasms in the small muscles.

He tried to make a fist. The fingers didn't fully close. Coordination compromised.

The building tilted steeper. Forty-five degrees. Jhace ran for the stairs.

The tremor spread up his wrists. Into his forearms. Each step harder to control than the last.

He found Chloen at the building entrance. She'd gotten the girl outside. Set her down on stable ground. Someone else had already started tending to her.

"Your hands," Chloen said.

Jhace looked down. The tremor was visible. Obvious. His whole hand shaking. Constant. No rhythm to it.

"I'm fine," he said.

The building collapsed behind them. Total structural failure. Three heartbeats from top to bottom. The sound hit like a physical blow.

Dust cloud spread across the terrace. Copper-gold particulate mixing with stone dust. The cloud swallowed everything.

Jhace and Chloen moved away from the collapse zone. Found clear air two terraces Crownward.

His hands still shook.

The breach in the sky spread wider. Light fell in sheets. The copper-gold dust rose to meet it. Columns of particulate connecting sky to ground.

Saint Merro wrapped in threads of light.

The subsonic pressure hit again. Harder. Jhace's knees buckled. He caught himself against a wall. Held on.

The pressure built. Built. Then released.

He vomited. Copper-gold saliva. Actual metallic taste. His stomach clenched. Nothing left to bring up. He dry-heaved twice. Stopped.

Chloen knelt beside him. "What did you do in there?"

"Lifted the beam."

"That's not what I meant."

He looked at his hands. The tremor hadn't stopped. Wouldn't stop. He could feel it now. Something changed. Permanently. The fine motor control was gone. The precision that let him work junction collars. That let him read torque by feel. Gone.

"I don't know what I did," he said.

The sky split wider.

And deeper below, in the Underface, the grid-hum screamed.

## Chapter Four: After the Shudder

The med station smelled like copper and burnt stone. Jhace sat on a supply crate. His hands still shook. Hadn't stopped in two hours.

A medic had checked him. Found no injury. No explanation for the tremor. Told him to rest. Gave him water. Moved on to people with visible wounds.

The station sat in mid-terrace. Temporary structure. Canvas walls. Insufficient light. Too many people.

Jhace counted forty-three injured. More coming in every few minutes.

Chloen sat across from him. Bandage around her ribs where the beam had caught her. She watched his hands.

"Still shaking," she said.

He nodded. Didn't try to hide it. No point.

"What happened in that room?"

"I lifted the beam."

"That beam weighed more than you do."

Jhace looked at his hands. The tremor ran constant. Small amplitude. High frequency. Like his muscles couldn't remember how to be still.

"The copper-gold dust. It changed something."

"Changed what?"

"The beam's weight. Or my strength. I don't know."

Chloen stood. "That doesn't make sense."

"I know."

A woman stumbled into the med station. Older. Maybe forty-five Turns. Hair streaked with white.

Jhace recognized her. Corvan. Metalworker from the Underface. They'd crossed paths on inspections. Professional acquaintance. Not friends.

She looked wrong. Way older than Jhace remembered. Her skin hung loose. Liver spots on her hands. Deep lines around her eyes. She moved like her joints hurt.

She saw Jhace. Stopped. Stared at his shaking hands.

"You too," she said.

"What?"

"You manifested."

Jhace stood. The tremor made it awkward. "I don't know what that means."

Corvan moved closer. Lowered her voice. "The copper-gold dust. The light from your hands. You changed something. Made it do what you needed instead of what physics says it should."

"How do you know that?"

"Because I did it too."

She held up her hands. They shook. Same pattern as Jhace's. Same high-frequency tremor. But her hands looked decades older. Thin skin. Prominent veins. Age spots.

"My daughter was dying," Corvan said. "Crush injuries. Internal bleeding. I put my hands on her wounds and thought: stop the bleeding. Close the damage. Fix her."

"Did it work?"

"She's alive. In the back section. Stable."

Corvan's voice stayed flat. Matter-of-fact.

"But look at me."

Jhace looked. Really looked. Corvan's hair had been dark brown three Tides ago when he'd seen her at Junction Fifteen. Now it was white. Her face had been smooth. Now it carried lines that belonged on someone twenty Turns older.

"What happened to you?" he asked.

"I paid the price. Healing isn't magic. It's transaction. I gave years of my life to repair her body. The copper-gold dust just made the exchange possible."

The tremor in Jhace's hands intensified.

"How many years?"

"Ten. Maybe more. I aged a decade in thirty heartbeats."

Chloen stood. "That's not possible."

"Jhace's hands say different."

Corvan looked at him. "What did you do?"

"Made a beam lighter. Lifted it off Chloen."

"And the cost?"

He raised his hands. Showed the tremor. "Fine motor control. Precision work. It's gone."

Corvan nodded. "The cost matches the action. You manipulated metal. Now your hands don't work right. I healed flesh. Now my body's aged."

She paused. "The dust doesn't give. It trades."

A medic approached. Told Corvan her daughter needed her. She left without another word.

Jhace sat back down. Stared at his hands. The tremor wouldn't stop.

He tried to make his fingers still through pure focus. Didn't work. The shake ran deeper than conscious control.

"You believe her?" Chloen asked.

"My hands believe her."

"There has to be another explanation."

"Like what?"

Chloen had no answer.

The med station entrance opened. Someone being carried in on a stretcher.

Jhace caught a glimpse. Woman. Leg injury. Blood-soaked bandage. She was conscious. Writing in a notebook while they moved her.

Something about the focus. The documentation instinct. It registered.

The medics set the stretcher down in the back section. Jhace stood. Moved closer. Got a better look.

He recognized her. The woman from the observatory. The one who'd been tracking Seven-Three.

Her leg was bad. Glass shard wound. Wrapped but still bleeding through. She'd need stitches. Maybe surgery.

But she kept writing. Kept logging data.

A medic tried to take her notebook. She pulled it away. Held it against her chest.

"I need to document this," she said.

Her voice carried. Clear. Precise.

"Exit event. Thermal bloom. Gravity shift. Personal status: mobile with compromise."

The medic tried again. "You need treatment."

"After I finish the entry."

She wrote for another thirty heartbeats. Then closed the notebook. Let the medic examine her leg.

Jhace watched. Recognized the instinct. The need to record. To document. To maintain accountability even when your body's failing.

He understood that.

The tremor in his hands had spread to his wrists. Maybe his forearms. He couldn't tell anymore. The shake felt like part of him now. Background noise. Constant.

He looked at the woman on the stretcher. Looked at Corvan sitting with her daughter. Looked at his own hands.

Three people. Three manifestations. Three costs.

The pattern was there. He just didn't know what it meant yet.

## Chapter Five: Where to Press

Tiffani's leg hurt. Not acute pain. Constant. Deep ache in the joint. The glass shard was out. Medic had stitched the wound. Wrapped it. Told her to stay off it.

She'd nodded. Waited for the medic to move to the next patient. Then stood up.

The leg held. Barely. She limped. Each step sent pain up through her knee. But she could move. That's what mattered.

Her documentation case sat on the cot beside her. She pulled it close. Opened the primary logbook. Found the last entry: Personal status: mobile with compromise.

She added: Treatment complete. Mobility confirmed. Resuming documentation.

The med station had filled. Maybe sixty people now. More arriving.

Something pulled at her attention. Not thought. Pressure. Direction.

She looked toward the entrance. The metalworker with shaking hands stood there. Watching. Cataloging. Same instinct she had.

She didn't know why she noticed him specifically. Just did.

Tiffani limped toward him. Each step measured. Her leg screamed. She ignored it.

"You're the one from the residential collapse," she said.

He looked at her. "You're the one from the observatory."

"Correct." She shifted weight. Tested the joint. It held. "You manifested during the rescue."

His fingers slipped on the slate. "How do you know that term?"

"The older metalworker. Corvan. She used it. I heard."

"You manifest too?"

"No. I documented the event. Observatory platform. Full visual on the sky breach."

She pulled her logbook out. Showed him the entry. "Exit event confirmed. Thermal bloom massive. Gravity shift detected."

He read the entry. His hands shook while he held the page. The tremor made the text wobble.

"You measured the breach?"

"Estimated. Two hundred strides vertical. Expanding horizontal at approximately twenty strides per minute during initial phase."

"That's precise."

"That's necessary."

She took the logbook back. "Your hands. The tremor. That's the cost of manifestation?"

"According to Corvan."

"What did you do?"

"Made a beam lighter. Lifted it."

Tiffani wrote: Manifestation type—mass manipulation. Cost—motor control compromise.

She showed him the entry. "This matches Corvan's pattern. Healing manifestation. Life-force cost. Aging."

"You're cataloging this."

"Someone has to."

A commotion at the entrance. Someone carried in on a stretcher. Unconscious. Young man. Maybe twenty Turns. His hands glowed. Faint copper-gold light. Visible even in the med station lamps.

The medics set him down. Checked his vitals. One of them shouted for the senior medic.

Tiffani moved closer. The metalworker followed.

They stood at the edge of the treatment area. Watched.

The young man's breathing was shallow. Rapid. His skin felt cold when the medic checked. But his hands stayed warm. The glow pulsed. Matched his heartbeat.

"What happened to him?" the senior medic asked.

The person who'd brought him answered. "Found him in a collapse zone. He was holding up a wall. Actually holding it. Hands on the stone. The wall wasn't falling. Then he passed out. The wall came down after."

The senior medic examined the glowing hands. "How long was he holding it?"

"Ten minutes. Maybe more."

Tiffani wrote: Prolonged manifestation = sustained cost accrual.

The young man convulsed. Brief. His back arched. Then he went still. Too still.

The medics worked. Tried to revive him. Called for equipment. Tried again.

He didn't come back.

The senior medic called time. Wrote the death record.

The copper-gold glow faded from the young man's hands. Left them looking normal. Unmarked.

Tiffani stood frozen. Watched them cover the body.

Her pen stayed hovering over the page. She needed to write this. Document it. But the words wouldn't come.

The metalworker's voice came quiet. "The cost can be fatal."

She wrote: Manifestation death confirmed. Cause: sustained reality override beyond physiological tolerance.

Her hand shook while she wrote. Not tremor. Just shock. She forced the letters steady. Finished the entry. Closed the logbook.

"We need to warn people," she said.

"Warn them about what? Not to save people?"

"Warn them about the limits. About sustained use. About—"

She stopped. Looked at the covered body. "About the price."

They stood in silence. The med station noise continued around them. Wounded arriving. Medics working.

"You know what I realized?" the metalworker said finally.

"What?"

"We're doing the same thing. You document reality until institutions listen. I worked junctions until the grid broke. Both of us trying to prevent casualties through method."

"Both failing," Tiffani said.

"Both trying anyway."

She looked at him. Really looked. "I'm Tiffani. Observatory specialist. Structural analysis."

"Jhace. Grid inspector. Metalwork."

"We should coordinate documentation," she said.

"Agreed," Jhace said.

## Chapter Six: The Mistake

Three Tides after the Piercing, Jhace made his first mistake.

A Crown engineer arrived at the med station two hours before that. Older. Structural sect marks. He carried measuring equipment and a face that had stopped hoping.

He found Tiffani at her documentation station. Set a slate on her workspace without asking permission.

"Grid resonance analysis," he said. "Completed this morning."

Tiffani read the data. Foundation integrity compromised across Terraces Four through Eight. The dual-frequency harmonic shift was propagating through substrate stone. Load-bearing columns showing micro-fracture patterns that matched pre-collapse signatures from Junction Seven.

"Time frame?" she asked.

"Three Anneals," he said. "Maybe four if manifestors keep stabilizing active failures. After that, cascading collapse becomes self-sustaining. Terrace Four schools go first—substrate's already compromised. Then water mains. Then we lose the Underface. Mid-terrace follows. Everything Downslope of Crown."

"Confidence level?"

"Seventy percent. Could be slower. Could be faster. The grid's behavior doesn't match historical models anymore."

Tiffani logged the projection. "You've reported this to Crown administration?"

"They want confirmation from the Architecture sect before committing resources. The Architecture sect wants the Religious sect's approval because the grid is 'sacred infrastructure.' The Religious sect is waiting for a sign." He picked up his slate. "I'm telling you because you're the only person documenting what's actually happening instead of what people want to believe is happening."

He left.

Tiffani wrote: Structural collapse timeline—three Anneals estimated. Current mitigation: manifestor intervention only. Institutional response: pending theological approval.

She looked across the med station. Jhace sat near the entrance. Hands shaking. Already compromised from two manifestations. The cost ledger said a third would take more. A fourth would likely kill him.

Three Anneals. Fifty collapses projected. Hundreds dead if no intervention.

The numbers were clear. The numbers were always clear.

A young woman approached him at the med station. Maybe nineteen Turns. Burn scars on her forearms. Guild metalworker's marks.

"You made the beam lighter," she said.

Jhace nodded.

"Can you teach me?"

His stomach tightened. "I don't know how I did it."

"But you did it." "That means it can be learned."

"That's not—"

He stopped. Looked at his shaking hands. "It cost me. Look."

She looked. "I see. But people are dying in collapses. If we could stabilize structures. Save them. The cost would be worth it."

"You don't know that."

"Don't I?" She held up her scarred arms. "I work junction collars. Hot metal. Steam pressure. I carry scars every day. This is just another scar."

Jhace wanted to refuse. Walk away. But she was right. People were dying. If manifestation could be controlled. Taught. Used safely.

The cost might be acceptable.

"I'll try," he said. "But I can't promise anything."

They moved to a quieter section. Found a broken support beam. Small. Maybe twenty kilograms. Manageable weight.

Tiffani appeared. Limping. "What are you doing?"

"Teaching," Jhace said.

Her leg pain spiked. "You don't know how manifestation works."

"I know it saved Chloen. If others can learn it—"

"You're guessing," she said. "Experimenting on people."

"She volunteered."

"That doesn't make it safe."

The young woman interrupted. "I want to try. My choice."

Tiffani pulled out her logbook. "Then I'm documenting this."

Jhace turned back to the woman. "When I manifested, I needed the beam to move. Needed it light enough to lift. Needed the person trapped to be free."

"Desperate need," Tiffani said.

"Correct," Jhace said.

"And the copper‑gold dust?" the woman asked.

Jhace looked around. The particulate still hung in the air. Thinner now, but present. He scooped some from a window ledge. Held it in his palm.

"I touched it. It touched the beam. The light came after."

The woman copied him. Scooped dust from the ledge. Held it.

"Now what?"

"Put your hands on the beam. Think about making it lighter."

She did. Pressed her palms to the metal. Closed her eyes. Concentrated.

Nothing happened.

She tried again. Harder focus. Visible strain in her face.

Still nothing.

"I don't feel anything," she said.

"Maybe the need has to be real," Jhace said. "Not practice."

"So I need to be in danger?"

His grip failed on the broken beam. This was wrong. Stupid. Reckless.

He started to tell her to stop.

The woman lifted a heavier beam. Sixty kilograms. Held it overhead. Arms shaking with the weight.

"This is danger," she said. "This could crush me if I drop it."

"That's not the same—"

She closed her eyes. Focused.

The copper‑gold dust on her hands started to glow.

Jhace's pulse spiked. "Wait. You need to—"

The light intensified. Spread into the beam. The woman gasped.

"I feel it," she said. "I feel the weight changing."

"Lower the beam," Jhace said. "Slowly."

She didn't. Kept pushing. Kept trying to make it lighter.

The glow brightened. Her hands shook. Not from weight. From something else. Cost starting to pull through her.

"Stop," Jhace said. "You need to stop now."

The woman screamed. Not pain. Something worse. The sound tore out of her throat.

The beam crashed down. Missed her by a hand's width.

She fell. Convulsed. Her whole body seizing.

Jhace grabbed her. Tried to hold her steady. The convulsions intensified. Blood came from her nose. Her eyes rolled back.

Medics ran over. Pushed Jhace aside. Worked on the woman. Tried to stabilize her.

Her convulsions stopped. She went limp. Too limp.

The senior medic checked her pulse. Checked her breathing.

Called time.

Dead.

Jhace stood frozen. Stared at the body. At his shaking hands. At the copper‑gold dust still dull on her palms.

He had told her to try. He had helped her.

He had killed her.

Tiffani appeared beside him. Limping. Her leg barely holding. She looked at the body. Looked at Jhace.

"What happened?" she asked.

"I tried to teach her," he said. "She manifested. It killed her."

Tiffani's expression didn't change. She pulled out her logbook. Wrote: manifestation attempt under instruction = fatal. Added: teaching manifestation not viable. Desperate need cannot be simulated.

"You're documenting this," Jhace said. Voice flat.

"Yes," she said.

"She's dead because I tried to help."

"She's dead because manifestation doesn't work that way," Tiffani said. "Now we know."

Jhace's hands shook harder. "That's all this is to you? Data?"

"That's what keeps more people from dying," she said. "Data. Understanding. Method."

She wrote another line. "Your guilt doesn't help anyone. Your documentation might."

Jhace wanted to hit her. Wanted to scream. Wanted to do something other than stand there with his useless shaking hands and watch them cover another body.

He did none of those things. Just stood. Breathed. Counted heartbeats.

Tiffani kept writing.

## Chapter Seven: Tiffani's Calculation

Four Tides after the Piercing, Tiffani made her mistake.

Crown officials posted shelter locations on a board outside the med station. She read the list, then cross‑referenced each location against her pre‑Piercing structural data. Three shelters marked safe sat on foundations she knew were compromised.

She limped to the posting board. Found the official who had put up the notices.

"These locations are wrong," she said.

The official looked at her leg, at the bloodstained wrappings. "You need to be resting."

"I need you to remove three shelters from the safe list."

"On what authority?"

"On the authority of structural analysis." She opened her logbook. "I have pre‑Piercing stress measurements. These buildings were already at or above threshold. They will not hold under aftershock load."

The official checked his slate. "These buildings passed last Turn's inspection."

"Last Turn's inspection didn't account for harmonic changes in the grid." "I documented dual‑frequency resonance two Tides before the Piercing. That indicates subsurface stress propagation. These shelters sit on compromised foundation."

"Do you have certification to make structural assessments?" he asked.

"I have data."

"That's not the same thing."

Tiffani held up the logbook so he could see. Calculations. Measurement sequences. Projection models.

"This building." She tapped a line on the posting. "Terrace Six administrative block. Foundation stress at one hundred twelve percent of safe threshold before the Piercing. Under aftershocks, it should already be failing. Anyone sheltering there is in direct collapse risk."

The official scanned the numbers. "These could be wrong."

"They're not."

"How do you know?"

"I measured them twice with calibrated instruments."

"You're one person with one notebook." "The inspection guild certified these buildings as safe."

"The inspection guild didn't measure harmonic shifts."

"Because harmonic shifts don't matter for structural integrity."

"They do now."

He closed his slate. "I can't strike official shelters on the word of a single observatory specialist." "People need somewhere to go. These buildings are on the safe list. They stay."

He walked away.

Tiffani stood there, jaw locked. The logbook weighed the same. The count had changed.

Something needled at her. She looked back down at the page. Terrace Six showed extreme stress. Terrace Eight—the shelter she had passed on the way here—logged lower values. Still elevated. Maybe acceptable if aftershocks stayed within current range.

She closed the logbook halfway.

On the next aftershock cycle, people would move. With the official notices fixed, the only variable she could change was direction.

She took position near the board. When families approached to read the shelters list, she pulled them aside.

"Terrace Eight shelter is more stable than Terrace Six," she told them. "Foundations are better. Go there instead."

Some listened. Some didn't. Enough did to matter.

Jhace found her three hours later.

"You're routing people to specific shelters," he said.

"Correct," she said. "Based on structural analysis."

"What if your analysis is wrong?"

"It's not."

"Junction Seven wasn't supposed to fail either." "The engineering report said acceptable parameters. Twenty‑three names ended on a plaque."

Tiffani's fingers tightened around the logbook. "That was different."

"How?"

"I have better data now." "Pre‑Piercing measurements. Harmonic tracking. Stress analysis the inspection guild never did."

"But you're still one person making projections." "If you're wrong—"

The aftershock hit.

Magnitude high enough to rattle the med station cots. Eight heartbeats of sustained shaking.

Tiffani counted. Logged the duration in her head out of habit.

Then the screaming started.

Not from Terrace Six. From Terrace Eight.

The shelter she had called safer. The one she had directed people toward.

The facade had failed.

Jhace was already moving. Tiffani followed as fast as her damaged leg allowed.

They reached the collapse zone. Smoke and dust. Copper‑gold tint in the air. Support columns broken. Roof slab down. Rescue crews pulling bodies free.

Twelve confirmed dead within the first hour. More trapped.

A Crown official pushed through the crowd. The same one from the posting board.

"You told people this building was safer," he said.

"My calculations—" she started.

"Your calculations killed twelve people. Maybe more."

"Terrace Six would have killed more."

"Terrace Six is still standing."

Tiffani opened the logbook with hands that did not feel like hers. The Terrace Six stress readings were clear. The building should have failed before Eight. It hadn't.

Her model had predicted the order of failure wrong.

The official took the logbook from her grip. "You're done making recommendations. Shelter guidance is Crown authority only from now on."

He walked away with her work in his hands.

Tiffani stayed at the edge of the rubble. Watched rescue crews move through broken stone. Every body they carried out passed through the space where her certainty had been.

Jhace came to stand beside her. His hands shook in the air between them.

"Twelve," he said.

"I logged the count," she said.

"You were trying to help."

"So were you," she said. "When you tried to teach manifestation. Your attempt killed her. My attempt killed them."

"Yes."

They watched the rubble. Two people who had used method to prevent casualties and produced different casualties instead.

"We need better data," Tiffani said.

"We need to stop pretending we understand this," Jhace said.

"If we don't try to understand, more people die."

"If we try to understand and are wrong, more people die."

"So what do we do?" she asked.

Jhace watched the rescue crews for a long moment. "Document. Observe. Stop making predictive calls until the model matches reality more often than not."

## Chapter Eight: Fragmentation

One Anneal after the Piercing, Junction Fourteen failed in a way that didn't match any documented collapse pattern.

Jhace heard about it from a guild runner. The pressure valves had reversed. Steam flowing backward through the system. Against physics. Against engineering. The gauge needles spun counterclockwise.

A maintenance worker tried to shut it down. The valve wheel turned under his hands. Opened further instead of closing. He pulled harder. The wheel accelerated. Spun so fast it stripped skin from his palms when he finally let go.

The junction sealed itself twenty minutes later. No human intervention. The valves closed in sequence. Pressure normalized. The gauges returned to standard readings.

The worker's hands required medical attention. The junction showed no damage. When he arrived at the med station, copper-gold grit lined his teeth.

Jhace logged it: grid behavior—autonomous valve control. Personnel injury: friction burns, both palms. Cause: infrastructure non-compliance with operator input.

He added: The grid is no longer responding to human intervention.

Tiffani read his entry when he showed her. "You're saying it has intent."

"I'm saying it acted without us," Jhace said. "Whether that's intent or malfunction doesn't change the outcome."

"It changes what we prepare for."

"Does it?" He looked at his shaking hands. "Either way, the grid is something we can't control anymore."

She wrote her own note: Infrastructure autonomy confirmed. Implications: unknown.

One Anneal after the Piercing, three Crown factions arrived at the med station within the same Tide.

First came the Religious sect. Elder Vrasten. Copper bands around both wrists. Scroll cases sealed against moisture. He walked the aisles between cots, looking at manifestors like they were scripture made visible.

Second came the economic guild. Representative Chelm. Clipboard. Guild seal pinned sharp at his collar. He asked manifestors what crafts they practiced, what guild marks they carried, whether they had "authorization to alter material properties."

Third came the military. Commander Tallec. Armored escort. She moved through the station with an aide taking down names, manifestation types, locations, and witness statements about "unregulated force deployment in civilian zones."

None of them spoke to each other. None coordinated. Each operated from a different theory about what manifestation was.

Tiffani watched from her cot. She documented all three approaches in the margin of her logbook. Wrote: Institutional fragmentation—no unified response. Three competing frameworks: divine, economic, military.

Near the entrance, Jhace stood where he could see the flow of bodies and uniforms. The girl Corvan had healed—the one who had cost Corvan ten Turns of her life—approached him.

"They're trying to take us," she said. "The military. They say we're weapons. That we have to be controlled."

"Are you a weapon?" Jhace asked.

"I don't know," she said. "I just want to find my family."

Commander Tallec reached them.

"You," she said to the girl. "Manifestor. Name and capability."

## Chapter Nine: Fourth Threshold

Ten Tides after the Piercing, another collapse hit Saint Merro. Terrace Four this time. Residential block. Fifty people trapped by initial reports.

Second had pulled the tremor into wrists and forearms.

"I know," she said.

"It's not your choice," he answered.
