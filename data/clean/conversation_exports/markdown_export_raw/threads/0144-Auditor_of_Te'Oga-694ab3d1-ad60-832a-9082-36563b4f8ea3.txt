def extract_section(text, heading):
    # heading exact match in ### etc
    pattern=re.compile(rf'^(###|####)\s+{re.escape(heading)}\s*$', re.M)
    m=pattern.search(text)
    if not m:
        return None
    start=m.end()
    # next heading at same or higher level (### or ##)
    nxt=re.search(r'^\s*#{2,4}\s+', text[start:], flags=re.M)
    end = start + (nxt.start() if nxt else len(text)-start)
    return text[start:end].strip()

def section_between(text, start_heading, end_heading=None):
    # find exact heading line
    m=re.search(rf'^##\s+{re.escape(start_heading)}\s*$', text, flags=re.M)
    if not m:
        return None
    start=m.end()
    if end_heading:
        n=re.search(rf'^##\s+{re.escape(end_heading)}\s*$', text[start:], flags=re.M)
        end=start + (n.start() if n else len(text)-start)
    else:
        n=re.search(r'^##\s+', text[start:], flags=re.M)
        end=start + (n.start() if n else len(text)-start)
    return text[start:end].strip()

Jhace kept his palm on the pipe because it told the truth faster than any gauge.

Heat pressed through the scale-crusted metal into his skin. Condensation ran in thin lines along the seam and dropped in steady clicks onto the stone channel beneath. The grid-hum traveled through the pipe wall and up his bones.

“Jhace.” The Sector Fourteen foreman’s voice came down the tunnel ahead of the light. “You done poking it? Third branch feeds the kiln quarter. Keep it open.”

Jhace swung his lantern higher. The flame made the damp walls flash and then go dull. Underface stone, patched in places with brick and mortar that had been cooked by leaking heat for too many Tides. Pipes ran in parallel rows, some wrapped, some bare, all scabbed with old repairs. The air held grit, oil, and the thin bite of minerals that settled on the back of the tongue.

A Worker with a pry-bar followed the foreman, shoulders hunched under a tool bag. Another carried a wedge pack and a coil of wire. They had the quick step of people who did not intend to be the ones blamed when a kiln went cold.

Jhace pointed his wrench at Junction Twelve without looking away from it. “Collar’s mis-seated.”

“It’s holding,” the foreman said.

“It’s holding wrong.” Jhace put the lantern on a dry ledge, set the flame shield, and knelt. He ran his fingers around the collar’s lip. One edge sat proud. The locking pins bit uneven. The joint had been forced and then left.

The foreman stepped closer. “It’ll make it through a Tide. We do the full seat after the shift rotation. You know the queue.”

Jhace heard the doubled harmonic deepen for a breath and then return to its previous pitch. The pipe under his hand trembled in a way that did not match the drip rhythm.

He tightened his grip on the wrench until his knuckles whitened and then eased off. Tight hands made mistakes.

“Third branch,” the foreman repeated, as if saying it again would change the geometry. “Kiln quarter. We’re already rationing flow. You close that collar, you choke them.”

Jhace rotated the collar a fraction with his fingertips. The metal scraped. The hum shifted and then snapped back into place. The second tone stayed.

He looked at the Workers behind the foreman. They watched his hands, not his face. They wanted his decision, not his opinion.

“Who seated it?” Jhace asked.

“Night crew,” the foreman said. “They were rushing. Heat-grid committee wants numbers.”

Jhace’s jaw tightened. Committees wanted numbers because numbers didn’t shake when the stone moved.

He set the wrench on the collar bolts and took the first bolt down in a measured turn-count. One. Two. Three. The foreman’s breath sharpened.

“What are you doing,” the foreman said, and the question carried threat wrapped in procedure.

“Fixing it.”

“You’re about to shut—”

“I’m about to seat it.” Jhace kept his eyes on the collar line. “If it slips under load, it shears the gasket. Then you lose the branch and the two flanking runs. Then your kiln quarter goes cold and you can tell them you saved a breath and bought a collapse.”

The foreman’s face went hard in the lantern light. “You’re making this bigger.”

Jhace did not answer. He finished backing the bolts, enough to relieve tension without dumping pressure. He braced his shoulder against the pipe, planted his boots on the wet stone, and levered the collar into alignment. The metal resisted, then gave with a short, ugly shift.

The doubled harmonic wavered. For a moment, it narrowed toward a single tone. Jhace felt the change through the pipe and held his breath.

Then the second tone returned, steady and indifferent.

“Seat,” he muttered, not to the foreman, not to the crew—just naming the action so his body stayed honest. He drove the bolts down in a star pattern, counting turns with each. One. One. One. Two. Two. Two. Three. He let the collar take the load in increments.

The foreman stared as if the collar might explode out of spite. “You can’t keep doing this,” he said. “You can’t keep slowing us.”

Jhace wiped his hands on his trousers and stood. “You can’t keep forcing collars. That’s slower.”

The Worker with the pry-bar shifted his weight. “Flow holding?”

Jhace pressed his palm to the pipe again. Heat steady. Hum steady. The second tone steady. The mis-seat was gone, but the wrongness remained.

“Holding,” he said. “Better seat now.”

The foreman’s nostrils flared. “Fine. Log it. Then get out. We’ve got a list.”

Jhace reached into his kit for a chalk stub and marked the junction brick beside the collar: **J12 / reseat**. Under it he wrote a second line and paused.

He could write *doubled harmonic*. That would get a laugh and then a dismissal. He could write *pressure under foundation*. That would get attention from people who did not come down into Underface unless something had already broken.

He wrote it anyway: **Pressure signal / non-local**.

The foreman saw it and made a sound in his throat that stopped short of a word. “You’re spooking yourself.”

Jhace set the chalk back. “I’m listening.”

He walked past them, lantern in hand, following the run deeper into Sector Fourteen’s third branch. The tunnel narrowed. The walls pressed closer to the pipes. In places the stone had been cut too tight and patched where cracks had been chiseled out and filled. The Underface held itself together by habit and sweat.

The hum traveled through his boots now, not just the pipe. A low tremor threaded the ground, faint enough that a person on the street might miss it entirely. Down here it was constant. It did not come in bursts from nearby work. It did not rise and fall with the opening of valves. It carried through junction after junction without damping.

Jhace stopped at the next support bracket and put his hand flat against the wall stone.

The tremor continued through the stone into his palm. It had a rhythm. Not the rhythm of carts overhead. Not the rhythm of a hammer line. A deeper sequence that repeated without the usual noise signatures riding it.

He tasted metal. Not from the air’s usual mineral grit. This was sharper—copper and iron, thin on the tongue.

He pulled in a slow breath through his nose and forced himself to catalog.

**One:** doubled harmonic across junctions.  
**Two:** tremor pattern not tied to local settlement.  
**Three:** taste shift with no obvious leak source.

A sound came down the tunnel from behind him: the foreman calling to the crew, voices tightening around schedule and flow demands. Jhace did not turn back. The third branch fed the kiln quarter, and the kiln quarter could yell. Stone didn’t yell. Stone just failed.

He moved to the next junction—Junction Thirteen—and checked the collar by touch. Seated. The pipe here carried the same doubled tone. He followed it.

A shallow ladder shaft branched off to his right, a vertical run that climbed upslope toward street access. Its iron rungs were wet with condensation and slick with grime. A faint draft came down from above, bringing a different smell: soot, cooked oil, and human presence. Street air, filtered through vents and cracks.

Jhace stopped at the base and looked up. The ladder vanished into darkness after a few spans. He could climb, report, let Sector Fourteen’s foreman file it away as a complaint. He could step back into noise and arguments and a line of urgent repairs that would keep him busy while whatever this was continued to move under the city.

The tremor under his boots deepened for a breath and then returned, and the doubled harmonic sharpened as if the pipe wall had tightened.

Jhace’s hand went to the pipe again. The metal burned through his callus. He held it anyway. The taste of copper stayed.

He turned away from the ladder and walked further into the third branch, toward the deeper junctions where the grid feed split into smaller lines and the stone pressed tighter around them.

Deeper first, then up.

He set his pace by the lantern swing and the drip rhythm and kept his attention on the pipe’s truth as the Underface carried the wrongness forward without explanation.
