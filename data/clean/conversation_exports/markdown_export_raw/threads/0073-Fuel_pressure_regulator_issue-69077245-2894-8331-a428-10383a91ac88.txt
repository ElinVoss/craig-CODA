That regulator’s vacuum/reference nipple is snapped off.  
See that jagged little stump on the tip? That used to be a hose barb. With it broken:

- The diaphragm never sees manifold vacuum, so rail pressure will be wrong (often too high at idle).  
- You’ve also got a vacuum leak there, which means rough idle, lean trims, misfires, maybe fuel stink.  
- If the diaphragm is torn too, you might even find fuel in the vacuum line.

What to do like a sane person:

1. Replace the regulator. Don’t glue it, don’t epoxy it, don’t pray at it. Fuel + heat + epoxy = future tow truck.  
2. Install a new O-ring, lightly oiled, and clean the bore before seating it.  
3. Check the vacuum hose for broken plastic bits and for any fuel inside. Replace the hose if it’s crusty.  
4. Afterward, verify fuel pressure and trims.

Short version: it’s broken at the nipple. Swap it out before the car keeps acting like it’s on a juice cleanse.

[ASSISTANT]
Got it. You’re showing the **fuel rail pressure sensor/regulator nose**, not a vacuum barb. And yeah, it’s not okay.

**What’s wrong with it**
- The **snout is mushroomed/chewed up**. That tip should be smooth and round. Yours looks hammered flat with impact marks. That can distort the internal diaphragm and make the sensor output peg high.
- The **tiny pressure port** next to the snout looks crusty. If that port gets damaged or partially plugged, the sensor reads garbage.
- With a mangled nose like that, the O-ring can still seal, but the sensor’s guts won’t be happy.

**Why you’re seeing “circuit high” (think P0193)**
- Most of the time “circuit high” is **electrical**: signal wire stuck at ~5 V because of a short to VREF or a lost ground.  
- A beat-up sensor like yours can also internally short and **drive the signal high**. So yes, the physical damage can absolutely cause the code.

**Quick, no-nonsense checks**
1. Key on, back-probe the connector: you should have **5 V ref**, solid **ground**, and **signal ~0.5–1.5 V KOEO**.  
   - Signal at 5 V with the sensor **unplugged** → harness short or PCM issue.  
   - Signal drops to normal when unplugged → **sensor is toast**.
2. Inspect the connector pins for **corrosion or fuel intrusion**. Wiggle test the harness.
3. If you want to be thorough, put a **mechanical gauge** on the rail and compare actual pressure to scan data. A stuck regulator/pump control can make real pressure high, but that’s rarer than the wiring/sensor failure.

**What to do**
- Replace the sensor/regulator. Do not try to file it “round” again unless you like doing the job twice.  
- Clean the rail bore, install a **new O-ring**, a dab of clean oil, and seat it straight.  
- Fix any wiring ugliness you find, clear codes, and verify trims/pressure.

Short version: the business end is physically trashed, and that’s perfectly capable of causing your **high circuit output**. Replace it and stop arguing with physics.

[ASSISTANT]
“Bubbling” at the fuel rail isn’t cute ambience. It’s a red flag. Given your mangled sensor/regulator nose and the insert point you’re listening at, here’s what that usually means, from most to least likely:

1) **O-ring/seat leak at the sensor port.**  
If the O-ring is nicked, wrong size, or the bore has a scratch, pressurized fuel squirts and aerates right at that hole. That sounds like gurgling/bubbling. You’ll also get fuel smell or a damp paper towel if you wrap it there and prime the pump. Replace the sensor and O-ring, oil the new O-ring, clean the bore, seat it straight, and torque the screws evenly. No epoxy, no RTV, no “bro, it’ll swell and seal.” It won’t.

2) **Cracked sensor body from that beat-up tip.**  
Internal crack = tiny leak path that hisses/gurgles under prime or idle and can also drive the signal “circuit high.” New sensor fixes both the noise and the code.

3) **Pump aeration/air in the rail.**  
Low fuel, sucking air at the pump sock, or a cavitating pump can send frothy fuel forward. You’ll hear a weird gurgle and see jumpy pressure. Easy check: fill the tank above half, key-on prime a few times, and watch actual fuel pressure with a gauge. If it stabilizes when full, the pump/pickup is the clown.

4) **EVAP purge making you chase ghosts.**  
A stuck-open purge valve can make a hollow gurgle/woosh near the intake rail area. Pinch the purge hose for 10 seconds at idle; if the sound quits, fix the purge valve. That won’t cause a “circuit high,” but it can mask real fuel noises.

**Do this, in order:**
- Replace the sensor/regulator and O-ring. Your old one’s tip looks like it fought a belt sander and lost.  
- Before you fire it, wrap a dry paper towel around the mounting base, key-on to prime twice. If the towel gets even a freckle wet, you still have a leak.  
- Verify **5 V ref, ground, and signal** at the connector. If signal sits at 5 V with the sensor unplugged, you’ve got a harness/PCM short. If it drops to normal unplugged, the old sensor was the villain.  
- Check actual rail pressure with a mechanical gauge. If pressure is frothy or falls off immediately after key-off, you’ve got aeration or a leakdown (pump check valve, injector leaking, or still leaking at that port).  
- If the bubbling persists after the sensor swap and there’s no wet fuel, pinch the purge line and listen again.

Short version: bubbling at the insert point almost always means **leak at the sensor/O-ring or a cracked sensor**. Fix that first. Fuel fires are great in movies, not next to your face.

Right, if the regulator/sensor is OFF the rail, the “bubbling” isn’t some spooky clue. That’s the rail open to atmosphere. Two things happen:

- If the engine was hot, the fuel in the rail **boils and percolates** once pressure is gone. Pressurized fuel stays liquid; crack it open and it fizzes like a cheap soda.  
- If you key-on with that hole open, the pump primes and **aerates the rail**, so you hear gurgling. Also you get a face full of gasoline, which is a bold skincare routine.

So the bubbling with the part removed is normal and not diagnostic. The issue is still your beat-up sensor nose and the “circuit high.”

Do it properly:

1) **Do not power the pump with the port open.** Cap it or wrap with a rag if you must move the car.  
2) **Inspect the rail bore and seat.** Bright light and fingertip test. Any nick where the O-ring seals will leak and make that gurgle later. If you feel a burr, very lightly deburr with ultra-fine paper or replace the rail.  
3) **Install a new pressure sensor/regulator with a fresh O-ring.** Light oil on the O-ring, seat it straight, torque evenly. No glue, no RTV.  
4) **Electrical sanity check KOEO:** 5 V ref present, solid ground, signal ~0.5–1.5 V.  
   - Signal stuck near 5 V with the sensor unplugged = harness short/PCM fault.  
   - Drops to normal when unplugged = the old sensor internally shorted.  
5) **Verify with a mechanical gauge** that actual pressure matches scan data. If it’s frothy or falls off immediately after key-off, chase pump/aeration issues.

Short version: the bubbling with the sensor off is just fuel losing pressure and boiling or the pump cavitating. Get a new sensor in there, make sure the rail seat is clean and smooth, and fix the wiring if the signal’s still pegged. Then you can stop listening to your fuel rail like it’s a seashell.
