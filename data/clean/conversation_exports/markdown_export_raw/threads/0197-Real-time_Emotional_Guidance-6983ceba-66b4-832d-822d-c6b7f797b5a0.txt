### What it would look like in practice
- You’re in a live conversation.
- Your earbud gives you **1–2 second micro-prompts**, not paragraphs, like:
  - “Pause. Breathe. Ask a question.”
  - “Reflect their point in one sentence.”
  - “Lower intensity: shorter, slower, fewer adjectives.”
  - “Don’t defend. Clarify.”
- Or it gives you **3 ready-to-say replies** in your voice style:

  2) “Help me understand what part matters most to you.”  
  3) “I get why that hit you. I’m going to respond carefully.”

### The actual hard problems (the stuff that makes or breaks it)
1. **Latency**: if it’s more than ~1–2 seconds behind, it becomes useless or distracting.
2. **Context tracking**: conversation meaning changes fast; models can misread intent and steer you wrong.
3. **Noise + overlap**: two people talking, interruptions, background sound → transcription gets sloppy.
4. **Cognitive load**: you can’t listen to them *and* listen to coaching *and* speak smoothly unless prompts are super short.
5. **Privacy / consent**: if it’s listening to other people, you’re in “recording laws + ethics” territory. If you’re doing this, the clean way is **explicit consent** and **no saving audio**.

### The safest “this could actually work” design
- **Not always-on.** Make it “tap-to-coach” or “hold-to-coach.”
- It only analyzes the **last 5–15 seconds** when you trigger it.
- It outputs **one line**: either a suggested sentence or a control instruction (“ask a clarifying question”).
- **No storage**, or local-only processing.

### A practical workaround you can use immediately (no sci-fi required)
Instead of live coaching, do **post-convo compression**:
- After the conversation, you summarize it into your phone (30–60 seconds).
- Then you get:
  - “Where you escalated”
  - “Where you defended instead of clarified”
  - “Three calmer phrases to use next time”
This avoids the privacy/latency mess and still improves your restraint fast.

### If you want the “in-ear” effect without tech

1) **Reflect**: “So you’re saying X, and it’s frustrating because Y.”  
2) **Boundary**: “I can’t do yelling/accusations. I can do specifics.”  
3) **Question**: “What’s the one thing you want to change right now?”

You’re describing a classic loop:

- She says something **indirect** that implies you’re failing.
- You clock the manipulation and respond to the **subtext** (“stop guilt-tripping me”).
- She feels exposed/invalidated, escalates, and now you’re both pissed and nobody’s talking about the actual need.

The fix isn’t “be nicer.” It’s **refuse the trap without naming it**. Address the *need/ask* and ignore the bait.

## The rule that stops 80% of blowups
Do **not** accuse motive in the moment (“you’re guilt-tripping / manipulating”).  
Even if you’re right, it lights the fuse. Treat it like a bad UI: don’t yell at the UI, click the right button.

## The “Guilt-Trip Translator”
Passive-aggressive guilt lines usually map to one of these:

1) **“I guess I’ll just…”** → “I want help / shared effort.”  
2) **“Must be nice…”** → “I feel overlooked / I want consideration.”  
3) **“Whatever. It’s fine.”** → “It’s not fine. I want you to ask.”  
4) **“You never / you always…”** → “I’m hurt and I want reliability.”  
5) **“If you cared, you would…”** → “I need reassurance + a specific action.”

You respond to the **translation**, not the original.

## The script: 3 moves, no drama
### 1) Name the feeling (lightly)
Not a speech. One sentence.
- “That sounds frustrating.”
- “I hear you’re feeling alone in this.”
- “It sounds like you’re hurt.”

### 2) Pull out the actual ask
- “What do you want me to do right now—specifically?”
- “Are you asking for time, help, or reassurance?”
- “What would ‘better’ look like tonight?”

### 3) Offer a real option + boundary
- “I can do A or B. I can’t do the sarcasm/jabs. Which do you want?”
- “I’m here, but I’m not doing a trial. Tell me the request.”

That’s it. Calm, adult, no courtroom.

## Ready-to-use lines for common guilt-bombs
### When she says: “I guess I’ll just do everything myself.”
You say:
- “I don’t want you carrying it alone. What part do you want me to take—right now?”
If she stays indirect:
- “I’m willing. I just need a clear request.”

### “Must be nice to have time for that.”
- “You feel like I’m choosing other stuff over you. What are you needing from me tonight?”

### “Whatever. It’s fine.”
- “I’m not going to pretend it’s fine. Tell me what’s not fine, in one sentence.”

### “If you cared, you’d know.”
- “I do care. I’m not a mind reader. Say the thing you want.”

### “You never listen.”
- “I’m listening now. Give me one concrete example from today, not ‘never.’”

### The nuclear one: “Do what you want.”
- “I’m not taking that as permission. I want agreement. What are the options?”

## What to do when you “see through it” and it backfires
You’re probably doing one of these (super common):
- **Calling it out as manipulation**
- **Explaining the tactic**
- **Proving you’re right**
- **Going cold / sarcastic back**

Instead, do this:
- “I’m not responding to the tone. I *am* responding to the issue. What’s the issue?”

It’s a judo move: you don’t accept the invitation to fight over intent.

## Tone control that actually works mid-argument
Use “**low bandwidth**” speaking:
- **Short sentences**
- **Slower pace**
- **No adjectives** (“unfair, insane, ridiculous” = gasoline)
- **No history** (don’t bring in last month’s wars)

A good restraint line:
- “I’m getting heated. I’m going to slow down so I don’t say something stupid.”

That line saves relationships because it’s honest and non-attacking.

## Boundary without escalation (the clean version)
Say it like a policy, not a punishment:
- “I will talk about anything. I won’t do sarcasm or guilt shots. If it continues, I’m taking 10 minutes and coming back.”

If she follows you / keeps poking:
- “I’m not leaving the relationship. I’m leaving this moment until we can talk clean.”

## When she insists you respond to the subtext
You can acknowledge it without submitting:
- “I get why you’d try to push me to feel it. I’d rather you just tell me what you need.”

## The one question that changes everything
“What are you needing—**comfort, accountability, or a plan**?”

It forces clarity. Most guilt-trips are sloppy requests for one of those three.
