Act I: Chapter 3 - The Flawed Gear

Act I: Chapter 4

The problem with a memory is that you can’t trust the details. Not the real, messy ones, anyway. The mind is a terrible archivist; it cleans up the corners, straightens the pictures on the wall, and paints over the rust. It gives you the story you can live with, not the one that actually happened. I tried, in my first drafts, to write it straight—the metallic tang of ozone, the sterile cold of the Panopticon. But the words just felt… hollow. A lie I was telling to myself, trying to make the past a simple, logical problem. So I’m trying the mask again. I’m trying to make the truth sound like a complicated riddle, something clever. Maybe if I dress it up in the right words, it won’t hurt so much. In the heart of the Order’s earliest artisan-colonies, there was a peculiar form of engineering known as haute horlogerie. Not just the simple, utilitarian clocks that told the time, you understand. I’m speaking of the masterpieces. The watches so delicate they could fit on the head of a pin, the automatons that mimicked the flight of a hummingbird. The true artistry wasn’t in the functionality, but in the ornamentation. The gilded filigree, the hand-engraved landscapes, the tiny jewels set into the mechanisms. They understood that the purpose of a watch wasn’t simply to mark the passing of time, but to render that passing beautiful, to make the unbearable fact of our finite existence a little more palatable. But inside, behind all that polished gold and glittering stone, there was a flaw. A tiny, almost imperceptible piece of grit. Not a defect, not really. It was, rather, a necessary compromise. A small imperfection in the machine’s design that allowed it to run at all. It was the friction between the cogs that prevented them from seizing up in the cold. A moment of glorious, beautiful imprecision that allowed for the illusion of perfect grace. My world was a masterpiece of haute horlogerie.
