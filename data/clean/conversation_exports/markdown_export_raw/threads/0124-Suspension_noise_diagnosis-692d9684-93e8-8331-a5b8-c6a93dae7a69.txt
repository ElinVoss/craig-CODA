Short version: something in that front suspension stack-up is moving in a way it shouldn’t, and it’s literally “sucking” and “popping” like a plunger every time the chassis loads/unloads.

On a 2006 Mustang (especially if it’s still on original-ish parts), the usual suspects near the shock/strut tower are:

1. Strut mount/top hat going bad  
- Rubber in the mount is torn, dry-rotted, or separated from the metal.  
- As the strut shaft moves up and down, the mount compresses and releases with a **squish-pop / plunger** sound.  
- Often worse at low speeds over small bumps, turning into driveways, or when you rock the car by hand.

2. Strut itself is blown or leaking  
- If the internal valving is shot and the fluid is moving wrong, you can get a wet, squelchy noise from the shaft moving.  
- Check for oily residue on the strut body; if it’s wet and dirty, that’s a pretty solid “replace me” flag.  
- Car will also feel extra floaty or bouncy.

3. Upper spring isolator or rubber bushings are crushed or missing  
- The coil spring sits on rubber isolators. When they’re worn through, the metal spring can twist and “stick-slip” against metal, sometimes making a **rubbery groan / plunger** type noise as the suspension cycles.  
- You’ll often hear this when the suspension loads/unloads slowly (pulling into a driveway, speed bumps).

4. Sway bar end link / bushing near the tower  
- If the noise *seems* like it’s from the tower, it can actually be the sway bar link or bushing right nearby.  
- Usually more of a clunk, but if the bushing is half-dead and grabbing/releasing, it can sound oddly squishy.

5. Aftermarket stuff (if you have it)  
- Coilovers or lowering springs installed without proper bump stops or with cheap top hats will absolutely make weird suction / plunger noises as they bind and release.  
- Poly bushings without grease can make awful, almost “wet” sounding creaks.

Quick driveway checks you can do

1. Bounce test  
- Park, engine off. Push down hard on the corner that’s noisy and let go.  
- Listen right at the shock tower under the hood.  
  - **If you hear the plunger noise while bouncing:** it’s almost certainly the strut, strut mount, or spring isolator.  
  - **If it’s quiet while parked but loud while driving:** could still be those, but also check sway bar links/bushings.

2. Visual inspection in the tower  
- Pop the hood, look at the top of the strut mount:  
  - Is the rubber cracked, split, mushroomed, or off-center?  
  - Does the center stud look like it’s sitting higher than the other side?  
  - Have someone turn the steering wheel lock-to-lock while you watch and listen—binding or jumping in the mount = bad top hat/bearing.

3. Compare sides  
- Whatever you see on the noisy side, compare to the quiet side. If one mount looks collapsed, crooked, or sits lower/higher, that’s your clue.

4. Check for fluid and torn boots  
- Look down the strut body: any wet, oily film = blown strut.  
- Torn dust boot doesn’t cause the sound itself but usually means the strut’s been eating dirt and is on its way out.

Real talk / what usually fixes it on a 2000s Mustang

Most of the time, this ends up being:
- Worn **strut mounts/top hats**,  
- And/or tired **front struts**,  
- And sometimes the **upper spring isolators** are toast too.

If you’re going in there, it’s usually smarter to replace:
- Both front struts  
- Both strut mounts  
- Both upper (and maybe lower) spring isolators  

…all in one go, rather than chasing one noise at a time.

If it’s stock and the **front struts were replaced recently but the “plunger” noise never changed**, that almost always means:

They changed the **damper**, but the **thing actually making the noise wasn’t replaced**.

On a 2005–2009 Mustang, the top 3 suspects are:

---

## 1. Original Strut Mount / Bearing Plate (most likely)

What stayed the same when the struts were replaced?  
Usually this stuff:

- Upper **strut mount (“top hat”)**
- The **bearing** inside it
- The **rubber isolator/seat** the spring presses into

When these get old, a few things happen:
- The **rubber collapses or tears**, so the center moves up/down in a squishy way.
- The **bearing dries out or rusts**, so instead of rotating smoothly it grabs and releases.
- That grab–release + compressed rubber = **exactly** that “wet plunger” or “suction pop” noise as the suspension cycles.

Why your new struts didn’t change the sound:
- If the shop re-used your **old mounts/bearings/isolators**, they basically bolted your new strut into the same noisy hardware.
- A lot of places do this to save parts cost.

What to look/listen for:
1. Pop the hood, look at the top of the strut:
   - Rubber around the center stud cracked, mushroomed, or off-center?
   - Does the center stud sit higher or at a weird angle compared to the other side?
2. Have someone:
   - Turn the steering wheel lock-to-lock in a parking spot.
   - Or slowly bounce that corner by pushing on the fender.
   - Put your hand right on the strut mount.
   - If you can **feel** the plunger-ish notch or hear it right there, the mount/bearing is toast.

Real fix here:
- Replace the **strut mounts (with bearings)** on both sides.
- If you want to be thorough, do the **upper spring isolators** at the same time.
- If the struts are truly new, you don’t need to replace those again.

---

## 2. Spring Isolators / Spring Twisting in the Seat

The coil spring sits in rubber isolators top and bottom. If those are:
- Crushed flat
- Torn
- Missing chunks

Then the spring can **stick and then slip** as it compresses/relaxes. That stick–slip can sound:
- Squishy
- Rubber-on-metal
- Sometimes like a plunger as the spring “unwinds” under load.

You’ll often hear this:
- Going up driveways at an angle
- Over slow speed bumps
- When the body rolls side to side

This also would’ve stayed the same when they did the struts unless they specifically replaced the isolators.

Fix:
- New **upper and lower spring isolators**.
- Cheap parts, annoying labor (spring compressor), but not complicated.

---

## 3. Nearby but Fake-Out: Sway Bar End Link / Bushing or Control Arm Bushing

Sometimes the sound feels like it’s dead in the tower but is actually:
- A **front sway bar end link** binding in its bushing
- A dry or torn **control arm bushing**

These usually sound more like:
- Creaks, groans, or rubbery squeaks
- Sometimes a dull “thunk-pop” that your brain interprets as plunger-ish

You can check:
- With the wheel off, grab the **sway bar link** and try to move it by hand.
- Look for torn boots, leaking grease, or a joint that moves too freely or notchy.
- Have someone bounce the car while you’re down there listening.

---

## Easy tests you can do right now

1. **Static bounce test at the noisy corner**
   - Car in park, engine off.
   - Bounce that corner hard a few times.
   - Listen at the **top of the tower under the hood**.
   - If you hear the plunger noise every bounce = mount/bearing/isolator.

2. **Steering test**
   - In a parking lot, car running but not moving.
   - Turn wheel lock-to-lock slowly.
   - Listen at the tower.
   - Plunger / groan / pop as you steer = mount bearing binding.

3. **Lube test (for diagnosis, not a real fix)**
   - Spray a little **silicone spray** (not WD-40) around the **top of the strut mount and spring seat**.
   - Do the bounce/steer test again.
   - If the noise changes or quiets down temporarily, that almost confirms it’s that top assembly.

---

## What I’d actually do on your car

If it were my 2006 Mustang with:
- Stock suspension
- Newish front struts
- Plunger noise before and after the swap

I’d plan this:

1. Replace on **both fronts**:
   - Strut mounts (with bearings)
   - Upper spring isolators (and lower if they look chewed)
2. While it’s apart, inspect:
   - Sway bar end links
   - Control arm bushings and ball joints

If money/time are tight and you just want to gamble smart:
- Do **front strut mounts + bearings** first.  
That’s the highest-probability fix.

Yeah, that’s not right, and it’s not just “eh whatever” level wrong. A top strut nut that just keeps spinning is a “fix this before you daily it” thing.

There are a few different ways this can behave, so I’ll hit the main ones and what they mean.

---

## 1. The shaft is spinning with the nut (most common)

On a lot of struts, the **center shaft will spin** unless you hold it still. If you just hit it with a ratchet, you’re turning the shaft and nut together, so it feels like it “never tightens” or always free-spins.

Signs this is what’s happening:
- The nut doesn’t snug up hard; it just kinda spins with light resistance.
- The stud doesn’t visibly pull down and clamp the top mount tighter.
- The very tip of the shaft is moving with the nut.

What to do:
1. Look at the top of the strut shaft:
   - Internal hex (Allen) in the middle?  
   - Or flats on the shaft?
2. Use:
   - An Allen key or wrench to **hold the shaft** still,  
   - And a box wrench on the nut to tighten it.
3. Ideally preload the suspension:
   - Put the car on jack stands,
   - Then jack up under the control arm/knuckle until the spring is slightly compressed.  
     This gives the shaft some friction and helps it grab while you tighten.

If you can now get it to tighten solid and it stops spinning freely, you’re probably fine.

---

## 2. The nut is bottomed out on something, not clamping (washer issue)

Yes, this is where your “missing washer or something?” suspicion comes in.

Typical stack-up (simplified) is something like:
- Strut shaft  
- Spring and upper spring seat/isolator  
- Bearing/strut mount  
- **Top washer**  
- Nut

If the **top washer is missing** or the wrong one is used, two bad things can happen:

1. The nut bottoms out on the unthreaded shoulder of the shaft or the wrong surface before it actually clamps the mount.
2. The nut is digging into rubber instead of a metal washer, so it kind of “sinks in” and still feels loose and weird.

How to check:
- Compare the noisy side to the **passenger side**:
  - Is there an obvious washer under the nut on the good side that’s missing on the bad side?
  - Do the stud and nut sit at the same height?
- Watch the mount while someone bounces the car:
  - If you see the nut and shaft move up/down separately from the body, it’s not clamped right.

If the washer is missing or wrong:
- You need to pull the assembly and fix the stack with the **correct washer** (or a proper replacement with the right inner/outer diameter and thickness).
- If a shop did the struts, they may have reused or lost hardware.

---

## 3. Threads are stripped (nut or shaft)

If someone drove for a while with the nut loose, or hammered it on with an impact, you may now have chewed threads.

Signs:
- Nut spins **truly freely** with very little resistance, doesn’t move up or down the threads.
- You can pull up on the shaft and the nut just slides / wobbles.
- Threads on the top of the shaft look flattened, shiny, or chewed.

If that’s what you’re seeing:
- At minimum, you need a **new nut**.
- If the **shaft threads** are junk, you’re buying another strut. There’s no real safe fix for stripped shaft threads.

---

## 4. How serious is this?

Loose / improperly clamped top nut can:
- Let the strut shaft move inside the mount,
- Trash the strut mount and bearing,
- In worst cases, let the shaft punch into the tower or lose control of the suspension.

So:
- If you can physically see or feel play between the shaft/mount/body, don’t be driving it until it’s correctly clamped.

---

## What I’d do next, step-by-step

1. Pop the other side’s cap and visually compare:
   - Same nut? Same washer? Same height?  
2. Try tightening correctly:
   - Preload suspension a bit with a jack under the control arm.
   - Hold the shaft (Allen key/flats).
   - Tighten the nut with a box wrench or torque wrench.
3. Watch for movement:
   - Have someone bounce that corner.
   - If the shaft/nut move relative to the tower = still not clamped right.
4. If it still free-spins:
   - Inspect threads very closely.
   - If they look okay, you probably have a **stack/washer problem**.
   - If they look chewed, plan on **new nut or new strut**.

If you want, describe exactly how it behaves:
- Does the nut spin with almost no resistance?
- Does it move down the shaft when you tighten or just sit there?
- Does the shaft spin with it?

Based on that, I can tell you whether you’re dealing with (1) shaft-spin, (2) missing/incorrect washer, or (3) stripped threads.
