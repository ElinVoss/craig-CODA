Nothing wanted to move, not at the start. Universe like a locked valve—tight, silent, ready to crack. Then Xilcore happened: a mistake, a sigh, a little grit in the gears. The stillness never stood a chance.

Chapter 1 – Morning Pressure

The pump station always woke before the sky. Jhace felt it not as a sound, but as a pressure in the bones of his feet—a deep, subterranean pulse that shivered up the iron catwalk. It was the station’s own heart, a steady, powerful rhythm of sixty revolutions per minute, a mechanical assurance that the lifeblood of Grindheim Dregs was flowing. He set his toolbox down, the clang of metal on metal a familiar prayer in the echoing chamber. His hand, calloused and knowing, spread over the cold brass rail, feeling the deep, resonant thrum that was more a part of him than his own pulse. He counted the beats, a familiar litany against the silence of the pre-dawn city.

He exhaled, his breath a plume of white in the lantern-light, a fleeting ghost that curled around the polished brass of the pressure gauges. Below, the great flywheel turned, a colossal gear of polished iron, its teeth slick with fresh oil. It moved with the slow, inexorable confidence of a god, each tooth catching the light in a repeating, silent affirmation. It was a grin of pure, mechanical certainty, and Jhace, seeing his own distorted reflection in its polished surface, offered a tight, tired smile in return. Here, in the rhythmic, metallic pulse of the pump station, was a conversation he understood. A language of pressure and release, of stress and tolerance, that asked for nothing but competence and offered, in return, a profound and unwavering companionship. The machinery was an honest god, demanding only skill and attention, and offering in return a world without pity, a world where a man was measured only by the integrity of his work.

Today, though, the grin felt forced, a crack in the calm facade of his solitude. He told himself it was the pre-dawn chill, a cold that had seeped into the very bones of the station overnight, shrinking the iron, thickening the oil. A routine bleed would cure it. He unlatched the gauge housing, his palm brushing against the etched numerals he could read in the dark: 60 psi, the gentle hum of a sleeping city; 80, the sharp intake of breath before a crisis; 100, the scream of tearing metal. The needle quivered at fifty-eight—low, but not yet a threat. Yet, the glass of the gauge was cold, unnervingly so. Brass was a greedy metal, always borrowing warmth, but today it felt like a dead man’s hand.

Jhace pressed his palm harder against the main pipe, closing his eyes, willing the metal to speak its secrets. A faint, discordant hum answered him, a vibration that was off-key, a restless, brittle sound like a hairline crack in a friend’s voice. It was a song of stress, a note of fear in the familiar mechanical symphony.

“Easy, girl,” he murmured, his voice a low rumble that was lost in the vastness of the chamber. He unscrewed the bleed valve, the hiss of escaping air a sigh of relief. “Just need to stretch.”

Condensation dripped from the flywheel’s spokes, a slow, reluctant applause on the iron grate below. He caught a drop on his fingertip. It was clear, with no sediment, no tell-tale sign of mineral deposits, but the water carried a sharp, metallic tang, a taste of fear. He wiped his hand on his trousers, leaving a grey streak that would dry into a map of yesterday’s labor.

The valve opened with a docile sigh. The needle on the gauge climbed, a slow, steady ascent to sixty, then sixty-two. Relief, warm and liquid, loosened the tight knot of tension in his shoulders. He closed the valve, wiped a smear of oil from his eyebrows, and reached for the logbook chained to the bulkhead. Numbers first, feelings later; data was a language that never lied.

Footsteps clanged on the iron stairs above, the sound of Mrs. Kettner on her way to open the bakery flue. Soon the scent of rye bread would drift down the airshaft, a warm, comforting aroma that would mingle with the sharp, clean scent of machine oil, the district’s true morning prayer. Jhace’s stomach rumbled, a hollow ache that he ignored. Hunger could wait. He tightened the gauge cover, a sense of satisfaction settling over him. The needle twitched. Sixty-five. Seventy.

A hiccup. A sudden, jarring tremor that ran through the entire station.

He froze, his pen hovering over the logbook. A plume of steam hissed from a joint he had rebuilt only last month, a venomous serpent of a warning. The flywheel’s steady rhythm faltered, its teeth clacking together like dice in a cup. Jhace’s own heart leaped into a frantic, chaotic rhythm that matched the machine’s distress. He flung the logbook aside and grabbed the resonance stethoscope from his hip: a palm-sized copper cup connected by a gutta-percha tube to a bone-conduction plate. He pressed the cup to the pipe and the plate to his sternum. Metal against bone, system to soul. The pump’s voice slammed into him—no longer a purr but a scream riding a cold wind. Images flashed behind his eyes: frost feathers blooming inside cast iron, micro-fissures widening, water hammer building like cannon shot. He jerked away, his ears ringing. The pipe was remembering winter’s bite, shrinking faster than any bleed could compensate.

He lunged for the bypass wheel, his muscles already burning with a desperate urgency. If he could just shunt the flow to Auxiliary Two before the hammer hit— Clank. The wheel refused to budge. Corrosion, a wedged gate, impossible—he’d serviced it himself only yesterday. He threw his weight against it again. Nothing.

Seventy-eight psi.

Sweat stung his eyes, mixing with the oil on his face to form grey tears. He needed leverage, a longer bar, another pair of hands. The thought lodged in his throat like a burr. No one else was certified for high-pressure work; he’d designed the protocols himself, a proud sentinel of self-reliance. A low whistle spiraled up the shaft, not from the pipes but from the district above—kettles waking, hearths pressurizing. Soon 800 people would be queuing at taps that might cough air instead of water. He pictured Mrs. Kettner’s dough going dry, children’s chalky mouths, the communal kettle cracking from a flash-boil when the flow returned too fast.

The needle kissed eighty.

Jhace’s knees hit the grating. He splayed both palms against the main riser, his skin chilling instantly. “Hold,” he begged, pouring his own resonance into the metal the way his mentor had once poured whiskey into a wound. The hum steadied—for two beats, three—then skittered higher, a mocking laugh. His vision tunneled, vertigo tugging at him. Ten minutes of full-focus healing was his recorded limit; he’d already burned five. But if he walked away now, the district would lose pressure, boilers would starve, lives would knot around his absence.

Still the wheel refused to move.

He sat back on his heels, his breath ragged. The station’s iron ribs groaned around him, the arches flexing like a cage testing its latch. For the first time in years, the sound felt lonely rather than welcoming.

“I need another pair of hands.”

The words escaped him, a small, desperate whisper beneath the machine’s tantrum. They tasted of rust and relief. He repeated them, louder this time, as if the sheer volume of his voice could summon help. No answer—only the rising shriek of water desperate to become wreckage.

Jhace dragged his sleeve across his mouth, leaving an oil-smeared mustache. Options flickered past his eyes like torn pages: run topside, bang on doors, beg for volunteers untrained in pressure protocol—waste precious minutes, risk a stranger’s limbs, and still fail. Or stay, burn himself hollow, and maybe, just maybe, hold the line long enough for—what? A miracle in work boots?

He laughed, a single, bitter sound. Miracles didn’t register in his logbook.

The needle edged past eighty-five. A hairline crack zipped across the gauge glass, a frozen lightning bolt. Cold dread flooded him, colder than any winter water. If the face shattered, debris would punch downward, carving shrapnel from brass, from bone.

He pressed his forehead to the pipe, listening past the scream for the pump’s true name, the heartbeat he’d fallen asleep to as an apprentice curled on this same catwalk. It wasn’t gone—only drowning. A partner rhythm, steady and sure, seemed to answer inside his skull: Let me in.

Hallucination or hope, he grabbed it. “Whoever’s out there—help me.”

Steam swallowed the plea. But the act of asking loosened something in his chest, a valve he hadn’t known was rusted shut. The station’s clamor faded beneath the rush of his own blood, counting time in duplicated beats: mine-yours, mine-yours.

He didn’t hear footsteps yet, but possibility began to warm the metal under his cheek. The pressure was still climbing—eighty-seven, eighty-eight—yet the rise felt momentary, a wave gathering before it broke against two shores instead of one.

Jhace straightened, rolled the ache from his shoulders, and wrapped both hands around the stubborn wheel. This time he didn’t brace alone; he braced for them, for whoever answered. The pump’s scream pitched higher, but beneath it, he caught a new vibration—lighter, quicker, approaching. Footfalls on iron. A second heartbeat.

He set his jaw, torque-ready. “Come on, then,” he whispered to the approaching rhythm. “Let’s teach this beast to sing in harmony.”

The needle trembled at ninety, waiting for the next move—not his, but theirs.

And in that very first act of gettin’ it right… he fucked up. Royally.

Chapter 2 – Unexpected Assistance

The stranger’s boots rang once on the iron stair, a sharp, clear note that cut through the rising chaos of the pump’s scream. Then, silence. A listening silence. Jhace, his hands welded to the bypass wheel, his shoulders a landscape of burning agony, didn’t look up. The gauge, a spiderweb of cracks spreading across its face, quivered at eighty-nine psi. The pump’s scream was a physical thing now, a blade scraping against the inside of his skull.

“Four-foot cheater, two shoulders,” he repeated, his voice a raw, desperate rasp. “Or we lose the main and every tap in the district runs dry by supper.”

“Brute force won’t help.” The voice was low and calm, a thread of reason in the maelstrom. It was the voice of an old fitter, patient and knowing, speaking to a green apprentice. “Your gate’s cold-locked. You need heat and a song.”

A slim shape knelt at his elbow. Work-worn canvas trousers, the knees stained dark with the ghost of old oil. An auburn braid, thick and heavy, fell forward over one shoulder, its tip brushing the iron grating like a painter’s brush. She set down a tin toolbox, no bigger than a loaf of bread, and opened it. A collapsible spirit lamp, its brass legs snapping into place, a quick spin of the valve, and a blue flame hissed to life. Her hands moved with a fluid, unhurried grace, a ballet of competence in the heart of the chaos.

Jhace risked a glance. Green eyes, a smudge of soot on one eyelid, and a constellation of freckles the color of cast-iron scale. A stranger, yes, but her movements, the way she handled her tools, sang a familiar mechanical litany, a language he had spoken since he was a boy.

“Who—”

“Tiffani Merrow. Traveling fitter.” She cupped the lamp around the bypass housing, the blue flame licking at the frozen threads. “Met a frost-lock like this in North-Hearth. The metal shrinks, the condensate flash-freezes. You torque it harder, you’ll shear the stem.”

The explanation landed with the force of a revelation, a page from a textbook he hadn’t known existed. He eased his weight off the wheel. “We’re minutes from a hammer-blow.”

“Then let’s give it a heartbeat.” She unscrewed a small copper vial and dripped an amber fluid along the seam. The scent of cedar and hot iron filled the air, a strange perfume in the steamy chaos. “Penetrating oil and capsaicin. It expands the micro-cracks, tricks the frost into letting go.”

While the mixture sizzled, she uncoiled a length of braided leather from her belt, looped it around a spoke of the wheel, and braced one boot against the flange. “On three, we rock—no yanking. One, two—”

They moved as one. The leather creaked, the metal groaned, and then, with a sigh of surrender that was almost organic, the threads gave way. The wheel spun a quarter-turn, then another. The pressure dropped, the needle on the gauge falling from eighty-six to eighty-three. The needle steadied.

He stared at her, his chest heaving. “That song have a second verse?”

“Always.” A quick, crooked smile, and she pointed to the resonance stethoscope still dangling at his hip. “But I’ll need your ears.”

A low rumble shuddered through the riser, and a fresh crack zipped across the paint. Time was running out.

Jhace handed her the cup. “Bone-plate to your sternum. Listen for the cold spots—they’ll hum flat.” She pressed the cup to the pipe, her eyes closing. Her soot-dusted lashes fanned out against her freckled skin. He watched her face, the subtle shifts of expression as she read the pump’s story, a story he had thought only he could understand. Her brow lifted at a discordant note, her jaw eased as the note trued. She was reading his patient, and she was reading it better than he could alone.

“Here.” She tapped a section of pipe six inches above the weld. “Ice sleeve. We warm it, or we lose the seam.”

Jhace hesitated. Direct flame on a pressurized line was madness, a fool’s game. Unless the flame obeyed. He pulled a thin sheet of copper from the scrap bin and rolled it into a collar. “A flame cage,” he said, his voice tight. “Even heat, no hot spots.”

They worked in a seamless, unspoken tandem. She fed the lamp, he shaped the collar. She adjusted the flame, he monitored the gauge. The pressure crept down, a slow, steady retreat from the brink. Eighty, seventy-eight. The flywheel’s frantic clatter smoothed into a contented purr.

Community voices, a chorus of fear and curiosity, drifted down the stairs. Mrs. Kettner’s reedy voice cut through the steam. “Jhace, dear? Is there water for the dough?”

He lifted a hand, a gesture of reassurance he didn’t feel, his eyes still locked on Tiffani. “Five minutes, ma’am.” The woman at his side mouthed the word, a silent promise. Promise?

He nodded. And for the first time, the word tasted of possibility.

When the collar glowed a dull cherry red, she killed the lamp. Together, they wrapped wet rags around the pipe, drawing the heat through the metal. A final, satisfying hiss—the sound of ice surrendering—and the gauge settled at a docile sixty-two psi.

Silence fell, a profound, echoing silence that was as loud as the cannon fire that had preceded it. Jhace realized he was gripping her forearm, his thumb leaving a sooty imprint on her skin. He let go, but the dark crescent remained.

Around them, the station exhaled, a long, slow breath of relief, releasing a tension he hadn’t even known it held. The pipes ticked and cooled, a chorus of contented cats.

He found his voice. “Grindheim Dregs owes you breakfast.”

She wiped her hands on a rag that, somehow, remained cleaner than his. “I’ll trade you for a wash-up and your name.”

“Jhace Torrins. District mechanic.” He offered her his hand, the one that still smelled of cedar oil. “And a permanent share of my toolbox if you stay the week.”

Her fingers slid into his, a mirror of his own calloused hand, warm despite the frost they had just chased away. “A week sounds like a beginning.”

Up on the catwalk, the first rays of dawn spilled through the louvers, turning every droplet of condensation into a tiny, glittering prism. Somewhere above, a kettle sang, boilers steadied, and taps opened to an honest flow of water. But Jhace heard only the small, steady pulse beneath his thumb—her pulse—keeping time with the pump they had gentled together.

He released a breath he felt he’d been holding since childhood. “Beginning it is, then.”

Leesa, now, she was different. Born from the star’s reflection in a drop of oil, all she wanted was to make things…smoother. To sand down the rough edges. A universe without friction. A universe without a point.

Chapter 3 – Shared Knowledge

The workshop smelled of coal smoke and last night’s rain, a damp, earthy scent that clung to the air. Jhace lifted the lantern, its light glinting off the brass jaws of the vise, a patient predator on the workbench. Tiffani paused on the threshold, her braid flicking rainwater onto the floorboards, three quick drops that sizzled and vanished like shy ghosts.

“First lesson,” he said, his voice a low rumble in the quiet space. He cleared a space among the gaskets and feeler gauges, a small island of order in the chaotic sea of his workbench. “We rebuild the valve we saved. You’ll do the hands, I’ll do the eyes.”

She lifted an eyebrow, a flicker of amusement in her green eyes. “Thought you’d already seen enough of my hands.”

A slow heat climbed his neck, but he kept his voice level. “I need to know your rhythm before I trust you inside a live line.”

A fair bargain. She stepped up to the bench, her sleeves already rolled up to her elbows. The valve body, liberated from Main One at dawn, lay between them, a shared secret still warm from its confession.

They worked in a comfortable, overlapping silence. He passed her tools, and she named them back—spanner, lap, lapping compound—each word a small, unconscious test that she passed without even noticing. The sharp, clean scent of copper polish filled the air, and the steam from the kettle on the pot-belly stove wrapped them in a private, intimate weather. At some point, their knees brushed against each other under the bench. Neither of them moved away.

“Tell me about North-Hearth,” he said, his voice low. He watched her spin the lap with the steady, hypnotic rhythm of a potter’s wheel.

“Cold,” she said, her eyes fixed on her work. “The stone conduits are all above ground. The pipes freeze if you so much as breathe on them wrong.” She didn’t look up. “I learned frost-splitting before I learned how to flirt.”

He laughed, a short, surprised sound. “And after?”

“Still learning.” A ghost of a grin touched her lips. “But brass warms faster than stone.”

The kettle clicked, and the conversation paused while he poured two mugs of chicory. He slid one across the scarred wooden bench, the brown rings from the mugs overlapping like two Venn diagrams of appetite.

She sipped the chicory, her eyes closing for a moment. “District blend?”

“Roasted dandelion root. It’s the water that makes the flavor. Ours is soft, low in minerals.”

“It tastes like someone believes tomorrow will behave.”

He considered that, unsure if it was a compliment or a warning.

Back to the valve. He showed her how to blue-fit the disc to the seat, how a perfect mate showed an unbroken halo of indigo. “Metal remembers,” he said, his voice soft. He tapped the faint blue ring with his finger. “Our job is to remind it.”

She traced the blue line with a sooty fingertip. “Does it remember kindness?”

The question lodged itself under his ribs. His mentor, old Bramarck, had spoken in the same way, of tools as living contracts. “It remembers care,” he allowed. “Maybe that’s the same thing.”

They finished with the bonnet gasket, a circle of leather sealed with beeswax. She pressed it gently into place. “It feels like skin after a day in the sun.”

His pulse stumbled. He busied himself with finding the lock-nuts.

Outside, the district carried on with its life, the sound of hammers on anvils, the rumble of carts on the cobblestones, the distant shouts of children. But in the small, cluttered workshop, there was only the quiet focus of their shared task, the scent of hot metal and chicory, and the growing awareness of each other’s presence.

“Your resonance,” she said, her voice pulling him back from his thoughts. “It’s… different.”

He looked at her, surprised. “Different how?”

“It’s a deep well,” she said, her eyes searching his. “But you only draw from the top.”

He didn’t know how to answer that. He had always thought of his resonance as a tool, a finite resource to be carefully managed. He had never considered its depths.

“We could try something,” she said, her voice hesitant. “When the job’s done. A harmony. Two voices, one song.”

The image sent a jolt of heat through him. He cleared his throat. “The theory says it’s possible. I’ve never tried.”

“So let’s try,” she said, her voice practical as ever. She released the pipe, but her gaze lingered on his for a moment longer than necessary.

The afternoon slid into evening. They rebuilt the actuator spring, tested the seat tightness under compressed air, and marked the valve as “born again” on its tag. When they were finished, they stood for a moment in the quiet workshop, the rebuilt valve a testament to their shared skill, a symbol of the connection that had been forged between them in the heart of the crisis.

From the star’s infuriating, perfect symmetry, Leesa, the Goddess of Wholeness, shimmered into being. She saw the friction, the rub between the light and the dark, and all she wanted was to smooth it all out.

Chapter 4 – The Weight of the World

He swallowed. “I can do that.”

They walked through the deepening twilight, the air cooling around them, the sounds of the district fading into a soft hum. The communal hearth was a beacon of warmth and light, a gathering place where the day’s worries were shed like old coats. The aroma of roasting meat and fresh bread filled the air, a comforting embrace after the tension of the day.

Jhace found himself watching Tiffani as she moved through the crowd, her presence a quiet force. She spoke with Mrs. Kettner, her laughter a low, melodic sound that resonated deep within him. He realized, with a jolt, that he had never heard such a sound in the pump station, only the rhythmic clang of metal and the hiss of steam. This was a different kind of music, a human melody that was both foreign and deeply appealing.

When she returned, a plate piled high with food in her hands, she settled beside him on the bench. “So,” she said, her eyes sparkling, “tell me about the optimism in your dandelion.”

He found himself talking, truly talking, for the first time in years. He spoke of the subtle shifts in pressure, the faint tremors in the pipes that only he could feel, the way the station breathed and sighed. He spoke of the responsibility, the weight of 800 lives resting on his ability to keep the water flowing. He spoke of the solitude, the quiet communion with the machinery that had been his only constant companion.

She listened, her gaze unwavering, her presence a steady anchor in the swirling currents of his words. She understood. Not just the technical details, but the unspoken burden, the silent language of his dedication. She understood the resonance of his life, the deep, internal vibration that connected him to the very heart of the district.

“It’s a heavy song,” she said softly, when he had finished. “But you don’t sing it alone anymore.”

He looked at her, a sudden, fierce warmth spreading through his chest. “No,” he said, his voice thick with emotion. “Not anymore.”

Later, much later, as the communal hearth began to empty and the stars began to prickle the night sky, they walked back to the pump station. The air was crisp and cold, and the silence was no longer empty, but filled with the unspoken understanding that had grown between them.

“About the resonance,” she said, her voice a whisper in the quiet night. “You said it costs. The copper taste, the vertigo.”

He nodded. “It’s a draining. A pulling from your own core. Like drawing too much water from a shallow well.”

“But what if the well isn’t shallow?” she asked, her eyes meeting his in the dim light. “What if it’s connected to something deeper? Something shared?”

He thought of the shared rhythm in the pump station, the way their movements had synchronized, the way they had anticipated each other’s needs. He thought of the way her presence had warmed the cold brass, had made the impossible seem possible.

“I don’t know,” he admitted, his voice barely audible. “I’ve never considered it.”

“Then let’s consider it,” she said, her hand finding his in the darkness. Her touch was a spark, a jolt of pure, vibrant energy that resonated through him, a new kind of hum that was both familiar and utterly new. “Let’s find out how deep the well goes.”

And in that moment, under the vast, indifferent sky, Jhace felt a shift within him. A loosening of old fears, a blossoming of new possibilities. The world, which had always been a solitary machine, suddenly felt vast and interconnected, a symphony of shared vibrations, a resonance that was no longer just his own.

And from the simple, undeniable promise that all things eventually gotta break, Seeri, the Goddess of Decay, just showed up. The necessary rot.

Chapter 5 – Pressure Points

Morning shift had barely whistled when the district gates clanged open, a sound that echoed through Jhace’s bones with a familiar dread. Three strangers in slate-grey coats, their brass buttons polished to mirror the overcast sky, strode into Grindheim Dregs. Word traveled faster than condensate through a hot pipe: Confluence Compliance Inspectors, led by Chief Inspector Corren himself, bearer of clipboards and the power to red-tag any line he deemed “non-standard.”

Jhace spotted the delegation from the upper catwalk, his stomach dropping like a dropped wrench. Today of all days. The rebuilt main valve—his and Tiffani’s collaborative child, born of shared crisis and whispered expertise—sat ready for re-installation. Its bonnet, however, bore fresh bronze patches and a gasket leather cut to her pattern. Pretty, efficient, utterly un-certified. A perfect machine, but one that defied the rigid, unfeeling logic of their regulations.

Below, residents paused in their tasks, their movements slowing, their eyes tracking the grey coats with the same wary precision that pipes tracked pressure spikes. Mrs. Kettner clutched a dish-rag, her usual bustling energy stilled. Young Bram clanged the forge quiet, the rhythmic hammer blows replaced by an uneasy silence. Even the steam seemed to hush in anticipation, holding its breath.

Tiffani appeared at Jhace’s elbow, her presence a quiet anchor in the rising tide of his anxiety. She wiped coal-dust from her palms, her movements economical and precise. “Guests?” she asked, her voice low, a counterpoint to the growing tension.

“Inspectors,” he muttered, the word tasting like rust in his mouth. “They arrive when systems change. Our valve counts as change.” He felt a familiar tightening in his chest, the old, solitary burden of responsibility. This was his district, his problem.

Her green eyes narrowed, calculating, assessing the situation with the same cool logic she applied to a failing pressure gauge. “Then we present it together. You know code; I know frost-proofing. Between us, the truth holds.” Her words were not a question, but a statement of fact, a shared certainty. The memory of yesterday’s shared pulse under copper pipe, the way their hands had moved in unison, overrode his ingrained habit of solitary defense. Shared load. The words resonated with a new, powerful truth. He exhaled, a long, slow release of tension. “Together.”

They descended shoulder-to-shoulder, a silent, unified front, meeting the party at the base of the catwalk. The inspectors, a phalanx of starched collars and disapproving gazes, seemed to radiate a cold, sterile efficiency that clashed with the warm, living hum of the pump station. Jhace felt the familiar prickle of defiance, but this time, it was tempered by the steady, reassuring presence beside him. He was not alone in this fight for the first time in his life.

Chapter 5 (Continued) – Pressure Points

Corren, the chief inspector, a man whose face was as unyielding as a cast-iron plate, offered a curt nod. “Torrens. Heard you averted casualty. Admirable. Records, please.”

Jhace handed him the logbook, the ink still drying on Tiffani’s appended notes. Corren’s brow furrowed, a faint tick appearing above his left eye, as he flipped through the pages, his gaze snagging on the unfamiliar handwriting. “Who authorized non-regulation components?”

“I did,” Jhace and Tiffani answered, their voices overlapping, a surprising synchronicity that made them both start. Their words had locked into sync like twin governors, a testament to the unspoken rhythm that had begun to develop between them.

Corren’s eyebrow rose, a flicker of something unreadable in his eyes. “Concurrent signatures carry weight. Explain.”

Jhace began to articulate the technical rationale—pressure drop curves, micro-fissure propagation—but the words tangled in his throat under the inspector’s cold, unwavering stare. Tiffani stepped in, smooth as oil, her voice a calm, steady counterpoint to his faltering explanation.

“Chief, regulation seats failed three winters running. We implemented a bronze-leather composite tolerant to freeze-thaw cycles. Tested under 120 psi static, zero creep.” She produced the bench sheet, its neat columns filled with data, her initials beside his on every line, a silent testament to their shared labor.

Corren tapped the page with a manicured finger. “Confluence spec mandates solid brass.”

“Spec was written for districts above the frost-line,” she countered, her voice unwavering. “Down here, water memory includes ice. Leather remembers shape when metal forgets.”

A murmur rippled through the gathering crowd—apprentices, bakers, pipe-runners—everyday faces who trusted their morning water more than distant ink. Corren sensed it, his mouth compressing into a thin line. “Demonstrate.”

Jhace felt the world narrow to the valve waiting on its cradle nearby. Now or never. He glanced at Tiffani; she was already rolling her sleeves, her movements a silent promise of partnership.

They wheeled the assembly under the inspector’s nose. Jhace fitted the resonance cup to the pipe; Tiffani connected the pressure hand-pump, gauge, and bleed. Corren crossed his arms, his posture a challenge.

“Pressurize to operational,” Jhace said, his voice steady despite the tremor in his hands. “You’ll see a steady sixty psi, no oscillation.”

“And if it fails under witness?” Corren’s voice was laced with a hint of challenge.

“Then you red-tag my career,” Jhace answered, his throat dry. “And I’ll rebuild by dusk.” Tiffani’s fingers brushed his wrist, a light, grounding touch that sent a jolt of reassurance through him. “Won’t fail,” she murmured, her voice a soft hum against the clamor of his fear. “We built it to sing.”

They pressurized. The needle climbed—fifty, fifty-five, sixty—then parked, rock-steady. Silence stretched, broken only by the faint tick of expanding bronze. Corren’s skepticism wavered, a hairline crack appearing in his impassive facade. He reached to tap the gauge, a habitual gesture of testing.

Tiffani intercepted, her palm hovering, a silent shield. “With respect, sudden impact induces a false dip. Read static.” Corren hesitated, then nodded, acknowledging the truth of her words. He recorded the number instead. Around them, shoulders relaxed; someone cheered softly.
