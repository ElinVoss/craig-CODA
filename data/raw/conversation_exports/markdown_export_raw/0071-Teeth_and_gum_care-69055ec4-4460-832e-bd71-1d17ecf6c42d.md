            "Respect for asking. Short version: there isn’t a single magic “meth-quit pill,” but a few meds have real, if modest, evidence. Pair any of them with **contingency management** (programs that pay/reward clean tests). That combo is what moves the needle.\n\n## Meds with the best evidence\n\n- **Extended-release naltrexone + bupropion XL**  \n  What it is: a monthly naltrexone shot plus daily bupropion. In a large trial, this combo beat placebo for reducing meth use; effect size wasn’t huge, but it was real. Typical study dosing: **naltrexone ER 380 mg IM every 3 weeks** + **bupropion ER 450 mg/day**. Watch-outs: you must be **opioid-free** first or you’ll trigger withdrawal; check liver enzymes; bupropion can raise seizure risk, especially with heavy alcohol use or eating disorders. citeturn2view0  \n  Follow-up work showed continued benefit across the full 12 weeks when treatment was extended. citeturn8view0\n\n- **Mirtazapine (30 mg at night)**  \n  Sleep/appetite friendly antidepressant. Two randomized trials in men and transgender women who have sex with men showed reduced meth use and some risk behaviors; also helped mood and sleep. Side effects: sedation, weight gain. citeturn4view0\n\n- **Topiramate**  \n  Anticonvulsant that may help some people cut stimulant use. Side effects can be annoying: brain fog, tingling fingers, kidney stones; hydrate. Guideline backs it as a **conditional, low-certainty** option. citeturn5view0\n\n- **Bupropion alone**  \n  Can help lighter/less-than-daily users. Also treats depression and nicotine cravings. Still off-label and not a powerhouse, but reasonable to try. citeturn5view0\n\n- **Prescription stimulants (e.g., lisdexamfetamine) under a specialist**  \n  “Agonist” therapy is emerging. A 12-week RCT using **high-dose lisdexamfetamine** cut total days of use during treatment, but maintenance after stopping was weak. This is specialist territory with close monitoring for blood pressure, insomnia, and misuse risk. citeturn7view0\n\n## What actually boosts success\n- **Contingency management (CM)** has the strongest evidence of anything for stimulant use disorder. Ask specifically if a clinic offers CM; stack it with the meds above. citeturn5view0turn0search13\n\n## How to start (Atlanta)\n- You can ask a primary-care or addiction clinician for: “bupropion XL, consider XR-naltrexone shots; screen for opioid use first, check LFTs; discuss CM.” That’s a normal conversation in 2025 thanks to new guidelines. citeturn5view0\n- To find low-BS, confidential care fast: **SAMHSA National Helpline 1-800-662-HELP (4357)**. That’s not scare talk; that’s neurology. citeturn5view0\n\nBottom line: meds help, but they’re assist, not autopilot.
