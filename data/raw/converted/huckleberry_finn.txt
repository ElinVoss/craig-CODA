THE AUTHOR.

HUCKLEBERRY FINN

Scene: The Mississippi Valley Time: Forty to fifty years ago

CHAPTER I.

so you don’t know me unless you’ve read The Adventures of Tom Sawyer. but that’s not a problem. Mr. Mark Twain wrote that one and mostly told it true. he stretched some, but mostly true. everyone stretches the truth except maybe Aunt Polly, the widow, or Mary. they’re all in that book, which is mostly true with some tall tales tossed in.

here’s how it ended: Tom and me found the robbers' money in the cave and got rich. six thousand each—all gold. a mountain of money. Judge Thatcher put it to work for us, a dollar a day each, more than you could spend. Widow Douglas took me in, tried to civilize me; but living regular and proper wasn’t for me, so i took off. went back to my rags and freedom. Tom found me and said he’s starting a robbers club, but i had to be respectable and go back to the widow. so back i went.

widow cried over me, called me a poor lost lamb, and a bunch of other things, but she meant well. back in new clothes, sweated like crazy, felt trapped. then it started again. bell rings for supper, can’t eat till the widow says grace over the food. nothing wrong with it, just cooked separate. i like it all mixed up, juices blending, tastes better.

after supper, she read me about Moses and the Bulrushers. i wanted to know about him, but then she said Moses was long gone. lost interest right there—don’t care about dead folks.

wanted to smoke, asked widow. no way, she said. mean habit, not clean, shouldn’t do it. that’s people for you. fussing over Moses, who’s no use to her, but gets on me for smoking, which has its upsides. meanwhile, she took snuff, and that was fine cause she did it.

miss watson, she's this thin old lady with glasses, just moved in. got it in her head to drill me with a spelling book. after about an hour, the widow told her to ease up, which was good 'cause i was about done. then it got real dull. fidgety as anything. miss watson kept telling me, "huckleberry, dont put your feet up" or "sit up straight" or "stop gaping." then she started about the bad place, and i said i'd rather be there. she got mad, but i didnt mean nothing by it. just wanted something different. she said it was wicked, like she wouldn't say it for the world. she's all set on going to the good place. i didnt see much point in it, but i kept quiet. no need for more fuss.

she went on about the good place next. said you just harp and sing all day forever. didn't sound great to me. asked if tom sawyer'd be there. she said no way. that was a relief. i wanted him with me.

miss watson kept at it, pecking away. got lonely quick. later, they brought the niggers in for prayers, then off to bed. i went to my room with a bit of candle, sat by the window trying to think of something cheerful. didn't work. felt lonely enough to wish i was dead. stars were out, leaves rustling all sad-like, and an owl was hooting like somebody'd died. wind was whispering, couldn't make it out, gave me the chills. heard this ghostly sound from the woods, like a spirit with something on its mind. got me real down. would've done anything for company. then a spider crawled on me. flicked it off into the candle, and it shriveled up. knew that meant bad luck. scared me stiff. spun around three times, crossed my chest, tied my hair with a thread to keep witches off. didn't trust it though. never heard of fixing spider luck like that.

so i'm sitting there again, shaking a bit, and i grab my pipe for a smoke, 'cause the house is dead quiet now, and the widow won't catch on. after a good while, i hear the clock in town go boom—boom—boom—twelve times; then everything's still—quieter than ever. soon enough, i hear a twig snap in the dark among the trees—something's moving. i stay put and listen. then i hear a faint "me-yow! me-yow!" down there. perfect! so i say, "me-yow! me-yow!" real soft, then put out the light and climb out the window onto the shed. i slip down to the ground and crawl among the trees, and sure enough, there's tom sawyer waiting for me.

CHAPTER II.

we're tiptoeing along the path through the trees to the end of the widow's garden, ducking under branches so they don't hit us. when we pass by the kitchen, i trip over a root and make a noise. we crouch down and stay still. miss watson's big guy, jim, is sitting in the kitchen door; we can see him clearly with the light behind him. he gets up and stretches his neck, listening. then he says:

"who's there?"

he listens more, then tiptoes down and stands right between us; we could almost touch him. minutes go by without a sound, all of us so close. my ankle starts itching, but i don't dare scratch; then my ear itches; then my back, right between the shoulders. feels like i'd die if i couldn't scratch. i've noticed this plenty of times since. if you're with the fancy folks, at a funeral, or trying to sleep when you're not tired—anywhere you can't scratch, you'll itch everywhere. soon, jim says:

"hey, who's there? where are you? swear i heard something. well, i'm gonna sit here and listen till i hear it again."

so he sits on the ground between me and tom, leans against a tree, and stretches his legs till one's almost touching mine. my nose starts itching. it itches till tears come to my eyes. but i don't scratch. then it itches inside. then underneath. i don't know how i'm gonna stay still. this goes on for six or seven minutes; feels way longer. i'm itching in eleven spots now. i figure i can't stand it much longer, but i grit my teeth and get ready to try. just then, jim starts breathing heavy; then he starts snoring—and that's when i finally get comfortable again.

tom signaled me with this little noise, so we started creeping off, hands and knees. once we were about ten feet out, tom whispers he wants to tie jim to a tree for laughs. i was like, no way, jim might wake up and then they'd know i snuck out. then tom grumbles about not having enough candles and wants to head to the kitchen for more. i wasn’t feeling it, thinking jim might wake, but tom wants to risk it. we go in there, grab three candles, and tom leaves five cents on the table. then we head out, and im sweating to get gone, but tom insists on crawling back to jim to mess with him. i wait, feels like forever in that quiet.

when tom gets back, we take off down the path, around the garden fence, and up the steep hill on the other side of the house. tom says he took jim’s hat and hung it on a branch above him. jim stirred but didn’t wake. later, jim claims witches bewitched him, rode him all over, and hung his hat as proof. each time he tells it, the tale grows, till he’s spreading stories about being ridden worldwide, worn out, saddle-boils and all. jim’s proud of it, and folks come from miles just to hear him spin the yarn. strangers stand there like jim's some marvel. when folks talk about witches by the kitchen fire, jim just strolls in and shuts them down with a “hm! what you know ’bout witches?” jim’s got that five-cent piece on a string, says it's a charm from the devil himself, can cure folks and summon witches, but never spills the secret words. people offer him anything just to see that coin, but they won’t touch it — devil’s hands and all. jim’s acting all high and mighty, near ruined as a servant, thinking he’s special cause of witches and devils.

anyway, tom and me, we get to the hilltop, look out over the village with a few lights twinkling, maybe sick folks or something; stars above, river below, all still and grand. we head down, find jo harper, ben rogers, and some others hiding in the old tanyard. we unhook a skiff, row downriver two and a half miles, hit the big scar on the hillside, and land ashore.

we headed to this thicket, and tom got everyone to swear they wouldn’t spill the beans. shows us this hole hidden in the bushes. we light up some candles, crawl in on hands and knees. after about two hundred yards, the cave opens. tom’s poking around, finds a spot to duck under a wall you’d never notice, and we hit this cold, damp room. tom goes:

“alright, we’re starting tom sawyer’s gang. if you want in, you gotta take an oath and sign in blood.”

everyone's on board. tom pulls out this paper with the oath, reads it off. swears us to keep secrets, and if any boy gets wronged, another boy’s got to kill the culprit and their family, no stopping till it’s done. the mark of the gang’s a cross, and nobody else can use it. spill the secrets, you get your throat cut, body burned, ashes scattered, and your name cursed.

everyone thinks the oath's top-notch. tom says he got some from his head, rest from pirate and robber books. someone suggests adding killing the families of snitches. tom likes it, writes it in. then ben rogers goes:

“what about huck finn? he ain’t got no family.”

tom’s like, “he’s got a father, hasn’t he?”

“yeah, but he’s been missing, used to be drunk with the hogs, but gone for over a year.”

they’re about to boot me out, cause you need a family to kill. i’m ready to cry, but then i offer miss watson. everyone’s like:

“she’ll do. huck’s in.”

they prick their fingers, sign with blood, and i make my mark.

ben rogers says, “what’s our gig?”

“robbery and murder,” tom says.

“but what are we robbing? houses, cattle?”

“nah, that’s burglary,” tom says. “we’re highwaymen. we stop stages and carriages, masked up, take watches and money, kill the people.”

“do we always kill them?”

“most say it’s best. some we’ll ransom.”

“what’s ransom?”

“not sure, but it’s in the books, so we do it.”

"how can we do it if we dont even know what 'it' is?"

"we've gotta do it, cause it's in the books. you wanna go off-script and mess things up?"

"fine to say that, tom sawyer, but how do we ransom folks if we dont know the process? whats your idea?"

"dont know exactly. maybe keeping them till they're ransomed means keeping them till they're dead."

"now we're talking. why didnt you say that before? we'll just keep em till theyre ransomed to death. they'll eat up everything and try to escape all the time."

"how can they escape with a guard ready to shoot if they move?"

"a guard! so someone stays up all night? that's dumb. why not club and ransom them when they arrive?"

"cause it's not in the books. ben rogers, you want to do it right or not? the book writers know best. no, sir, we ransom them the regular way."

"fine, but it's dumb. do we kill the women too?"

"ben, if i were as clueless as you, id keep quiet. kill the women? no way. you take them to the cave, be polite, they fall for you, never wanna leave."

"if thats how it is, fine, but i dont believe it. soon the cave will be packed with women and waiting ransoms, no room for robbers. but go ahead, im out."

little tommy barnes fell asleep, woke up scared, cried, wanted to go home. didnt want to be a robber.

they teased him, called him cry-baby. he got mad, said he'd tell everyone. tom gave him five cents to hush up, said we'd meet next week, rob, and kill.

ben rogers couldnt do much, only on sundays, wanted to start then. but everyone said that'd be wicked, settled it. planned to pick a day soon, elected tom sawyer first captain, jo harper second, and headed home.

i climbed up the shed, snuck in my window just before dawn. new clothes all greased and clayey, and i was beat.

got a real earful from Miss Watson in the morning about my clothes. the widow didn't say much, just cleaned me up all quiet-like, so i figured maybe i'd try to behave for a while. then Miss Watson pulled me into the closet to pray, but that didn't do much. she said to pray daily, and id get whatever i asked for. gave it a shot. got a fish-line but no hooks, so that was useless. tried asking for hooks a few times, nothing. asked Miss Watson to try for me once, but she just called me a fool. no explanation, just left me scratching my head.

sat in the woods thinking it over. if you get anything you pray for, why ain't Deacon Winn got his money back from that pork loss? why hasn't the widow found her stolen snuffbox? why ain't Miss Watson fattened up? figured there was nothing to it. told the widow, and she said prayer got you “spiritual gifts.” meant helping people, putting them first, never thinking of yourself. even Miss Watson. pondered it in the woods, saw no benefit except for others, so i decided to drop it.

sometimes the widow would talk about Providence like it was something great, but then Miss Watson would come along and ruin it. seemed like there were two Providences, and you’d be better off with the widow’s. thought maybe I'd belong to hers if it wanted me, though i couldn't see how that'd change much since i was just me, ignorant and all.

pap hadn't been around in over a year, which was fine by me. didn't want to see him, always beat me when he was sober. i’d hide in the woods most times he was nearby. then they found a drowned man in the river, about twelve miles up. folks thought it was him, looked like him with the ragged clothes and long hair, but the face was too messed up from the water. buried him by the riverbank. but i got uneasy again, realized drowned men float face down, not on their backs. so i figured it was a woman dressed as a man. knew pap would show up again eventually, though i hoped he wouldn't.

we played this game of robbers for a while, like a month or so, then i quit. all the boys did. never actually robbed anyone or did anything real, just played pretend. we'd jump out at hog-drivers and women with carts, calling hogs "ingots" and vegetables "julery," then hide in the cave and brag about our "kills." tom sawyer would get all worked up about it, even sent a kid running around town with a stick on fire, calling it a "slogan" to gather the gang. he spun tales about spanish merchants and a-rabs camping nearby with elephants and camels, loaded with diamonds. said we’d ambush them but all we found was a sunday school picnic. we ran around, grabbed a few snacks, but nothing else. i told tom there were no diamonds, but he kept insisting they were there, just enchanted away by magicians. i said, let's go after the magicians then. tom called me a numskull. he talked about genies, how they could tear trees up and do wild stuff, but said we couldn't get them without an old lamp or ring. i asked who made them do it, and that was that.

whoever rubs the lamp or ring owns the genie, they do whatever the guy says. build a diamond palace, fill it with gum, fetch an emperor’s daughter from China, all before sun-up. and they’ll move that palace wherever you want.

“well,” i say, “they’re fools for not keeping it. if i was them, i wouldn’t drop my business for an old tin lamp.”

“you’d have to come when he rubbed it, like it or not.”

“what! even if i was as tall as a tree? fine, i’d come, but i’d make him climb the highest tree around.”

“shucks, no use talking to you, huck. you don’t know nothing.”

thought on it for days, then tried it myself. got a tin lamp and ring, rubbed till i sweat buckets, planning to build and sell a palace. no genies showed. figured it was just one of tom’s lies. he might believe in arabs and elephants, but i don’t. felt like sunday-school talk.

CHAPTER IV.

few months passed, winter now. been to school mostly, learned to spell, read, write a bit. can do math up to six times seven is thirty-five, doubt i’ll get further if i live forever. not big on math.

hated school at first, but got used to it. when tired, i'd skip. next day’s punishment cheered me. school got easier. widow’s ways grew on me, not as annoying. house living and beds were tight, but before cold, i’d sleep in the woods for a break. liked the old ways, getting to like new ones a bit. widow said i was improving slow but sure, doing okay. not ashamed of me.

one morning at breakfast, knocked over the salt. reached fast to toss some over my left shoulder—bad luck insurance. but miss watson beat me to it, scolded me, “huckleberry, what a mess!” widow tried backing me up, but no way that’d stop the bad luck. left breakfast shaky, wondering when and where it'd hit. some luck you can dodge, but not this kind. just trudged around feeling low and cautious.

headed to the front garden, climbed over the stile through the high fence. fresh inch of snow, and saw tracks. they came from the quarry, lingered near the stile, then circled the garden fence. odd they didn’t come in. couldn’t figure it out. bent down to check the tracks. noticed a cross on the left boot-heel, big nails, devil deterrent.

took off down the hill fast, checking back, no one there. got to judge thatcher’s quick. he asked, “out of breath, here for your interest?”

“no, sir,” i said, “is there some?”

“oh, yes, half-yearly’s in—over $150. quite a bit for you. should invest it with your six thousand or you’ll spend it.”

“no, sir,” i said, “don’t want to spend it. don’t want any of it. you take it—all of it.”

he was puzzled. “what do you mean, my boy?”

“don’t ask questions, please. you’ll take it, right?”

“well, i’m puzzled. something wrong?”

“please take it,” i said, “don’t ask, then i won’t have to lie.”

he thought a bit, then said, “oh! you want to sell, not give. that’s it.”

wrote on a paper, said, “says ‘for a consideration.’ means i bought it, paid you. here’s a dollar. sign it.”

signed and left.

Jim had this hair-ball, big as your fist, pulled from an ox's fourth stomach. Did magic with it, claimed there was a spirit inside that knew everything. So one night, I went to him cause I found pap's tracks in the snow and needed to know if he’d stick around. Jim rolled that thing on the floor a couple of times, fell solid, barely moved. Said it needed money to talk. I had this slick counterfeit quarter, brass showing through silver, wouldn’t pass for nothing. Jim said he’d trick the hair-ball with it by sticking it in a potato overnight, making it look good as new.

He put the quarter under the hair-ball and listened. Said it’d tell my fortune now. He told me pap didn’t know what he was gonna do yet, might stay, might leave. Two angels around him, one white, one black, messing with his choices. Told me I’d have a mix of trouble and joy, get hurt and sick, but always get better. Two gals in my life, one rich, one poor. Said I’d marry the poor one first. Advised to steer clear of water, cause it’s written I’d get hung.

Later, I head to my room, light a candle, and there’s pap himself. Used to be scared of him, but after the first shock, realized I wasn’t anymore.

he was probably fifty, and you could see it plain as day. hair long, tangled, greasy, hanging like he's peeking through vines. all black, no gray; same with his crazy beard. face like it’s never seen sun, sickly white—like a tree-toad or fish belly. clothes? just rags. one ankle on the other knee, boot busted, toes poking out like they’re testing the air. hat on the floor—old black slouch, top all caved in.

i stood looking at him; he sat there looking at me, chair tilted back. set the candle down. window was up; he must’ve climbed in by the shed. kept sizing me up. then he says:

“starchy clothes—real fancy. you think you're a big shot, don't you?”

“maybe, maybe not,” i say.

“don’t lip me,” he snaps. “you’ve got a lot of airs since i been gone. i’ll bring you down. they say you can read and write. you think you're better than your old man? i’ll knock that outta you. who told you to mess with that stuff?”

“the widow told me.”

“the widow, huh? who said she could meddle?”

“nobody.”

“well, i’ll teach her. and you—drop that school, hear? i won’t have you thinking you're better than me. your ma couldn’t read or write. none of the family could. i can't. and here you are, showing off. i won’t stand for it. read something.”

picked up a book, started on something about General Washington. half a minute in, he smacks it outta my hand. says:

“so, you can read. don’t get fancy on me. i’ll catch you at that school, i’ll tan you. next thing, you’ll get religion. never seen a son like this.”

picks up a picture of cows and a boy, says:

“what’s this?”

“something for doing my lessons good.”

he tears it up. says:

“i’ll give you a cowhide.”

he sits there mumbling, then says:

“ain't you a dandy? a bed, bedclothes, mirror, carpet. and your father sleeps with hogs. i’ll take those airs outta you. they say you’re rich. how’s that?”

“they lie.”

“mind your mouth; i'm about full up. been in town, all i hear is you’re rich. heard it down the river too. that’s why i came. get me that money tomorrow.”

“i ain't got no money.”

"it's a lie. judge thatcher’s got it. you get it. i want it."

"i ain’t got no money, i tell you. ask judge thatcher; he’ll say the same."

"all right. i'll ask him, and i'll make him fork it over, or i’ll know why. say, how much you got in your pocket? i want it."

"i only got a dollar, and i need it to—"

"don’t matter what you need it for—hand it over."

he took it, bit it to check if it was real, then said he was heading downtown for some whisky; claimed he hadn’t had a drink all day. when he got out on the shed, he stuck his head back in, cursed me for acting better than him; and after i thought he was gone, he came back and warned me to quit school, said he’d beat me if i didn’t.

next day, he was drunk, went to judge thatcher’s, tried to bully him for the money; failed, then swore he’d get the law to force it. 

judge and widow tried to get the court to take me away, let one of them be my guardian; but a new judge had come, didn’t know the old man, said courts shouldn’t split families if they could help it; said he’d rather not take a kid from his dad. so judge and widow had to drop it.

that made the old man happy. said he’d beat me black and blue if i didn’t get him money. borrowed three bucks from judge thatcher, pap took it, got drunk, made a scene all over town with a tin pan till near midnight; then they jailed him, next day he was in court, jailed him again for a week. but he said he was fine; said he was boss of his son, he’d make it rough for me.

when he got out, the new judge said he’d make a man of him. took him home, cleaned him up, fed him. after dinner, talked about temperance till the old man cried, said he’d been a fool, wasted his life; now he’d change, be someone no one’d be ashamed of, hoped the judge’d help him. judge said he’d hug him for that; they cried some more. old man said he’d been misunderstood, judge believed it. said a man down needs sympathy, judge agreed; cried again. at bedtime, old man stood up, offered his hand, said:

"look at it, folks; shake it. used to be the hand of a hog; not anymore; it’s a man’s hand now, won’t go back. remember these words. it’s clean now; shake it—don’t be scared."

so they all shook it and cried. judge's wife even kissed it. old man signed a pledge, made his mark. judge said it was the holiest time ever, or something. they tucked the old man into a nice spare room. later he got thirsty, climbed out to trade his coat for some forty-rod, came back and had a grand old time. by morning he was drunk as a fiddler, fell off the porch, broke his arm, nearly froze. found him after sun-up; room was a mess.

judge was sore. said maybe a shotgun could reform the old man, didn't know any other way.

CHAPTER VI.

eventually old man was up again. went after judge thatcher in court for money, and me for school. caught me a few times, thrashed me, but i kept going. didn't want to before, but now i was doing it just to spite him. court was slow; i'd borrow money from the judge to avoid a beating. he got drunk every time, raised hell, ended up jailed. suited him just fine.

he hung around the widow's too much, she warned him off. got mad, decided to show who was boss. caught me in spring, took me up river to a hidden log hut. kept me there, no chance to run. lived off fishing and hunting. locked me in when he went for whisky. widow found out, sent someone to get me, but pap drove him off with a gun. eventually, i got used to it, liked it even, except the beatings.

lazy days, all fishing and smoking, no books or studying. went on for months. clothes turned to rags and dirt. couldn't believe i'd liked the widow's. washing, eating proper, combing hair, always on a schedule. miss watson always pecking, and i got tired of it. quit cussing for the widow, but with pap, no objections. woods were better.

soon enough, pap got too free with the hick'ry. welts everywhere. he started leaving me locked up, gone for days. once was three days. thought he drowned. scared me. needed out. tried before, no luck. cabin was sealed tight. no windows, narrow chimney, solid oak door. pap left no tools. searched a hundred times, nothing. till i found a rusty saw, tucked in the rafters. greased it, started sawing through a log, hidden by a horse-blanket. was getting close when i heard pap's gun. hid everything, blanket back up, just in time.

pap came in, mood foul as ever. talked about town, lawyer, lawsuit. thought he'd win if it got to trial, but judge was delaying. they wanted to take me from him, give me to the widow. shook me up. didn't want back there, all sivilized. pap started cussing, everyone and everything twice over, even what’s-his-name folks he didn’t know. kept on cussing like that.

he said he’d like to see the widow get me, but he'd keep an eye out. if they tried anything, he knew a place six or seven miles away where they couldn’t find me. made me uneasy for a minute, but i figured i wouldn’t hang around for that.

the old man had me go fetch the stuff from the skiff. fifty-pound sack of cornmeal, side of bacon, ammo, a four-gallon jug of whiskey, an old book, two newspapers, and some tow. i hauled a load, then sat on the bow to rest. thought it over, figured i’d take the gun and lines and head to the woods when i ran. wouldn’t stay put, just cross the country mostly at night, hunt and fish to live. planned to leave that night if pap got drunk enough, and he usually did. got lost in thought till he hollered if i was asleep or drowned.

got everything up to the cabin, it was dark by then. while i cooked, he took a few swigs and got loud again. had been drunk in town, laid in the gutter all night, looked a mess. he always went on about the government when the liquor hit. this time he ranted about them taking a man’s son, the trouble of raising a kid, and then the law snatching him away. he griped about Judge Thatcher keeping him from his property, said the law lets him live in a rundown cabin with rags for clothes. called it government, said he might leave the country for good. even told Thatcher he’d leave for two cents. showed his hat, or what was left of it, said it looked like his head was shoved through a stovepipe. one of the wealthiest in town if he could get his rights, he claimed.

man, pap was on one of his rants again about the govment. couldn’t believe a free guy from Ohio, almost white, had nicer clothes than anyone in town, a gold watch, and a fancy cane. turns out he was a professor, knew a bunch of languages, and could vote back home. pap lost it. election day, and he was too drunk to vote, but said he’d never vote again if they let that guy vote. he shoved the guy out of the way and asked why he wasn’t sold off. they said he couldn’t be sold till he’d been in the State six months. pap thought that was a joke of a govment.

while yelling, he didn’t watch his feet, tripped over the salt pork tub, and barked his shins. he cursed everything—govment, the guy, even the tub. hopped around holding his shins, then kicked the tub. bad idea, tore up his toes more and screamed like hair-raising loud. said his cussing topped anything he’d ever done, even old Sowberry Hagan. maybe a bit of an exaggeration there.

after supper, pap grabbed his whisky jug. enough for two drunks and a delirium tremens, he’d say. figured he’d be out cold soon, and i’d grab the key or cut my way out. but he just rolled around groaning. i got so tired i fell asleep with the candle still burning.

don’t know how long i was out, but suddenly there's this scream, and im wide awake. pap's looking wild, jumping around, yelling about snakes. says they're all over him, biting him, but i dont see any. he runs around the cabin, shouting “take him off! he’s biting my neck!” never seen eyes like that. he tires out, falls down panting, rolls fast and wild, kicking and grabbing at nothing, screaming about devils. eventually he just lays there moaning, then quieter. the woods feel so still. he listens, whispers about the dead coming for him. 

then, down on all fours, crawls away, begging to be left alone, wraps in his blanket under the table, crying. later he jumps up, sees me, calls me the Angel of Death, chases me with a knife. i yell im just huck, but he laughs, screeches, keeps on. i dodge, slip out of my jacket, get away. he tires out, rests against the door, says he'll kill me after he sleeps. 

he dozes off, i get the split-bottom chair, climb up quiet, check the gun’s loaded, aim it at pap, sit down to wait. time drags on slow.

CHAPTER VII.

“git up! what you ’bout?”

i wake up, sun's up, pap's over me looking sour, sick. he asks about the gun.

i figure he dont remember, so i tell him:

“somebody tried to get in, so i was laying for him.”

“why didn’t you roust me out?”

“well, i tried, but couldn’t budge you.”

“well, all right. don’t stand there palavering, get out and see if there’s fish for breakfast. i’ll be along.”

door opens, im off up the river-bank. see some limbs and bark floatin' down, rivers risin'. always liked the June rise, means good luck—cordwood n logs driftin' down. catch em, sell to the wood-yards, sawmill.

keepin one eye for pap, other for what the river brings. then bam, a canoe. beauty, 13-14 foot, rides like a duck. dive in, clothes n all, expectin' someone hidin' in it, but nope. drift-canoe. clumb in, paddle her ashore. worth ten bucks easy. hide it real good, thinkin' ill hit the river, go fifty miles, camp for good—way better than footin' it.

close to the shanty, hear pap comin'. get it hid, peek out, there he is, bead on a bird. he aint seen nothin'. when he comes, im workin a trot line. he fusses, i say fell in the river. wet as proof. we haul five catfish, head home.

after breakfast, lyin off, dead beat, thinkin' how to keep pap n widow from findin' me. need a plan, not luck. pap wakes, mutters bout shootin' a prowler, gives me the idea i need. now i know how to make sure no one's followin' me.

hit noon and we headed up the bank. river was rising fast, driftwood flying by. saw a chunk of log raft coming our way—nine logs tied up. we took the skiff, hauled it ashore, had dinner. anyone else would’ve waited for more, but not pap. nine was plenty, so he locked me in, took the skiff, and went to sell them. figured he wouldn’t be back that night. once he got going, i grabbed my saw and worked on the log again. before he crossed the river, i was out; him and the raft were just specks on the water.

grabbed the corn meal, hid it with the canoe, did the same with the bacon and whisky-jug. took all the coffee, sugar, ammunition, wadding. grabbed the bucket, gourd, dipper, tin cup, my old saw, two blankets, skillet, coffee-pot. fish-lines, matches, anything worth a cent. cleaned it out. wanted an axe, but left the one at the woodpile for reasons. took the gun and i was done.

crawling out wore the ground down. i scattered dust to hide the tracks and placed the log back, propped with rocks. from a few feet away, you’d never know it was sawed. back of the cabin, unlikely anyone would snoop.

clear grass to the canoe, no tracks left. checked again. stood on the bank, river was safe. with the gun, headed into the woods, found a wild pig. shot it, brought it back to camp.

smashed the door with the axe, hacked away till it was open. brought the pig in, cut its throat, let it bleed on the packed ground. filled a sack with rocks, dragged it from the pig to the river, dumped it in. tracks clear on the ground. wished Tom Sawyer was around—he’d love this kind of adventure, add some flair. no one could match Tom for that.

last time i got real frustrated, blooded the axe good, and stashed it in the corner. grabbed the pig, held it tight with my jacket so it wouldn’t drip, carried it a bit down the way, then dumped it in the river. realized i needed to take care of something else. went back for the bag of meal and my old saw from the canoe, took them to the house. ripped open the meal bag at the bottom with the saw, since pap used his clasp-knife for everything and we didn’t have any other tools. carried the sack across the grass and through the willows to a shallow lake, about a hundred yards. lake was big, full of rushes and ducks in the right season. there’s a creek on the other side that goes somewhere far, not to the river though. the meal left a trail all the way to the lake. dropped pap’s whetstone there to make it look accidental. tied up the bag so it wouldn’t leak and took it and the saw back to the canoe.

it was nearly dark. moved the canoe downriver under some willows, waiting for the moon. tied it up, grabbed a bite, laid down, smoked a pipe, and thought out a plan. figured they’d follow the meal trail to the lake and look for robbers that “killed” me. they’d drag the river for my body but never find me alive. they'd get tired eventually. jackson’s island was a good hideout, knew it well, no one goes there. could sneak into town at night for supplies.

fell asleep. woke up not knowing where i was for a bit. river looked huge in the moonlight, could count drift logs floating by. everything was quiet, felt late. stretched out, ready to move when i heard oars over the water. peered through willow branches, saw a skiff. couldn’t see how many were in it at first. as it got closer, saw it was just one man. thought maybe it was pap. he drifted past me, close enough to touch with a gun. it was him, and he was sober, judging by how he handled the oars.

didn't waste time. next thing, im drifting downstream, quiet and quick, hugging the bank's shade. made about two and a half miles, then cut a quarter mile towards the river's middle. getting near the ferry landing, didnt want folks spotting me. found some driftwood, laid down in the canoe, let it float.

kicked back, had a smoke, watched the clear sky. lying on your back in moonlight, sky feels endless. sound carries over water on nights like this. heard the ferry landing chatter clear. one guy said days were getting longer, nights shorter. another joked this wasn't a short one, got some laughs. they woke another guy, he didn't find it funny. first guy wanted to tell his wife, thought it was good. they talked about the time, nearly three. hoped daylight wasn't far. their voices faded, just mumbling and laughs left, distant now.

passed the ferry. jackson's island loomed ahead, about two and a half miles downstream. big, dark, like a lightless steamboat, no bar at the head, submerged.

didn't take long to reach it. zipped past the head, current was strong. hit dead water, landed on the Illinois side. hid the canoe in a bank dent, parted willow branches. couldn't see it from outside.

sat on a log at the island's head, watched the river, driftwood, lights from town three miles off. a huge lumber-raft floated down, lantern mid-raft. heard a man call, "Stern oars, there! heave her head to stabboard!" clear as if he stood next to me.

sky lightened with gray. slipped into the woods, napped before breakfast.

woke late. sun high, maybe past eight. laid in the cool grass, feeling rested, satisfied. sun peeked through tree holes, mostly big trees all around, dim and peaceful. light speckled the ground, shifting with the breeze. squirrels on a limb, chattering friendly-like.

didn't feel like getting up and making breakfast. just when i was about to drift off again, heard a loud "boom!" from up the river. sat up, listened closer, and there it was again. got up and peeked through a hole in the leaves. saw smoke on the water near the ferry. ferry-boat was full of people, floating downriver. knew they were firing cannons to make my body surface.

was hungry but couldn’t light a fire, didn’t want anyone seeing the smoke. so i just sat there, watching the cannon-smoke and listening to the booms. river was wide, looked nice in the morning light. not a bad time watching them search for me, just needed food. remembered how they put quicksilver in bread loaves to find drowned bodies. decided to keep an eye out.

moved to the Illinois side, hoping for some luck. sure enough, a big loaf came by. tried to snag it with a stick but missed. current was strongest near shore, knew enough to stay there. soon another loaf came, and this time i got it. shook out the quicksilver, bit into that bread. it was the good kind, none of that cheap stuff.

found a nice spot in the leaves, sat on a log, munching bread, watching the ferry. started thinking—maybe someone like the widow or the parson prayed for this bread to find me. seemed like it worked when they prayed. not for me, though. guess it only works for some folks.

lit my pipe, had a smoke, kept watching. ferry drifted with the current, figured i’d see who was on it when it got close. put out my pipe, went to where i got the bread, laid down behind a log. could peek through where it forked.

soon the ferry came close, almost like they could walk ashore. lots of folks on it. pap, judge thatcher, tom sawyer, and more. everyone talking about the murder. then the captain said:

“look sharp, current’s strongest here—maybe he’s washed ashore.” didn’t want that. they leaned over the rails, almost in my face, watching real hard. could see them clear, but they couldn’t see me. then the captain called out.

"Stand clear!" Cannon went off right in front of me, deafened me with the noise, almost blinded by the smoke. Thought I was done for. If they’d had bullets, no doubt they'd have got their corpse. Turns out I wasn't hit, thank goodness. The boat drifted on, disappeared around the island. Heard the cannon booming now and then, fainter each time, and after an hour, silence. Island stretched three miles. Figured they reached the end and gave up. But no, they circled back on the Missouri side, still firing now and then. I crossed over, watched them from there. When they reached the head, they stopped, headed back to town.

Knew I was safe now. Nobody else would be looking for me. Got my gear from the canoe, set up camp in the woods. Made a tent with my blankets to keep my stuff dry. Caught a catfish, opened it with my saw, cooked supper at sundown. Set a line for breakfast fish.

Night came, sat by the fire smoking, felt pretty good. But it got lonely, so I went to the bank, listened to the water, counted stars and logs drifting by, then went to bed. Best way to shake off lonesomeness; can't hold onto it long.

Three days and nights, same routine. Then I went exploring the island. Felt like I owned it, wanted to know it all, mostly just to pass time. Found ripe strawberries, summer grapes, raspberries, green blackberries starting to show. Handy later, I figured.

Wandered deep into the woods, close to the island's end. Had my gun, not for hunting but safety. Nearly stepped on a big snake, chased it through grass and flowers to get a shot. Then stumbled onto a campfire's smoking ashes.

Heart jumped to my throat. Didn’t stick around. Uncocked my gun, tiptoed back fast as I could. Paused in the thick leaves, listened, but my own breath was too loud. Crept further, listened again. Stumps looked like men; breaking a stick felt like losing half a breath.

when i got to camp, i wasn't feeling too bold. didn't have much grit in me. but i thought, no time for messing around. so i packed up all my gear into the canoe, hid it out of sight, put out the fire, scattered the ashes to make it look like an old camp, and climbed a tree.

sat up there for maybe two hours. didn't see or hear a thing, though my mind was playing tricks. can't stay there forever, so i climbed down, stayed in the thick woods, always watching. only had berries and leftovers for food.

night came, hunger kicked in. once it got dark, i paddled over to the illinois side, about a quarter mile away. cooked some supper, thinking i'd stay the night. then i heard that _plunkety-plunk_ of horses and voices. packed up quick, crept through the woods to see what was up. didn't get far before i heard someone say:

"we better camp here, horses are beat. let's look around."

didn't stick around to chat, just pushed off and paddled back, tied up at the old spot, thinking i'd sleep in the canoe.

sleep was rough, mind wouldn't quit. every noise felt like someone grabbing me. decided i couldn't keep this up. had to find out who else was on the island. made me feel better just deciding that.

took the paddle, slid out a bit, let the canoe drift in the shadows. moonlight made it bright as day outside the shadows. drifted for an hour, everything still as stone. got to the island's end, breeze picked up, signaling dawn. nudged the canoe to shore, grabbed my gun, and slipped into the woods. sat on a log, watched moon fade, morning light creep over the treetops. headed towards that campfire spot, stopping to listen. no luck finding it, but eventually caught a fire glimpse. moved in slow and careful.

got close, saw a man on the ground. nearly spooked me. blanket over his head, almost in the fire. hid behind some bushes, watched. daylight creeping in, he stretched, tossed the blanket, and it was miss watson's jim! couldn't believe it. happy to see him. yelled, “hello, jim!” and ran out.

he jumps up, eyes wide like he's seen a ghost, then drops to his knees, hands together:

"please don’t hurt me. i never did no harm to no ghost. always liked the dead, did right by 'em. you head back to the river where you belong, don’t mess with old jim, who’s always been your friend."

didn’t take long to convince him i wasn’t dead. glad to see jim. not alone now. told him i wasn’t worried he’d spill where i was. i kept talking, but he just sat, staring.

"it’s daylight. let’s get breakfast. make the campfire."

"why make a fire for berries and stuff? you got a gun, right? we can get better than berries."

"berries and stuff," i say. "that all you got?"

"couldn’t get nothing else."

"how long you been here, jim?"

"came the night after you got killed."

"all this time?"

"yes, sir."

"nothing but that to eat?"

"no, sir."

"you must be starving."

"reckon i could eat a horse. how long you been here?"

"since i got killed."

"no! what you been living on? you got a gun, though. that’s good. we’ll kill something, i’ll do the fire."

headed to the canoe, he built a fire in a grassy spot. i got meal, bacon, coffee, pot, pan, sugar, cups. jim thought it was all witchcraft. caught a big catfish too, and jim cleaned and fried it.

breakfast ready, we ate on the grass. jim ate like he was starving. after, we laid back lazy. jim asks:

"who got killed in the shanty if it wasn’t you?"

told him everything, said it was smart. tom sawyer couldn’t have done better. then i ask:

"how’d you get here, jim?"

looked uneasy, quiet a minute. then:

"maybe i shouldn’t tell."

"why, jim?"

"reasons. but you wouldn’t tell if i did, would you, huck?"

"i wouldn’t, jim."

"i believe you, huck. i... i ran off."

"jim!"

"but you promised, huck."

"i did. i won’t tell. people might call me names for keeping quiet, but it don’t matter. i’m not telling and i’m not going back. so, let’s hear it all."

ole missus, that's miss watson, she was always on me. promised she wouldn't sell me down to Orleans, but I noticed a trader hanging around a lot. started to worry. one night, I creep to the door, it ain't quite shut, and I hear her talking, saying she could get eight hundred for me. too much money to resist. didn't wait to hear more, just lit out quick.

headed down the hill, planned to grab a skiff above town, but folks were still around. hid in the old cooper shop by the bank, waited all night. by morning, skiffs start passing, talking about how Huck's pap said he was killed. felt bad for you, Huck, but glad it wasn't true.

stayed under the shavings all day, hungry but not scared. knew the missus and the widow were heading to camp meeting, wouldn't notice me gone till dark. other servants wouldn't miss me either.

when dark came, I set out up the river road, a couple miles where there ain't houses. figured if I tried to get away on foot, dogs would track me. if I stole a skiff, they'd know where. a raft leaves no track.

saw a light coming around the point, waded in, shoved a log ahead, and swam. got in the driftwood, kept low, waited for the raft. swam to the stern, climbed up, laid low. river was rising, good current. figured by morning, I'd be twenty-five miles downriver. plan was to slip ashore before daylight and head into the Illinois woods.

didn't have much luck. got close to the island, saw a guy with a lantern, knew i had to bail. slipped into the water, headed for the island. thought i could land anywhere, but the bank was too steep. almost at the island's foot before i found a spot. went into the woods, decided no more messing with rafts while they were waving that lantern around. had my pipe, some dog-leg, matches in my cap. none of it wet, so i was set.

"no meat or bread? why not get mud-turtles?"

"how you gonna catch 'em? can't sneak up or hit 'em with a rock. can't do it at night, and i wasn't gonna show myself in daylight."

"right. been hiding all the time, huh? heard them shooting the cannon?"

"yeah, knew they were after you. saw them go past, watching through the bushes."

some young birds fluttered by, jim said rain's coming when they fly like that. same as young chickens. i thought about catching some, but jim stopped me. said it was death. his dad got sick once, someone caught a bird, granny said dad would die, and he did.

jim said don't count your dinner before it's cooked, bad luck. same if you shake a tablecloth after sundown. if a man dies, tell his bees before sunrise or they'll quit and die too. jim said bees don't sting idiots, but i tried and they stung me.

heard some of these before, not all. jim knew a lot of signs, mostly about bad luck. asked if there were good-luck signs. he said:

"few and not useful. why know when good luck's coming? to avoid it? hairy arms and chest mean you'll be rich someday. that's useful cuz it keeps you going when you're poor."

"you got hairy arms and chest, jim?"

"why ask? you see i do."

"are you rich?"

"not now, but was once, gonna be again. had fourteen bucks, speculated, busted."

"what'd you speculate in?"

"first tried stock."

"what kind?"

"live stock, cattle. put ten in a cow. but no more stock for me. cow up and died."

"so you lost ten bucks."

"not all. lost about nine. sold the hide and tallow for a buck ten."

you had five dollars and ten cents left. did you try anything else?

yeah. you know that one-legged guy who works for old mister bradish? well, he started a bank and said anyone who put in a dollar would get four more at the end of the year. everyone put in what they had, but i was the only one with much. so i pushed for more than four dollars, and said if i didn’t get it, i'd start my own bank. he didn’t want the competition, so he let me put in my five dollars and promised thirty-five at the end of the year.

so i did it. then i figured i'd invest the thirty-five right away to keep things moving. there was a guy named bob with a stolen wood-flat, and i bought it from him, telling him to take the thirty-five when the year ended. but someone stole the flat that night, and the one-legged guy said the bank went bust. none of us got any money.

what about the ten cents?

i was gonna spend it, but had a dream telling me to give it to a guy named balum. they call him balum’s ass. he's lucky, they say, and i knew i wasn't. the dream said let balum invest the ten cents, and he'd make a raise for me. balum gave the money to the poor after hearing a preacher say whoever gives to the poor lends to the lord, and will get it back a hundredfold. he waited to see what’d come of it.

and what came of it?

nothing. neither of us could collect that money. i won’t lend any more without seeing the security. preacher said you’d get back a hundred times. if i could just get the ten cents back, i'd be happy.

well, as long as you’re going to be rich again sometime.

yeah, and i’m rich now when you look at it. i own myself, and i’m worth eight hundred dollars. wish i had that money; i wouldn’t need more.

--

i wanted to check out a spot in the middle of the island i found while exploring. we got there quick since the island’s only three miles long and a quarter mile wide.

it was a long, steep ridge about forty feet high. getting to the top was rough because the sides were steep and bushes thick. we scrambled around and found a big cavern near the top on the Illinois side. the cavern was as big as two or three rooms, and jim could stand up in it. it was cool inside. jim wanted to stash our gear there right away, but i said climbing up and down all the time wasn’t practical.

jim said if we hid the canoe well and had everything in the cavern, we could rush there if anyone came to the island. they wouldn’t find us without dogs. plus, he said the birds hinted it’d rain, and did i want our stuff getting wet?

so we grabbed the canoe and paddled up to the cavern, then hauled all the traps up there. found a spot to stash the canoe in the willows, pulled some fish off the lines and set them again, started getting dinner ready.

the cavern door was huge, big enough to roll a hogshead in. there was this flat spot on one side, perfect for building a fire. so we built one and cooked dinner. spread some blankets out inside, used them as a carpet, and ate dinner in there. stashed all our other stuff at the back of the cavern.

pretty soon it got dark and started thundering and lightning. birds were right about the storm coming. then it started raining, and i mean raining, like the sky was pouring down water. never seen the wind blow like that before. it was one of them summer storms. got so dark it looked blue-black outside, and the rain was coming down so thick it made the trees look all dim and spider-webby. then a gust would come through and bend the trees down, showing the pale underside of the leaves. and then another gust would follow and make the branches go wild. and just when it was at its darkest, it'd be as bright as day, and you'd catch a glimpse of the treetops way off in the distance. then it'd be dark again, and you'd hear the thunder crashing and rumbling down the sky.

"jim, this is nice," i said. "dont think id want to be anywhere else. pass me some more fish and cornbread."

"you wouldnt be here if it wasnt for me," jim said. "youd be down in the woods, getting soaked and going hungry. chickens know when its gonna rain, and so do birds."

the river just kept rising for days, till it was over the banks. water was three or four feet deep in the low spots on the island, and on the illinois side it was miles wide. but on the missouri side, it was still just a half mile across, since the shore was all high bluffs.

“ain’t that something? and what do they do, huck?”

“they don’t do nothing. just sit around.”

“no; really?”

“yep. just sit around—unless there’s a war, then they go. otherwise, they laze around or go hawking.” then we heard a noise.

we checked it out, just a steamboat wheel fluttering down the river. came back.

“yes,” i said, “and sometimes they fuss with parliament; if things don’t go right, they whack heads off. mostly, they hang around the harem.”

“round the what?”

“harem.”

“what’s the harem?”

“where he keeps his wives. solomon had one, about a million wives.”

"yeah, thats right; id forgot. a harem's like a boarding house, i reckon. probably a racket in the nursery. wives probably quarrel a lot, adding to the racket. and they say solomon was the wisest man ever. i dont buy that. why would a wise man live in all that noise? no way. a wise man would build a boiler factory, then shut it down when he needs rest."

"well, the widow said he was the wisest."

"i dont care what she said, he wasnt a wise man. he had some of the craziest ideas. you know about the kid he was gonna chop in two?"

"yeah, the widow told me."

"well then! wasnt that the craziest idea? look at it. there's the stump, there's one woman; here's you, you're the other woman; i'm solomon; and this dollar bill's the kid. both of you claim it. what do i do? do i ask around to find out who it belongs to and give it to the right one? no; i cut the bill in half and give each of you a piece. that's what solomon was gonna do with the kid. now tell me: what's the use of half a bill? can't buy anything with it. and what's the use of half a kid? wouldn't give a darn for a million of them."

"but jim, you missed the point."

"who, me? dont talk to me about your points. i know sense when i see it; and there's no sense in that. the dispute wasn't about half a kid, it was about a whole kid; and anyone who thinks they can settle a dispute about a whole kid with half a kid doesn't know enough to come out of the rain. dont talk to me about solomon, huck, i know him too well."

"but you don't get the point."

"to heck with the point! i know what i know. the real point's deeper. it's in how solomon was raised. a man with only one or two kids won't be wasteful with them. he knows their value. but a man with five million kids running around? different story. he'd chop a kid in two like a cat. plenty more where that came from. a kid or two didn't matter to solomon, darn him!"

never saw anyone like him. once he got an idea, it stuck. he was more down on solomon than anyone. so i started talking about other kings, let solomon go. told him about louis sixteenth who got his head cut off in france, and his boy, the dolphin, who would've been king but got locked up, and some say he died there.

"poor little guy."

"but some say he got away, came to america."

"that's good! but he'd be lonely—no kings here, right, huck?"

"no."

"then he can't get a job. what's he gonna do?"

"well, i don't know. some get on the police, some teach folks to speak french."

ok so jim's asking why french people talk different from us. i told him you cant understand a word they say. he's all confused, like how does that work. i said i got some of their words from a book, like "polly-voo-franzy". he didnt know what to make of it, thought it was some kinda insult. 

i tried to explain, its just how they talk. like, a cat dont talk like a cow, and a cow dont talk like us. thats just how it is. but jim's not having it, says a frenchman should talk like a man. i guess he thinks if youre a man, you should talk like us. 

anyway, we were on the river, trying to get to cairo. we figured three more nights and we'd be there, sell the raft, and get on a steamboat. but the second night, this fog rolls in. we're trying to tie up to a tow-head, but the current's too strong. the raft breaks free and takes off. i'm stuck in the canoe, trying to catch up. i paddle back to the stern, grab the paddle, and try to head back. but i forgot to untie the canoe. 

i'm freaking out, hands shaking, trying to untie it. finally i get going, take off after the raft. but i fly by the end of the tow-head and into the fog. no idea where i'm going.

man, paddling wont do here; I'll just run into something. gotta stay still and float, but holding my hands still's making me itch. I whooped out and listened. way down there I hear a small whoop, and felt a bit better. started after it, trying to catch it again. next whoop, I'm off course to the right. then too far left—and not getting closer either, just spinning around while it keeps straight.

wishing he'd just bang a tin pan non-stop, but nope. those silent gaps between whoops were throwing me off. kept at it, then hear the whoop _behind_ me. tangled up now. maybe someone else, or I'm turned around.

chucked the paddle. hear the whoop again; still behind but different spot; kept coming, kept changing, I kept responding, till it was in front again. figured the current swung the canoe downstream, and I was good if that was Jim not some other raft guy hollering. can't tell voices in fog, nothing's right in a fog.

whooping carried on, then suddenly hit a cut bank with ghostly trees, current shoved me left, snags roaring past, current flying. then white and still again. stayed still, heart pounding like crazy, probably didn't breathe for a hundred beats.

gave up then. knew the deal. that cut bank was an island, Jim went down the other side. not just a tow-head to float by quick. big timber, full island, maybe five, six miles long, half a mile wide.

stayed quiet, listening hard, fifteen minutes maybe. floating along, four or five miles an hour, but feels like sitting dead still. snag slips by, not thinking how fast _you're_ moving, just thinking how that snag's flying. it's dismal out there, foggy, night, alone. try it, you'll see.

kept whooping for half an hour, finally hear an answer far off, tried to follow, couldn't. figured I was in a nest of tow-heads, glimpses on both sides, narrow channels, some unseen but hear the current washing against dead brush. lost the whoops in there; didn't chase long, worse than chasing a Jack-o’-lantern. sound dodges around, swaps places too fast.

i was having to paddle away from the bank pretty regular to avoid hitting the islands, so i figured the raft must be running into the bank every now and then or it would've gotten way ahead of me. it was moving a little faster than i was, anyway.

next thing i knew, i was out in the open river again, but i couldn't hear jim anywhere. i thought maybe he'd gotten stuck on a snag or something and that was it for him. i was beat, so i laid down in the canoe and decided i wasn't gonna bother anymore. didnt want to sleep, but i was so tired i couldn't help it, so i figured id just take a quick nap.

but i guess it was more than a nap, cuz when i woke up the stars were out, the fog was gone, and i was drifting down a big bend backwards. at first, i had no idea where i was - thought i was dreaming. when things started coming back to me, they seemed to come up from out of nowhere.

this part of the river was huge, with massive trees on both banks - just a solid wall as far as i could see by the stars. i looked downstream and saw a black speck on the water, so i went after it. turned out it was just a couple of logs tied together. then i saw another speck, chased that one too, and finally found the raft.

when i got to it, jim was sitting there with his head between his knees, asleep, with his arm hanging over the steering oar. the other oar was busted, and the raft was covered in leaves and branches - she'd had a rough time, that's for sure.

i tied up and laid down on the raft next to jim, started stretching and said, "hey, jim, was i asleep? why didnt you wake me up?" 

jim's eyes popped open and he said, "goodness gracious, is that you, huck? you aint dead, you aint drowned - youre back again? its too good to be true, honey." he started feeling me all over, like he was making sure i was real. "you aint dead, youre back again, alive and sound - same old huck."

i said, "whats wrong with you, jim? you been drinking or something?" 

"drinking? me? i aint had a chance to drink nothing." 

"then what's going on? why you talking all wild like that?" 

"how am i talking wild?" 

"you been talking about me coming back and all that, like i'd been gone or something." 

jim looked me dead in the eye and said, "huck, you look me in the eye - _haint_ you been gone away?" 

i said, "gone away? what are you talking about? i havent gone anywhere. where would i go?" 

jim just shook his head and said, "sumf'n wrong, boss - sumf'n wrong. is i me, or who is i? is i here, or where am i?" 

i told him, "jim, you're here, plain enough - you're just being a fool." 

he said, "i am? well, you answer me this: didnt you take out the line in the canoe to tie up to the tow-head?" 

i said, "no, i didnt. what tow-head? i havent seen any tow-head." 

jim just looked at me and said, "you haint seen no tow-head? didnt the line come loose and the raft take off down the river, leaving you and the canoe behind in the fog?" 

i just said, "what fog?"

"man, i'm tellin you, we was stuck in dat fog all night, and didnt you holler, and didnt i holler, till we got all mixed up in them islands and one of us got lost and tother one was just as good as lost, cause he didnt know where he was? and didnt i run into a bunch of them islands and have a terrible time and almost get drowned? now thats what happened, right?

"hold up, jim, you aint makin no sense, we been sittin here talkin all night, and i aint seen no fog, no islands, no nothin. you musta been dreamin.

"how i'm gonna dream all that in ten minutes?

"you did dream it, cause none of it happened.

"but huck, it's all just as clear to me as -

"don't matter how clear it is, jim, it didnt happen. i know cause i been here the whole time.

"jim just sat there for a bit, thinkin. then he says:

"alright, den, i reckon i did dream it, huck, but damn if it aint the most real dream i ever had. and i aint never had no dream before that wore me out like this one.

"okay, so you had a wild dream, tell me about it.

"so jim starts tellin me the whole thing, just like it happened, but he added a bunch of extra stuff. then he says we gotta figure out what it means, cause it's a warnin. he says the first part of the dream, with the tow-head, that's a guy who's gonna try to help us, but the current, that's another guy who's gonna try to take us away from him. the hollerin, that's warnings we gotta listen to, or we'll get into trouble. the bunch of tow-heads, that's all the trouble we're gonna have with mean folks, but if we keep our heads down and dont mess with em, we'll make it through and get to the free states.

"it was gettin dark when i first got on the raft, but it's clearin up now.

"alright, jim, that's all well and good, but what about all this stuff on the raft? the leaves and the broke oar?

"jim looks at the trash, then at me, then back at the trash. he's still got the dream stuck in his head, but when he finally snaps out of it, he looks at me straight, no smile, and says:"

i'm gonna tell you what happened. so i was all worn out from work and calling out for you, and i went to sleep feeling pretty broke because you were lost. didnt care what happened to me or the raft no more. then i woke up and found you safe and sound, and i got all teary-eyed, i was so thankful. but all you were thinking about was playing a trick on ole jim with a lie. that truck is trash, and people who mess with their friends like that are trash too.

he got up slow and walked to the wigwam without saying a word. but that was enough. it made me feel so bad i almost wanted to apologize to him. 

it took me 15 minutes to work up the nerve to go and apologize to him, but i did it. and i aint sorry i did. i didnt play no more tricks on him, and i wouldnt have done that one if i knew it would hurt him like that.

we slept most of the day and started out at night, following this huge raft. it was as long as a procession, with four sweeps at each end, and we figured it had around 30 men on it. it had five big wigwams and a campfire in the middle, and a flagpole at each end. it was a pretty stylish setup. being a raftsman on a craft like that would be something.

we drifted into a big bend, and the night got all cloudy and hot. the river was wide, with solid timber on both sides, and you couldnt see no lights or nothing. we talked about cairo, and wondered if we'd know it when we got there. i said we might not, since i heard it was just a dozen houses or so, and if they didnt have no lights on, how would we know we were passing a town? jim said if the two rivers joined up, that would be a sign. but i said maybe we'd just think we were passing an island and getting back into the same old river. that got jim and me both worried. so what to do? i said we should paddle ashore when we see a light, and tell them my pap was coming along with a trading scow, and he's new to the business, and wants to know how far it is to cairo. jim thought it was a good idea, so we took a smoke and waited.

now all we can do is keep an eye out for the town, and not pass it without seeing it. jim's gonna be on the lookout, because if he sees it, he's a free man, but if he misses it, he's back in slave country. every little while, he jumps up and says:

"dah she is?"

so we're floating along, and jim thinks he sees cairo. but it's just lightning bugs or something. he's all worked up, talking about being close to freedom, and it's getting to me. i started thinking, man, this guy's really free, and i'm the one who let it happen. my conscience is eating at me, saying i should've paddled ashore and told someone. i try to tell myself it's not my fault, but that dont work. i think about miss watson, how she was good to me, and how i'm basically helping her slave escape. that's when it hits me - i'm in the wrong here.

i'm feeling pretty miserable, just pacing back and forth on the raft, and jim's doing the same. every time he says "that's cairo", it's like a kick in the gut. i think if it really is cairo, i'll just about lose it. jim's talking out loud, saying he's gonna save up money and buy his wife and kids when he's free. but then he says he'll get an abolitionist to steal them if he has to. that just about freezes me. i never thought he'd talk like that. it's like, what have i gotten myself into?

my conscience is going crazy, telling me i should've stopped this whole thing. but then i think, it's not too late, i can still paddle ashore and tell someone. that thought makes me feel better, like a weight's been lifted. i start looking out for a light, and i'm kinda humming to myself. then jim sees something and starts yelling - "we's safe, huck, we's safe!" i tell him, "hold up, let me check it out". i dont want to get my hopes up just to be let down.

he got the canoe set up quick, put his old coat down for me to sit on, and handed me the paddle. as i pushed off, he says:

"soon i'll be shouting for joy, all thanks to Huck. i'm free cause of Huck. won't ever forget you, Huck; you're the best friend old Jim's got."

i was ready to rat him out, sweating as i paddled off, but those words hit me hard. slowed me down, made me unsure if starting this was the right call. about fifty yards out, Jim calls:

"there goes Huck, the only white gentleman who keeps his promises to old Jim."

felt sick. but i thought, i gotta do it—no way around it. then a skiff with two guys and guns shows up, stops me. one says:

"what's that?"

"raft piece," i say.

"you belong on it?"

"yes, sir."

"anyone else?"

"just one, sir."

"five runaways went up yonder. yours white or black?"

couldn't answer quick. words stuck. tried to spit it out, couldn't. gave up and said:

"he's white."

"we'll check ourselves."

"please do," i say, "it's pap there, maybe help me tow it. he's sick—so's mam and Mary Ann."

"we're in a hurry, boy. but guess we have to. paddle up."

paddled, they rowed. made a few strokes, i say:

"pap'll thank you. everyone leaves when i need help with the raft."

"mighty mean. odd too. what's wrong with your father?"

"it's—uh—nothing much."

they stopped. close to the raft now. one says:

"boy, that's a lie. what's wrong with your pap? answer straight."

"i will, sir, don't leave us. just pull up, let me throw the line, no need to come close—please."

"back off, John!" they backed up. "stay away, boy—downwind. wind's blown it to us. pap's got smallpox, you knew it. why not say? want it spreading?"

"told everyone before, they just left us," i say, tearing up.

“Man, I get it. I feel for you, but we don’t want smallpox. Here’s the deal. Don’t try landing on your own or you’ll wreck it all. Drift down about twenty miles. You’ll hit a town on the left side of the river. Sun'll be up by then. Tell them your folks got chills and fever. Don’t let folks guess what's really up. We’re trying to help; just keep a good distance, alright? That light over there’s just a wood-yard, no good landing there. Your dad’s in a tough spot. Here’s a twenty-dollar gold piece on this board. Grab it when it floats by. Hate to leave you, but can’t risk smallpox, you see?”

“Wait up, Parker,” says the other guy, “I’m adding a twenty to the board too. Goodbye, boy; listen to Mr. Parker and you’ll be fine.”

“That’s right, son—goodbye. If you spot any runaway slaves, get help and nab them. You’ll earn something.”

“Goodbye, sir,” I say. “I won’t let any runaway slaves slip by if I can help it.”

They left, and I got on the raft feeling low. Knew I messed up, figured there was no point in trying to do right; if you don’t start off right, you’ve got no shot—when it counts, you’ve got nothing to hold you steady, so you lose. Then I thought, if I'd done right and turned Jim in, would I feel better? No, I’d feel just as bad. So why bother learning to do right when it’s hard and doing wrong is easy, and the payoff's the same? Couldn’t answer that. Decided I’d just do whatever’s easiest from now on.

Went to the wigwam; Jim wasn’t there. Looked around; nowhere. I called out:

“Jim!”

“Here I am, Huck. They out of sight yet? Don’t talk loud.”

He was in the river under the stern oar, just his nose showing. I told him they were gone, so he came aboard. He said:

“I was listening to all that talk, slipped into the river, was ready to head to shore if they came aboard. Then I’d swim back when they left. Man, you sure fooled them, Huck! That was the smartest trick! You saved old Jim—he won’t forget that.”

We talked about the money. Solid haul—twenty bucks each. Jim said we could take deck passage on a steamboat now, money would last us to the free States. Twenty more miles wasn’t far, but he wished we were there already.

Towards daybreak, we tied up. Jim was super careful hiding the raft. Spent all day getting things packed, ready to leave rafting behind.

That night around ten, we saw the lights of a town in a left-hand bend.

i paddled out to ask some guy in a skiff if that was cairo. he's setting a trot-line, and i says:

"mister, is that cairo?"

he just looks at me and says "cairo? no. you must be a blame fool."

i ask him what town it is, and he tells me to go find out myself. says if i stick around he'll give me something i dont want. so i paddle back to the raft. jim's pretty disappointed, but i tell him never mind, we'll get to cairo next.

we pass another town before daylight, but it's high ground so i dont go check it out. no high ground around cairo, jim says. i forgot that. we hang out on a tow-head for the day, close to the left bank. i start thinking maybe we passed cairo in the fog that night. jim thinks so too. he says:

"doan le's talk about it, huck. po niggers cant have no luck."

i wish i never saw that snake skin. jim says it aint my fault, i didnt know. but now we're in a bind. we cant take the raft up the stream, and we cant go to shore. so we wait for dark, and then we're gonna take the canoe and head back. but when we get back to the raft, the canoe's gone.

we dont say a word for a while. we both know it's that snake skin's work. so we just wait. later we talk about what to do, and we figure we gotta go down with the raft till we can buy a new canoe. we cant borrow one, thats just asking for trouble.

so we shove out after dark. been three hours, and we still havent seen any rafts laid up at shore where we can buy a canoe. it's getting gray and thick, like fog but not quite. cant see the river, cant see far. then a steamboat comes up the river, and we light the lantern, hoping they'll see it. usually they dont come close to us, but on nights like this they just bull on up the channel.

we heard her coming, but didn’t see her till she was close. aimed right at us. they do that, try to see how close without hitting. sometimes the wheel clips a bit, and the pilot thinks he’s smart. well, she was a big one, looked like a black cloud with glow-worms, but then she bulged out, furnace doors like red-hot teeth, her bows right over us. someone yelled, bells jingled to stop engines, steam whistled—and as Jim went overboard one side and me the other, she smashed through the raft.

i dived deep, wanted the wheel to have room. i can stay under a minute, maybe did a minute and a half this time. then shot up quick, nearly busting. popped out, blew water out my nose. current was strong; boat started engines again quick, didn’t care for raftsmen, churned up river, gone in the thick weather, but i could hear her.

yelled for Jim a dozen times, no answer; grabbed a plank, struck for shore. saw the current drift left, so i went that way.

one of those long crossings, took a while. landed safe, climbed the bank. couldn’t see far, poked along rough ground a quarter mile or more, then stumbled on a big old double log-house. thought to rush by, but dogs jumped out barking, knew better than to move.

CHAPTER XVII.

someone spoke from a window, not showing their face:

“be done, boys! who’s there?”

“it’s me.”

“who’s me?”

“george jackson, sir.”

“what do you want?”

“don’t want nothing, sir. just trying to pass, but dogs won’t let me.”

“why prowling around this time of night?”

“wasn’t prowling, sir, fell overboard from the steamboat.”

“oh, you did? light a candle, someone. what’s your name again?”

“george jackson, sir. just a boy.”

“if you’re honest, nothing to fear. stand still. rouse bob and tom, fetch guns. george jackson, anyone with you?”

“no, sir, nobody.”

heard stirring inside, saw a light. man yelled:

“hide that light, betsy—use your sense! put it behind the front door. bob, tom, ready?”

“all set.”

“george jackson, you know the shepherdsons?”

“no, sir; never heard of them.”

"maybe. maybe not. alright, step up, George Jackson. dont rush—go slow. if someones with you, keep em back—they show, they get shot. now come on, slow; push the door open yourself, just enough to squeeze in, you hear?"

i wasnt in a hurry; couldnt be even if i wanted. took one slow step at a time, dead quiet, only my heart thudding. dogs, silent like the people, trailed behind. reached the three log steps, heard locks, bars, bolts. put my hand on the door, pushed a bit and a bit more till someone said, "there, stop—stick your head in." i did, expecting it might get taken off.

candle on the floor, all of them staring at me, me at them, for a moment: three big guys with guns pointed, made me flinch, i swear; oldest was gray, around sixty, other two maybe thirty, all solid and handsome—and the sweetest old gray-haired lady, with two young women behind her i couldnt quite see. old man goes:

"there; looks alright. come in."

once inside, old guy locked, barred, bolted the door, told the young men to come in with guns, all went to a parlor with a new rag carpet, huddled in a corner out of window range—no side ones. they held the candle, eyed me, said, "why, _he_ ain’t a Shepherdson—no, nothing Shepherdson about him." then the old man said he hoped i wouldnt mind a search for arms, no harm meant—just checking. didnt dig in my pockets, just felt outside, said it was fine. told me to settle in and tell them about myself; but the old lady says:

"why, bless you, Saul, he’s soaked; and dont you think he’s hungry?"

"right you are, Rachel—slipped my mind."

so the old lady says:

"Betsy" (she was a black woman), "hurry and get him something to eat quick, poor thing; and one of you girls wake Buck—oh, here he is. Buck, take this little stranger, get his wet clothes off, and dress him in your dry ones."

Buck was my age—thirteen, fourteen, maybe a bit bigger. just in a shirt, hair all messy. came in yawning, rubbing an eye, dragging a gun with the other hand. he says:

"no Shepherdsons around?"

they said no, false alarm.

"well," he says, "if there'd been some, id have got one."

they all laughed, and Bob goes:

"Buck, they might have scalped us all, you were so slow."

"well, no one came for me, and it’s not fair im always kept down; i dont get a shot."

"never mind, Buck, son," says the old man, "you’ll have your turn, all in good time, dont worry. go on, do what your mom said."

i'm sitting in this room, wearing some other dude's clothes, and he's telling me about catching a bluejay and a rabbit. then he asks me where moses was when the candle went out. i dont know, never heard of it. he tells me to guess, but i dont see the point. "which candle?" i ask. he just says any candle. i still dont get it. 

he says its a riddle, and that i gotta stay with them forever. we can have some good times, no school and all that. he's got a dog that can fetch chips from the river. i'm eating cold corn-pone, corn-beef, butter, and buttermilk - thats some good stuff. everyone's smoking cob pipes, except the women. they're all asking me questions, so i tell them about my family, how we lived on a farm in arkansaw. my sister ran off, bill went to find her and never came back, tom and mort died. then it was just me and pap, but he died too. so i took what was left and started up the river. 

next thing i know, im waking up in the morning and i cant remember my own name. i lay there for a bit, trying to think. buck wakes up and i ask him if he can spell. he says yeah, so i bet him he cant spell my name. he does, though - g-e-o-r-g-e j-a-x-o-n. i didnt think he could do it. now i'm trying to memorize it so i can rattle it off easy, just in case somebody asks me to spell it next.

i walked into this house out in the country and it was something else. had a brass knob on the front door, not one of them iron latches or a wooden one with a buckskin string. looked like a house in town, all fancy. no bed in the parlor, just a big fireplace with bricks that were kept clean and red. they even used that spanish-brown paint on them, same as they do in town. had big brass dog-irons, too, that could hold up a saw-log. and a clock on the mantelpiece, with a picture of a town on the glass front. that clock would tick away, and sometimes it'd strike a hundred and fifty times before it got tired.

there were these weird parrots on either side of the clock, made out of something like chalk and painted up all gaudy. had a crockery cat and dog next to them, and when you pressed down on them, they'd squeak. didnt open their mouths or look interested, just squeaked from underneath. had some big wild-turkey-wing fans behind them, too. on the table in the middle of the room was this crockery basket with fake fruit in it - apples, oranges, peaches, grapes. they were all redder and yellower and prettier than the real thing, but you could see where they were chipped and showed the white underneath.

the table had a cover made out of oilcloth, with a red and blue spread-eagle painted on it. said it came all the way from philadelphia. had some books piled up on the corners of the table, too. a big family bible with pictures, pilgrim's progress, friendship's offering with all this poetry in it. didnt really read the poetry, though. had henry clay's speeches, dr. gunn's family medicine - that one told you what to do if someone was sick or dead. had a hymn book, too, and some other books. and the chairs, they were nice, split-bottom and sound. not all busted like an old basket.

i walked into this room and saw all these old pictures on the walls - washington, lafayette, some battle scenes, and this one called "signing the declaration". there were these crayon things too, made by one of the daughters who died when she was 15. they were different from anything i'd seen before - real dark. one of them was a woman in a black dress, leaning on a tombstone under a weeping willow, with a handkerchief and a reticule, and it said "shall i never see thee more alas" underneath. another one had a lady crying with a dead bird in her hand, and it said "i shall never hear thy sweet chirrup more alas". 

they had this other picture of a lady looking out the window at the moon, tears running down her face, with a locket and a letter, and it said "and art thou gone yes thou art gone alas". i dont know, these pictures just didnt do it for me - they gave me the fan-tods. everybody felt bad she died, cause she had a lot more pictures planned out, and you could see what they were missing. but i think she was having a better time in the graveyard, considering her disposition.

she was working on this one picture when she got sick - a woman in a white gown, standing on a bridge, getting ready to jump, with her hair down and tears on her face. she had all these arms - some folded, some stretched out, some reaching up to the moon. the idea was to pick which ones looked best and scratch out the rest, but she died before she could finish. now they keep this picture in her room, and on her birthday, they hang flowers on it.

this girl kept a scrapbook, pasting in obituaries and accidents from the presbyterian observer, and writing her own poetry after them. it was actually pretty good. like this one about some kid named stephen dowling bots who fell down a well:

ode to stephen dowling bots, dec'd

and did young stephen sicken,
    and did young stephen die?
and did the sad hearts thicken,
    and did the mourners cry?

no; such was not the fate of
    young stephen dowling bots;
though sad hearts round him thickened,
    ’twas not from sickness’ shots.

so i was reading this poem about stephen dowling bots. it goes like this: the kid didnt die from whooping cough or measles. no, he fell down a well. they got him out but it was too late. his spirit was gone.

thats some dark stuff. emmeline grangerford, she was a poet too. buck said she could write poetry like nothing. just slap down a line and go from there. didnt matter what it was about, as long as it was sad. she wrote about dead people all the time. the neighbors said she was always on hand with her tributes before the person was even cold.

i liked emmeline, even though she was a bit much sometimes. after she died, i used to go up to her room and read her old scrapbook. it was kinda weird, but i felt like i had to. nobody wrote poetry about her after she was gone, so i tried to write some myself. didnt really work out, though.

anyway, the house we were staying in was pretty nice. had white curtains with castles painted on them, and a little old piano that the young ladies would play. the walls were plastered and the floors had carpets. it was a double house with a big open space in the middle, and sometimes we'd set the table there and eat. the cooking was good, and there was a lot of it.

col. grangerford was a real gentleman, top to bottom, and so was his family. well-bred, like a prized horse, the widow douglas used to say, and nobody argued with her. even pap, who was pretty much a mudcat, agreed. col. grangerford was tall, slim, with a pale complexion, and not a hint of red anywhere. shaved clean every morning, thin lips, thin nostrils, high nose, heavy brows, and eyes so deep-set they seemed to peer from caves. his forehead was high, hair black and straight down to his shoulders. long thin hands, always in a white linen suit so bright it hurt to look at. sundays meant a blue coat with brass buttons, and he carried a mahogany cane with a silver head. no nonsense, not loud, just kind. you felt it, trusted him. sometimes he smiled, and it was like sunshine. but when he stood straight and his eyes flashed, you wanted to find a tree first and questions later. no one needed manners reminders around him. everyone loved him; he was like good weather. when he got dark, it was brief, and nothing went wrong for a week after.

mornings, him and the old lady came down, and everyone got up to greet them, sat only after they did. tom and bob mixed bitters for him, waited till theirs were ready, bowed, and said, “our duty to you, sir, and madam.” they nodded slightly, said thanks, and they all drank. tom and bob poured a little water on sugar, added some whiskey or apple brandy for us, and we toasted the old folks too.

bob and tom were tall, handsome, broad-shouldered, with brown faces, long black hair, and black eyes. dressed like the old man, white linen, broad panama hats.

then there was miss charlotte, twenty-five, tall, proud, grand. good-hearted unless riled up, then she could freeze you. and miss sophia, gentle and sweet like a dove, only twenty.

each had their own servant—buck too. mine had it easy, wasn’t used to being waited on, but buck’s was always busy.

that was the family now. there used to be more—three sons gone, and emmeline who passed.

old guy owned a ton of farms and over a hundred slaves. every now and then, folks would ride in from ten, fifteen miles out. they'd stay for days, have parties by the river, dances, picnics in the woods during the day, and balls at night. mostly family. men always brought guns. a real high-class crowd, i tell you.

another fancy group nearby—five or six families—mostly Shepherdsons. just as high-up as the Grangerfords. both used the same steamboat landing, about two miles up from us. so when i'd go up there with our folks, i'd see Shepherdsons on their fancy horses.

one day, Buck and me were hunting in the woods, heard a horse coming. we were crossing the road. Buck says, “Quick! Jump for the woods!” we did, and peeked through the leaves. a young man came riding down the road, looking sharp, like a soldier. it was young Harney Shepherdson. Buck fires, knocks Harney's hat off. Harney grabs his gun, heads our way. we didn’t stick around. ran through the woods. wasn’t thick, so i kept looking back. saw Harney aim at Buck twice, but he rode off. we ran home fast. old gentleman’s eyes lit up for a second—pleasure, i’d say—then he goes soft:

“don’t like shooting from cover. why not step out, boy?”

“Shepherdsons don’t, father. they take the advantage.”

Miss Charlotte, she’s like a queen, nostrils flaring, eyes snapping. the young men look dark, but say nothing. Miss Sophia went pale, but eased up when she heard nobody was hurt.

got Buck alone by the corn-cribs, asked: “did you want to kill him, Buck?”

“bet i did.”

“what’d he do to you?”

“him? nothing.”

“then why kill him?”

“it's the feud.”

“what’s a feud?”

“where were you raised? a feud’s when one man kills another over a fight, then brothers get involved, then cousins. keeps going till everyone's dead. slow, takes time.”

“been going long, Buck?”

“sure has! started thirty years back. trouble over something, lawsuit, suit went bad, guy shot the winner. natural move, anyone would.”

“what was it over, Buck? land?”

“maybe, dunno.”

“who shot first? Grangerford or Shepherdson?”

“how should i know? too long ago.”

“nobody knows?”

“oh, pa probably does, some old folks too, but they’ve forgotten what started it.”

"many killed, Buck?"

"yeah; plenty of funerals. not always dead though. pa's got some buckshot in him; doesn't mind since he doesn't weigh much. bob’s been cut with a bowie, and tom’s been hurt a couple times."

"anyone killed this year, Buck?"

"yep; we got one, they got one. 'bout three months back, cousin Bud, fourteen, riding through the woods unarmed, which was real dumb, hears a horse behind him. sees old Baldy Shepherdson with a gun, white hair flying. instead of jumping off and hiding, Bud thought he could outrun him; they raced for five miles. Bud realized it was useless, stopped and faced him, wanted bullet holes in front, you know. old man rode up and shot him. but didn't enjoy it long, our folks got him inside a week."

"reckon that old man was a coward, Buck."

"not a chance. ain't a coward in the Shepherdsons. none in the Grangerfords either. that old man held his own against three Grangerfords, won too. all on horseback; he got behind a woodpile, used his horse for cover; Grangerfords stayed mounted, shot at him, he shot back. him and his horse went home leaky, but Grangerfords had to be fetched, one dead, another died next day. if you're looking for cowards, don't waste time with Shepherdsons."

next Sunday we all headed to church, three miles, everyone on horseback. men brought guns, Buck too, kept them close or leaned against the wall. Shepherdsons did the same. sermon was on brotherly love, and all that boring stuff; but everyone said it was good. talked it over heading home, lots to say about faith, good works, free grace, predestination, and more. roughest Sunday i'd seen yet.

i'm sittin on the porch, dinner's done, everybody's dozin off. buck's out on the grass with a dog, both of them asleep. i head up to our room, thinkin i'll catch a nap myself. that's when i see miss sophia standin in her doorway, next to ours. she takes me in her room, shuts the door soft, and asks if i like her. i tell her i do. then she asks if i'll do somethin for her, keep it quiet, and i say okay. she forgot her testament at church, left it between two other books, and wants me to slip out and grab it for her. no big deal, right. so i slip out, head to the church. it's empty, except for maybe a hog or two. they like the floor in summer, it's cool.

i'm thinkin, somethin's up. a girl don't usually sweat about a testament. so i shake it, and out drops a piece of paper with "half-past two" on it. i search the book, but that's all i find. i put the paper back, head home. miss sophia's waitin for me, pulls me in, shuts the door. she finds the paper, looks glad, gives me a squeeze. says i'm the best boy in the world, but don't tell anybody. she's all red in the face, eyes light up, makes her look pretty powerful. i'm a bit surprised, but i ask her what's the deal with the paper. she says it's just a bookmark, and i can go play now.

i head down to the river, thinkin about this thing. my boy's followin me, and when we're out of sight, he comes runnin up. says if i come to the swamp, he'll show me a bunch of water-moccasins. that's weird, he said that yesterday. what's he up to. so i tell him okay, let's go. we walk a half mile, then he heads into the swamp, wades in ankle deep. we come to a little flat spot, dry and thick with trees and bushes. he says i should check it out, but he's not comin with me. i poke around, find a little open spot, and there's a man sleepin. and it's my old jim.

woke him up, figured he'd be shocked to see me, but nah. almost cried happy, but just not surprised. he trailed me that night, heard me yelling, but stayed quiet. scared of getting snatched back into slavery. he said:

“got hurt a bit, couldn’t swim fast, so ended up way behind towards the end; when you landed, thought i’d catch up without yelling, saw that house, slowed down. too far to hear what they said—scared of dogs; when it got quiet, knew you were inside, so i hit the woods till daylight. morning comes, some folks headed to the fields took me here, where dogs can’t track 'cause of the water, bring me food every night, tell me how you’re doing.”

“why didn’t you get jack to bring me here sooner, jim?”

“well, no point bothering you, huck, till we had a plan—but we’re good now. been buying pots, pans, food when i can, fixing up the raft at night—”

“what raft, jim?”

“our old raft.”

“you mean to say our old raft wasn’t completely smashed?”

“nah. tore up one end, but nothing major, just lost most of our stuff. if we hadn’t dived deep and swum far in the dark, scared and all, we’d have seen the raft. but it’s just as well we didn’t, ’cause now she’s almost good as new, and we’ve got new stuff to replace what was lost.”

“how’d you get the raft back, jim—did you catch her?”

“how’m i gonna catch her in the woods? no; some folks found her snagged in the bend, hid her in a creek among the willows. lots of arguing over who she belonged to, till i heard about it, settled it by saying she’s ours. asked if they wanted trouble grabbing a young white man’s property, gave 'em ten cents each, and they were happy. these folks are good to me, do whatever i ask, don’t need to ask twice. that jack’s a good guy, pretty smart.”

“yeah, he is. never told me you were here; said he’d show me snakes. if anything happens, he’s clear; he can say he never saw us together, and it’s true.”

don’t want to go on about the next day. keeping it short. woke up at dawn, was gonna roll over and sleep some more, then noticed how quiet it was—nobody moving. not normal. buck was up and gone. got up, wondering, went downstairs—nobody around; quiet as a mouse. same outside. thought, what’s up with this? down by the woodpile, found jack, asked:

“what’s going on?”

he said:

i'm walking down the road when someone tells me miss sophia's run off with harney shepherdson. they think she got married to him, but nobody knows for sure. her family found out like half an hour ago and they're all riled up. they're grabbing guns and horses, trying to catch harney before he can get across the river with miss sophia. 

i took off down the river road as fast as i could. after a while, i start hearing guns in the distance. when i get near the log store and the woodpile, i sneak along under the trees and brush till i find a good spot. i climb up into a cottonwood tree and watch. there's a wood-rank a little ways in front of me, but i'm glad i didn't hide behind it.

there's a bunch of men on horses, cussing and yelling, trying to get to these two young chaps behind the wood-rank. but every time one of the men shows himself, he gets shot at. the two boys are squatting back to back, watching both ways. 

then the men stop yelling and start riding towards the store. one of the boys stands up, takes aim, and drops one of the men out of his saddle. the men jump off their horses and start carrying the hurt one to the store. that's when the two boys take off running. they get halfway to my tree before the men notice. then the men jump on their horses and take off after them. they're gaining on the boys, but the boys had a good head start. they make it to the woodpile in front of my tree and slip behind it, so they're safe again. one of the boys is buck, and the other's some slim young guy about nineteen.

the guys tore through for a bit, then rode off. soon as they were gone, i yelled to Buck from the tree. he was all confused at first, hearing my voice from up there. but he got it quick and told me to keep watch and let him know when they showed up again. said they were up to no good and wouldn't be gone long. i wanted out of that tree bad, but didn't dare move. Buck started crying, said him and his cousin Joe would get even one day. his dad and brothers were killed, and some of the enemy too. Shepherdsons ambushed them. Buck said they should've waited for backup. asked about Harney and Miss Sophia, and he said they crossed the river safe. i was relieved, but Buck was mad he missed Harney when he had the chance.

suddenly, bang! bang! guns went off—the men snuck around and came from behind without horses. Buck and Joe dove for the river, both hurt, swimming as the men ran the bank shooting and yelling "kill them!" made me sick to my stomach, nearly fell out of the tree. can't go into all of it, just makes me sick thinking about it. wished i'd never come ashore that night. those images stuck with me, haunting my dreams.

stayed in the tree till dark, too scared to come down. heard gunshots in the distance, saw groups with guns. troubles weren't over. felt real low, decided never to go near that house again, thought maybe it was my fault. figured that note meant Miss Sophia was meeting Harney to run off; maybe if i'd told her dad about it, locked her up, all this mess wouldn't have happened.

when i climbed down, crept along the river, found the bodies at the water's edge. pulled them ashore, covered their faces, and left quick. cried a bit covering Buck's face—he was good to me.

darkness settled. avoided the house, headed through the woods to the swamp. Jim wasn't on his island, so hurried to the creek, pushed through the willows, desperate to get out of that place. but the raft was gone! scared me to death, couldn't breathe for a moment. then i yelled. a voice nearby said:

"Good lan’! is dat you, honey? Doan’ make no noise."

Jim's voice—never heard anything better. ran along the bank, got aboard, and Jim hugged me tight, glad to see me. he says:

ok so we're on the raft now, just me and jim. he was worried i was dead, thought i got shot or something. he was getting the raft ready to leave, figured they'd think i was killed and floated down the river. i told him to just shove off, get us out of there as fast as we can.

we didn't stop till we were a couple miles down, out in the middle of the mississippi. then we hung up our lantern, figured we were safe. i was starving, hadn't eaten since yesterday, so jim got out some corn-dodgers and buttermilk, pork and cabbage, and greens. man, there's nothing like that stuff when it's cooked right. we just sat there eating, talking, having a good time. we both agreed, there's no place like a raft. other places feel cramped, but a raft, you feel free, easy, comfortable.

so we just drifted for a few days, nights too. it was smooth, quiet, lovely. we'd run at night, hide up during the day. we'd tie up near a tow-head, cut some cottonwoods and willows to hide the raft, then set out our lines. after that, we'd swim, cool off, then sit on the sandy bottom, just watch the daylight come. it was perfectly still, just the bullfrogs making noise sometimes. you could see the woods on the other side, a dull line, then the sky would lighten up, and the river would change from black to gray. you could see trading scows, rafts, and hear the sweep screeching, or people talking, it was that still. 

then you could see the mist curl up off the water, the east would redden up, and you could make out a log-cabin in the woods, probably a woodyard. the breeze would come up, cool and fresh, smelling like woods and flowers, but sometimes it'd be rank from dead fish. and then the sun would be up, everything would be smiling, and the song-birds would be going crazy.

couldn't really see much smoke now, so we took some fish off the lines for breakfast. after that, just watched the river go by, feeling lazy, and eventually dozed off. waking up, sometimes a steamboat would be chugging along way off—you couldn't tell much about it, just if it was stern-wheel or side-wheel. for a while, nothing but quiet. then maybe a raft would slide by, someone chopping on it, the sound lagging behind the motion.

we'd spend the day like that, lounging, listening to the quiet. once a thick fog rolled in, rafts were clanging tin pans so steamboats wouldn't hit them. heard folks cussing and laughing clear but couldn't see them—felt eerie, like ghosts. jim thought they were spirits; i told him:

“nah; spirits don’t say, ‘dern the dern fog.’”

as soon as it got dark, we'd push off and let the current take us. lit pipes, legs dangling in the water, talking about everything—we stayed naked when mosquitoes didn't bother us, those new clothes were too fancy to be comfy, and clothes weren’t my thing anyway.

sometimes had the whole river to ourselves. just banks and islands out there; maybe a spark in a cabin window, or on a raft, a scow—maybe hear a fiddle or a song. life on a raft was something else. stars above, we'd lay back and talk about them—jim thought they were made, i figured they just happened; too many to make. he reckoned the moon laid them; seemed reasonable. i'd seen frogs lay nearly as much, so why not? watched shooting stars too, jim said they were tossed out for being spoiled.

once or twice at night, a steamboat slipped by, sparks flying from the chimneys, landing in the river—looked pretty. then she'd round a corner, lights gone, noise quiet, leaving the river still. later, her waves would reach us, gently rock the raft, and then nothing but frogs or whatever for who knows how long.

so we're drifting along at night, and once the people on shore go to bed, the whole place goes dark. no more lights in the windows, thats how we keep track of time. first light we see in the morning, we know its time to find a place to hide.

one morning i grabbed a canoe and paddled over to the main shore, just a couple hundred yards. then i headed up this crick, maybe a mile, to see if i could find some berries. thats when i saw these two guys running down a cowpath, tearing it up. i figured they were after me or jim, so i was about to bail, but they saw me and begged me to save them. said they werent doing anything, just being chased. they wanted to jump in the canoe, but i told them to chill, didnt hear the dogs yet. told them to crowd through the brush, get up the crick a bit, then wade down and get in. thats what they did.

we lit out for our spot, and after a bit we heard the dogs and men shouting, but they were far off. we kept moving, and by the time we hit the river, everything was quiet. we hid out in some cottonwoods and were good.

one of these guys was old, maybe seventy, bald head, gray whiskers. had on some ratty old clothes, and a big carpet-bag. the other guy was younger, maybe thirty, dressed just as rough. after breakfast, we all just laid off and talked. turned out they didnt even know each other.

the old guy asked the younger one what got him into trouble. younger guy said he was selling some tooth cleaner that actually worked, but it also took off the enamel. he stuck around one night too long, and ran into the old guy on the trail. old guy told him they were coming for him, so they decided to bail together. thats their story, i guess.

so i'd been runnin this little temperance revival for about a week. women folks loved it, cause i was really givin it to the drinkers. bringin in five or six bucks a night—ten cents a head, kids and black folks free. business was boomin. then last night some rumor spreads i got a private jug on the side. a guy wakes me up this morning, says folks are gatherin quietly, with dogs and horses, gonna give me a half-hour head start, then chase me down. if they catch me, it's tar and feather time. skipped breakfast—no appetite for that mess.

"old man," says the younger guy, "how 'bout teamin up?"

"im open to it. what's your deal?"

"printer by trade; dabble in patent meds; theater actor—tragedy stuff; do mesmerism and phrenology when i can; teach singin-geography school for kicks; throw in a lecture here and there—do a lot, as long as it ain't real work. what about you?"

"done a lot of doctorin in my day. layin' on hands for cancer, paralysis, those things; fortune tellin's alright if someone's there to dig up the facts. preachin and workin camp meetin's and missionary stuff too."

nobody said anything for a bit. then the young guy sighs, "alas!"

"what's got you sayin 'alas'?" asks the baldhead.

"just thinkin how i've sunk to this life, low company."

"company not good enough?" baldhead snaps.

"it's good enough; as good as i deserve. brought myself down. don't blame y'all, i deserve it. world can take everything, but not my grave. one day i'll rest there, forget it all."

"why's your broken heart our problem?" baldhead says.

"not blamin you. did it myself. gotta suffer—it's right."

"from where'd you fall?"

"you wouldn't believe. world never does. secret of my birth—"

"secret of your birth!" baldhead cuts in.

"gentlemen," the young guy says solemnly, "i'll tell you. i'm a duke."

jim's eyes bug out, mine too. baldhead goes, "no! really?"

so my great-grandfather, supposedly the eldest son of some duke, came to this country a while back, looking for freedom or something. he married, had a kid, and died around the same time as his dad. the next son in line took over the titles and estates, and my great-grandfather's kid got ignored. im supposedly a direct descendant of that kid, which makes me the rightful duke of bridgewater. but here i am, on a raft with felons, feeling pretty down on my luck.

jim and i felt bad for the guy, so we tried to cheer him up. he said it would mean a lot if we just acknowledged him, so we said we would. he told us to bow when we talk to him, call him "your grace" or "my lord", and wait on him at dinner. we did all that, and you could tell it made him happy.

but then he started getting quiet, like something was bothering him. finally, he speaks up and says, "look, im sorry for you, but youre not the only one whos had troubles like that." he starts talking about how hes not the only person whos been wronged, and how hes got a secret about his birth. then he starts crying.

we were both pretty surprised, and we asked him what he meant. he asked if he could trust us, and we said yeah, so he tells us hes the late dauphin. jim and i just stared at him, and then he tells us hes the son of looy the sixteen and marie antonette. we were like, "youre way too old to be that guy, youd have to be hundreds of years old."

he just says trouble has aged him, and thats why hes got gray hair and is balding. hes pretty convinced hes the rightful king of france, and hes just a guy in blue jeans, feeling sorry for himself.

well, he broke down on us, sobbing, and jim and i were lost on what to do. glad we had him, but feeling sorry too. we tried comforting him like we did with the duke. he said nothing would help except being dead and done with it all. but, it eased him up a bit when folks treated him right, calling him “your majesty,” waiting on him first at meals, and standing till he said sit. so, jim and i did all that, and it cheered him up. 

the duke didn’t seem too pleased though, stayed sour for a while, despite the king being friendly, talking about how the duke’s great-grandfather was respected by his own father. eventually, the king said, “look, we’re stuck on this raft together, bilgewater, no point in being sour. i wasn’t born a duke, you weren’t born a king, so why worry? make the best of it. we got food, an easy life—let’s be friends.” 

the duke agreed, and jim and i were relieved. a raft’s no place for bad blood; everyone needs to feel right with each other. 

didn’t take long to figure those guys weren’t kings or dukes, just frauds. kept that to myself though. no point starting trouble. if they wanted to be called kings and dukes, fine by me, keeps the peace. didn’t tell jim either, ‘cause it wouldn’t help. learned from pap, with folks like them, you let them have their way.

they asked a bunch of questions, curious why we covered up the raft, hid by day instead of moving. asked if jim was a runaway. i said, “goodness sakes, would a runaway head south?” they agreed he wouldn’t. had to come up with something, so i said:

my folks lived in pike county, missouri, where i was born. they all died off except me, pa, and my brother ike. pa said he'd move in with uncle ben, who's got a small place on the river, 44 miles below orleans. pa was pretty poor and had some debts, so when he paid them off, we were left with 16 dollars and our slave, jim. that wasn't enough to take us 1400 miles, no matter how we traveled. when the river rose, pa got lucky and found a piece of a raft, so we decided to head to orleans on it. but pa's luck didn't last - a steamboat ran over the front of the raft one night, and we all went overboard. jim and i made it out okay, but pa was drunk and ike was only 4, so they didn't make it.

the duke says he's gonna figure out a way for us to travel during the day without getting caught. he's thinking about it, trying to come up with a plan. we're gonna lay low for now, don't wanna go through that town in daylight, might not be safe.

later that night, it started looking like rain, heat lightning flashing low in the sky, leaves shivering. the duke and the king started checking out our shelter, seeing what kind of beds we had. my bed was a straw tick, better than jim's corn-shuck tick. those things are uncomfortable, cobs poking into you, making a rustling sound when you move. the duke wanted my bed, but the king said no. he told the duke to take the corn-shuck bed himself, saying it wasn't fit for someone of his rank.

jim and i were worried for a minute, thought there'd be more trouble between them. but then the duke just gave in, said he's used to being mistreated, that he can handle it.

we slipped out as soon as it got dark. king told us to hang back towards the middle of the river, no lights till we're a ways past the town. we caught sight of the town lights after a bit, just a small cluster of 'em, and we glided on by, about half a mile out, no problem. when we're three-quarters of a mile downriver, we hoist up our signal lantern. around ten, the storm hits - rain, wind, thunder, lightning, the whole deal. king tells us to both keep watch till the weather lets up, then him and the duke crawl into the wigwam and crash for the night. i'm on watch till twelve, but i wouldn't've slept even if i had a bed, 'cause you dont see storms like that every day. wind's screaming, lightning's flashing every few seconds, and you can see the islands and trees getting whipped around. thunder's rumbling, then another flash and boom. waves are washing over the raft, but i'm not wearing anything, so i dont mind. we're not worried about snags, lightning's flashing enough that we can see 'em coming.

i'm supposed to take the middle watch, but i'm getting sleepy, so jim says he'll cover the first half for me. he's always doing stuff like that for me, jim is. i crawl into the wigwam, but the king and duke are sprawled out, so i just lay outside. rain's warm, waves aren't too high, so i'm good. around two, they start coming up again, and jim's gonna wake me, but he decides not to, thinks the waves aren't high enough to be a problem. but then this huge wave comes out of nowhere and washes me overboard. jim's laughing so hard he's almost crying. he's always been easy to get laughing, anyway.

i take the watch, and jim crashes out. by the time the storm lets up for good, first light of dawn's coming up, and i wake jim, we hide the raft for the day.

after breakfast, king breaks out this old deck of cards, and him and the duke play seven-up for a bit, five cents a game. then they get bored, decide to "lay out a campaign". duke digs into his bag, pulls out a bunch of little printed bills, starts reading 'em out loud. one says he's "the celebrated Dr. Armand de Montalban, of Paris", gonna lecture on phrenology, ten cents a head, and do character charts for twenty-five cents. duke says that's him. another bill says he's "the world-renowned Shakespearian tragedian, Garrick the Younger, of Drury Lane, London". got a bunch of other names and claims, like finding water and gold with a divining rod, breaking witch spells, all that. then he says:

"but the histrionic muse is the darling. you ever tread the boards, Royalty?"

king says "no".

so the duke tells me we're gonna do this play thing in the next town we hit. gonna hire a hall and do some scenes from richard iii and romeo and juliet. i'm down, anything for some cash, but i dont know the first thing about actin. duke says its easy, so we start right away.

he tells me about romeo and juliet, says he's used to playin romeo and the king can be juliet. king's a bit worried bout lookin odd as juliet, what with his white whiskers and all, but duke says dont worry, the costumes will take care of that. he pulls out some curtain-calico suits and a white nightshirt and ruffled nightcap. king's happy with that, so duke starts readin the parts out loud, prancin around and actin it out.

we hit this little one-horse town down the bend, and duke says he's figured out how to get some stuff done without puttin jim in danger. king wants to come along, see if he can scrounge up some work. we're out of coffee, so jim says i should tag along and grab some.

town's dead, everybody's gone to some camp meetin in the woods. we find this sick guy in a backyard, and he gives us directions. king's gonna hit up the camp meetin, see what he can find. duke's lookin for a print shop, and we find it, this little hole over a carpenter shop. everybody's gone to the meetin, so we just walk on in.

its a mess, ink everywhere, handbills on the walls with horses and runaways on em. duke sheds his coat, says he's good to go. me and the king take off for the camp meetin.

we get there, and its a real scene. thousand people, teams and wagons everywhere, sheds sellin lemonade and gingerbread. duke's gonna do his thing, and i'm just along for the ride.

preaching's happening under big sheds, same as before but larger. benches made from log slabs, round side drilled for stick legs. no backs. preachers on high platforms. women in sun-bonnets, some linsey-woolsey, some gingham, few young ones in calico. some young men barefoot, kids just in tow-linen shirts. old women knitting, young folks sneaking around courting.

first shed we hit, preacher's lining a hymn. two lines, everyone sings, real grand with the crowd. repeat. folks get louder, start groaning, shouting. preacher gets into it, pacing the platform, leaning over, arms going wild. holds up his Bible, waving it, shouting about the brazen serpent: “Look and live!” crowd shouts back, “Glory! Amen!”

so the king gets up and starts talking, loud enough for everybody to hear. he tells them he's a pirate, been doing it for thirty years in the indian ocean. his crew got pretty beat up last spring, and he's home now looking for new guys. but he got robbed last night and ended up on shore without any money, and he's actually glad about it. he says it's the best thing that's happened to him, 'cause now he's a changed man and happy for the first time in his life. 

he's gonna start working his way back to the indian ocean, and he's gonna try to get all the pirates to change their ways. he thinks he can do it 'cause he knows all the crews out there. it's gonna take him a while, but he's gonna do it, and every time he convinces a pirate, he's gonna give credit to the people at the pokeville camp-meeting.

then he starts crying, and everybody else does too. somebody yells out to take up a collection for him, and a bunch of people jump up to do it. but then somebody says let him pass the hat around himself, and everybody agrees. 

so the king goes around the crowd with his hat, wiping his eyes and thanking people and stuff. girls are crying and asking to kiss him, and he's hugging and kissing them all. he gets invited to stay for a week, but he says he's gotta get to the indian ocean as soon as possible. 

when we get back to the raft, he counts up the money and he's got eighty-seven dollars and seventy-five cents. and he also found a three-gallon jug of whisky under a wagon in the woods. he says it's been a pretty good day, better than any day he's had doing missionary work. he says pirates are way easier to work with than heathens at a camp-meeting.

so the duke thought he was doing alright till the king showed up, then he didnt think so much. he'd printed off a couple little jobs at the printing office, like horse bills, and got paid four bucks for it. and he got ten bucks worth of ads, but he told them he'd put em in for four bucks if they paid upfront, so they did. the paper was supposed to be two bucks a year, but he got three subscriptions for half a buck each, as long as they paid cash - they were gonna pay in cordwood and onions, but he said he just bought the place and was gonna run it for cash. 

he even set up this little poem he made himself, three verses, kinda sad - it was called "yes, crush, cold world, this breaking heart". he left it all set up to print and didnt charge nothing for it. he made nine fifty and said he'd done a pretty good day's work.

then he shows us this other thing he printed, had a picture of a runaway slave with a bundle on a stick, said "200 dollar reward" - it was all about jim, described him perfect. said he ran away from st jacques' plantation last winter, probably headed north, and whoever caught him could get the reward.

"now," the duke says, "after tonight we can travel in the daytime. whenever we see someone coming, we can tie jim up and show this handbill, say we caught him up the river and are too poor to take a steamboat, so we got this raft on credit and are headed down to get the reward." 

we all thought the duke was pretty smart, figured we could make some miles that night and get out of town before things got messy. we laid low till about ten, then we slid on out, kept our lantern down till we were out of sight.

when jim woke me up for my watch at four, he asked if we were gonna run into any more kings. i told him no, i didnt think so. he said thats alright, he dont mind one or two kings, but the one we met was pretty drunk, and the duke wasnt much better. 

i found out jim had been trying to get the duke to talk french, just so he could hear what it sounded like, but the duke said he'd been in this country too long and had too much trouble, so he forgot it.

sun's up now, but we kept on drifting. king and duke dragged themselves out looking rough, but a quick jump in the river perked them right up. after breakfast, king kicked back on the raft, boots off, pants rolled up, legs in the water, pipe lit, diving into Romeo and Juliet. once he had it down, they started rehearsing. duke had to coach him on every line, had him sighing and clutching his heart. finally said he was getting it, "but you can't yell _Romeo!_ like a bull—more like a whisper, all soft. R-o-o-meo, that's it; Juliet's no barn animal."

next, they pulled out swords the duke made from oak laths and started a Richard III sword fight. they were all over the raft, real spectacle. but king tripped, took a dunk, so they paused, swapped stories from the river days.

after lunch, duke says, "Capet, we need to up our game for a real show. gotta have something for encores."

king asks, "What's encores, Bilgewater?"

duke explains, then says, "I'll do the Highland fling or sailor's hornpipe; you—i got it—you'll do Hamlet’s soliloquy."

"Hamlet’s what?"

"His soliloquy, most famous Shakespeare bit. it's grand, always kills. don’t got it in the book, but i'll dig it from memory."

so he paced, thinking hard, frowning, raising eyebrows, smacking his head, even squeezed out a tear. finally, he nailed it. told us to watch. struck a pose, leg out, arms up, head back, skyward. then he tore into it, teeth gritting, chest puffed, blew away anything i'd seen. learned it easy while he drilled the king.

to be or not to be, that's the real question. life's long because we bear so much until we face the end. fear of what's beyond kills our rest, the natural second act. we toss ourselves into fate's wild swings rather than face the unknown. that's where we stop and think: if only we could wake up the dead like duncan with a knock. who takes the hits from time, the oppressor's wrong, the proud man's disdain, the law's delays, and the quietus we long for? in the dead middle of the night, when the world yawns, the undiscovered country where no one returns breathes trouble on us. resolution fades with worry. all the clouds over us shift away, and action's lost. it's a wish to be completed. but hold on, ophelia: don't open your heavy marble jaws. just get to a nunnery—go!

the old man got into that speech, like he was made for it. when he was in the zone, it was something to watch him tear into it.

soon as we could, the duke got some show bills made, and for a few days on the raft, it was all sword-fighting and what he called rehearsing. one morning, we spotted a little town in a bend, tied up in a creek lined with cypress, and all of us but jim took the canoe to see if there was a chance for our show.

we got lucky; a circus was coming that afternoon, folks were rolling in on old wagons and horses. circus'd clear out by night so our show had a shot. duke rented the courthouse, stuck up our bills. they read like this:

so we're wandering around this town and it's all old, dried-up frame houses and stores on stilts, i guess to keep them out of the water when the river overflows. they got little gardens, but they're all weeds and sunflowers and trash, you know, old boots and bottles and rags. the fences are all crooked and made from different boards, and they got these leather hinges on the gates. some of them used to be whitewashed, but that was a long time ago.

all the stores are on one street, and they got these white awnings out front. people hitch their horses to the posts and just leave them there. there's always these guys just hanging out under the awnings, whittling on boxes and chewing tobacco. they're all wearing these wide straw hats and they just talk slow and lazy, using a lot of cuss words. they all know each other, calling each other bill and buck and hank. they're always asking each other for tobacco, like:

"hey, hank, gimme a chaw"

and hank's all "cant, i only got one left, ask bill"

so then he asks bill, and maybe bill gives him some, or maybe he just lies and says he dont got none. some of these guys never got any money or tobacco of their own, they just borrow it all the time. they'll tell you they just gave their last chaw to someone else, but it's usually a lie. like, i heard this one guy say:

"i wisht you'd len me a chaw, jack, i just gave ben thompson my last one"

and jack's all "yeah right, your sister's cat's grandmother got some too". he's not buying it, so he tells the guy to pay him back the chaws he already borrowed before he'll lend him any more.

they mostly chew this natural leaf tobacco, and when they borrow some, they just gnaw on it with their teeth till it breaks in two. then the guy who owns the tobacco looks at it all sad and says:

"here, take the plug, you can have the chaw"

streets were just mud. nothing but mud. black as tar and a foot deep in spots. two, three inches everywhere else. hogs everywhere too, just loafing about. muddy sow and her pigs would plop right in the street, folks had to walk around them. sow looked happy, like she was getting paid to do nothing but lie there and wave her ears. then you’d hear some loafer yell, “get him, Tige!” and the sow would run, squealing, with dogs hanging off her ears and more chasing. loafers loved it, watched and laughed till a dog fight broke out. nothing got them going like a dog fight, unless they were lighting up a dog with turpentine or chasing one with a tin pan tied to its tail.

by the river, houses leaned like they’d fall any minute. some folks moved out, others stayed in the dangerous ones. bits of land kept collapsing into the river, making the town crawl back to stay safe.

as noon came, streets filled with wagons and horses. families from the country ate in their wagons. lots of whisky, saw three fights. someone shouted, “here comes old Boggs, in for his monthly drunk!” loafers got excited, Boggs was their entertainment. one said, “wonder who he’s after this time.” another joked, “wish he’d threaten me, then I’d know I’d live forever.”

Boggs rode in, loud and drunk, waving around in the saddle, red-faced and over fifty. everyone laughed and shouted, and he shouted back, saying he was gonna kill Colonel Sherburn first, then deal with everyone else.

he rode by me, asked if I was ready to die, then moved on. I was scared, but a guy told me Boggs was harmless, just mouthy when drunk. best-natured fool in Arkansas, never hurt anyone. Boggs rode to the biggest store, leaned down, and yelled under the awning.

so i'm standing there, watching this whole thing go down. some guy, boggs, is yelling at sherburn, calling him out for something. the whole street is packed, people laughing and having a good time. then sherburn comes out, looking all calm and collected, and tells boggs to shut up. says he'll put up with it till one o'clock, but after that, boggs is done. 

boggs, he just wont quit. keeps yelling and cussing, even after sherburn goes back inside. some guys try to calm him down, tell him to go home, but he wont listen. he's still going strong when somebody says to go get his daughter, maybe she can talk some sense into him. 

next thing i know, boggs is stumbling down the street, no horse, just him and a couple friends trying to hold him up. he looks pretty rough. then i hear somebody call out "boggs" and i see sherburn standing there, pistol in his hand. boggs sees it too, and his eyes go wide. he throws up his hands and starts begging, but sherburn just fires, twice. boggs goes down hard, and his daughter comes running, screaming. she throws herself on him, crying, and the crowd just surges in, trying to get a look. 

sherburn, he just drops his pistol and walks away, like nothing happened.

i was at the window, close to boggs, when they laid him on the floor. they put a bible under his head and another one on his chest, but first they had to tear open his shirt to see where the bullet went in. he took about a dozen long gasps, his chest lifting the bible up and down, and then he just stopped. they pulled his daughter away, screaming and crying, and took her off. she was maybe sixteen, pale and scared.

everybody in town was there, pushing to get a look. people were saying "you've had your turn, give somebody else a chance" but nobody was moving. i figured there might be trouble so i slid out. the streets were full, everybody excited, telling their version of what happened. this one guy, tall and lanky with a big hat and cane, was acting out the whole thing, marking where boggs and sherburn stood. he even fell down on his back, saying "bang" like he was sherburn. people were impressed, said he got it just right. they were all treating him with bottles.

then somebody said sherburn should be lynched and next thing you know everybody's yelling for it. they were snatching down clotheslines, anything to use for a hanging. they swarmed up towards sherburn's house, whooping and raging, kids running ahead, women screaming in the windows. it was a mess. they were all packed in front of sherburn's fence, yelling "tear it down" and then they just started ripping and tearing at it. the whole front wall of the crowd just rolled in.

sherburn steps out on the porch, double-barrel gun in hand, and just stands there, calm, looking down at the crowd. the noise stops, and everyone takes a step back. 

he dont say a word, just runs his eye over the crowd, and wherever he looks, people try to stare him down, but they cant, they end up looking away, all sneaky. then sherburn laughs, not a pleasant laugh, more like when you eat bread with sand in it. 

he says, slow and scornful: "you think you got what it takes to lynch somebody? thats amusing. you think you're brave enough to take on a man? you're only brave when you're picking on someone who cant fight back, like some poor woman who dont got nobody. but when it comes to a man, you're not so brave. 

"i know you, i know your kind. average man's a coward, whether its in the north or the south. you're only brave when you're in a group, and even then, you're just as brave as anyone else. you dont hang murderers because you're afraid of their friends, thats why. 

"you made a mistake, you didnt bring a real man with you, and you didnt come at night with masks. you brought buck harkness, and if he hadnt been there to get you riled up, you wouldnt have done nothing. 

"you dont like trouble, none of you do. but when someone like buck harkness starts yelling, you're afraid to back down, afraid people will think you're a coward. so you follow him, like a bunch of cowards. 

"a mob's the worst thing there is, thats what an army is, a mob. they dont fight with real courage, they fight because they're afraid of what others will think. and a mob without a real man leading it, thats just pathetic. 

"you should just go home, and hide. if anyone's gonna do any real lynching, it'll be done in the dark, with masks, and with a real man leading the way. now leave, and take buck harkness with you." 

the crowd breaks apart, and runs off in all directions, buck harkness following after them, looking pretty pathetic. i couldve stayed, but i didnt want to.

i snuck into the circus, waited for the watchman to walk by, then dove under the tent. had my twenty-dollar gold piece and some other cash, but figured id better save it, you never know when youre gonna need it on the road with strangers around. cant be too careful, right. i dont mind spending money on circuses if its the only way, but no point in wasting it.

this circus was something else. when they all rode in, two by two, it was a real sight. gentlemen and ladies, side by side, just in their drawers and undershirts, no shoes or stirrups, looking all easy and comfortable. mustve been twenty of them, and every lady was a looker, perfect complexion, beautiful, like royalty or something. and the clothes, man, they were dressed to the nines, diamonds everywhere. it was a powerful fine sight, never seen anything like it. then they started getting up, one by one, and weaving around the ring, all gentle and wavy, men looking tall and straight, ladies dresses flapping around their hips. 

they picked up speed, all of them dancing, horses leaning in, ring-master cracking his whip, clown telling jokes. then they all dropped the reins, ladies put their hands on their hips, gentlemen folded their arms, and man, the horses just about bent in half. one by one they skipped off into the ring, made these sweet bows, and scampered out. everybody went wild, clapping and cheering.

man, the whole circus was wild. that clown just kept firing back at the ringmaster with the funniest stuff, like he had jokes on tap, ready to go. no clue how he managed it. then this drunk guy tries to jump in, claiming he's some riding pro. they try to push him out, but he won't budge, and the crowd's going off, yelling to throw him out. women screaming, the whole deal. 

ringmaster steps in, says to let him ride if he promises to chill out. folks laugh, he gets on, and the horse goes nuts. circus guys are holding on for dear life, and this guy's flopping around like a ragdoll. crowd's losing it, but i'm just thinking he's gonna break his neck. 

somehow, he pulls himself up, stands on the horse, and starts stripping—seventeen layers! turns out he's one of their own, pulling a fast one on everyone. ringmaster looks like he bit into a lemon. i felt dumb, but man, i wouldn't trade spots with that ringmaster for anything. best circus i've ever seen, and it's got my vote anytime.

that night we had our show. only about twelve folks showed up, barely enough to cover costs. they laughed the whole time, which ticked the duke off, and everyone left before it was over. except one kid who was asleep. duke figured these Arkansaw folks didn’t get Shakespeare; they wanted low comedy or worse. so he decided to give them what they wanted. next morning, he made some big posters with black paint and slapped them all over town. they read:

AT THE COURT HOUSE! FOR 3 NIGHTS ONLY! _The World-Renowned Tragedians_ DAVID GARRICK THE YOUNGER! AND EDMUND KEAN THE ELDER! _Of the London and Continental Theatres_, In their Thrilling Tragedy of THE KING’S CAMELOPARD OR THE ROYAL NONESUCH!!! _Admission 50 cents_.

biggest line at the bottom said:

LADIES AND CHILDREN NOT ADMITTED.

“there,” he says, “if that line don’t bring them in, i dont know Arkansaw!”

all day, him and the king set up a stage, curtain, and some candles for footlights. that night, the place filled with men quick. once it was packed, the duke quit the door, slipped to the stage, and started bragging up the tragedy and Edmund Kean the Elder’s big part. when he got their hopes up, he rolled up the curtain. out pranced the king on all fours, naked, painted like a rainbow. the rest of his getup? wild and funny as hell. folks nearly died laughing. when he finished, they clapped and cheered till he did it again, and then once more for good measure. 

duke dropped the curtain, bowed, and announced the tragedy would play two more nights due to pressing London gigs. said if they liked it, tell their friends to come. 

twenty folks yelled, “What, is it over? Is that _all?_”

duke said yep. then it got rowdy. folks shouted “Sold!” and started for the stage. but a big guy jumped up and said, “Hold on! we’re sold bad, but let’s not be the town's joke. let’s talk up the show and sell the rest of them! that way we’re all in the same boat.” everyone agreed, “the judge is right!” so they left quietly, planning to reel in the rest.

so next day all you could hear was how good that show was. house was packed again that night and we sold the crowd the same way. when me and the king and the duke got back to the raft we had supper, then around midnight they had jim and me take her out and float her down the middle of the river, hide her about two miles below town.

third night the house was crammed, but it was the same people from the other two nights. i stood by the duke at the door, saw every guy that went in had his pockets bulging or something under his coat - and it wasnt perfume, thats for sure. i could smell rotten eggs and cabbages, and if i know the smell of a dead cat, there was a bunch of them in there. i went in for a minute but it was too much, couldnt take it. when the place was full the duke gave some guy a quarter to watch the door and we slipped out, him and me. as soon as we were in the dark he says:

"walk fast till we get away from the houses, then make a run for the raft"

i did, he did, and in two seconds we were gliding down the river, dark and quiet. i figured the king was in for a rough time with the crowd, but nope - he just crawls out from under the wigwam and asks how it went. he hadnt even been to town.

we didnt show a light till we were ten miles below the village. then we lit up, had supper, and the king and the duke were laughing about how they took those people for a ride. the duke says:

"greenhorns, we knew theyd fall for it - first house keeps quiet, lets the rest of the town get roped in, and theyd lay for us the third night"

they took in 465 dollars in three nights. i never saw money come in like that before. later, when they were asleep, jim says:

"you surprised how them kings act, huck?"

"no"

"why not?"

"its just how they are, i reckon - all alike"

"but huck, these kings of ours are something else"

you read about em once, you'll see. look at henry the eighth; this one's a sunday-school teacher next to him. and look at charles the second, louis fourteen and fifteen, james the second, edward the second, richard the third, and a bunch more, not to mention all those saxon heptarchies that used to cause chaos. henry, when he was in his prime, he was something. married a new wife every day, chopped her head off next morning like he was ordering eggs. 'fetch up nell gwynn,' he says. they bring her up. next day, 'chop off her head!' and they do it. ‘fetch up jane shore,’ and she comes. next morning, same thing. 'ring up fair rosamun.' she answers. next day, head gone. and he made em tell him tales every night till he got a thousand and one tales, put em in a book, called it domesday book. good name. you don't know kings, jim, but i do; and this old rip of ours is one of the cleanest in history. henry decides to stir up trouble here. does he give notice? no. just dumps tea in boston harbor, declares independence, dares em to come. his style. had suspicions of his father, the duke of wellington. what does he do? drown him in a butt of malmsey. left money around him? he took it. paid him for something? better make sure he does it, or he won't. opened his mouth? if he didn’t shut it quick, he'd lose a lie every time. that's henry for you. if we had him, he'd have fooled the town worse than ours did. i’m not saying ours are lambs. they ain’t. but they’re nothing like that old ram. kings are kings, you gotta make allowances. they’re a rough lot. it's how they're raised.

"but this one smells so bad, huck."

"well, they all do, jim. we can’t help a king's smell; history doesn’t say how."

"now the duke, he's kind of alright in some ways."

"i feel the same, jim. but they're on our hands. we gotta remember what they are and make allowances. sometimes i wish there was a country without kings."

what's the use telling jim these ain't real kings and dukes? wouldn't help; and like i said, you can't tell em from the real kind.

i woke up at daybreak, jim was sittin there with his head down, moanin. he does that a lot, thinkin about his family. he's never been away from home before, so its tough on him. i know how he feels, he's always talkin about his wife and kids when he thinks i'm asleep. says stuff like "po little elizabeth, po little johnny, it's mighty hard, i spec i aint ever gwyne to see you no mo".

this time i started talkin to him about his family, and he tells me about the time he slapped his little girl elizabeth. she was standin by the door, and he told her to shut it, but she just stood there, smilin at him. he got mad, and slapped her, then he left the room. when he came back, the door was still open, and elizabeth was standin there, cryin. then the wind slammed the door shut behind her, and she never moved. jim was scared, thought he'd hurt her bad. but she was just deaf and dumb, and he felt terrible for treatin her bad.

next day, we stopped under a willow tree, and the duke and king started plottin how to work the towns on either side of the river. jim told the duke he hoped it wouldn't take long, cuz bein tied up all day in the wigwam was gettin to him. we gotta tie him up when we leave him alone, or it wont look like he's a runaway. the duke said he'd figure out a way to get around it, cuz it's tough on jim.

that duke was sharp, no doubt. got Jim all kitted out like King Lear—long curtain-calico gown, white horse-hair wig and whiskers. then painted him dead blue all over, like he'd been floating face down for days. can't say I'd seen a more godawful sight. then the duke wrote up a sign on a shingle:

_Sick Arab—but harmless when not out of his head._

nailed it up front of the wigwam. Jim was alright with it, said it beat being tied up and scared every time the wind blew. duke told him to relax, but if anyone came poking around, to jump out and howl like a wild beast. smart thinking. though truth be told, most folks wouldn't stick around long enough for a howl. he looked worse than dead.

those two scoundrels wanted to try the Nonesuch again, money was good, but they figured it was too risky now. news might've traveled. couldn't settle on a scheme, so duke said he'd think on it, maybe cook up something for the Arkansas village. king figured he'd just wing it in another town, trusting 'Providence'—likely meant the devil. we bought some store clothes last stop. king put on his, told me to do the same. I did. he looked sharp, all in black. amazing what clothes can do. before, he was just an old rip, but now, with a bow and a smile, he looked like he stepped outta the Bible.

Jim cleaned up the canoe, I got the paddle ready. big steamboat up the shore, loading freight. king says,

"seein' how I'm dressed, maybe I should say I'm from St. Louis or Cincinnati. Huck, fetch the steamboat; we'll come down to the village on her."

didn't need telling twice. took off, found a young guy on a log, sweating like it's summer. had two big carpet-bags.

"run her nose in shore," king says. I did. "where you headed, son?"

"get aboard," says the king. "hold on, my servant'll help with the bags. jump out and help the gentleman, Adolphus." meaning me, I figured.

so we headed off again, all three of us. that young guy was real grateful, said it was tough lugging his stuff in this weather. he asked the king where he was heading, and the king said he came down the river and landed in another village this morning, now going a few miles up to see an old friend at a farm. the young guy goes:

“when i first saw you, i thought, ‘that's mr. wilks, for sure, almost made it here in time.’ but then i thought, ‘nope, can't be him, or he'd not be paddling upriver.’ you _ain't_ him, are you?”

“nope, name's blodgett—elexander blodgett—_reverend_ elexander blodgett, to be exact, one of the lord's humble servants. but i feel for mr. wilks not getting here in time, hope he didn't miss anything big.”

“well, he won't miss any property, that's for sure; but he missed seeing his brother peter pass. might not mind, hard to say, but his brother would've given anything to see _him_ before he went. talked of nothing else these past three weeks; hadn't seen him since they were boys and never met his brother william—the deaf and dumb one. william's not more than thirty, thirty-five. only peter and george came out here; george was the married one, both he and his wife died last year. harvey and william are the only ones left, and like i said, they didn't make it in time.”

“oh, yeah, a month or two ago when peter first got sick; peter felt he wasn't going to get better this time. pretty old, and george’s girls were too young to keep him company, except mary jane, the redhead; kinda lonely after george and his wife died. desperately wanted to see harvey—and william too, for that matter—because he hated making a will. left a letter for harvey, said it had where his money was and how he wanted the rest divided so george's girls would be okay—george left nothing. that letter was all they got him to write.”

“oh, he lives in england—sheffield—he preaches there—never been to this country. probably didn’t have much time—or might not have got the letter, you know.”

“yeah, but that's just part of it. catching a ship next wednesday for rio de janeiro, where my uncle lives.”

“long trip. but sounds lovely; wish i was going. is mary jane the oldest? how old are the others?”

“mary jane's nineteen, susan's fifteen, and joanna's about fourteen—she's the one that’s into good deeds and has a harelip.”

“well, they ain't too bad off. old peter had friends who won't let them come to harm. there's hobson, the baptist preacher; deacon lot hovey, ben rucker, abner shackleford, levi bell the lawyer; dr. robinson, their wives, the widow bartley, and more. these were peter's closest folks, wrote about them when he wrote home, so harvey will know where to find friends when he gets here.”

“why’d you walk all the way to the steamboat?”

“she’s a big orleans boat. worried she wouldn’t stop. deep boats don’t stop for a hail. cincy boats do, but this is st. louis.”

“yeah, pretty well. owned land and houses. left like 3-4k stashed somewhere.”

“when’d he pass?”

“didn’t say, but last night.”

“yup, midday.”

“all sad, but we all go sometime. gotta be ready.”

“right, mom always said that.”

we hit the boat just as it finished loading, but the king didn’t mention boarding, so bye-bye ride. once the boat left, he made me paddle another mile to a lonely spot. he gets off, says:

“hustle back, fetch the duke, and the carpet bags. if he’s crossed, go get him. tell him to dress up nice. move it.”

i knew his game, kept quiet. got back with the duke, hid the canoe. then they sat on a log, and the king repeated everything the young guy said, trying to sound british. did a decent job for a slob. can’t mimic it, so i won’t try. then he asks the duke:

“how are you with the deaf and dumb act?”

duke says he’s got it covered, played it before. they waited for a steamboat.

middle of the afternoon, a couple of small boats passed, but from too low upriver. finally, a big one came, and they hailed it. we got on board, it was from cincy. crew got mad when they found we only needed a short ride. king stayed cool:

“if folks pay a buck a mile to get ferried in a yawl, a boat can carry them, right?”

they calmed down, agreed. when we reached the village, they rowed us ashore. about two dozen men gathered when they saw the yawl. king asks:

“can anyone tell me where mr. peter wilks lives?” they nodded like, “told you so.” one man gently says:

“sorry sir, but best we can do is tell you where he lived yesterday.”

just like that, the old coot collapses, cries on the guy’s shoulder:

“alas, our poor brother—gone, and we never got to see him; oh, it’s too hard!”

so these two clowns start blubbering and making a scene, and the duke drops his bag and starts crying too. i mean, i've seen some fake stuff in my time, but these guys took the cake. 

the men around them ate it up, though - they started sympathizing with them, carrying their bags and letting them lean on them. they told the king all about his brother's last moments, and he relayed it all to the duke in sign language. they both went on and on about this dead tanner like they'd lost their best friend. it was ridiculous.

word spread like wildfire - in two minutes, the whole town knew, and people were running from every direction to get a look. some of them were still putting on their coats as they came. we were in the middle of a huge crowd, and the noise was like a marching band. every window and yard was packed, and people kept asking, "is it them?"

but king's not havin it. he's all, "we need to be square about this, count it in front of everybody." then duke's like, "hold up, lets just make up the difference," and he starts pullin out cash from his pocket. king's all, "thats a great idea, duke," and he starts pullin out cash too.

he's goin on and on, and then he starts makin signs to duke with his hands. duke looks confused at first, but then he catches on, and he's all excited, huggin king and stuff. king's all, "see, i told you, william's on board. here, girls, take the money, its a gift from the dead guy."

mary jane went straight for him, susan and the hare-lip aimed for the duke, and man, the hugging and kissing they all started—I ain't never seen anything like it. everyone crowded around with tears in their eyes, shaking hands with those frauds, going on like:

"you dear good souls!—so lovely!—how could you!"

"—they being close friends of the deceased. that's why they're invited tonight; but tomorrow, we want everyone to come—he respected all, liked all, so it's fitting his funeral orgies be public."

"poor william, even with his condition, his heart's right. asks me to invite everyone, make 'em welcome. but no need to worry—it was what i was doing."

then he continues on, calm as ever, throwing in "funeral orgies" again and again. third time he says:

"i say orgies, not the common term, cause it ain't—obsequies is common—but orgies is right. obsequies ain't used in england no more—it’s out. we say orgies now. more exact. from greek orgo, outside; hebrew jeesum, to plant; hence inter. so, funeral orgies is an open funeral."

worst i ever saw. iron-jawed man laughs in his face. everyone shocked. "why, doctor!" abner shackleford says:

"robinson, haven’t you heard? this is harvey wilks."

king smiles, eager, reaches out, says:

"is it my poor brother’s dear friend and physician? i—"

"keep your hands off!" says the doctor. "you talk like an englishman, don't you? worst imitation ever. you, peter wilks's brother? fraud, that's what you are!"

they all took on! crowded the doctor, tried calming him, explaining how harvey showed he was harvey, knew everyone, even the dogs' names, begged him not to hurt harvey's and the girls' feelings. no use; he stormed on, saying anyone pretending to be an englishman and failing that bad was a fraud and a liar. poor girls hanging on the king, crying; and suddenly the doctor turns on them. he says:

i was your dad’s friend, im your friend, and this is me talking as a friend: steer clear of that fraud. he’s a con with his fake Greek and Hebrew nonsense. he’s got nothing but empty words, and you’re letting yourselves get fooled by him. Mary Jane, you know im genuine. listen to me and toss this fool out. im asking you.

Mary Jane stood tall, looking real sharp, and said, “here’s my answer.” she handed the king a bag of money and told him, “take this six grand, invest it for me and my sisters however you want, no receipt needed.”

then she hugged the king with Susan and the hare-lip joining in. the crowd went wild, clapping and stomping. the king just grinned proudly. the doctor said, “fine, im done. but a day’ll come when you’ll regret this.” and off he went.

the king mocked him, “sure, doc, we’ll call you,” and everyone laughed, saying it was a good jab.

CHAPTER XXVI.

once they were gone, the king asked Mary Jane about spare rooms. she said there was one for Uncle William, and she’d give her room to Uncle Harvey, bunk with her sisters on a cot. the attic had a little cubby for me.

Mary Jane showed us around. the rooms were plain but nice. she offered to clear her things for Uncle Harvey, but he didn’t mind. dresses hung on the wall with a calico curtain, an old trunk in the corner, a guitar-box, and other bits and bobs that made the room feel homey. the king liked it just as it was.

that night, they had a big supper. i stood behind the king and duke, serving them, while the others were served by the help. Mary Jane sat at the table’s head, with Susan beside her, and started the usual “bad biscuits, tough chicken” talk to fish for compliments. everyone knew the food was great and showered praise: “how do you get biscuits so perfect?” and “where’d you find these amazing pickles?” just the usual supper chatter.

so i'm eating with this girl, the hare-lip, in the kitchen. she's asking me about england, and i'm trying to keep up. she says:

"did you ever see the king?"

"william fourth? yeah, he goes to our church." i know he's been dead for years, but i'm not letting on. so she says:

"what, regular?"

"yeah, regular. his pew's right across from ours, on the other side of the pulpit."

"but i thought he lived in london?"

"where else would he live?" i'm thinking, gotta keep this going.

"but i thought you lived in sheffield?"

i'm stuck. so i pretend to choke on a chicken bone to get some time to think. then i say:

"i mean, he goes to our church regular when he's in sheffield. that's just in the summer, when he comes to take the sea baths."

"but sheffield ain't on the sea," she says.

"who said it was?" i'm trying to cover.

"you did," she says.

"i didn't," i say.

"you did," she says back.

we go back and forth like this for a bit. finally, i say:

"look, did you ever see any congress-water?"

"yeah," she says.

"did you have to go to congress to get it?"

"no," she says.

"neither does william fourth have to go to the sea to get a sea bath. they just get it in barrels, like people do with congress-water. they heat it up in furnaces at the palace in sheffield."

she says, "oh, i see. you could've just said that in the first place and saved time."

i'm relieved, think i'm out of the woods now. then she asks:

"do you go to church too?"

"yeah, regular," i say.

"where do you sit?"

"in our pew," i say.

"whose pew?"

"ours. your uncle harvey's."

"his? what's he want with a pew?"

"wants it to sit in. what did you think he wanted with it?" i'm trying to play it cool.

"i thought he'd be in the pulpit," she says.

i forgot her uncle's a preacher. so i pretend to choke on another chicken bone, get some time to think. then i say:

"do you think there's only one preacher per church?"

"why would they need more?" she says.

"to preach before a king, that's why. they've got seventeen preachers at our church."

"seventeen? that's crazy. what do they all do?"

"they don't all preach at once. just one of them does. the rest... they just kinda hang out, pass the plate, and do other stuff. mainly, they don't do much."

"what are they for, then?"

"they're for style, that's all," i say.

she asks about how servants are treated in england, and i tell her they're treated worse than dogs. no holidays, no nothing. she's surprised. i say:

"a servant's nobody in england. they're treated bad, all the time."

"don't they even get to go to church?" she says.

"nope," i say. "not even church."

i was in a tight spot again. forgot i was supposed to be this old guy's servant. next thing i know, i'm spinning some explanation about how a valley is different from a regular servant and has to go to church with the family, its the law. didnt pull it off too well, though. when i finished, i could tell she wasnt buying it. she says:

"honest injun, you been telling me a bunch of lies?"

"honest injun," i say.

"none of it true?"

"none of it. not a single lie," i say.

"put your hand on this book and say it."

it was just a dictionary, so i put my hand on it and said it. after that, she seemed a little more satisfied. she says:

"alright, i'll believe some of it. but i'm not swallowing all of it."

"what dont you believe, joe?" mary jane steps in, with susan right behind her. "it's not right to talk to him like that, he's a stranger and far from home. how would you like it if you were in his shoes?"

"that's just like you, maim," joe says. "always jumping in to defend somebody. i didnt do anything to him. he's told some tall tales, and i said i wouldnt believe it all. that's all i said. i think he can handle it."

"i dont care if it was a small thing or not," mary jane says. "he's in our house, and he's a stranger. it wasnt kind of you to say that. if you were in his place, it would make you feel ashamed. so you shouldnt say things that make others feel ashamed."

"but mam, he said—"

"it doesnt matter what he said," she says. "what matters is that you treat him with kindness, and dont say things that make him feel like he doesnt belong."

i think to myself, this girl is getting robbed by that old guy, and i'm letting it happen.

then susan jumps in, and man, she tore into hare-lip. 

i think to myself, this is another girl who's getting robbed, and i'm just standing by.

mary jane takes another turn, and she's all sweet and lovely again. when she's done, hare-lip is pretty much destroyed. so she apologizes.

"alright, then," the other girls say. "you just ask his pardon."

and she does. it's beautiful, actually. i wish i could hear her do it again. 

i think to myself, this is another girl who's getting robbed, and i'm not doing anything about it. when she's done, they all try to make me feel at home, like i'm part of the family. but i feel like dirt. i think to myself, my mind's made up. i'm gonna get that money for them, no matter what.

lit out for bed, thinking it over solo. i wondered, should i tell the doc about these cons? nah, too risky—doc might spill, and the king and duke would cook me. maybe tell Mary Jane? no, her face would give it away—they'd be gone with the cash. if she called for help, id be tangled up before it's over. only one way—gotta swipe that money without them knowing. they'll stick around till they've bled this place dry, so i'll find a moment. hide the stash, then later, when im downriver, i'll send Mary Jane a note on where it's at. better do it tonight, in case the doc's still sniffing around.

headed to search their rooms. hall's dark, found the duke's room, felt around, but figured the king wouldn't trust anyone else with the cash. checked his room, but needed a candle, and lighting one was out of the question. had to eavesdrop. heard footsteps, went to dive under the bed, but it wasn't there. hit the curtain hiding Mary Jane's dresses, slipped behind it, and froze.

they walked in, shut the door. first thing, duke looks under the bed. glad i didn't hide there. natural instinct, though. they sit, king says:

“what is it? keep it short, better for us to be downstairs mourning than up here talking.”

“this is it, Capet. uneasy about that doctor. want to know your plans. got an idea, and it's sound.”

“what is it, duke?”

“we better leave before three, head downriver with what we've got. got it easy—given back to us. i'm for cutting out.”

felt bad. would've been different before, but now it's a letdown. king snaps:

“what! and not sell the rest of the property? leave thousands lying around? it's good, salable stuff.”

duke grumbles; says the gold's enough, doesn't want to rob orphans of everything.

ok so the king tells this guy they're just gonna take the money, not hurt anyone. says the people who buy the property are the ones who'll really get screwed when it's found out they didn't own it. and those orphans, they'll get their house back, no big deal. they're young, they can earn a living. 

king talks him into it, but the guy's still not sure. king says forget the doctor, they've got the whole town on their side. so they head back downstairs. duke says they didn't hide the money in a good spot. that got my attention, i was waiting for a chance to move. 

king asks why, duke says mary jane will be in mourning and the servant will pack up the clothes and might find the money. king thinks that's a good point, so he moves the money to under the straw tick. they think it's safe there, but i knew better. i got it out of there before they were even halfway down the stairs. 

i hid it in my cubby for the time being, figured i'd move it outside later. didn't want them to find it and tear the house apart. i tried to sleep but couldn't, too worried about getting everything done. waited till the king and duke came back up, then i laid low, waiting to see what would happen. nothing did. 

so i waited till it was quiet, then i slipped back down the ladder.

i snuck up to their doors, listened, and they were snoring. so i tiptoed along, made it downstairs without a sound. i peeked through a crack in the dining room door, saw the guys watching the corpse, all asleep on their chairs. the door to the parlor was open, candle lit, and the corpse was laying there. i slipped by, but the front door was locked, key was gone. then i heard someone coming down the stairs behind me. i darted into the parlor, took a quick look around, and the only place to hide the bag was in the coffin. lid was open a foot, showing the dead guy's face, wet cloth over it, shroud on. i tucked the money bag under the lid, near his crossed hands, which were freezing, and that gave me the creeps. then i ran back across the room, hid behind the door.

it was mary jane coming down. she went to the coffin, kneeled, looked in, and started crying, back to me. i slipped out, checked on the watchers in the dining room, and they were still out cold. i made my way back to bed, feeling pretty down, considering all the trouble i went through. figured if the money could just stay put, i could write to mary jane once we're down the river, and she could dig it up, but that's not gonna happen. they'll find the money when they screw on the lid, and the king'll get it back. didnt want to try to sneak it out, too risky, and i didnt want to get caught with six thousand dollars on me. 

next morning, i went downstairs, and the parlor was closed up, watchers were gone. just the family and our crew were around. i watched their faces, but couldnt tell if anything was up. undertaker showed up with his guy, set the coffin in the middle of the room, and arranged the chairs. i saw the coffin lid was still open a bit, but i didnt dare look under it with all those people around.

they had borrowed this old melodeum, and when everything was set, some woman started playing it. it sounded pretty rough, but everyone joined in and sang anyway. then the reverend started talking, slow and serious, but just as he got going, this crazy loud dog started barking in the cellar. it was one dog, but it sounded like a whole pack. the reverend just stood there, waiting for the noise to stop, but it didn't. it was pretty awkward, and nobody knew what to do.

“no, your majesty”—always called him that when just us.

she poured her heart out. it was the niggers. she couldn’t stand the thought of them being separated. her trip to England felt ruined. i blurted out, “but they will see each other again—inside of two weeks!” before i knew it, she had her arms around my neck, begging me to say it again.

“if i prove how i know the niggers will reunite, will you go?”

“all right,” i replied, “i just need your word.” she smiled, and i asked if i could close the door. then i sat back down and said:

dont start hollering. just sit tight and take it straight. gotta be honest here, and brace yourself, Miss Mary, cause it's bad news and hard to swallow, but no avoiding it. those uncles of yours? not uncles at all. they're frauds. good-for-nothin' deadbeats. now that the worst's out, the rest's easier.

hit her like a ton of bricks, sure, but i was past the rough part, so i kept going, her eyes blazing more with each word. told her everything, from when we met that fool heading to the steamboat, all the way to when she threw herself at the king's chest at the door and he kissed her a bunch. then she jumps up, face all fired up like a sunset, and says:

"the brute! let's not waste a second—let's get them tarred, feathered, and tossed in the river!"

i say:

"right, but you mean before heading to Mr. Lothrop's, or—"

"oh," she says, realizing, "what am i thinking!" and sits back down. "ignore what I said—please—you won't, right?" she says, laying her hand on mine, and i swear id rather die than break that promise. "i didn't think, just got too riled up," she says; "go on, i won't do it again. just tell me what to do, and i'll do it."

"well," i say, "they're a rough pair, those two, and i gotta stick with them a bit longer, like it or not—cant say why; if you expose them, this town'd pull me out of their clutches, and id be fine; but someone else you dont know would be in deep trouble. so, we gotta protect him, right? sure thing. so, we won't expose them."

those words sparked a plan in my head. figured how maybe i could ditch those frauds, get them jailed here. but i didn't want to run the raft alone in daylight, answering questions solo; the plan needed to wait till late tonight. i say:

"Miss Mary Jane, here's what we'll do, and you won't have to stay at Mr. Lothrop's long, either. how far is it?"

"just under four miles—out in the country, back there."

"that'll work. you go out there, stay low till nine or half-past tonight, then have them bring you back—say you remembered something. if you get here before eleven, put a candle in this window, wait till eleven, and if I don't show, it means I'm gone, safe. then, spread the word and get these frauds jailed."

"good," she says, "i'll do it."

"and if I don’t manage to get away, but end up caught with them, you gotta say I told you everything ahead of time, and back me up as much as you can."

"back you up! you bet I will. they won't lay a finger on you!" she says, nostrils flaring, eyes snapping with fire.

ok so we gotta get you outta here before your uncles show up. you cant face them without giving something away, youre too easy to read. go before breakfast, dont wanna risk running into them. ill take care of your sisters, they can stay here for now. if anyone asks, ill tell them you went to see a friend or something, youll be back tonight. 

now about that bag of money, they dont have it. who do you think does?

wish I knew, but I don't. Had it once, stole it from them for you. Hid it, but it might not be there now. Really sorry, Miss Mary Jane. Did my best, honest. Almost got caught, had to stash it quick. Bad spot.

stop blaming yourself—it's not right. You couldn't help it. Where'd you hide it?

didn't want to remind her about her troubles. Couldn't bring myself to say what'd make her imagine that corpse with the money. Stayed quiet. Then said: 

"I'd rather not tell you where, Miss Mary Jane, if you don't mind. I'll write it down, you can read it on your way to Mr. Lothrop's. That work?"

"Yes."

So I wrote: "Put it in the coffin. It was there when you were crying. Saw you from behind the door, felt sorry, Miss Mary Jane."

Remembering her crying got me teary. Them devils shaming and robbing her under her own roof. Gave her the note, saw her eyes well up too. She shook my hand hard, said:

"Good-bye. I'll do everything you said. If I never see you again, I won't forget you. I'll pray for you, too!" And she left.

Pray for me! If she knew me, she'd find a better use for her prayers. But I bet she did it anyway—just her kind of grit. She’d pray for Judas if she wanted to—no backing down. Say what you will, but she had more spirit than anyone. Not flattery, just fact. As for beauty and goodness, she topped them all. Haven't seen her since she walked out that door, but thought of her a million times, and her promise to pray for me. If I thought it’d help, I'd pray for her too.

Mary Jane slipped out back, I guess. No one saw her leave. When I ran into Susan and the hare-lip, I asked:

"What's the name of those folks across the river you visit?"

They said:

"Mostly the Proctors."

"That's it," I said. "Nearly forgot. Miss Mary Jane told me she's gone there in a rush—one's sick."

"Which one?"

"Can't remember exactly, but I think it’s—"

"Hope it ain't Hanner!"

"Sorry, but it's Hanner."

"My goodness, she was just fine last week! Is it bad?"

"No name for it. They stayed up all night. Miss Mary Jane said she might not last."

"think about it, now! what’s up with her?"

i couldn’t come up with anything smart right off, so i says:

"mumps."

"mumps? no way! they don’t sit up with folks who got mumps."

"they don’t, huh? better believe they do with these mumps. these mumps are different. miss mary jane said so."

"how’s it different?"

"it’s mixed with other stuff."

"what other stuff?"

"well, measles, whooping cough, erysipelas, consumption, yellow jaundice, brain fever, and i don’t know what else."

"my word! and they call it mumps?"

"that’s what miss mary jane said."

"why call it mumps then?"

"cause it starts with the mumps."

"no sense in that. someone could stump his toe, get poisoned, fall down a well, break his neck, and smash his brains, and someone would say, ‘he stumped his toe.’ any sense in that? none. same with this. is it catching?"

"catching? how you talk. is a harrow catching in the dark? miss one tooth, catch another, right? can’t get away from that tooth without bringing the whole harrow along. these mumps are like a harrow, and no slouch of one either, once it’s hitched on."

"it’s awful, i think," says the hare-lip. "i’ll go to uncle harvey and—"

"oh sure," i says, "i would. sure i would. wouldn’t waste no time."

"why not?"

"think about it. your uncles need to get home to england quick, right? would they leave you to go alone? they’ll wait for you. uncle harvey’s a preacher, right? would a preacher trick a steamboat clerk? trick a ship clerk to get mary jane on board? you know he won’t. what’ll he do? he’ll say, ‘church matters have to wait; niece exposed to pluribus-unum mumps, bound to stay here three months to see.’ but if you think telling uncle harvey’s best—"

"shucks, stay here when we could be in england having fun while waiting to see if mary jane’s got it? you talk like a fool."

"maybe tell some neighbors."

"listen to that. you do beat all for stupidness. can’t you see they’d tell? best not to tell anyone."

"maybe you’re right—yeah, you are right."

"but we should tell uncle harvey she’s out, so he’s not worried?"

"yeah, miss mary jane wanted that. she says, ‘tell uncle harvey and william love and kisses, and i’ve gone over the river to see mr.’—mr.—what’s the name of that rich family uncle peter liked?—i mean the one—"

"you mean the apthorps, right?"

sure, those kind of names, they slip your mind half the time. anyway, she ran over to make sure the Apthorps would come to the auction and buy the house, cause she figures her uncle Peter would prefer them over anyone else. she’s gonna stick with them till they agree, and if she’s not too tired, she’ll head home. otherwise, she’ll be back in the morning. she said not to mention the Proctors, just the Apthorps, which is true enough since she’s talking to them about the house. i know cause she told me so.

“all right,” they said, and took off to deliver the love and kisses and the message to their uncles.

everything was smooth now. the girls wouldn’t say anything since they wanted to go to England, and the king and the duke preferred Mary Jane busy with the auction than near Doctor Robinson. i felt pretty good about it, figured i did it neatly—Tom Sawyer couldn’t have done it better. he’d add more flair, but that’s not my style.

the auction went down in the public square, dragging through the afternoon. the old man was up there looking as serious as ever, tossing in bits of scripture or goody-goody sayings, and the duke was fishing for sympathy all over the place.

eventually, they sold everything but some little lot in the graveyard. the king, he was like a giraffe trying to swallow it all. then a steamboat landed, and a crowd came up, whooping and shouting:

“here’s your opposition line! here’s your two sets of heirs to old Peter Wilks—you pay your money and make your choice!”

they brought along a nice old gentleman and a younger one with his arm in a sling. folks were yelling and laughing, but i didn’t see the joke, and figured it’d rattle the duke and king. but no, they didn’t flinch. the duke kept on like nothing was up, goo-gooing around like a jug of buttermilk. the king, he just looked down on the newcomers like it hurt him to see such frauds. he played his part well. lots gathered around him, showing support. the old gent started talking, and i could tell right off he had the English accent—better than the king’s attempt. can’t mimic his words, but he turned to the crowd and said something like this:

this caught me off guard; wasn't ready for it. got some bad luck lately—my brother broke his arm, and our stuff got left behind last night by mistake. i'm harvey wilks, and this here is my brother william. he can't hear or speak, and now he can't really sign much with one hand. we're who we say, and once i get our luggage, i'll prove it. until then, i'm heading to the hotel.

so he and the quiet guy took off. the king starts laughing, going on about:

“broke his arm—yeah, right—convenient for a fake who can't sign. lost their bags! clever under the circumstances!”

everyone laughed except a few. one was a doctor; another was a sharp-looking guy with an old carpet bag, just off the steamboat, talking low with him and watching the king. it was levi bell, the lawyer. another was a big rough guy listening to the old man and the king. when the king finished, this guy says:

“so if you’re harvey wilks, when’d you get to town?”

“day before the funeral,” says the king.

“what time?”

“evening—an hour or two before sundown.”

“_how’d_ you get here?”

“came down on the susan powell from cincinnati.”

“how come you were up at the pint in the _morning_—in a canoe?”

“i wasn’t there in the morning.”

“that’s a lie.”

some jumped in, asking him to be respectful.

“preacher or not, he’s a fraud and a liar. he was at the pint that morning. i live there, saw him. came in a canoe with tim collins and a boy.”

the doctor asks:

“would you know the boy again?”

“reckon i would, and there he is.” points at me. doctor says:

“folks, i’m not sure about the new ones, but if these two ain’t frauds, then i’m an idiot. we gotta keep 'em here till we sort this out. come on, hines; come on, everyone. we'll confront them at the tavern, and i bet we find out _something_.”

the crowd loved it. maybe not the king’s pals. we all moved out, with the doctor holding on to me, kind but firm.

we gathered in a big hotel room, lit candles, and brought in the new pair. the doctor starts:

i think these guys are fakes, and they might have buddies we don't know about. if they do, those buddies could make off with that bag of gold Peter Wilks left. if these guys aren't fakes, they shouldn't mind letting us hold onto the money until they prove themselves—sound fair?

everyone agreed. seemed like they had us cornered. but the king just looked sad and said:

“gentlemen, i wish the money was here. i'm all for a fair investigation, but sadly, it ain't. you can check if you want.”

“where is it, then?”

“well, my niece gave it to me for safekeeping, and i hid it in my bed's straw tick. didn't want to bank it, thought the bed was safe. didn't expect the niggers to take it, but they did the next morning. when i sold them, i hadn't noticed the money was gone yet. they got away with it. my servant can back me up.”

the doctor and some others snorted, not buying it. one guy asked if i saw the niggers steal it. i said no, just saw them sneaking out, thought they were avoiding trouble. that was all they wanted from me. then the doctor turned to me:

“are you english, too?”

i said yes; they laughed, calling it nonsense.

then they dove into the investigation, going at it for hours, forgetting supper. it was a mess. they made the king and the old gentleman tell their stories. anyone not biased could see the old guy was truthful, the king was lying. eventually, they called me up. the king gave me a look, so i knew to talk right. started with Sheffield and the Wilkses, but the doctor laughed, and Levi Bell, the lawyer, said:

“sit down, kid; don't push it. lying ain't your skill; you need practice. you're awkward at it.”

didn't care for the comment but was glad to be off the hook.

the doctor started to say something, turned to Levi Bell:

“if you’d been here from the start, Levi Bell—” the king cut in:

“why, is this my brother's old friend he wrote about?”

they shook hands, lawyer was pleased, talked for a bit, then quietly. finally, the lawyer said:

“that’ll do it. i'll take the order and send it with your brother's. they'll know it’s fine.”

they grabbed some paper and a pen. king sat down, twisted his head like he was trying to remember where he left his keys, and scribbled something. then they handed the pen to the duke—and suddenly the duke turned pale. but he wrote anyway. the lawyer then turns to the new old guy and says:

“you and your brother, write a few lines and sign.”

the old guy wrote, but it looked like chicken scratch. lawyer looked puzzled, says:

“well, i’ll be...” and pulls out a bunch of old letters, comparing them to the scrawl. says, “these are from harvey wilks. these two handwritings don’t match at all” (king and duke looked like fools, seeing they’d been caught), “and this old man’s writing, well, it ain’t writing at all. now, here’s some letters from—”

the new old guy jumps in:

“let me explain. nobody reads my scribble but my brother—so he writes for me. his hand you’ve got, not mine.”

“well!” the lawyer says, “this is something. i’ve got some of william’s letters too; get him to write a bit.”

“he can’t write with his left hand,” says the old guy. “if he used his right, you’d see he wrote his own and my letters. check them—they’re the same.”

lawyer checks, says:

“i think it’s true—or close enough. thought we had this cracked, but it’s gone sideways. but one thing’s clear—these two ain’t wilkses”—nodding at king and duke.

then that stubborn old goat wouldn’t admit it. said the test was unfair. his brother william was a prankster, hadn’t even tried to write. he started believing his own nonsense until the new guy cut him off:

“thought of something. anyone here help lay out my, uh, late brother peter?”

“yeah,” someone says, “me and ab turner did. we’re here.”

old guy turns to king, says:

“maybe this gentleman knows what was tattooed on his chest?”

king almost folded like a cheap chair. didn’t see it coming. how would he know the tattoo? he turned pale; the room went dead quiet, everyone leaning in. i thought, _now_ he’ll give up—no way out. but nope. figured he’d hold out till people got bored and left, so he and the duke could slip away. he sat there, started smiling, and says:

man, that was a mess. old guy’s got the nerve, i'll give him that. tried to pin the king with some story about a tattoo. just a thin blue arrow, he says. asks the crowd if they ever saw it on peter wilks.

crowd wasn't buying it. both guys with him, they say nope, no such mark. old guy turns it around, says the real mark was initials: P—B—W. writes it down for them. asks if that rings a bell.

nope again. crowd starts getting rowdy, yelling fraud. they’re ready to run us out on a rail. lawyer jumps up, says hold on. let's dig up the body, check for ourselves.

that idea caught on. crowd's all for it. lawyer says grab the suspects, too—meaning us. if they don't find the marks, we're done for.

i was sweating bullets. no way out. they had us locked up tight, marching us to the graveyard, whole town trailing behind, making a racket. dark sky, wind picking up. felt like the world's end.

i wished i hadn’t sent mary jane away. she could’ve helped. but here we were, heading to the grave, nothing but those marks between us and disaster. had to stick it out, no choice.

when we reached the graveyard, they flooded in like a storm surge. plenty of shovels, but no lanterns. digging by lightning flashes, sent someone for a light. chaos all around.

they kept digging, not caring about the storm rolling in. lightning flashed, thunder roared, and the rain came down hard, but they were too focused. one moment everything was lit up, faces, dirt flying, and then pitch black again.

finally, they got the coffin open. people pushed and shoved to get a look. Hines yanked my wrist so hard, he forgot i was even there in all the excitement.

then a lightning bolt lit everything up, and someone shouted, "there's the gold!" Hines let go, and i took off. hit the road fast, faster than i've ever run, just me, the darkness, and the storm.

when i got to town, not a soul out. i dashed straight through the main street. felt a pang seeing our house dark, but then Mary Jane’s window lit up. heart about burst. kept running, left it behind.

found a canoe, slipped the rope, and paddled to the raft. dead tired when i got there but no time to rest. yelled out to Jim, “let’s go! we’re free!” 

Jim was coming for a hug, but the lightning showed him like some ghostly King Lear, scared me half to death. he pulled me back on board, ready to celebrate, but i said, “save it for breakfast, let’s get moving!”

two seconds and we're off sliding down the river. felt good to be free again, just us and the big water. nobody around to bug us. had to skip a bit, jump up, crack my heels. couldn't help it. but then that sound hit — knew it too well. held my breath, listened, waited. next flash over the water, there they were. rowing hard, making that skiff hum. the king and the duke.

just about collapsed on the planks then, felt like crying.

when they boarded, the king shook me by the collar. "tryin' to ditch us, huh? tired of us, are ya?"

"no, your majesty, honest—please don't!"

"then spill it, or i'll shake the insides outta you!"

"i'll tell everything, your majesty. the man holding me was kind, said he had a boy like me who died. when they found the gold, he let go, whispered to run, or they'd hang me. so i ran, found the canoe, told Jim to hurry before they caught me. thought you and the duke were gone, was relieved to see you. ask Jim if i didn't."

Jim backed me up. king told him to shut up, threatened to drown me. but the duke goes, "let him go, you old fool! you'd have done the same. did you look for him when you got loose? don't remember it."

the king let go, started cussing the town. duke said, "cuss yourself, you're the one without sense. only smart move was that imaginary blue-arrow mark. saved us. without it, we'd be jailed and then worse. the gold was our real luck, got the fools rushing to see and freed us."

they went quiet, thinking. king says, "and we thought the niggers stole it."

that made me squirm.

"yeah," says the duke, slow and sarcastic, "we did."

half a minute later the king drawls, "leastways, i did."

duke replies, "actually, i did."

king bristles, "what're you getting at, Bilgewater?"

duke fires back, "what were you getting at?"

“shucks!” says the king, real sarcastic, “maybe you were asleep and didn’t know what you were doing.”

the duke fires back:

“cut the crap; do you think i’m a fool? don’t you reckon i know who put that money in the coffin?”

“yes, sir! you know because you did it yourself!”

“it’s a lie!”—and the duke lunges at him. the king yells:

“get off me! let go! i take it all back!”

the duke says:

“admit you hid that money there, planning to ditch me and come back for it yourself.”

“hold up, duke—answer me this; if you didn’t stash the money, say it, and i’ll believe you.”

“you scoundrel, i didn’t, and you know it.”

“well, then, i believe you. but just one more—don’t get mad; didn’t you think about swiping it?”

the duke pauses, then says:

“well, maybe i thought it, but i didn’t do it. but you did.”

“i swear i didn’t do it, duke. i won’t say i wasn’t going to, because i was; but somebody got there before me.”

“lie! you did it, admit it, or—”

the king starts to choke, then gasps:

“enough! i admit it!”

i felt relieved hearing that. so the duke lets go and says:

“deny it again, and i’ll drown you. it’s fitting you’re blubbering like a kid. i trusted you like a father. you should’ve been ashamed, letting it fall on those poor folks. makes me feel foolish for believing you. you were just after all the money.”

the king says, still sniffling:

“duke, you said make up the deficit; not me.”

“shut it! i don’t want to hear any more from you!” says the duke. “now you see what you got. they got their money back, and most of ours too. go to bed, and don’t bring up deficits again!”

so the king slinks off to his bottle, and the duke follows suit. soon enough, they’re thick as thieves again, snoring in each other’s arms. they both got real friendly, but the king didn’t forget not to deny hiding that money again. that eased my mind. after they started snoring, jim and i had a long chat, and i told him everything.

we didnt stop at any towns for days, just kept going down the river. we were down south now in the warm weather, far from home. started seeing trees with Spanish moss hanging like long gray beards. first time seeing them grow, made the woods look pretty solemn and dismal. with the frauds thinking they're safe, they started hitting the villages again.

first, they tried a temperance lecture; didn't earn enough for both to get drunk. then a dancing school; but they danced like kangaroos, got chased out fast. tried yellocution; didn't last long before the crowd cussed them out. they tried everything: missionary work, mesmerizing, doctoring, fortune-telling, but nothing worked. eventually, they were broke, just laying around the raft, thinking and saying nothing for hours, looking blue and desperate.

finally, they started plotting in the wigwam, talking low for hours. jim and me got nervous. figured they were planning something worse. maybe a house break-in or counterfeit scheme. we decided to steer clear of their plans and if given the chance, we'd ditch them and take off. one morning, we hid the raft near a shabby village called pikesville. the king went ashore to sniff around, see if anyone caught wind of the Royal Nonesuch. ("more like a house to rob," i thought. "and by the time you're done, me, jim, and the raft will be long gone.") he said if he wasn't back by midday, it'd be safe for the duke and me to come along.

stuck in place, duke pacing like a caged cat, mad at the world. everything wrong, everything our fault. something was cooking, no doubt. by noon, no king in sight, i figured a change was coming—maybe even _the_ change. me and duke hit the village, looking for the king, and found him, drunk as a skunk. loafers having a laugh at his expense, him all bluster and no balance. duke starts laying into him, king sassing right back, and that’s when i saw my shot. booked it down the river road, heart pounding, but finally free.

“let her loose, Jim! we’re clear!” i yelled. silence. no Jim. panic hit me like a brick. searched, yelling my head off, but nothing. sat down, cried like a kid. couldn’t stay still though. hit the road, thinking what now, and ran into a boy. asked if he’d seen Jim. he had.

“down at Silas Phelps’, two miles down,” he says. “they got him.”

“not looking for him,” i bluffed. “ran into him in the woods, said he’d gut me if i hollered.”

“safe now, they caught him. runaway from down south.”

“good job,” i said, but my heart sank. two hundred bucks on Jim. like finding money in the dirt, he said. stranger sold the chance for forty bucks, had to leave. “i’d wait years for that,” he said. “me too,” i agreed, but something felt off.

“straight as a string,” he assured. handbill matched Jim to a tee. no doubts, just business. asked for a chaw, i had none. he left. back to the raft, brain in knots. couldn’t make sense of it. after all we’d done, everything busted, Jim sold for forty lousy bucks.

once thought writing to tom sawyer to tell miss watson about jim would be right. figured jim’d be better off back home even as a slave. but then, two problems hit me. first, she’d be mad at him leaving and probably sell him downriver. second, folks would look down on him for being ungrateful. and then me—everyone would know i helped a slave run. i’d be shamed, ready to grovel if i saw anyone from town.

my conscience was grinding away, telling me how low-down and wicked i was. felt like providence gave me a smack, reminding me i was being watched while stealing jim, who never did me harm. tried blaming my upbringing, saying i wasn’t all to blame. but something inside said, “sunday school was there. they’d a taught you better.”

shivered at the thought. decided to pray, to change and be better. kneeled down but couldn’t find the words. knew why—they wouldn’t come because i wasn’t right inside. was pretending to give up sin, but holding onto the biggest one. couldn’t pray a lie—found that out.

trouble filled me up. then an idea hit: write the letter, then try praying. felt light as a feather soon as i decided. grabbed paper and pencil, full of excitement, and wrote:

miss watson, your runaway jim’s two miles below pikesville with mr. phelps. he’ll give him up for the reward.

- huck finn

felt like id finally scrubbed the grime off my soul for the first time ever. knew i could pray now, but i didnt jump right in. just sat there, paper in hand, thinking on it all. about how close id come to losing it all and how lucky i was. kept drifting back to our river trip, jim always in my mind. day or night, storms or moonlight, we just floated on, talking, laughing. couldnt find a reason to harden against him, only reasons not to. id picture him taking my watch so i could sleep, see his joy when i found my way back after the fog, and all those times he called me honey and did everything for me. remembered saving him by saying we had smallpox onboard. he said i was the best friend he ever had, the only one now. then saw that paper again.

it was tight. held it in my hand, shaking. knew it was decision time, forever. took a deep breath, and then said:

"all right, then, ill go to hell"—and ripped it up.

awful thoughts, awful words. but i let them stand. didnt think about reforming again. pushed it all out of my head, decided id go back to wickedness, since thats how i was raised and the other way wasnt for me. figured id steal jim out of slavery again, and if i could think of something worse, id do that too. if i was going in, might as well dive deep.

started plotting, turning over plans in my mind till i hit one that fit. took the bearings of a woody island downriver, waited for dark, then slipped out with the raft and hid it there. slept through the night, up before dawn, ate, put on my store clothes, packed some things, took the canoe and headed for shore. landed below where i figured phelps's place was, hid my bundle in the woods, filled the canoe with water, loaded it with rocks, and sunk it where i could find it later, near a little steam sawmill on the bank.

so i head up the road, pass the mill, see a sign—“Phelps’s Sawmill.” few hundred yards more, hit the farmhouses. eyes open, but no one around. good daylight now. didn't mind. didn't want anyone just yet. just scoping things out. plan was to come up from the village, not below. took a look, kept moving, straight to town. first guy i see there? the duke. pasting a bill for the Royal Nonesuch—same old scam. couldn’t dodge him. he looks surprised, says:

“Hel-_lo!_ Where you from?” then, eager-like, “Where’s the raft?—got her safe?”

i go:

“well, that's what i was gonna ask you.”

he's not so happy now, asks:

“why ask me?”

i say, “saw the king drunk in the tavern yesterday, knew we couldn’t get him home soon. so i wandered town. some guy offers ten cents to help haul a skiff over the river for a sheep. i help. but the sheep breaks free while loading. no dog, so we chase it everywhere. got it by dark. head back, raft’s gone. thought you guys hit trouble, took Jim—my only nigger. now i'm stuck in this strange place, no property, no nothing. sat down and cried. slept in the woods. but where's the raft, and Jim?”

“i don't know—what happened to the raft. that old fool made a trade, got forty bucks. found him drunk, lost all but what he spent on whiskey. raft gone, figured you stole it and ran.”

“wouldn't ditch my nigger—the only one i had.”

“we didn’t think. figured he was ours after the trouble we had. raft gone, broke, only choice was Royal Nonesuch again. been scraping since. where’s that ten cents? hand it over.”

had some cash, gave him ten cents. asked him to buy food, share it. hadn’t eaten since yesterday. he stays quiet. then spins on me, asks:

“think that nigger would snitch? we’d skin him if he did!”

“how? isn’t he gone?”

“no! that fool sold him, kept the money. all gone.”

"sold him?" i said, starting to cry. "he was mine, and that was my money. where's he at?—i want him back."

"well, you cant get him, so stop crying. listen—are you gonna rat us out? dont think i'd trust you. if you did—"

he stopped, but ive never seen the duke look so mean. kept whimpering, said:

"aint blowing on nobody; got no time for that. gotta find him."

he looked a bit stuck, standing there with those bills, thinking hard. finally he goes:

"i’ll tell you something. we're here three days. promise you wont blow, and wont let him blow, i'll tell you where."

i promised, and he said:

"a farmer named silas ph—" then he stopped. he was gonna spill it, but thought better. didnt trust me; wanted me gone for those three days. so he goes:

"the guy who bought him is abram foster—abram g. foster. lives forty miles back, headed to lafayette."

"all right," i said, "i can walk it in three days. starting this afternoon."

"no you wont, you start now; dont waste time, no talking on the way. keep your mouth shut and move or you'll get in trouble with us, got it?"

thats what i needed, the order i was angling for. needed room to work my plan.

"so clear out," he said. "tell mr. foster whatever you want. maybe he'll buy that he's yours—some folks dont need proof—least ive heard that down south here. tell him the handbill and reward are fake, maybe he'll buy it when you explain the scam. go on now, say what you want; but keep quiet till then."

i left, headed into the back country. didnt look back, but felt him watching. knew i could outlast him. went a mile out before doubling back through the woods towards phelps’. figured i better kick off my plan right away—wanted to keep jim quiet till these guys were gone. no interest in their kind of trouble. seen enough of them, just wanted them gone.

when i got there, it was all still and sunday-like, hot and sunny; hands were out in the fields; those faint bug and fly dronings filled the air, making it feel lonely like everyone’s dead; and if a breeze rustles leaves, it’s like spirits whispering—spirits long gone—and they always seem to talk about you. makes you wish you were dead too, done with it all.

phelps' place was one of those small cotton plantations, all looking the same. rail fence around a two-acre yard. steps made from sawed logs like barrels, used to climb the fence or for women to mount horses. sickly grass patches here and there, but mostly bare ground, like an old hat worn smooth. big double log house for the white folks, logs hewed and the gaps filled with mud or mortar, once whitewashed. kitchen joined to the house by a broad roofed passage. smokehouse behind the kitchen. three little log cabins in a row on the other side. a lone hut by the back fence. some outbuildings scattered about. ash-hopper and big kettle for soap by the hut. bench by the kitchen door with a water bucket and gourd. hound asleep in the sun, more hounds napping around. a few shade trees off in a corner. some currant and gooseberry bushes by the fence. outside the fence, a garden and watermelon patch, then the cotton fields, then woods.

went over the back stile by the ash-hopper, aimed for the kitchen. heard a spinning-wheel hum, rising and falling. loneliest sound there is. no plan, just hoping for the right words when needed. providence usually brings them if i don't meddle.

halfway there, hounds came at me. stopped, faced them, stayed still. the noise was wild. soon i was like the hub of a wheel, dogs all around, barking and howling. more hounds coming, leaping fences and corners.

a black woman rushed out of the kitchen with a rolling pin, shouting at the dogs, giving them a clip. first one, then another went howling off, others followed. half came back, tails wagging, friendly now. hounds aren't much harm anyway.

behind her, a little black girl and two boys, just wearing tow-linen shirts, clung to their mom, peeking out shy. then a white woman ran from the house, around forty-five or fifty, no hat, spinning-stick in hand. her kids behind her, acting like the others. she was grinning wide.

“it's you, ain't it?”

“yes'm,” i said without thinking.

she grabbed me tight and shook me like she couldn't stop. tears running down, she kept saying, "you dont look like your mama, but im so glad to see you! kids, it's your cousin tom! say hi." 

the kids just hid behind her. she said, "lize, get him a hot breakfast—unless you ate on the boat?"

told her i ate on the boat. she led me to the house, kids trailing behind. sat me down in a split-bottomed chair, took my hands, and said, "finally get a good look at you. we've been waiting for days. what held you up? boat got stuck?"

"yes'm—uh, aunt sally. she got stuck on a bar."

didn't really know, but my gut said it was coming up from orleans. had to think fast, so i said, "wasn't the grounding. we blew a cylinder-head."

"anyone hurt?"

"no'm. just killed a nigger."

"well, lucky. your uncle silas had a man crippled when his boat blew. he died later. uncle silas knows a family in baton rouge who knew him. they've been expecting you. uncle's gone to town and should be back soon. you must’ve met him on the road?"

"no, i came the back way. left my baggage on the wharf-boat."

"child, it'll be stolen!"

"not where i hid it."

"how'd you get breakfast early on the boat?"

thin ice here, but i said, "captain took me to the officers' lunch."

was gettin so uneasy i couldnt listen right. mind was stuck on the kids, wanted to pull 'em aside, figure out who i was. but mrs. phelps didnt give a break, just kept on. soon she gives me chills down my back:

“but here we’re a-going on, and you haven’t told me a word about sis, or any of them. now i’ll rest, you start; tell me _everything_—all about ’em; how they are, what they’re doing, what they told you to tell me; every last thing.”

well, i was stumped good. providence got me this far, but now i was stuck. no use going on—had to risk the truth. opened my mouth; but she grabs me, hustles me behind the bed, says:

“here he comes! get down—there, that’s good; cant be seen. don’t let on you’re here. i’ll play a joke. kids, don’t say a word.”

i was in a fix. but no use worrying; just had to hold still, be ready when the storm hit.

got one glimpse of the old man when he came in; then the bed hid him. mrs. phelps jumps for him, says:

“has he come?”

“no,” says her husband.

“goodness gracious!” she says, “where in the world is he?”

“cant imagine,” says the old man; “makes me uneasy.”

“uneasy!” she says; “i’m distracted! he _must_ have come; you’ve missed him. i _know_ it.”

“sally, i _couldn’t_ miss him.”

“but oh, dear, what _will_ sis say! he must have come! you missed him.”

“don’t distress me more. i don’t know what to make of it. i’m scared. no hope he’s come; couldn’t come and me miss him. sally, something’s happened to the boat, sure!”

“silas! look yonder!—up the road!—ain’t that someone?”

he jumps to the window, gives mrs. phelps her chance. she pulls me out; when he turns, there she is, beaming, me standing all meek and sweaty. the old man stares, says:

“who’s that?”

“who d'you think?”

“no idea. who _is_ it?”

“it’s _tom sawyer!_”

by jings, almost dropped through the floor! but no time to swap knives; old man grabs my hand, shakes it, and they fire off questions about sid, mary, the rest.

if they was happy, it was nothing to what i felt; like being born again. i was so glad figuring out who i was. they clung to me for two hours, and when my chin got so tired it could barely move, id told them more about my family—the Sawyer family—than ever happened to any six Sawyer families. i even explained how we blew out a cylinder-head at the mouth of White River, took three days to fix it. worked fine cause they didn’t know it wouldn’t take three days. calling it a bolthead'd work just as well.

i was feeling pretty settled on one side, not so much on the other. being Tom Sawyer was easy until i hear a steamboat coughing down the river. then i think, what if Tom Sawyer comes down on that boat? and what if he walks in here any second and calls my name before i can warn him? couldn’t have that. wouldn’t do. so i decided to go up the road and intercept him. told them i’d head up to town to grab my baggage. the old man wanted to come, but i said i’d handle the horse myself, no need for him to bother.

CHAPTER XXXIII.

hit the road in the wagon, saw another wagon halfway, and sure enough, it was Tom Sawyer. i stopped and waited. said “Hold on!” it stopped, and his mouth dropped open like a trunk, stayed there. he swallowed a few times like his throat was dry, then said:

“I ain’t done you no harm. you know that. so, what you want to come back and haunt me for?”

i said:

“I ain’t come back—I ain’t been gone.”

hearing my voice straightened him up, but he still wasn’t sure. said:

“Don’t play nothing on me, cause I wouldn’t on you. Honest injun now, you ain’t a ghost?”

“Honest injun, I ain’t,” i said.

“Well—that ought to settle it, of course; but i can’t seem to understand it. wasn’t you ever murdered?”

“No. never murdered—played it on them. come feel of me if you don’t believe.”

he did, and it satisfied him. he was that glad to see me, didn’t know what to do. wanted to know all about it right then, cause it was a grand adventure and mysterious. but i said wait, told his driver to hold on, and we drove off a bit. i told him the fix i was in, asked what we should do. he said let him think. after a while, he said:

“It’s alright; i’ve got it. take my trunk in your wagon, pretend it’s yours; you turn back, go slow, get to the house when you should; i’ll head toward town, come in later, and you act like you don’t know me at first.”

i said:

“All right; but wait. there’s one more thing—nobody knows but me. there’s a guy here i’m trying to get out of slavery, named Jim—old Miss Watson’s Jim.”

“what! why, jim is—”

he stopped, thinking. i said:

“i know what you’ll say. you’ll say it’s dirty, low-down business; but what if it is? i’m low down; and i’m gonna steal him, and i want you to keep quiet. will you?”

his eyes lit up, and he said:

“i’ll help you steal him!”

well, i just about fell over. most surprising thing i ever heard. tom sawyer, a nigger stealer?

“oh, shucks!” i said; “you’re joking.”

“i ain’t joking.”

“well then,” i said, “joking or not, if you hear anything about a runaway, remember you don’t know nothing, and i don’t know nothing.”

we loaded the trunk in my wagon, and he went his way, and i went mine. but i was so caught up in my thoughts, i forgot to drive slow and got home too quick. the old man was at the door and said:

“why, this is amazing! who’d have thought that mare had it in her? i wish we’d timed her. not a hair of sweat. wouldn’t take a hundred dollars for her now.”

that’s all he said. the most innocent, best soul i ever saw. but not surprising, being a farmer and a preacher. built his own little log church and never charged for preaching. plenty like him down south.

half an hour later, tom’s wagon pulled up, and aunt sally saw it through the window.

“somebody’s coming! i do believe it’s a stranger. jimmy, tell lize to set another plate for dinner.”

everyone rushed to the door. strangers don’t show up every year. tom, in his store clothes, loved an audience. he came up like he owned the place, tipped his hat like it was holding sleeping butterflies, and said:

“mr. archibald nichols, i presume?”

“no, son,” said the old man, “your driver’s misled you; nichols’s place is about three miles more. come in.”

tom looked back. “too late—he’s gone.”

“yes, he’s gone, so come in and eat with us. then we’ll take you to nichols’s.”

“oh, i can’t trouble you. i’ll walk.”

“but we won’t let you walk. wouldn’t be southern hospitality.”

"oh, you gotta," says Aunt Sally. "it ain't trouble at all. you gotta stay. it's a long, dusty three miles, and we can't let you walk. besides, already had 'em set another plate when i saw you coming. don't disappoint us. come on in and make yourself at home."

so Tom thanked 'em real nice, let himself be talked into it, and stepped in. once inside, he says he's a stranger from Hicksville, Ohio, name's William Thompson, and he gives another bow.

well, he kept going on, making up all sorts about Hicksville and folks there, and i'm getting a bit nervious, wondering how this helps my mess; then, still chatting, he leans over, kisses Aunt Sally right on the mouth, leans back in his chair comfy, and keeps talking. but she jumps up, wipes it off with her hand, and says:

"you owdacious puppy!"

he looks hurt, says:

“i’m surprised at you, m’am.”

"you're surprised—why, what do you think i am? i'm tempted to—what do you mean by kissing me?"

he looks kinda humble, says:

"i didn’t mean nothing, m’am. didn’t mean no harm. i thought you’d like it."

“why, you born fool!” she grabs a spinning stick, looks like she’s holding back from giving him a whack. “what made you think i’d like it?”

“well, i don’t know. only, they—they—told me you would.”

“they told you i would. whoever told you’s another lunatic. never heard the like. who’s they?”

“why, everybody. they all said so, m’am.”

she’s barely holding in; eyes snapping, fingers itching to scratch him; she says:

“who's ‘everybody’? out with their names, or there’ll be an idiot short.”

he gets up, looking distressed, fumbles his hat, says:

“i’m sorry, and i wasn’t expecting it. they told me to. they all told me to. they all said, kiss her; said she’d like it. they all said it—every one of them. but i’m sorry, m’am, and i won’t do it no more—i won’t, honest.”

“you won’t, won’t you? well, i reckon you won’t!”

“no’m, i’m honest about it; i won’t ever do it again—till you ask me.”

“till i ask you! well, i never seen the like in my born days! you’ll be the Methusalem-numskull of creation before i ask you—or the likes of you.”

“well,” he says, “it surprises me. can’t make it out. they said you would, thought you would. but—” he stops, looks around slow, hoping for a friendly eye, lands on the old gentleman’s, says, “didn’t you think she’d like me to kiss her, sir?”

“why, no; i—I—well, no, i believe i didn’t.”

then he looks my way, says:

“Tom, didn’t you think Aunt Sally’d open her arms and say, ‘Sid Sawyer—’”

“my land!” she says, jumping for him, “you impudent young rascal, to fool a body so—” going to hug him, but he fends her off, says:

“no, not till you’ve asked me first.”

so she doesn't waste time, asks him, hugs and kisses him over and over, then turns him over to the old man, who takes what's left. and after they settle down, she says:

“why, dear me, never seen such a surprise. we weren’t expecting you at all, only Tom. Sis never wrote about anyone coming but him.”

"Wasn't meant for any of us but Tom," he says. "I begged and begged, and last minute she let me come too. So me and Tom thought it'd be a real surprise for him if Tom came here first, and I tagged along later, playing like I was a stranger. But that was wrong, Aunt Sally. This ain't a place for strangers."

"No, not cheeky pups, Sid. You should've had your ears boxed; I ain't been this mad since I don't know when. But I don't mind, I'd take a thousand jokes like that just to have you here. That was some show! I won't lie, I was shocked when you gave me that smack."

We ate dinner outside between the house and kitchen; enough food for seven families, all hot too—none of that cold, tough stuff that tastes like it's been in a cellar all night. Uncle Silas said a long blessing over it, but it was worth it; didn’t cool it down any, unlike other times. We talked a lot that afternoon, me and Tom listening in, but no one said anything about any runaway, and we were too afraid to bring it up. But at supper, one little boy asks:

"Pa, can Tom and Sid and me go to the show?"

"No," says the old man, "I reckon there ain't going to be any; and you couldn't go if there was; because the runaway told Burton and me about that scandalous show, and Burton said he'd tell everyone; so I reckon they've run those loafers out of town by now."

So there it was!—but I couldn't help it. Tom and me were sharing a room and bed; tired, we said goodnight and went up, climbed out the window and down the lightning rod, headed for town; 'cause I didn't think anyone was going to warn the king and the duke, and if I didn't hurry up, they'd get in trouble for sure.

On the way, Tom told me all about how they thought I was murdered, how pap disappeared soon after, never came back, and the fuss when Jim ran away; and I told Tom about our Royal Nonesuch rascals, and as much of the raft trip as I could; as we hit the town, around half-past eight, here comes a crowd with torches, yelling and banging tin pans, blowing horns; we jumped aside to let them pass; and I saw they had the king and duke on a rail—knew it was them even though they were covered in tar and feathers, didn't look human, just like big soldier plumes. It made me sick; I felt sorry for those poor fools, couldn't hold a grudge. It was terrible to see. People can be awful cruel to each other.

we realized we were too late—nothing we could do. asked some folks hanging around what happened. turns out, everyone went to the show looking all innocent, waited till the old king was doing his thing on stage, then someone signaled, and the whole crowd jumped him.

so we headed back home, and i wasn't feeling as good as before. felt kinda low and guilty, even though i hadn't done anything. but that's conscience for you, doesn't matter if you're right or wrong, it'll bug you anyway. if i had a dog as clueless as a conscience, i'd get rid of it. takes up more space than anything else inside you and still useless. tom says the same.

stopped talking, started thinking. then tom goes:

"look here, huck, we're fools for not thinking of it sooner! i bet i know where jim is."

"no way! where?"

"in that hut by the ash-hopper. remember at dinner, saw a guy go in there with food?"

"yeah."

"what'd you think it was for?"

"for a dog."

"me too. but it wasn't for a dog."

"why?"

"part of it was watermelon."

"you're right. dogs don't eat watermelon. shows how we see but don't see."

"well, he unlocked the padlock going in and locked it after. gave uncle a key when we got up—same key, i bet. watermelon means man, lock means prisoner; can't be two prisoners on such a small place, and with kind folks. jim's the prisoner. glad we figured it out like detectives; wouldn't want it any other way. now, let's each think up a plan to steal jim, and take the one we like best."

man, what a brain for a kid! if i had tom's head, i wouldn't trade it for anything. started thinking up a plan, but knew the right one would come from tom. soon tom says:

"ready?"

"yeah," i say.

"alright—let's hear it."

"here's my plan. we check if jim's in there. then i'll get my canoe tomorrow, bring my raft from the island. the first dark night, swipe the key from the old man's pants after he sleeps, and head down the river with jim, hiding by day, running by night, like before. wouldn't that work?"

"work? sure, like rats fighting. but it's too simple; nothing to it. what's the point of a plan that's no harder than that? it's as mild as goose-milk. wouldn't make more noise than breaking into a soap factory."

i didn't say anything, because i wasn't expecting any different; knew tom's plan wouldn't have those problems.

so he tells me what he's got in mind, and i see right off it's got style, worth fifteen of mine and all. might get us all killed too, but it’d make jim free, so i’m game. i ain't gonna spell it out since i knew he'd change it a hundred times over, adding his own twists whenever he felt like it. and he did just that.

one thing's clear—tom sawyer was serious about busting jim out of slavery. couldn't wrap my head around it. here's a kid who's respectable, well-raised, bright, and kind. but he's jumping headfirst into this mess, risking his own name and his family's. i _couldn't_ get why. i figured i should tell him to stop, be a good friend, but he shut me down quick:

“don’t you think i know what i’m doing?”

“yes.”

“didn’t i _say_ i was gonna help steal the nigger?”

“yes.”

“_well_, then.”

that was that. no point arguing more. if tom said he'd do something, he did it. still couldn't see why he'd want this, so i just let it be.

when we got home, all was dark and quiet. headed down to the hut by the ash-hopper to check it out. dogs knew us, didn't raise a fuss. at the cabin, found a window-hole on the north side, board nailed across it. i say:

“here's our way in. jim can squeeze through if we pull the board.”

tom goes, “too easy. let's find something more complicated.”

“how about sawing him out, like when they thought i was dead?”

“better,” he says. “mysterious, tough, good. but let's look for something even longer. no rush.”

between the hut and the fence, there was this lean-to, all plank and narrow, just six feet wide, running as long as the hut. Padlocked door at the south end. Tom rummages around the soap-kettle, grabs that iron doohickey for lifting lids, and pries out a staple. Chain drops, door opens, we head in, strike a match. Turns out, this shed's just stuck up against the cabin, no floor, nothing but rusty old tools and a busted plow. Match dies, so we bail, put the staple back, and lock it good as new. Tom's pumped. Says:

“we’re set. we’ll dig him out. about a week!”

then we head to the house. i go in through the back, just a buckskin latch to pull. But Tom, nah, he wants drama. Tries climbing the lightning-rod. Three times up, three falls, almost cracks his skull. Rested up, he gives it one last shot and nails it.

next morning, crack of dawn, we're down at the cabins to buddy up with the dogs and the guy feeding Jim—if it's Jim. Folks finishing breakfast, heading to the fields. Jim’s guy’s loading up a pan with food. Key comes from the house.

this guy’s got a friendly face, hair all tied in bunches with thread. He’s on about witches messing with him, seeing things, hearing weird stuff. Worked up, forgets his chores. Tom asks:

“what’s the vittles for? feeding dogs?”

the guy grins slow, like a brick in mud, says:

“yes, Mars Sid, _a_ dog. funny dog, too. wanna see?”

“yeah.”

i nudge Tom, whisper:

“you going now? not the plan.”

“nope, but it is now.”

so, darn it, we go. i’m uneasy. it’s dark in there, but Jim’s there, spots us, shouts:

“why, _Huck!_ and good _lord!_ ain’t that Misto Tom?”

knew it’d happen. didn’t know what to do, and even if i did, couldn’t act ’cause that guy jumps in:

“why, mercy! he know you guys?”

we can see better now. Tom eyeballs the guy, puzzled, says:

“know _who_?”

“this runaway guy.”

“don’t think he does; why do you ask?”

“why _ask_? didn’t he just yell like he knew you?”

Tom’s all confused, says:

"who shouted? when? what'd they shout?" he turns to me, all calm, and asks, "did you hear anything?"

well, there was only one answer, so i said, "no, i ain't heard nothing."

then he eyes jim like he's a stranger and asks, "did you shout?"

"no, sah," jim says, "i ain't said nothing."

"not a word?"

"no, sah, not a word."

"you ever seen us before?"

"no, sah, not that i knows of."

so tom turns to the guy looking all wild and worked up, and says, serious-like, "what do you think's wrong with you? why'd you think someone shouted?"

"oh, them darn witches, sah, i wish i was dead. they always after me, sah, scaring me bad. please don't tell nobody, sah, or old mars silas will scold me; he says there ain't no witches. i wish to goodness he was here now—then what'd he say! i bet he couldn't argue his way out of it this time. but folks that're set in their ways, stay set; they won't look into nothing themselves, and when you find it out and tell them, they don't believe you."

tom handed him a dime, promised we wouldn't tell, and told him to get some more thread for his hair. then he turns to jim and says, "i wonder if uncle silas is planning to hang this guy. if i caught one that ran off, i wouldn't give him up, i'd hang him." and when the guy stepped out to check the dime, tom whispers to jim, "don't ever let on you know us. if you hear digging at night, it's us; we're setting you free."

jim just had time to squeeze our hands before the guy came back. we said we'd visit again if he wanted. he said he would, especially if it was dark, since the witches mostly bothered him then and he liked company.

chapter xxxv.

breakfast was still an hour off, so we headed into the woods. tom said we needed some light to see by for digging, but a lantern would be too bright and risky. what we needed was fox-fire, that soft glow from rotten wood. we gathered a bunch, hid it in the weeds, and sat to rest. tom looked kind of dissatisfied:

blame it, this whole plan is a mess. too easy and too awkward. no guard to trick, no guard dog. jim's chained by one leg to the bed with a ten-foot chain. just lift the bed, slip the chain off. uncle silas trusts everyone too much. sends the key with no one watching. jim could've escaped through the window but not with a chain that long. drat it, huck, it's the stupidest setup. got to make up all the problems ourselves. but hey, more honor in getting him out with self-made difficulties. like that lantern idea. technically, we could parade with torches if we wanted. oh, and we need to find something to make a saw next chance we get.

"why a saw?" you ask.

"to saw off jim's bed leg for the chain."

"but you said just lift the bed."

"oh huck, that's too basic. never read any escape books? the best way's to saw the bed-leg, eat the sawdust, hide the cut. no one the wiser. then when ready, kick the leg, slip the chain, and go. it's grand, huck. wish there was a moat. maybe if there's time, we'll dig one."

"why need a moat when we can just drag him out from under the cabin?"

he doesn’t hear me, lost in thought. sighs, shakes his head.

"nah, not enough necessity for what?"

"sawing jim’s leg off," he says.

"good lord, no need for that. why even think of it?"

well, some folks just cut their hand off to get the chain off. a leg would be better, but we aint doing that. not enough reason, plus Jim wouldn’t get it. it’s a Europe thing. but he can get a rope ladder. we’ll tear up some sheets and make it. send it in a pie, like it’s done. i’ve had worse pies.

"Tom, why does Jim need a rope ladder?" i ask.

"he needs one," Tom says. "you don’t know. he’s gotta have it; all of them do."

"what’s he gonna do with it?"

"hide it in his bed, Huck. it’s what they do. he needs a clue for when he’s gone. you’d leave no clues? that’d be a mess."

"fine," i say, "if it’s the rule, let him have it. but tearing sheets will get Aunt Sally mad. a hickory-bark ladder's easier and wastes nothing."

"shucks, Huck, nobody escapes with hickory bark. that’s silly."

"okay, Tom, do your thing. but i’d borrow a sheet from the line."

he agrees and adds, "borrow a shirt too."

"why a shirt, Tom?"

"Jim needs it for a journal."

"Jim can’t write."

"he can mark it if we give him a pen from a pewter spoon."

"we can make a better pen from a goose feather."

"prisoners don’t have geese, Huck. they make pens from tough old metal, takes ages. regular way."

"fine, what about the ink?"

many folks use iron-rust and tears, but that's the usual way. best ones use their own blood. jim can do that. when he wants to send a message out, he can write on a tin plate with a fork and toss it out the window. the Iron Mask used to do it. works real good.

jim's got no tin plates. just a pan.

no problem; we can get him some.

nobody can read his plates, though.

doesn't matter, Huck Finn. all he needs is to write on it and throw it out. reading it ain't the point. prisoners' writing on plates usually don't get read anyway.

then why waste plates?

they ain't his plates.

but they belong to somebody, don't they?

Tom climbed down the lightning rod to check things out; the guy at the back door was asleep, so Tom tucked it in his neck and came back. This letter said:

dont turn on me, i want to be your ally. a wild gang from the indian territory is planning to snatch your runaway tonight. they've been trying to spook you into staying inside so you wont interfere. im part of the gang but ive found religion and want out. ill spill the plan. they'll come from the north side by the fence at midnight with a fake key to get into the cabin. my job's to blow a horn if there's trouble, but instead im gonna baa like a sheep once they're in. while they're busy with the chains, you lock them in and can deal with them at your pace. follow my directions exactly, or they'll catch on and start raising hell. i don't need anything in return, just want to do right.

UNKNOWN FRIEND

CHAPTER XL.

after breakfast, we felt good, took my canoe, crossed the river to fish, packed a lunch. checked the raft, all fine, got home late for supper. found them in a panic, didn't know up from down, sent us to bed right after eating. didn't say why, didn't need to, we already knew about the letter. halfway upstairs, we sneaked to the cellar, grabbed a good bite, back to the room, then bed. got up around eleven-thirty. tom put on aunt sally's dress he swiped, ready to go with the food.

"where's the butter?" tom asks.

"left it on a corn-pone," i tell him.

"you left it alright—it ain't here."

"we'll do without," i say.

"we'll do with it too," he says. "slide down and fetch it. then head down the lightning-rod and catch up. I'll fix the straw in jim's clothes to look like his mom, ready to baa and move when you're back."

off he goes, down i go. butter's where i left it, grab the slab, blow out the light, head upstairs quiet. reach the main floor, here comes aunt sally with a candle. stash the stuff in my hat, hat on my head, she spots me.

"you been down cellar?"

"yes'm."

"what you doing down there?"

"nothin."

"_nothin!_"

"no'm."

"then why down there at this hour?"

"i don’t know ma'am."

"you don’t _know?_ don’t sass me. tom, i wanna know what you did down there."

"i didn’t do a thing, aunt sally, hope to gracious."

thought she'd let me go, normally would, but too much weird stuff going on, she was wound up. so she says firm:

"you march to the sitting-room and wait. you've been up to no good, and i’ll find out what before i'm done with you."

so she left when i opened the door. man, what a crowd. fifteen farmers, all armed. felt sick, sunk into a chair. they were trying to look calm but weren't. hats off, on, fidgeting. me, i kept my hat on.

wishing aunt sally would show up, get it over with. tell her we messed up and need to bail with jim before things get ugly.

finally, she comes asking questions. can't think straight. farmers getting antsy, wanting to go after "desperadoes". others saying wait. aunt sally keeps pressing, and i'm shaking, sweating butter. then one guy wants to storm the cabin, and i nearly drop. butter running down my face, and aunt sally freaks, thinks i'm losing it.

everyone rushes over. she pulls my hat, out comes butter and bread. hugs me, relieved. tells me to get lost till morning.

i'm upstairs, down the lightning rod, running to tom. can't speak right, but tell him men with guns are here. his eyes light up, thinks it's great. wants to bring more, delay.

"no time!" i say. "where's jim?"

"right here. ready to go." we move to give the signal.

but then, footsteps at the door, fumbling with the lock.

told you we'd be early. door's locked, they're not here yet. alright, i'll lock some of you in the cabin, wait in the dark, and take them out when they arrive. rest of you, spread out and listen.

so they come in, can't see us, stumbling over us as we scrambled under the bed. got under and out through the hole, quick and quiet—jim first, me next, tom last, just like tom said. now we're in the lean-to, footsteps right outside. we creep to the door, tom listens at the crack, can't see a thing. he says when he nudges, jim goes first, him last. he listens, waits, nudges, and we slip out, silent, crouched. we get to the fence, jim and me over, but tom snags his britches. hears them coming, pulls free with a snap. someone yells, “who’s there? answer or i’ll shoot!” we don’t. just run. shots fired, bullets whizzing past. 

“here they are! they’ve broke for the river! after ’em, boys, and let the dogs loose!”

they chase us loud, we’re quiet, no boots, no yelling. we dodge into the bush, let them pass, then follow behind. dogs come, but they know us, so just a quick greeting and they're off to the noise. we head for the mill, up through the bush, find my canoe, and paddle out easy, quiet. aim for the island where my raft waits. yelling fades as we drift away. on the raft, i tell jim:

“now, jim, you’re free again. bet you won’t be a slave no more.”

“mighty good job, huck. planned and done beautiful; nobody could mix up a plan better.”

we’re all happy, but tom’s the happiest—he's got a bullet in his leg.

me and jim felt a bit less bold once we realized how bad off tom was, bleeding and all. we laid him down in the wigwam and tore up one of the duke’s shirts to bandage him. tom insisted, “give me the rags; i’ll do it myself. keep moving; don’t mess around here with the plan going so well. man the sweeps, and let’s go! we pulled it off, boys! wish we’d handled Louis XVI.; wouldn’t be no ‘Son of Saint Louis, ascend to heaven!’ in his story. we’d have sent him packing across the border, easy as pie. man the sweeps!”

but me and jim were thinking. after a bit, i told jim, “go ahead.”

he said, “look here, huck. if tom was getting freed and one of us got shot, would he say, ‘go on, leave me, and forget a doctor for the wounded one?’ that’s not tom sawyer. he wouldn’t say that! well, jim’s not saying it either. i’m not leaving without a doctor, not for anything.”

i knew jim had a good heart, and i figured he’d say what he did. so i told tom i was going for a doctor. tom kicked up a fuss, but me and jim stood firm. tom even tried to crawl out to set the raft loose himself, but we wouldn’t let him. he argued, but it didn’t change a thing.

when he saw me readying the canoe, he said, “if you’re going, here’s how to handle it in the village. blindfold the doctor, make him swear to silence, slip him a purse of gold, and lead him through the back alleys. bring him here by canoe, and search him for chalk before you send him back, so he can’t mark the raft.”

i agreed and headed off, with jim to hide in the woods when the doctor came around.

CHAPTER XLI.

the doctor was a nice old fellow when i found him. i told him about me and my brother camping on a raft over on Spanish Island, how he kicked his gun in his sleep and shot himself in the leg. we needed him to patch it up and keep quiet, since we planned to surprise our folks, the Phelpses, that evening.

he asked, “who are your folks?”

“the Phelpses, down yonder.”

after a pause, he asked, “how’d you say he got shot?”

“he dreamed it,” i said.

“strange dream,” he replied.

he lit his lantern, grabbed his saddle-bags, and we set off. but when he saw the canoe, he hesitated, claiming it seemed too small for two. i assured him, “don’t worry, sir, she carried the three of us and the guns just fine.”

“oh,” he said.

he just rocked the boat a bit, said he’d look for a bigger one, but they were all locked up. took my canoe instead, said I could wait or check around more, or head home to set up the surprise. I passed on that. showed him where the raft was, and he took off.

then I had a thought: what if fixing that leg takes longer than a few minutes? what if it’s days? we can’t just hang around. if he delays, I’ll get there myself, even if I gotta swim. tie him up, head downriver, and when Tom’s done, pay him off, let him go.

I crashed in a lumber pile, woke up with the sun high. rushed to the doc’s place, but he’d left overnight. looked bad for Tom, so I bolted for the island. almost smacked into Uncle Silas. 

“Tom, where’ve you been?” he asked.

“nowhere, just chasing the runaway with Sid.”

“your aunt’s worried,” he said.

“no need,” I replied. “we followed the men and dogs, lost them, thought we heard them on the water. got a canoe, couldn’t find them. got tired, slept, then came here for news. Sid’s at the post office, and I’m getting food, then we’re heading home.”

went to find Sid, but he wasn’t there. Uncle Silas got a letter, waited a bit, Sid didn’t show. decided we’d go home, let Sid find his way back. couldn’t convince him to wait with me. 

Aunt Sally was glad to see me, laughed, cried, hugged me, gave me one of her harmless scoldings, said Sid’d get the same. house was packed with farmers and their wives, all chatter, especially Mrs. Hotchkiss, couldn’t stop talking.

well Sister Phelps, I tore through that cabin, and I’m tellin ya, that guy was nuts. told Sister Damrell the same thing, didnt I, Sister Damrell? i said, he’s off his rocker. you all heard me. look at that grindstone. no sane person’s gonna scratch up a grindstone with all that nonsense. here’s someone busted a gut, and here’s another one dragged on for thirty-seven years, natural son of who knows who, and endless junk. he’s crazy, i said it before, and i’m saying it now—crazy as Nebuchadnezzar.

and look at that rag ladder, Sister Hotchkiss. what on earth would he need that for, said old Mrs. Damrell. the very words i was sayin just a minute ago to Sister Utterback. look at that rag ladder, i said. what could he need it for, i said. Sister Hotchkiss, you see it too.

but how’d they ever get that grindstone in there anyway? and who dug that hole? and who—

my words, Brer Penrod! i was just saying—pass that molasses, will ya?—i was just saying to Sister Dunlap, how did they get that grindstone in there, i said. without help? no way. there was help, i said; plenty of it. a dozen helping, i bet, and i’d find out who did it if it was the last thing i did.

a dozen, you say! forty couldn’t have done it all. look at those case-knife saws, how tedious they’ve been made; look at that bed-leg sawed off. weeks of work for six men. look at that straw dummy on the bed.

you’re right, Brer Hightower! just what i was saying to Brer Phelps himself. what do you think of it, Sister Hotchkiss? think of that bed-leg sawed off like that? it didn’t saw itself off. someone sawed it, that’s my opinion. if someone’s got a better one, let’s hear it. that’s all i’m saying.

dog my cats, there musta been a house full of guys in there every night for weeks to get all that done, Sister Phelps. look at that shirt, covered in secret African writing done in blood! musta been a raft of em at it constantly. i’d pay to have it read to me; and those who wrote it, i’d lash em till—

folks to _help_ him, Brother Marples? you'd _think_ so if you’d been around this place lately. they've swiped everything they could grab—and we’re watching all the time, mind you. they even nabbed that shirt off the line! and that sheet they turned into a rag ladder? no telling how often they _didn't_ steal that; along with flour, candles, candlesticks, spoons, the old warming-pan, and a ton of stuff i can't even remember, plus my new calico dress; and me, Silas, my Sid, and Tom on constant watch day _and_ night, like i told you, but not a peep or glimpse of them; and then at the last minute, they slide right in under our noses and fool us, and not just _us_ but the Injun Territory robbers too, and they actually get _away_ with that man safe and sound, with sixteen men and twenty-two dogs right on their heels! i swear, it just beats anything i've ever _heard_ of. why, _sperits_ couldn’t have done it better. and i reckon they must've _been_ sperits—because, _you_ know our dogs, and they’re the best; well, those dogs never even got on the _track_ of ’em once! explain _that_ to me!—_any_ of you!

“Well, it does beat—”

“Laws alive, I never—”

“So help me, I wouldn’t a be—”

“_House_-thieves as well as—”

“Goodnessgracioussakes, I’d a been afraid to _live_ in such a—”

“’Fraid to _live_!—I was so scared I hardly dared go to bed, or get up, or lay down, or _sit_ down, Sister Ridgeway. why, they’d steal the very—guess what kind of a fluster _I_ was in by the time midnight hit last night. I was scared they’d swipe some o’ the family! my reasoning faculties were gone. looks silly enough _now_, in daylight; but I thought to myself, there’s my two poor boys asleep, way up in that lonely room, and I was so uneasy I crept up and locked ’em in! I _did_. anyone would. because when you get scared like that, and it just builds up, and your wits start addling, you do all sorts of wild things, and then you think, suppose _I_ was a boy, way up there, and the door wasn’t locked, and you—” She stopped, looked kind of wondering, then turned her head slow, and when her eye lit on me—I got up and took a walk.

thought i'd explain why we weren't in that room this morning. needed a moment to think it through, but didn't stray too far or she'd send for me. later, when folks left, i told her the noise and shooting woke me and "Sid" up. found the door locked, so we climbed down the lightning rod to see what was up. got banged up a bit and decided not to try that stunt again. told her the same story i gave Uncle Silas. she forgave us, saying boys are a wild bunch anyway. no harm done, so she chose to be grateful we were safe.

she kissed me, patted my head, and then suddenly realized it was late and Sid wasn’t back. i offered to go find him, but she stopped me. said one lost boy was enough. uncle went after supper since Sid didn’t show up.

uncle came back around ten, no sign of Sid. Aunt Sally was worried, but Uncle Silas said boys will be boys and he'd likely turn up fine by morning. she decided to wait up, keeping a light on for him.

when i went to bed, she took her candle, tucked me in, and talked about what a great kid Sid was. asked if i thought he might be lost or hurt. tears fell as she worried, and i kept saying Sid would be back by morning. she held my hand, kissed me, and asked me to say it again, found comfort in it.

before leaving, she looked me in the eyes and said, "the door's not locked, Tom, and there's the window and rod. but you'll be good, won't you? for my sake." wanted to go check on Tom real bad, but after that, wouldn't go, not for anything.

couldn't sleep. too much on my mind. her, tom. kept going out to check. saw her by the candle, tears in her eyes, staring at the road. felt useless. promised myself id never cause her pain again. at dawn, went again. she was asleep, candle almost gone.

next morning, old man uptown before breakfast. no sign of tom. we sat at the table, quiet, coffee cold. then he remembered a letter from the post-office. thought he'd lost it, found it, and handed it over. turned out to be from sis in st. petersburg. 

before she could open it, saw something that made her run. tom on a mattress, doctor, and jim in her dress, hands tied. she thought tom was dead, then realized he was alive. relief. she ran to get the bed ready, barking orders to everyone.

followed them to see what theyd do with jim. some wanted to hang him as an example, but others said no. not their nigger, owner would make them pay. that quieted them down. irony's thick. these folks love punishment, hate paying for it.

they gave Jim a rough time, cursed him a lot, cuffed him a bit too, but he kept quiet, didn't let on he knew me. took him back to the cabin, put his clothes on, chained him not to the bed-leg this time, but a big staple in the floor, hands and legs both. said he'd get bread and water till his owner showed up or he got sold at auction if he didn't. filled up our escape hole, put farmers with guns on watch, and a bulldog at the door during the day. after all that, they ended with some more cussing, and then the old doc shows up, says:

“don’t be rougher than you have to. he ain’t bad. when i found the boy, i needed help to get the bullet out, couldn’t leave, and the boy got worse, lost his head, talked crazy. said if i marked his raft he’d kill me. couldn't do much, so i said i needed help. this nigger crawls out, offers help, did real well. figured he was a runaway, but there i was, stuck all day and night. had patients with chills up in town, but couldn’t leave—if he got away, that's on me. no skiffs close enough to call. stuck till daylight. never seen a better nurse, risking freedom to help, all tired out, been worked hard. liked him for that—worth a thousand dollars, easy, and good treatment too. had all i needed, boy doing as well as he could, quiet. stuck till dawn, then some men in a skiff come by. good luck, the nigger was asleep by the pallet. motioned them quiet, grabbed and tied him before he knew. no trouble. boy was sleeping light, muffled the oars, hitched the raft, towed over quiet. nigger didn’t make a fuss or say a word. he ain’t bad, gentlemen.”

somebody says, “well, sounds good, doc.”

then the others eased up a bit too. i was really thankful to that old doc for helping jim out; kinda matched what i thought of him from the start. seemed like a good guy. they all agreed jim did well, deserved some recognition. promised they wouldn’t cuss him no more.

but they still locked him up. hoped they’d at least take off a couple chains or give him better food, but nope. figured i shouldn't get involved, just planned to tell aunt sally the doc's take once i got through explaining how i forgot to mention sid getting shot when me and him were out paddling around chasing that runaway.

had time though. aunt sally was in the sick-room all day and night, and i kept out of uncle silas’s way.

next morning, heard tom was doing better, aunt sally went to rest. i sneaked to see tom, maybe we could work out a story for the family. he was asleep, looking better, not all fiery-faced. so i waited. half an hour later, aunt sally comes in, sits by me, whispers that everything’s looking good, tom might wake up sound.

we watched. tom wakes, looks around, says:

“hello! i’m home! how’s that? where’s the raft?”

“it’s all right,” i said.

“and jim?”

“same,” i replied, not too confident. but he didn’t notice.

“good! splendid! now we’re all right and safe! did you tell aunty?”

i was about to say yes, but she cuts in, “about what, sid?”

“about how the whole thing was done.”

“what whole thing?”

“the whole thing. only one there is; how we set the runaway free—me and tom.”

“good land! set the run— what’s he talking about! dear, dear, out of his head again!”

no, i ain't lost it; i know exactly what im saying. we set him free—me and tom. we planned it and pulled it off. did it real slick too. she just sat there, eyes fixed, letting me roll on. i knew there was no point in me saying anything else. "aunty, it took us a ton of work—weeks of it, hours every night while y'all slept. stealing candles, sheets, shirts, your dress, spoons, plates, knives, warming-pan, grindstone, flour, and all sorts. making saws, pens, inscriptions, and stuff. you can't imagine the work or the fun. we had to draw coffins, write anonymous notes, climb the lightning rod, dig into the cabin, make a rope ladder, sneak it in a pie, and hide spoons in your apron."

"mercy sakes!"

"and filled the cabin with rats and snakes for company for jim. then, you kept tom with butter in his hat, nearly ruined it. men showed up before we got out, had to sprint, dodged them, dogs ignored us, got to the canoe, reached the raft, and jim was free, all by ourselves. wasn't it something, aunty?"

"never heard anything like it! so it was you two causing all this chaos, turning us inside out, scaring us to death. i should tan the hide off you right now. night after night, and you just get well, you young scamp, and i'll teach you!"

but tom, he was so proud, couldn't hold it in, tongue just went on. she kept chipping in, spitting fire, both talking over each other like a cat fight. she says:

"enjoy it now, but if i catch you with him again—"

"meddling with who?" tom drops his smile, surprised.

"who? the runaway, of course. who else?"

tom looks at me serious, says:

"tom, didn't you say he's alright? hasn't he got away?"

"him?" says aunt sally; "the runaway? no, they got him back, safe, in that cabin again, bread and water, chained up, till claimed or sold!"

tom jumps up, eyes hot, nostrils flaring, yells at me:

"they got no right to lock him up! move! don't waste a second. get him out! he ain't no slave; he's as free as anyone!"

"what does the child mean?"

"i mean every word, aunt sally. if nobody goes, i'll go. known him all my life, and tom too. old miss watson died two months back, ashamed she ever planned to sell him down the river, said so herself; set him free in her will."

"why'd you wanna set him free if he was already free?"

"it's all about the adventure. i'd wade through anything for it—oh no, AUNT POLLY!"

and there she was, right inside the door, looking sweet as pie. Aunt Sally rushed over, almost squeezing Aunt Polly's head right off, tears and all. i figured the spot under the bed looked pretty good about then. peeked out, saw Tom's Aunt Polly eyeing him over her glasses like she could bury him right there. then she goes:

"yeah, you might wanna look away, Tom."

Aunt Sally goes, "is he that different? that's not Tom, it's Sid. where's Tom gone?"

"you mean Huck Finn. no way i'd mistake my Tom. come out from under there, Huck."

so i crawled out, but not too bold. Aunt Sally looked more mixed-up than anyone, except maybe Uncle Silas when he heard the whole mess. left him confused all day, and he preached a sermon that night no one could follow. Aunt Polly spilled who i was, and i had to explain how i ended up pretending to be Tom Sawyer. Aunt Sally, she says, "call me Aunt Sally, no need to change now." and i stuck with it, knowing Tom would turn it into an adventure, make it all good. turned out he was pretending to be Sid to make things easier for me.

Aunt Polly then said Miss Watson freed Jim in her will. Tom went through all that trouble for no reason, but i finally got why he'd help set someone free. Aunt Polly said when she got word that Tom and Sid were all right, she thought:

"i should've known, letting him go off alone. now i gotta travel all this way to see what he's up to, since you weren't answering."

Aunt Sally says, "never heard from you."

"never got a reply?" Aunt Polly says, turning sharp, "Tom!"

"what now?" Tom grumbles.

"don't you 'what' me. give me those letters."

"what letters?"

"those letters. don't make me come after you."

they're in the trunk. right there. same as when i pulled them outta the office. i haven't checked them, not touched 'em. but i figured they'd cause trouble. thought if you weren't in a rush, i'd—

well, you sure need a talking to, no doubt about it. i wrote another to say i was coming; i guess he—

no, it came yesterday; i haven't read it yet, but it's fine, i got it.

i wanted to bet two bucks she didn’t, but maybe it was better not to. so i just stayed quiet.

CHAPTER THE LAST

first chance i got tom alone, i asked him about his plan during that escape? what was his big idea if the escape worked and he freed a guy who was already free? he said, his plan was to float jim down the river on the raft, have adventures all the way to the river's mouth, then tell him he’s free, take him back home on a steamboat, pay him for lost time, and set up a big show with all the folks, like a hero's welcome. but i guess it ended up fine the way it was.

we got jim out of the chains real quick, and when aunt polly, uncle silas, and aunt sally saw how he helped the doc with tom, they treated him like a king, gave him all he wanted, good times, and nothing to do. we had him up in the sick-room, talked things over, and tom gave jim forty bucks for being such a good sport. jim was thrilled, said:

“what’d i tell you, huck? told you i had a hairy chest, and it was a sign; i was rich once, gonna be rich again; and here it is! signs are signs, i told you!”

then tom kept talking about sneaking off for some wild adventures in the Territory with the indians for a couple weeks. i said, sounds good, but i’m broke, can’t get money from home since pap probably took it all from judge thatcher and drank it up.

“no, he didn’t,” tom says. “it’s all there—six grand and more; your pap hasn’t been back since. hadn’t when i left, anyway.”

jim gets kinda serious:

“he ain’t comin’ back, huck.”

i ask:

“why, jim?”

“don’t matter why, huck—but he ain’t.”

but i kept asking; finally he says:

“remember the house floatin' down the river, with a man inside, all covered up? i went in, uncovered him, didn’t let you see? well, that was him.”

tom's doing good, wearing his bullet around his neck like a watch-guard. keeps checking the time. nothing much left to say, and im glad. didnt know writing a book was such a hassle, wont be doing it again. got to head out to the Territory before everyone else. aunt sally wants to adopt and civilize me, but ive been through that already. can't handle it.

the end. yours truly, huck finn.
