ben franklin, born jan 6, 1706, milk street, boston. josiah franklin, his dad, was a tallow chandler. ben was the youngest of 17 kids. ended school at ten, apprenticed at twelve to his brother james, a printer with "new england courant." ben contributed, then edited, but they fought. ben ran off to new york, then philly, october 1723. got a printer gig, but governor keith’s promises took him to london, empty promises, so back to printing. merchant denman brought him back to philly, gave him a job. denman dies, ben sets up his own print shop, publishes "pennsylvania gazette" and pushes local reforms. 1732, starts "poor richard's almanac," full of sharp sayings. 1758, stops writing for it, but prints "father abraham's sermon"—biggest colonial american lit piece.

franklin kept busy with public stuff. he pitched an idea for an academy, which eventually became the university of pennsylvania. started the "american philosophical society" so science folks could share their discoveries. he was deep into electrical experiments too, mixing it up with money-making and politics. sold his business in 1748 to study more, having built up some wealth. made some big discoveries that impressed europe.

in politics, he was sharp, both as an administrator and in debates. but he did use his position to help his family, which wasn't great. reformed the postal system and got famous for his work with britain and france. in 1757, went to england to fight the penn family's control over the colony, stayed five years trying to educate them about colonial issues. back in america, he got involved in the paxton affair, lost his assembly seat, but went back to england in 1764 to petition the king directly.

fought against the stamp act but got in hot water for getting a friend a job as stamp agent. even his role in repealing the act left people suspicious. kept pushing the colonial case as revolution loomed. visited france in 1767, was honored there, but lost his postmaster gig in 1775 due to some leaked letters. back in philly, joined the continental congress, then went to france in 1777 as a u.s. commissioner. stayed till 1785, loved by french society, and did so well he was second only to washington in independence heroics. he died april 17, 1790.

his autobiography, started in england in 1771, continued in 1784-5, and finished in 1788, covers up to 1757. after a wild journey, the manuscript got printed by john bigelow. it's a big deal for showing colonial life and ranks as a top autobiography.

benjamin franklin

his autobiography

1706-1757

twyford, at bishop of st. asaph's, 1771.

hey son, always found some joy digging up stories about our family. remember that time in England when we went chasing relatives? yeah, that was part of it. figured you might want to know the twists and turns in my life too, some of which might surprise you. got a quiet week out here in the country, so im writing it all down for you. 

started from nothing, made my way up to a decent spot in life. thought you'd like to hear about the choices and chances that got me here, maybe pick up a trick or two for your own journey.

sometimes when i think back, id almost relive it all, same way. sure, id fix a few mistakes, swap out some bad days for better ones, but even if i couldnt, id still take the deal. since i cant hit rewind, putting it all in words feels like the next best thing.

old folks like to ramble on about their past, and im no different. but this way, you're not stuck listening unless you want to. and yeah, maybe its a bit of vanity, but a little ego isnt all bad. sometimes it drives us to do better things.

speaking of which, i owe a lot to good luck or divine help, whatever you call it. hope that luck sticks around, but if things go south, i trust ill find a way through. only time will tell, and its in bigger hands than mine.

Found some old notes from my uncle, who loved family stories as much as i do. Turns out, our family stuck around Ecton in Northamptonshire for at least 300 years. We had about 30 acres and ran a smithy, passed down to the eldest son, like a tradition. I checked Ecton's records and traced us back to 1555. I’m the youngest son of a youngest son, stretching back five generations. Grandfather Thomas, born 1598, lived there till he was too old for work. Then he moved to his son John, my dad's apprenticeship place, in Banbury, Oxfordshire. He died there, and we saw his gravestone in 1758. Thomas, the eldest, got the Ecton place and left it to his daughter, who sold it to Mr. Isted, now the manor lord. Granddad had four sons: Thomas, John, Benjamin, and Josiah. I’ll tell you what i can remember about them without my papers. If they’re not lost, you’ll find more details in them.

Thomas started as a smith too but had a knack for learning. Thanks to Esquire Palmer’s encouragement, he became a scrivener and a big deal in Northampton. He pushed public projects and got noticed by Lord Halifax. Died January 6, 1702, four years before i was born. You once said our lives were so similar it felt like a reincarnation.

John was into dyeing, wool I think. Benjamin did silk dyeing, apprenticed in London. He was sharp. I remember him well cause when I was a kid, he stayed with my dad in Boston for years. Lived to a ripe age. His grandson, Samuel Franklin, is in Boston now. He left behind two poetry volumes, handwritten, full of pieces for friends and family. He had a shorthand system he taught me, but I lost it not using it. I was named after him since he and my dad were close. Very pious guy, always at sermons, noted them down in his shorthand, had tons of volumes. A bit too political for his place maybe. In London, I stumbled on a stack of pamphlets he collected from 1641 to 1717. Some volumes missing, but I got eight folios, and twenty-four quartos and octavos. A book dealer brought them to me, knew I buy sometimes. Seems my uncle left them when he went to America fifty years back. Margins full of his notes.

Our family was into the Reformation early, stayed Protestants through Queen Mary's reign, dodging trouble for being anti-Catholic. They had an English Bible, hid it by fastening it in a joint-stool. My great-great-grandfather would flip the stool to read it, a kid would watch the door for apparitors. If one came, the stool flipped back, Bible hidden. Got this story from Uncle Benjamin. The family stayed Church of England till the end of Charles II's reign, then Benjamin and Josiah switched to nonconformist meetings in Northamptonshire, sticking with that till the end. Rest stayed Episcopal.

my dad, josiah, married young and moved the family to new england around 1682. bunch of his friends wanted to escape the laws against their religious meetings and convinced him to join. had four more kids with his first wife there, then married again and had ten more with my mom, abiah folger. in total, 17 kids. i remember 13 of us sitting at the table. i was the youngest boy, born in boston. my mom’s dad, peter folger, got mentioned by cotton mather as a "godly, learned Englishman" in his book. he wrote some stuff, but i only saw one piece from 1675. it was in simple verse, pushing for religious freedom and against persecution, blaming the indian wars and troubles on those laws. he wanted people to know he wrote it, saying:

"because to be a libeller i hate it with my heart; from sherburne town, where now i dwell my name i do put here; without offense your real friend, it is peter folgier."

my brothers got lined up for different trades, but i got sent to grammar school at eight. dad had this idea of me becoming the church guy, like a tithe from his sons. i was quick with reading, couldn’t even remember not knowing how, and everyone thought i’d be a good scholar. uncle benjamin was onboard too, even said he'd give me his shorthand sermon books if i learned his style.

but, i didn’t stick at grammar school for long. i was moving up fast, middle to top of the class, then into the next one. then dad changed his mind. college was too pricey with so many kids, and a lot of grads didn’t end up well off. so he pulled me out and sent me to mr. george brownell’s school for writing and arithmetic. brownell was famous for his gentle ways. got my writing down quick, but arithmetic didn’t stick. by ten, i was at home, cutting wicks and stuff for dad’s tallow-chandler and soap business. not his original job, but it was what he could do after moving to new england. i didn’t like it, wanted the sea, but dad wasn’t having it. still, living by water, i learned to swim and handle boats early. often ended up leading other kids, especially when things got tricky. 

once, there was this salt-marsh by the mill-pond, a mess from all our fishing. i suggested we build a wharf. saw a pile of stones meant for a new house, figured they’d work. so when the workers left, i got the gang together and we moved those stones, built our wharf. next day, the workers noticed. we got found out, and our dads weren’t happy. even though i argued it was useful, dad taught me it wasn’t right if it wasn’t honest.

so here's the rundown on him. solid build, middle height, strong as they come. had a knack for drawing, dabbled in music with a voice that held its own. he'd play psalm tunes on his violin in the evening — nice way to wrap a day. mechanical mind, could handle any tool you tossed his way. his real talent was in his judgment — folks came to him for advice on both personal and town matters. respected for his opinion, though he never worked in public roles, too busy with family and trade.

at the table, it was all about the conversation. liked having sharp folks around, sparking discussions that made us think. food wasn't the focus; it was more about feeding the mind. that's why to this day i couldn't tell you what i ate for dinner, and it's never bothered me during travels when others fussed over what's on the plate.

my mom, strong constitution like dad, raised all ten of us herself. they lived long lives, only got sick at the end. buried together in Boston. i put a marble over their grave with a fitting inscription — they lived well, raised a big family with integrity and hard work. no wealth, just diligence and providence.

and yeah, as i write this, i notice i'm all over the place. guess that's age. used to be more organized, but then again, you don't dress the same for a night in as you do for a ball. maybe i'm just being lazy.

stuck in the family business till i was twelve. brother john left, got married, went to rhode island. seemed like i was next in line to become a tallow-chandler. but i hated it. dad worried id run off to sea like josiah, another brother, did. so he took me around to see different trades: joiners, bricklayers, turners. wanted to see what i liked. i still love watching skilled hands at work. learned enough to fix things around the house and build little machines for experiments when ideas were fresh. dad thought maybe cutler's trade was right. tried working with cousin samuel, but dad didnt like the fee he wanted, so he pulled me out.

always loved reading. spent what little money i had on books. started with john bunyan's works, then moved on to r. burton's historical collections. dad's library was full of religious books. read those too, though i wished for more useful stuff since i wasnt gonna be a clergyman. read a lot of plutarch's lives, still think it was worth the time. found de foe's essay on projects and mather's essays to do good, which got me thinking differently about life.

this love for books pushed dad to make me a printer. my brother james was already one. he came back from england with a press in 1717. liked it better than dad's trade, but still dreamed of the sea. dad rushed to bind me to james as an apprentice. held out for a bit, but signed at twelve. had to work till i was twenty-one, but got journeyman's wages in the last year. got good at it fast, became a real help to james. more books came my way. knew some bookseller apprentices, borrowed books, read them through the night, and always returned them clean.

so this guy, matthew adams, had a nice stash of books and hung around our printing spot. one day he noticed me and invited me to his library, letting me borrow whatever i wanted. i started getting into poetry, wrote a few pieces. my brother thought maybe we could make some cash, so he pushed me to write ballads. one was "The Lighthouse Tragedy" about captain worthilake drowning with his daughters. another was a sailor's song about catching blackbeard. they were junk, honestly, but when we printed them, he had me go sell them around town. the first one sold like crazy because the event was fresh on people's minds. that gave my ego a boost, but my dad shot me down, saying poets usually end up broke, so i dodged that bullet. prose turned out to be more useful and helped me get ahead. i'll tell you how i picked up those skills.

there was another kid in town, john collins, who was into books too. we liked to argue, always trying to prove each other wrong. it's a bad habit, though, makes you a pain to be around and can ruin friendships. i got it from reading my dad's religious debate books. smart folks, except maybe lawyers and college guys, usually avoid it.

once, collins and i got into it over whether women should be educated. he thought they shouldn't, said they couldn't handle it. i disagreed, partly just to argue. he was smoother with words and often seemed to win by talking fast, not by making sense. since we didn't settle it and weren't meeting soon, i wrote down my points and sent them to him. he replied, i responded, back and forth we went. my dad found my papers, read them, and pointed out that while i spelled and punctuated better than collins (thanks to the print shop), i was worse in style and clarity. he showed me examples, and i saw he was right. so i started paying more attention to how i wrote and worked on getting better.

around 16, i stumbled on this book by Tryon, all about going veggie. decided to give it a shot. my brother, not married yet, had us all boarded elsewhere. my no-meat stance was kinda a hassle. got some flack for it. learned some of Tryon’s recipes—boiled potatoes, rice, hasty pudding. then told my brother if he gave me half of what he paid for my board, id handle my own meals. he was in. turned out i could save half of that. more money for books. plus, while everyone left the print shop for meals, id stay back, eat quick—a biscuit, maybe some raisins or a tart, and water—and use that time to study. noticed i was sharper, clearer; guess that's what eating light does. 

then there was this odd volume of the Spectator i found. third one. never seen any before. bought it, read it over and over. liked the writing. wanted to copy it. so i took notes on the ideas, put the book away for a bit, then tried to rewrite it from memory. compared my version to the original, found mistakes, fixed 'em. realized i needed more words, like making verses would have helped. so i turned some tales into verse and back again. mixed up my notes, then sorted them back to practice organizing thoughts. comparing later, saw more faults, corrected. sometimes thought i improved on the original a bit, which gave me hope i'd be a decent writer someday. did all this reading and writing after work or early morning, sometimes Sundays, sneaking off to the print shop alone, dodging church my dad insisted on. felt like a duty but didn’t have time for it anymore.

got embarrassed once about not knowing math. flunked it twice in school, so i grabbed Cocker's Arithmetick and knocked it out solo. moved on to Seller's and Shermy's Navigation books, picked up some geometry basics, but didn't go deep. around then, i read Locke's Human Understanding and the Art of Thinking from Port Royal. 

while working on my language skills, i found an English grammar book (probably Greenwood's). it had bits on rhetoric and logic, ending with a Socratic debate example. then i got into Xenophon's Socrates, which was full of that method. loved it, switched my style from arguing hard to asking questions. reading Shaftesbury and Collins turned me into a doubter on some religious stuff. using the Socratic method, i found it easy to trip up folks with more knowledge, getting them to agree to things they didn't expect. got pretty good at it, though my wins weren't always deserved. eventually, i backed off from using it all the time, but kept the habit of speaking with caution. never say “certainly” or “undoubtedly.” use “i think” or “it seems.” it’s served me well in pushing my ideas and persuading folks. 

conversation's about sharing info or persuading, so a pushy style usually backfires. even Pope said, teach like you're not teaching, and present new ideas as reminders of forgotten ones. he also suggested speaking with seeming diffidence. and about modesty, he noted: “immodest words admit of no defense, for want of modesty is want of sense.” but if someone lacks sense, maybe that's why they're not modest. maybe it should be: “immodest words admit but this defense, that want of modesty is want of sense.”

my brother started printing a newspaper back in 1720 or 1721. it was the New England Courant, second in America after the Boston News-Letter. folks told him not to bother, saying one was enough for the whole country. now it's 1771 and there’s at least twenty-five. he pushed ahead anyway, and after setting type and printing, i was the one running the papers through the streets.

he had some clever friends writing for it, which made it popular. listening to them talk about their work, i wanted to try too. but being a kid, i knew my brother wouldn’t print my stuff if he knew it was mine. so i wrote something, disguised my handwriting, and slipped it under the printing-house door. they found it, read it, and liked it, thinking it was from someone with a bit of learning. i guess i got lucky with my judges, maybe they weren’t as sharp as i thought.

that got me going, so i wrote more pieces the same way. they got good feedback until i ran out of ideas. when i revealed it was me, my brother's friends started noticing me more, which didn’t sit well with him. he saw himself as my master, me the apprentice, with all the duties. i expected a bit more slack since we were brothers. we argued a lot, usually in front of our dad, and i usually got the better of it. but my brother was hot-headed and sometimes hit me, which i hated. i wanted out of my apprenticeship, and finally, a chance came.

one article upset the Assembly, leading to my brother’s arrest. they questioned me too, but i didn’t give them much. they let me go, maybe because i was just an apprentice keeping my master's secrets.

during my brother's lock up, which i didn't take too kindly to, even with our differences, i ran the paper. gave the rulers some jabs in it. my brother liked it, but others started seeing me as a young troublemaker. when my brother got out, they told him he couldn't print the New England Courant anymore.

we had a little meeting in the print shop to figure out what to do. some said, just change the name of the paper, but my brother thought that'd be messy. so we decided to print it under my name, benjamin franklin. to keep it under wraps, they gave me my old indenture back with a discharge, but i secretly signed new ones to keep working for him. a weak plan, but it worked for a while.

eventually, my brother and i clashed again. i decided to claim my freedom, betting he wouldn't show the new indentures. not my finest moment, but i was mad about the times he'd hit me. he wasn't a bad guy, but i could be a real pain.

once he knew i was leaving, he made sure no other print shop in town would hire me. so i thought about heading to new york. boston was getting too hot with my past antics, and i didn't want more trouble. my religious debates weren't winning me fans either. figured new york was a better bet.

my dad sided with my brother, so i had to sneak out. my friend collins helped me out. he set it up with a sloop captain to take me to new york, saying i was a young guy in a bind. sold some books for cash, got on board quietly, and after three days, landed in new york. 17, broke, and knowing no one.

by then, my sea itch was gone, so i turned to my trade. figured i was a decent enough printer, so i offered my services to old mr. william bradford, the first printer in pennsylvania. but he didn’t have work for me, said his son in philadelphia might, though, since he just lost his main guy, aquila rose. philly was a hundred miles out, but i hopped a boat to amboy anyway, sent my stuff by sea.

crossing the bay, we hit a squall that shredded our sails. couldn’t make it into the Kill, and ended up on long island. there was a drunk dutchman on board, fell overboard, and i managed to pull him back by his hair. he sobered up a bit, handed me a book to dry. turned out to be bunyan’s pilgrim’s progress in dutch, fancy print, copper cuts. bunyan was the first i knew to mix story and dialogue like that. defoe and richardson followed suit.

when we got near the island, couldn’t land – surf was too rough. anchored close, tried yelling at folks on shore, but couldn’t make out a word. they had canoes but didn’t come for us, so we just hunkered down for the night. crammed into the scuttle with the dutchman, spray soaking us through. not much rest, but when the wind died down next day, we pushed on to amboy. thirty hours on water, no food, just nasty rum and saltwater.

felt feverish by evening, so i hit the sack. remembered reading about cold water for fever, drank a bunch, sweated it out, and felt better by morning. crossed the ferry and hoofed it the fifty miles to burlington, hoping to catch a boat to philadelphia.

rained all day, soaked to the bone by noon. stopped at a shabby inn, stayed overnight, wishing i’d just stayed home. must've looked pitiful cause folks thought i was a runaway servant. worried they'd nab me. next day pushed on, reached an inn near Burlington. met Dr. Brown there, had a chat over some food. guy knew a lot about Europe, seemed like he traveled plenty. bit of a skeptic, tried turning the Bible into silly verse. never got published, thankfully.

crashed at his place, then made it to Burlington next morning. missed the boats by a hair, none till Tuesday, and it was Saturday. went back to an old woman I’d bought gingerbread from. she let me stay till a boat showed up. wanted me to settle there as a printer, not knowing what that'd take. gave me dinner, all she wanted was a pot of ale in return. thought id be there till Tuesday, but found a boat to Philly that evening. no wind, so we rowed. midnight came, nobody knew where we were, so we camped by a creek, lit a fire with old fence rails. morning light showed we were at Cooper's Creek, just above Philly. got there by eight or nine on Sunday morning, landed at Market-street wharf.

describing this so you can see how far i came from such a rough start. showed up in work clothes, good ones were coming by sea. dirty, pockets stuffed with shirts and socks, and i didn't know a soul. tired, hungry, had a Dutch dollar and some change. gave the change to the boat folks for the ride, they didn’t want it since i helped row, but i insisted. funny how a man gives more when he’s got less in his pocket, maybe scared folks will think he’s broke.

walked up the street, keeping an eye out near the market-house. ran into a kid with bread. remembered having bread meals before, so asked where he got it. he pointed me to a baker on Second-street. asked for bisket like back in Boston, but Philly didn't make 'em. asked for a three-penny loaf instead, but they had none. not knowing the money difference, asked for three-penny worth of anything. ended up with three big rolls. surprised at the size, carried two under my arms, munching one. headed up Market-street past Mr. Read's place. his daughter saw me, must've looked awkward. went down Chestnut, then Walnut, eating as I walked. ended back at Market-street wharf, grabbed some river water, and gave the extra rolls to a woman and her kid from the boat.

after that, walked up the street again. more people were around, all dressed up, going the same way. followed them into the Quaker meeting-house near the market. sat down, looked around, but nothing was happening. tired from the trip, fell asleep till the meeting ended. someone woke me up. first place I slept in Philly.

headed back to the river, looking at faces. met a young Quaker guy, liked his look, asked for lodging advice. near the Three Mariners sign, he said, "not a great spot, but follow me for better." took me to the Crooked Billet on Water-street. grabbed some dinner. folks there asked questions, like they thought I was a runaway.

after eating, got sleepy again. found a bed, crashed without undressing. slept till six, had supper, slept early till morning. tidied up, went to Andrew Bradford the printer. his dad was there, met him in New York. dad introduced me to his son. Bradford was nice, gave me breakfast, but didn't need help right then. mentioned another printer, Keimer, who might need me. said I could stay at his place and pick up odd jobs till more work showed up.

the old guy said he'd come with me to the new printer. when we got there, Bradford goes, "hey neighbor, here's a young man in your line of work. maybe you need him." the printer asked me a few things, handed me a composing stick to see how i did, and said he'd hire me soon, but nothing for now. he thought Bradford was just a local being friendly, so they started talking about his plans and hopes. Bradford, playing along, got him to spill the beans on his strategy by asking crafty questions. i was just standing there, thinking one of these guys knows the game, the other, not so much. Bradford left and the printer was shocked when i said who Bradford really was.

Keimer's setup was sad. an old busted press, one worn-out font of English. he was composing an Elegy on Aquila Rose, a solid guy who was well liked and a decent poet. Keimer dabbled in poetry too, but wasn't any good. he composed straight from his head into type, no written copy. with just one pair of cases and all the letters tied up in the Elegy, no one else could help. i tried to get his press sorted out since he hadn't used it yet and didn't have a clue. i promised to come back to print his Elegy once it was ready and went back to Bradford's for some work and a place to stay. a few days later, Keimer called me to print the Elegy. he'd gotten another pair of cases and had a pamphlet to reprint. that's what i got busy with.

both these printers were lacking. Bradford wasn't trained and barely literate. Keimer was a bit of a scholar, but just a compositor, clueless about presswork. he used to be one of those French prophets, acting all animated. didn't stick to one religion, but a bit of everything when it suited him. pretty naive about the world, and, as i learned later, a bit of a conman. he didn't like me staying at Bradford's while working for him. he had a house but no furniture, so couldn't put me up. he found me a place at Mr. Read's, who owned his house. my stuff arrived by then, so i looked more respectable to Miss Read than when she first saw me eating a roll on the street.

got to know a few folks around town who loved reading as much as i did. spent evenings with them, saving money by working hard, living pretty well, and trying to leave Boston behind. only Collins knew where i was. then an event brought me back sooner than planned. my brother-in-law, Robert Holmes, ran a sloop between Boston and Delaware and heard about me in Newcastle. he wrote me about how folks in Boston were worried when i left. said they'd welcome me back and sort things out if i returned. i thanked him but laid out why i left, to show i wasnt wrong.

Sir William Keith, the governor, was at Newcastle when my letter got to him. Holmes showed it to Keith. Surprised by my age, Keith thought i had potential and should be encouraged. he said the printers in Philadelphia were terrible and i could do well there. he offered to help me set up and get public business. Holmes told me this in Boston later, but i didnt know then. one day Keimer and i were at work by the window when Keith and another man, Colonel French, came straight to us. 

Keimer thought they were visiting him, but Keith asked for me. he was surprisingly polite, said he wanted to know me, and regretted i hadnt reached out sooner. he invited me to the tavern with him and French to try some Madeira. i was shocked; Keimer looked stunned. joined them at a tavern on Third Street. over the wine, Keith suggested i start my business and promised support. i hesitated about my father helping, but Keith offered to write him a letter explaining the benefits and was sure he'd convince him. plan was to head back to Boston on the next ship with Keith's letter. kept it quiet and kept working with Keimer. Keith had me over for dinners sometimes, which was a big deal for me.

end of april 1724, hopped on a little boat headed for boston. told keimer im off to see family. governor handed me a letter, full of praise, pushing my philly business idea. hit a shoal, boat sprung a leak, rough seas. had to pump water out a lot. made it to boston in two weeks. gone seven months, no word from me, surprised everyone except my brother. visited him at his print shop, dressed sharp, new suit, watch, pockets full. he barely looked at me, back to work.

journeymen curious about where id been, asked about the place, liked it a lot, told em i planned to go back. showed em my silver, they werent used to seeing that, boston runs on paper. flashed my watch too. tossed them a piece of eight to drink, left. brother was ticked off, thought i insulted him. mom tried to talk him around, no good.

dad got the governor's letter, didnt say much. when capt. holmes returned, asked him about keith. thought the idea was foolish, setting me up in business at my age. holmes defended it, but dad shut it down, wrote a polite decline to sir william. said i was too young for that kind of responsibility.

collins, my buddy from the post office, liked what he heard about the new place, decided to go. while i waited on dad's decision, he took off to rhode island, left his books with me to bring to new york. planned to meet up there.

my dad wasn’t thrilled with Sir William’s offer, but he liked that I got a good rep from someone important and managed to set myself up pretty well. no chance of me and my brother patching things up, so dad said ok, go back to Philly. he told me to be respectful, win folks over, and cut it out with the roasting and writing that could get me in trouble. he figured if I saved up smartly, I’d have enough by 21 to start fresh, and he’d chip in if needed. all I got was their blessing and a few small gifts when I headed back to New York.

stopped in Newport, Rhode Island, to see my brother John, who’s been settled there. always good to see him, he’s a solid guy. his buddy Vernon had some cash owed to him in Pennsylvania and asked if I’d collect it. he gave me an order, which later caused a bit of a headache.

picked up passengers in Newport for New York. a couple of young women and a serious Quaker lady with her crew were onboard. I helped the Quaker lady with some small stuff, and she seemed to appreciate it. when she saw me getting chummy with the young women, she pulled me aside. she warned me they were trouble and that I should steer clear. at first, I wasn’t convinced, but she pointed out some things I missed, and I came around. thanked her and promised to stay away. when we hit New York, the women told me where they lived and invited me over, but I dodged it. good call, because the next day, the captain found out they’d stolen a silver spoon and more. he nailed them with a warrant and got his stuff back. so yeah, dodging that was probably my best move, even more than missing that rock we scraped over.

hit New York and found my buddy Collins already there. we’d been tight since kids, read the same stuff, but he had more time to study and was a math whiz, way ahead of me. back in Boston, we hung out whenever I had free time. he was serious and worked hard, respected by the clergy and others for his smarts, seemed like he’d do well. but while I was gone, he picked up a bad habit with brandy. heard he’d been drunk every day since landing in New York and acting strange. he gambled too, lost all his cash, so I had to cover his rent and get him to Philly — not convenient for me.

the governor of New York, Burnet, heard from the captain I had a lot of books and wanted to meet. went to see him. would’ve taken Collins, but he wasn’t sober. the governor was nice, showed me his massive library, and we chatted about books and authors. second governor to notice me, which felt good for a broke kid.

headed to Philly. picked up money from Vernon on the way, barely finished the trip without it. Collins wanted a job in a counting house, but maybe they smelled the booze on him or noticed his behavior. despite recommendations, he struck out, kept staying with me, at my expense. he knew I had Vernon’s money and kept borrowing, promising to pay back once he got a job. he borrowed so much I worried what I’d do if Vernon asked for it back.

his drinking kept up, and we’d argue sometimes cause he got real cranky when he drank too much. one time we’re out on the Delaware, and he won’t take his turn rowing. “i’ll be rowed home,” he says. “we won’t row you,” i tell him. “fine, stay out here all night then,” he shoots back. the others figured it wasn’t worth the fight, but his other antics had me fed up, so i stood my ground. then he tries to get tough, says he’ll throw me overboard if i don’t row. he comes at me, stepping on the thwarts, and swings, but i slip my hand under his crutch and flip him right into the river. i knew he could swim, wasn’t worried, but we pulled the boat just out of his reach every time he got close, asking if he’d row now. he was fuming, wouldn’t give in, so we let him tire out before hauling him back in, soaking wet. after that, we barely spoke. a West India captain later picked him up for a tutoring gig in Barbadoes. he promised to pay back what he owed me, but that was the last i heard of him.

breaking into Vernon's money was a big screw-up on my part; my father wasn’t wrong thinking i was too young for big responsibilities. but Sir William, reading my dad’s letter, said he was too cautious. people differ; age doesn’t always bring sense, and youth isn’t always without it. “since he won’t back you,” Sir William says, “i will. list what you need from England, and i’ll get it. pay me back when you can; i want a good printer here, and you’re the one to do it.” he sounded so genuine, i didn’t doubt him. i hadn’t told anyone in Philly about setting up my own shop, kept it under wraps. if folks knew i was banking on the governor, they might’ve warned me he was all talk. still, unsolicited, his offer seemed real. i thought he was one of the good ones.

i handed him a list for a small print shop, reckoned it’d cost about a hundred pounds. he liked it, asked if going to England myself to pick the types would help. “you could make connections there,” he said. made sense. “go with Annis,” he says, the annual ship to London. Annis wouldn’t leave for months, so i stayed with Keimer, stewing over the money Collins took and worrying Vernon would come calling. that didn’t happen for some years though.

First trip from Boston, we got stuck near Block Island. Everyone started fishing for cod. I'd been avoiding meat, thought it was kinda like murder. Fish never did anything wrong. Made sense at the time. But man, when that fish came out of the pan smelling so good, I had to rethink. Saw smaller fish inside the bigger ones' stomachs. Figured if they eat each other, why can't we? So I dug in, went back to eating meat with everyone else. But every now and then, I'd go back to veggies. Shows how easy it is to justify whatever you want to do.

Me and Keimer got along okay. He didn't know I was planning my own setup. Loved arguing, so we had our share of debates. I'd use my questions to lead him into a corner. Got so wary he wouldn’t answer without checking my angle. Thought I was so good at it he wanted me as a partner in some new sect he was dreaming up. He'd preach, I'd debunk the critics. But when he explained the doctrines, I found some puzzles. Wanted to add my own ideas too.

Keimer kept his beard long 'cause the Mosaic law said so. Also observed the Sabbath. Didn't like either, but I agreed if he'd go vegetarian. He doubted he could handle it. I told him he’d feel better. He’d eat a lot, and I thought it'd be fun to watch him struggle. He tried it out if I joined him. We did it for three months, got meals from a woman nearby. She cooked from a list of forty dishes I gave her, no meat, fish, or bird. Cheap too, about eighteenpence each a week. I've gone through several Lents since, switching diets suddenly with no problem, so gradual change advice is overrated. I managed fine, but Keimer struggled, missed meat, and ordered a roast pig. He invited me and two women over, but he couldn’t wait and ate it all before we got there.

so i had this thing going with Miss Read. liked her a lot, and i thought she felt the same. but i was about to head out on a long trip, and we were both just eighteen. her mom figured it'd be better to wait till i got back and settled in my work. maybe she thought i was getting ahead of myself with my plans.

had a few buddies back then—Charles Osborne, Joseph Watson, James Ralph. all bookworms. Osborne and Watson were clerks for a big-time scrivener, Charles Brogden. Ralph worked for a merchant. Watson was smart and honest, pretty religious. Osborne was sharp, honest with friends, but loved to critique. Ralph was smooth, could talk your ear off, and thought he had a shot at making it big with poetry. we’d take these long Sunday walks by the Schuylkill, reading and talking shop.

Ralph was set on poetry, thought he’d make it big. Osborne told him straight—stick to what he knew, business. in time, he could make something of himself there. i was cool with dabbling in poetry for fun, but not for a career.

we decided to bring our own pieces to the next meet-up, aiming to work on language and style. no new ideas—just a translation of the eighteenth Psalm. Ralph finished his early, showed it to me. i hadn’t done mine, but his was solid. he asked me to pass it off as mine to see if Osborne’s reaction changed. i copied it out in my handwriting, ready to see how it played out.

met up with the group. read watson's piece first. had some nice bits, but lots of issues. osborne's came next. way better. ralph pointed out some flaws but praised the good stuff. he didn't have anything to show. i hesitated, wanted out, said i hadn't had time to fix mine. no way out though. had to share it. read it out, even repeated it. watson and osborne gave up, clapped for it. ralph had some critiques, suggested changes, but i stood by my words. osborne backed me up and told ralph he wasn't much of a critic. ralph dropped it. osborne later told me he liked my work even more than he'd let on, didn't want to flatter me too much. "who'd have thought franklin could do this," he said. "such force and fire! even better than the original." funny how in conversation i'd stumble over words, but writing was different. next time we met, ralph caught on to our little trick, and osborne got a laugh.

ralph was set on being a poet after that. i tried to talk him out of it, but he kept writing poems till pope knocked him out of it. turned out to be a decent prose writer though. more on him later. as for the others, watson died in my arms a few years on, best of our group. osborne went to the west indies, became a successful lawyer, died young. we had a pact to visit the other if one of us died first. he never did.

the governor liked having me around, had me over a lot. always talked about setting me up with a print shop. said i'd get letters of credit and introduction to buy what i needed. kept telling me to come back for them, but always postponed. finally, the ship's departure was near, and still no letters. went to say goodbye and pick them up, but dr. bard, his secretary, said the governor was busy writing, would meet me at newcastle with the letters.

ralph, married with a kid, decided to come along. thought he was setting up business deals, but later found out he was leaving his wife and didn't plan to come back. said my goodbyes, promised miss read i'd return, and left philly on the ship. anchored at newcastle. governor was there, but his secretary came with a polite message that the governor was too busy to see me, would send the letters on board, wished me well. got back on the ship a bit confused, but still believed i'd get them.

andrew hamilton, big-time lawyer from philly, hopped on the same ship as me and ralph. him, his son james (later gov), mr. denham the quaker merchant, and a couple of iron guys from maryland snagged the fancy cabin. me and ralph? steerage. nobody knew us, so we were just two regular guys. but before we set sail, hamilton got called back for some big case, and colonel french came on board. he showed me some respect, which scored us an invite to the cabin since there was space now.

colonel french brought the governor's letters, so i asked the captain for the ones i was supposed to handle. captain said he'd let me dig through the bag before we hit england. worked for me. cabin crew was good company, and we got to enjoy hamilton's supplies. denham and i hit it off, stayed friends till the end. trip wasn't all sunshine though—lots of bad weather.

as we hit the channel, captain kept his word. i dug through the bag, found a few letters with familiar handwriting, figured they were the ones. we landed in london, december 1724. first place i went was the stationer, handed over a letter from gov keith. stationer was like, "who?" then he read it, found out it was from riddlesden, whom he called a "complete rascal." tossed the letter back at me and moved on. realized these weren't the governor's letters after all. talked to denham, told him everything. he clued me in on keith—zero reliability, no letters, no credit, just a joke. denham suggested i find work with local printers to sharpen up, then head back to america with better skills.

we both knew riddlesden, the attorney, was a real snake. he'd nearly ruined miss read's dad by convincing him to back him financially. the letter showed a secret plan that'd hurt hamilton, who was supposed to be joining us, and keith was in on it with riddlesden. denham, a friend of hamilton's, thought he should know, so when i got to england, i gave hamilton the letter. he was grateful, and from then on, he had my back, which helped me a lot later.

but seriously, what kind of governor plays these cheap tricks on a clueless kid? he had a habit of trying to please everyone, giving out promises instead of real things. otherwise, he was smart, a decent writer, and did well for the people, even if he sometimes ignored what the proprietaries wanted. some of his best laws passed while he was in charge.

ralph and i were tight. we shared a place in little britain for three shillings and sixpence a week, all we could afford. his relatives were too poor to help, and he planned to stay in london, never returning to philadelphia. he'd spent all his money on the trip over, so he borrowed from me while looking for work. he tried to get into acting, but wilkes told him he wouldn't make it. then he pitched writing a weekly paper to roberts, but that didn't pan out. he even tried freelance writing for stationers and lawyers, but nothing came up.

i found work at palmer's, a well-known print shop in bartholomew close, and stayed nearly a year. worked hard, but spent a lot with ralph on plays and other fun. we blew through all my money and were barely getting by. he forgot his family, and i, slowly, my promise to miss read. only sent her one letter saying i wouldn't be back soon. that was a big mistake in my life i'd fix if i could do it over. our spending kept me from saving enough to get back home.

at Palmer's, i was working on the second edition of Wollaston's "Religion of Nature." some of his ideas didn't sit right with me, so i wrote a little piece called "A Dissertation on Liberty and Necessity, Pleasure and Pain." dedicated it to my friend Ralph. printed a small batch. Mr. Palmer noticed, thought it was clever, but wasn't a fan of my ideas—said they were awful. printing that pamphlet was another mistake. while i was living in Little Britain, i met Wilcox, a bookseller next door. he had tons of second-hand books. no circulating libraries back then, but we made a deal—i could borrow and return any book. took full advantage.

my pamphlet ended up with Lyons, a surgeon who wrote "The Infallibility of Human Judgment." got us acquainted. he liked chatting about those topics, took me to the Horns alehouse on Cheapside, where i met Dr. Mandeville, the guy behind "Fable of the Bees." Lyons also introduced me to Dr. Pemberton at Batson's Coffee-house. promised he'd get me to meet Sir Isaac Newton, but that never happened.

brought some curiosities with me, like a purse made of asbestos, which cleans in fire. Sir Hans Sloane heard about it, visited, and invited me to Bloomsbury Square. showed me his collection and bought the purse for a good price.

a young milliner lived in our house, had a shop in the Cloisters. well-bred and great to talk to. Ralph read plays to her, they got close, she moved, and he joined her. they lived together, but his lack of work and her income couldn't support them and her child. he decided to leave London to try teaching in the country. thought he was qualified—good handwriting, knew arithmetic. but thought it was beneath him, so he changed his name to mine. later got a letter from him saying he was teaching in a small village, maybe Berkshire, and asked me to look out for Mrs. T------. wanted me to write back to "Mr. Franklin, schoolmaster."

he kept sending pieces of this epic poem he was working on, asking for my feedback. i'd send back notes, but mostly tried to steer him away from it. Young's Satires had just come out, so i copied a chunk and sent it to him to show the futility of chasing the Muses for a living. didn't matter; the poem pages kept arriving. meanwhile, Mrs. T------ was in a tough spot, losing friends and business because of him, and she'd borrow money from me. i liked spending time with her, and without any religious bounds, i acted out of line. she rightly turned me down and told him. that wrecked our friendship, and when he came back to London, he made it clear i wasn't getting back what i'd lent him. didn't bother me much since he was broke, and losing his friendship felt like losing a burden. decided to get ahead financially, moved from Palmer's to Watts's, a bigger print shop near Lincoln's Inn Fields. stuck around there for the rest of my London stint.

when i joined the new shop, i hit the press work needing the physical side i was used to back in America. stuck to water while the other fifty guys guzzled beer. i could carry a big form of types with each hand, while they struggled with one in both. they called me the Water-American, shocked i was stronger without the beer. there was a boy always around to fetch beer. my press mate drank a pint before breakfast, with breakfast, mid-morning, at lunch, around six, and post-work. figured it was a lousy habit, but he thought beer made him strong for work. tried telling him beer's strength only came from the barley's flour, and a pennyworth of bread with water would give more strength than a quart of beer. he kept drinking and spent four or five shillings a week on it, while i saved mine. poor guys kept themselves stuck like that.

watts wanted me in the composing-room after a few weeks, so i left the pressmen. the compositors demanded five shillings for a drink fund. felt like a scam, since i'd already paid with the pressmen. the master agreed and told me not to pay. held out for a couple weeks. they messed with my work whenever i left the room, blaming it on the "chappel ghost." even with the master's backing, i had to cough up the money. learned it's not smart to be on bad terms with folks you're around all the time.

once i paid, things got better. had influence now. suggested some changes to their chappel laws, got them passed. folks started copying me, ditching their beer and cheese mornings for hot water-gruel with pepper, bread, and butter. cheaper than beer and kept your head clear. those still on beer were often out of credit and asked me to cover them. i'd keep track of what they owed on payday, sometimes up to thirty shillings a week. being a decent satirist helped too. showed up every day, unlike those who made monday a holiday. master liked my reliability and speed, so i got the rush jobs with better pay. life got smoother.

my old place in Little Britain was too far out, so i found a spot on Duke-street, right by the Romish Chapel. second floor, back of an Italian warehouse. a widow ran the house with her daughter, a maid, and a journeyman for the warehouse who stayed elsewhere. she checked my story with my last place and let me stay for 3s. 6d. a week. cheaper, cause she liked having a man around. she was a protestant-turned-catholic, had stories from way back to Charles II. gout kept her in her room, so id visit when she wanted company. supper was simple – half an anchovy, bread, and shared ale, but her stories were the real treat. i kept to my schedule, no trouble, so when i mentioned a cheaper place, she dropped my rent to 1s. 6d., and i stayed as long as i was in London.

up in the garret, a seventy-year-old maiden lady lived quietly. my landlady said she was a catholic, tried the nun life abroad but came back when it didn't suit her. no nunneries here, so she lived like one anyway. gave away her estate, kept just twelve pounds a year, still gave to charity, lived on water-gruel, and only boiled that. she'd been there for years, rent-free, seen as a blessing by catholic tenants. a priest visited daily for confession. i asked how she filled a confession daily. she said vain thoughts are always around. i got to visit her once. she was bright and friendly. her room was sparse – a mattress, table with a crucifix, stool for me, and a picture of Saint Veronica with Christ's image. she explained it seriously. looked pale but never ill. shows you can live healthy on little.

met this guy Wygate at Watts's printing-house. He was sharp, had moneyed family, knew some Latin, spoke French, loved books. i taught him and his buddy to swim in just a couple of sessions, and they got good fast. they introduced me to some country gents, and we took a boat trip to Chelsea to check out the College and Don Saltero's curiosities. on the way back, Wygate got everyone curious about my swimming skills, so i jumped in the river and swam from Chelsea to Blackfryar's, showing off a bunch of tricks both above and below water. they were impressed.

since i was a kid, i loved swimming. studied Thevenot's techniques, added my own moves, trying to make it look smooth and useful. showed off to the group, and they were into it. Wygate wanted to get really good at it too, so we got closer. he even suggested we travel around Europe, working our trade. i was tempted, but ran it by my friend Mr. Denham, who told me to focus on getting back to Pennsylvania. 

Denham was a solid guy. used to run a business in Bristol, went bust, and moved to America. worked hard as a merchant, made a fortune. when we sailed back to England, he threw a party for his old creditors, thanked them for the break they gave him, and surprised them with full payments, with interest. 

Denham told me he was heading back to Philadelphia, planning to open a store with a big shipment of goods. he offered me a job as his clerk, to manage books, copy letters, and help at the store. said once i got the hang of it, he'd send me with cargo to the West Indies and set up commissions, which could be profitable. liked the idea. was tired of London, missed Pennsylvania, and this was a good chance to go back. agreed to fifty pounds a year, even if it was less than what i made as a compositor, it promised more.

left printing behind, thought it was for good. daily grind with mr. denham, buying stuff, packing it, running errands, keeping everyone moving. got a few days off when everything was shipped. out of nowhere, this big shot, sir william wyndham, wanted to see me. only knew him by name. turns out he heard about my swim from chelsea to blackfriar's, and teaching some guys to swim quick. he had two sons heading on travels, wanted them taught swimming. would've paid me well. they weren't in town yet, and i didn't know how long i'd be around, so i had to pass. but it got me thinking, maybe a swimming school would make good money here. if he'd asked sooner, might've stuck around. years later, ended up dealing with one of his sons, now earl of egremont. more on that later.

spent about 18 months in london. mostly working hard, didn't spend much on myself, just plays and books. my buddy ralph kept me broke, owed me 27 pounds i'd never see. big chunk of my small earnings. still liked the guy, he had good traits. didn't boost my fortune, but met some smart folks, learned a lot from them. read a ton.

sailed from gravesend on july 23, 1726. trip details are in my journal. most important part? the plan i sketched out for my life. young when i made it, but stuck to it pretty well over the years.

landed back in philly on october 11. some changes. keith out as governor, replaced by major gordon. ran into keith on the street, looked a bit ashamed, didn't say anything. would've been awkward seeing miss read, but her friends talked her into marrying a potter named rogers when they figured i wasn't coming back. she wasn't happy, left him. turned out he was married to someone else. rogers was a mess, good at his craft but not much else. took off to the west indies, died there. meanwhile, keimer had upgraded—better shop, more supplies, more workers, but none too good. seemed busy enough.

denham got a place on water street, so we set up shop there. i dove into the work, got good at selling quick. we ate and lived together, and he looked out for me like a dad. i really respected him. things were good until february 1726-7, when we both got sick. i had a pleurisy, almost didn't make it. was ready to check out, so waking up better was a bit of a downer. he didn't pull through though, left me a small legacy, and i was alone again. executors took the store, job over.

my brother-in-law, holmes, was in philly and said go back to the trade. keimer dangled a big paycheck to manage his print shop, so he could focus on his stationer store. i'd heard bad things about him, but options were thin, so i signed on. found a crew there: hugh meredith, good guy but liked his drink, stephen potts, smart but lazy, and a few others. keimer got them cheap, promised a shilling raise every few months if they got better. but really, he wanted me to train them up so he could cut me loose later. 

i got to work, sorted the mess in the print shop, and got the guys to improve. had them working better eventually.

it was strange seeing an Oxford scholar working as a hired servant. he couldn't have been more than eighteen, and he told me his story. born in Gloucester, got his education at a grammar school there, stood out in plays, joined the Witty Club, wrote some pieces that got printed in the Gloucester papers. then he went to Oxford, lasted a year but wasn't satisfied, all he wanted was to see London and become a player. when his quarterly allowance of fifteen guineas came, instead of paying off debts, he left town, hid his gown in a bush, and walked to London. no friends there, he got in with the wrong crowd, spent his money, couldn't get into the theater scene, pawned his clothes, and was starving. one day, he got handed a bill promising food and a job in America if he signed up. so he did, got on a ship, and never told his folks what happened. he was lively and witty but careless and imprudent.

john, the Irish guy, ran off soon enough; the rest respected me more. they learned daily from me since Keimer had nothing to teach. Saturdays were off work due to Keimer's Sabbath, so i had time to read. met some clever folks in town. Keimer treated me well, but my debt to Vernon still bothered me, though he never asked for it back.

our printing shop often lacked type, and no letter-founder was around in America. i'd seen it done in London, so i made a mold, used our letters as punches, struck matrices in lead, and filled the gaps. i also engraved when needed, made ink, handled the warehouse — basically did it all.

despite my usefulness, as others improved, my importance dwindled. when Keimer paid my second quarter's wages, he hinted they were too high and wanted a cut. his attitude changed, more like a boss, more faults found, ready for a bust-up. i stayed patient, thinking his financial trouble played a part. but a small incident broke us apart. some noise near the courthouse caught my attention, and i looked out the window. Keimer saw me and yelled to mind my business, adding public insults. he came up, the quarrel continued, and he gave me the quarter's notice. i told him i'd leave immediately and walked out, asking Meredith to grab my stuff and bring it to my place.

Meredith showed up in the evening, and we got into my situation. He’d taken a real liking to me and didn’t want me leaving while he was still around. He talked me out of heading back home, which I was considering. He pointed out that Keimer was deep in debt, his creditors were getting restless, and his shop was a mess, losing money on sales and giving out credit without tracking. He was bound to fail, and that’d leave an opening I could use. I mentioned I didn’t have the funds. Meredith then told me his dad thought highly of me and would likely put up the money if I partnered with him. "My time with Keimer's up in the spring," he said, "by then, we can get our press and types from London. I know I’m not much of a workman; your skill can match the stock I bring, and we’ll split the profits."

I agreed. His dad was in town and liked the idea, especially since I’d convinced Meredith to cut down on the drinking, hoping I’d help him quit for good once we partnered. I gave his dad a list, he took it to a merchant, and we ordered the supplies, keeping it under wraps until they arrived. I tried to find work at the other print shop but no luck, so I was idle for a bit. Then Keimer, needing help for a New Jersey paper money job requiring skills only I had, sent a nice message asking me back. Meredith thought it’d be good for his learning, so I went back, and we worked better than before. We got the New Jersey job, and I engineered a copperplate press for it — first in the country. I handled the whole thing in Burlington, making enough to keep Keimer afloat longer.

In Burlington, I met some bigwigs, many of whom were on a committee to oversee the printing. They’d hang around the press, often bringing friends. My reading had sharpened my mind more than Keimer's, so they valued my conversation more, inviting me to their homes and treating me well, while Keimer, despite being the boss, got overlooked. He was an odd one — dirty, liked to argue rudely, and had some peculiar religious ideas, plus a bit of a schemer.

stayed there about three months. made some solid friends—Judge Allen, Samuel Bustill, Isaac Pearson, Joseph Cooper, a few Smiths from the Assembly, and Isaac Decow, the surveyor-general. Decow was sharp, started out hauling clay, learned writing late, then got into surveying. built himself a decent life. He looked at me and said, "You'll outwork him and make it big in Philly." He didn't even know I planned to set up there. these folks really helped me later, and I helped them too. stayed close till they passed.

now, before jumping into my business side, gotta tell you where my head was at about principles and morals. my folks raised me religiously, but by 15, I started doubting everything. read some books against Deism that had the opposite effect on me. the Deists' arguments seemed stronger, so I became a Deist. got others like Collins and Ralph into it too. but they wronged me later, and thinking about Keith, Vernon, Miss Read—made me question if this belief was right. my London pamphlet with those Dryden lines seemed less smart now. started thinking maybe there was an error in my logic, like how metaphysical stuff often sneaks in mistakes.

found out that being honest and straight with folks is key to really enjoying life. wrote this down in my journal, and ive stuck with it. not big on revelations or commandments, but figured if something was forbidden, it might be bad for us, and if it was commanded, probably good. thinking like that, plus luck or whatever you call it, kept me out of major trouble even when i was young and away from my dad's watchful eye. sure, i messed up a bit, mostly because i was inexperienced and folks were shady, but nothing too major. ended up with a decent rep to start in life, and i wanted to keep it that way.

back in philly, our new types arrived from london. settled up with keimer and left him quietly. rented a place near the market, split it with thomas godfrey, a glazier, to cut costs. barely got our gear set up when george house brought us our first customer. we were broke, so that five shillings felt like a jackpot. always felt grateful to george for that start, made me want to help other newbies when i could.

plenty of doom-sayers around, like samuel mickle. he stopped by, told me opening a print shop was a waste since philly was going downhill. he had a lot to say about how everything was going to ruin, left me feeling kinda down. if id listened, might not have started the business. funny thing, sam stuck around, kept moaning about the city's decline, but eventually paid five times the price for a house. guess his warnings didnt pan out, huh?

last fall, i pulled together a club with some of my brighter pals, called it the Junto. we’d meet up Friday nights. i set up some rules: everyone had to bring up a question on Morals, Politics, or Natural Philosophy for discussion, and once every three months, write and read an essay on whatever topic they wanted. debates ran under a president's eye, aimed at truth, not just arguing to win. to keep it cool, we banned strong opinions and direct contradictions. if you slipped, you'd pay a small fine.

first members included Joseph Breintnal, who copied deeds, nice guy, loved poetry, and was good at making little gadgets. Thomas Godfrey, a math whiz who invented Hadley's Quadrant, though he was a bit too precise for casual chats. he didn’t last long in the group. Nicholas Scull, liked books, wrote some verses, became surveyor-general. William Parsons, started as a shoemaker, got into math thinking about astrology, then ditched that idea. he also turned surveyor-general. William Maugridge, a joiner, solid mechanic, sensible guy.

Hugh Meredith, Stephen Potts, and George Webb, i mentioned them before. Robert Grace, young, had some money, fun and witty, loved puns and his friends. William Coleman, same age as me, started as a merchant's clerk, sharp mind, good heart, exact morals. he became a big merchant and judge. our friendship lasted over forty years, and the club almost that long. it was the best spot for learning philosophy, morality, and politics. we read up on topics before debates, sharpened our conversation skills. that's why the club lasted so long, and you'll hear more about it later.

so here's the deal. we hustled hard to get business. breintnal hooked us up with the quakers for printing some of their history. about forty sheets worth while keimer did the rest. the price was low, so we had to grind. it was a folio, big size, using pica and long primer notes. i'd crank out a sheet a day, and meredith would handle the press. sometimes i was up till eleven or later setting up for the next day. small jobs from friends slowed us down, but i was dead set on finishing a sheet a day. one night, i busted a form and had to redo two pages before bed. neighbors noticed our hustle, and it started building our rep. at the merchants' club, folks were betting we'd fail with two printers already in town. but dr. baird backed us, saying, "that franklin's work ethic is unmatched. i see him working late and early." that caught attention, and offers came in for supplies, but we weren’t ready for shop business.

i share this story to highlight the power of hard work. my descendants should see how it benefited me.

george webb, who bought his freedom from keimer with a friend's help, wanted to work with us. we couldn't hire him then, but i told him i'd start a newspaper soon and might need him. i thought bradford's paper was weak, and a good one could succeed. told webb to keep it quiet, but he spilled to keimer, who rushed to launch his own paper. to counter him, i wrote for bradford's as 'the busy body', mocking keimer's proposals. his paper flopped with only ninety subscribers, and he sold it to me cheap. once i took over, it became real profitable.

i often say 'i' since i ran everything. meredith couldn't compose, wasn't great on the press, and drank too much. my friends worried about my partnership, but i had to make it work.

first papers looked way different from anything else around here — better type, better print. i wrote some spicy stuff on the Burnet vs. Massachusetts Assembly drama, and it got people talking. next thing we knew, everyone wanted in and started subscribing.

more folks followed suit, and our numbers kept climbing. learning to scribble a bit paid off. the big shots saw the paper was in hands that knew how to write, so they backed me. meanwhile, bradford was still doing the official prints but in a sloppy way. we reprinted a House address all neat and sharp, sent it to each member. they noticed the upgrade. it got us the gig as printers for the next year.

can't forget Mr. Hamilton from the House, had my back big time, even after he got back from England. helped out in this and other things until he passed. as for Mr. Vernon, he reminded me about the money i owed him. wrote him a honest note, asked for more time, and he gave it. paid him back with interest when i could, so that mistake got sorted.

then hit a snag i didn't see coming. Meredith's dad was supposed to pay for our printing setup but only managed a hundred pounds. we owed more, and the merchant was losing patience, sued us. we put up bail, but without the money, things looked grim — everything would get sold off cheap.

two friends stepped up, William Coleman and Robert Grace. didn't ask them, but they offered the cash to buy out the whole setup if needed, though they weren't fans of my partner Meredith, who was often seen drunk and gambling. told them i'd stick with the Merediths as long as there was a chance they'd pull through. but if the partnership had to end, i'd take their help.

so my partner's dad wasn't thrilled about our deal. i told him if that's the case, id step back and handle my own stuff. but he said his dad just couldn't help more and he felt bad pushing him. he realized he wasn't cut out for this business, being a farmer and all, and wanted to head to north carolina with other welsh folks. he offered to split if i took on the company debts, returned his dad's investment, paid his personal debts, and gave him some cash and a saddle. we drafted that up, signed it, and he left for carolina. he sent back these great letters on the land and farming there, which i printed in the papers and people loved.

after he left, i turned to my two buddies who'd offered help. split their offers, paid off the debts, and took over the business solo. advertised the partnership was done. think this was around 1729.

then there was this buzz about needing more paper money. only 15k pounds were circulating and about to be retired. rich folks didn't want more, fearing it'd lose value like in new england. but in our junto discussions, i was for it. saw how the first issue in 1723 boosted trade and population, filled old houses, and sparked new builds. remembered when i first got to philly, lots of places were empty.

got so into the topic, i wrote an anonymous pamphlet, "the nature and necessity of a paper currency." the common folks liked it, but the rich didn't. it stirred more demand for money. since they had no good writers to counter, they backed off, and the house agreed to more currency. my friends there thought i helped the cause, so they let me print the money—big win for me, money-wise. another perk of knowing how to write.

currency's utility became clear over time. it jumped from fifty-five thousand pounds to eighty thousand in 1739, and hit over three hundred and fifty thousand during war. trade and population grew too, but there's a limit where it might backfire.

through my buddy hamilton, i landed the job of printing newcastle's paper money. seemed like a big deal back then. also got the printing for the laws and votes of that gov, kept that gig as long as i stayed in the business.

opened a small stationer's shop with top-notch blanks, thanks to my friend breintnal. sold paper, parchment, chapmen's books. whitemash, a skilled guy i knew from london, joined up. i took on an apprentice, the son of aquila rose.

started paying off the printing-house debt. kept things above board, worked hard, dressed simple, skipped idle stuff. if anything, books were my only distraction. hauled my own paper through the streets on a wheelbarrow to show i wasn't above the work. merchants liked that, offered me their business, and things went well. meanwhile, keimer's business fell apart. he had to sell his printing house and ended up struggling in barbadoes.

his apprentice, david harry, set up shop in philly. thought he'd be tough competition, so i offered a partnership. he turned it down. he lived large, spent big, ignored his work. business dried up, and he followed keimer to barbadoes. they didn't get along. harry went broke and had to sell his stuff, headed back to pennsylvania. the buyer hired keimer to work the types, but he didn't last long.

In Philly, it was just me and the old rival, Bradford. He was rich, took it easy, only printed a bit here and there with some random help. But since he ran the post office, folks thought he had the inside track on news. His paper got more ads than mine, which was good money for him and a hit for me. Even though I did manage to get papers sent out through the post, people assumed otherwise. I had to pay off the riders to take them secretly since Bradford, being a real piece of work, wouldn't allow it. I thought pretty low of him for that. When I ended up in his spot later, I made sure not to act like he did.

I was still boarding with Godfrey, who had his glazier shop in part of my place. He didn't work much—always lost in his math. His wife, Mrs. Godfrey, tried to set me up with a relative's daughter. She kept bringing us together until I got serious. The girl was worth it. Her folks kept inviting me over, leaving us alone, pushing things along. But when it came down to talking business, I told Mrs. Godfrey I needed enough money from them to clear my debt on the printing house—about a hundred pounds. She said they didn't have it. I suggested they could mortgage their house. Then they came back saying they didn't like the match. Bradford had told them the printing gig wasn't worth it—the types would wear out, and I'd probably fail like S. Keimer and D. Harry. Next thing, I'm banned from the house, and the girl gets locked up.

Not sure if they really changed their minds or just played us, thinking we'd elope and then they could decide what to give. Either way, I got suspicious, and I was done. Mrs. Godfrey tried to smooth things over later, but I stuck to my guns—no more dealings with them. Godfreys didn't like it, we fell out, and they moved on, leaving me the whole place. I decided no more boarders.

thinking about marriage got me looking around, but being a printer wasn't seen as a moneymaker, so no luck there unless i settled, which i didn't want. instead, i ended up in a few messy situations with women where i shouldn't have been. cost me time and money, and i dodged a real health scare by some miracle. stayed in touch with mrs. read's family, always liked them. miss read was down a lot, and i knew my past decisions had a part in that. her mom blamed herself more, for stopping our marriage and pushing another match. we still cared for each other, but getting together wasn't simple. the other guy supposedly had a wife back in england, hard to prove. even if true, he left debts. still, we went ahead, married in 1730. all turned out fine, she was a great partner, helped at the shop, and we made it work.

our club met in a small room at mr. grace's, and i suggested pooling our books for easy access during meetings. everyone liked it, filled a corner with books we could spare. wasn't as many as planned, and after a year, due to some care issues, we split the collection back up.

then i kicked off my first public project: a subscription library. wrote proposals, got help from brockden to formalize them, and with the junto's support, we got 50 subscribers to start. later, expanded to 100. this library idea caught on in north america. these libraries have raised the level of conversation, making even tradesmen and farmers as sharp as gentlemen elsewhere, and might've helped in the colonies standing up for their rights.

memo. started this with family stuff. not big news. then the revolution messed things up. now writing for the public.

letter from benjamin vaughan. paris, jan 31, 1783.

hey, read through your life's story that your Quaker buddy put together. promised id send thoughts on why you should finish and publish it. took me a while to get to it, busy with other stuff, but here goes. 

your life's a story worth telling. if you dont, someone else will, and they might not do it justice. your biography will give insight into your country's growth and could draw people who want to live right. with your reputation, it's like a big ad for the place. 

your life connects with the rise of a people. even Cæsar and Tacitus got nothing on this for someone who really gets human nature. but the real kicker is how your story could shape future greats. combine it with your Art of Virtue and it's a guide for self-education. 

schools miss the mark with clunky systems. your way's simple and true. people gotta know they can shape their own path. influence starts young. habits, choices, life paths—all decided in youth. gotta set the course right from the start. 

your story won't just teach self-education, but how to be wise. even the wisest can learn from another's journey. why keep this from folks when we've been stumbling in the dark forever? show the way, inspire others to be wise and to live well. 

we see enough cruelty and absurdity from leaders. your story will show it's possible to be great and grounded, admirable but still good-natured.

write down those little private moments too. they're useful, especially when we need practical advice for everyday stuff. seeing how you handle things can be like a life manual, explaining what everyone should understand to get wise before they learn the hard way. when you share stories, it feels like we're living through them, and that's valuable. your writing makes things stand out, whether simple or important, and i bet you've managed your life with as much creativity as if you were debating politics or philosophy. what could be more worth experimenting on than life itself?

you got folks who play it safe, dream big, or play dirty. then there's you. i know whatever you put out there will be smart, useful, and good. your life story doesn’t hide anything. you show that where you start doesn’t matter as much as what you do with it. you had a plan, but it was simple—just nature, virtue, thought, habit. you knew when to step up in life, and you didn’t rush it. you’ve lived with patience and contentment, not regrets. those who’ve praised your hard work and thriftiness missed your modesty and selflessness. without those, you couldn’t have waited for your moment. that shows the emptiness of glory and the need to keep our minds in check.

if folks really knew your reputation, they’d see how your past work will draw attention to your story and your thoughts on virtue. they’d see the benefit of having a diverse character. it’s more useful than people realize since many don’t know how to improve themselves, even if they want to. your life story, if written, would stand out in a world full of heroes and fools. it could inspire more to live lives worth writing about. but enough imagining—it’s time to be real. i want you to share your true self, especially with the world in such turmoil. no one knows your life and thoughts like you do. with your influence, it’s key to show that virtuous principles have guided you. it’ll matter for america, england, europe.

we’ve got to prove man isn’t all bad and can be better. your life can help show there are good people out there. if everyone thinks all men are lost causes, they’ll stop trying to do good. show yourself as just, free, and united since you were young. let englishmen respect and love you, and make them think well of your country, and vice versa. aim big. improve humanity. i haven’t read your life story, but i know your character. i’m confident it’ll meet my high hopes. even if it doesn’t achieve everything, you’ll still offer something engaging and innocent, adding a bit of light to a world too often dark and painful. so i hope you’ll take this to heart and act on it, my dear sir.

signed, benj. vaughan.

continuation of my life account, started at passy, near paris, 1784.

been a while since i got those letters, but too busy till now to do anything about it. would've been better at home with my papers, but who knows when i'll head back. got a bit of time now, so i'll try to remember and jot down what i can. if i make it home, i can fix it up then.

don't have a copy of what's already written here, so not sure if i covered the philadelphia public library story. think i stopped somewhere around 1730, so let's start there. if it's a repeat, just cut it.

when i set up in pennsylvania, no good bookshops south of boston. in new york and philly, printers sold paper, almanacs, few school books. book lovers had to get their stuff from england; each of us in the junto had a few. we ditched the alehouse and got a room for our club. i suggested we all bring our books there. easy to consult and borrow.

seeing how well it worked, i pitched a public subscription library. sketched up a plan, got charles brockden to draft it. each person paid a bit upfront and yearly for more books. readers were rare in philly, most of us broke, but scraped together fifty people, mostly young tradesmen, to pay forty shillings upfront, ten annually. we kicked off with that. books came in, library opened once a week. if someone didn't return a book, they paid double. soon, other towns copied us. libraries grew with donations, reading caught on, and without distractions, folks got smarter. visitors noticed how well-read our people were compared to others.

when we were about to sign those articles, brockden said, "you're young, but unlikely to see the fifty-year term end." some of us are still around, but the charter later made it all official and permanent.

asking folks to help fund a project, i hit a wall. turns out, when you’re the face of an idea that hints at boosting your reputation, people get hesitant. so i stepped back and pitched it as a plan cooked up by some friends who thought it’d appeal to book lovers. suddenly, things smoothed out. it became my go-to strategy. and i recommend it. swallow a bit of pride now, and it pays off. someone vain might try to claim the credit, but envy will eventually sort things out, giving credit where it’s due.

that library was my ticket to self-education. i carved out an hour or two daily for study, making up for the formal schooling i missed. reading was my only pastime. no bars, no games, no distractions. i worked hard at my printing business. i owed money on it, had a family to support, and faced competition from two established printers. but slowly, my situation improved. frugality helped. my dad always quoted Solomon’s proverb about diligence leading to success. it stuck with me. funny enough, i did end up before kings. five of them. even shared dinner with the King of Denmark.

there's this saying in england: "he that would thrive, must ask his wife." lucky for me, my wife was as industrious and thrifty as i was. she helped with the business, folding pamphlets, running the shop, buying rags for paper. no lazy servants, simple meals, cheap furniture. breakfast was bread and milk in a cheap bowl with a pewter spoon. then one day, breakfast came in a china bowl with a silver spoon. she bought them for 23 shillings, saying i deserved them. first sign of luxury in our house, and as we made more money, that collection grew.

grew up Presbyterian, learned all the usual stuff. some things like eternal decrees and election never made sense to me, and others seemed questionable. so, i stopped going to church meetings early on. Sundays became my study days. but i always held onto some core beliefs. never doubted God exists, made the world, and runs it with Providence. believe doing good is the best way to serve God. souls are immortal, and crimes get punished, virtues get rewarded, here or later. figured these were the basics in all religions, so i respected them all. didn’t matter how they mixed other stuff in, even if it just caused division. kept my mouth shut if it’d mess with someone’s faith. when new places of worship popped up, i chipped in regardless of the sect.

didn't go to services much, but saw their value. paid my dues to support the only Presbyterian minister in Philly. he’d visit sometimes, and try to get me to attend. i went a few times, even five Sundays in a row once. but he wasn’t a great speaker in my book, all polemics and sect doctrines. no moral teachings, just trying to make Presbyterians, not decent folks.

then he preached on a verse from Philippians about thinking on what's true and good. thought, finally, some moral teachings. but nope, he just hit five points: Sabbath, reading Scriptures, attending worship, Sacrament, respecting ministers. fine points, but not what i wanted from that text. got fed up, stopped attending.

years back, made my own prayer book for personal use in 1728. went back to that, skipped public assemblies. maybe i was wrong, but just laying out the facts, not making excuses.

Tried to hit moral perfection once. Thought I could live without slipping up. Figured if I knew right from wrong, I could just stick to doing right. Turns out, tougher than it looked. Guard against one fault, another sneaks in. Habits catch you off guard. Sometimes, what you want overpowers what you know. Realized just knowing what's right wasn't enough. Had to break bad habits, build good ones before I could count on being consistent. So, came up with a plan.

Read a lot on moral virtues. Found everyone's got their own list, some short, some long. Like temperance — some say it's just about eating and drinking, others say it's about controlling all pleasures and desires. Decided to use more names with fewer ideas attached to each, instead of fewer names with more ideas. Settled on thirteen virtues with clear precepts for each.

1. Temperance. Don't eat till you're stuffed; don't drink till you're drunk.
2. Silence. Only say what helps or matters; skip the small talk.
3. Order. Everything in its place; give time to each task.
4. Resolution. Do what you should; follow through on your promises.
5. Frugality. Spend only if it’s for good; don't waste.
6. Industry. Don’t waste time; always do something useful; cut the fluff.
7. Sincerity. No harmful lies; think fair, speak fair.
8. Justice. Don't harm or neglect your duties.
9. Moderation. Avoid extremes; don’t overreact.
10. Cleanliness. Stay clean in body, clothes, and home.
11. Tranquillity. Don’t sweat the small stuff or the unavoidable.
12. Chastity. Sex for health or kids, not for dullness or harm.
13. Humility. Be like Jesus and Socrates.

aim was to get the hang of all these virtues one by one. figured it'd be smarter not to try 'em all at once. start with one, master it, then move on. some virtues make the next ones easier, so i lined 'em up that way. temperance first, keeps your head clear. that's key when old habits and temptations are always lurking. once you nail that, silence gets easier. i wanted to learn and improve, and silence lets you listen more than talk. i was talking too much, making jokes, and hanging with folks that didn't push me. so silence was second. order was next, more time for projects and studies. resolution keeps you on track for the rest. frugality and industry clear debts, build independence, making sincerity and justice easier. thought daily check-ins would help, like pythagoras suggested.

made a small book, gave each virtue a page. red ink lines for seven days a week. marked each day's column and each virtue's line. black dot for every slip-up.

here's how it looked:

focused hard on one virtue a week. first week, avoid any slip in temperance, mark other faults as they come. if i kept temperance line clear, i'd add the next one, and so on. finish in thirteen weeks, four rounds a year. like weeding a garden bed by bed, not all at once. leaves you with a clean slate to see progress, a full book clear of dots after steady work.

book had a motto from addison's cato.

holding onto this belief that some higher power delights in virtue, i figured that if it loves virtue, then it brings happiness. got that from Cicero too, praising philosophy as the guide of life and virtues, saying even a single day lived well beats any sinful eternity. then Solomon mentions wisdom, saying it brings long life and peace. 

thinking of God as the source of wisdom, i decided to ask for help. made a short prayer for this, stuck it to my exam tables for daily use. it went: "O powerful Goodness! bountiful Father! merciful Guide! Increase in me that wisdom which discovers my truest interest. Strengthen my resolutions to perform what that wisdom dictates. Accept my kind offices to thy other children as the only return in my power for thy continual favors to me."

also borrowed a prayer from Thomson's Poems asking to be taught what’s good and to keep me away from foolishness.

following the idea of Order, i set a daily schedule:

MORNING: 
- wake up, wash, plan the day, breakfast.

WORK till noon, then:
- read, check accounts, lunch.

WORK again till evening, then:
- put stuff away, supper, some music or talk. end with the day's review.

NIGHT: sleep.

started this to track myself, did it off and on. surprised at my own faults showing up, but at least they got fewer. tired of redoing the paper book, switched to an ivory one with erasable lines. ran through it yearly, then less often, eventually stopping due to travel and business. always kept it with me though.

order really kicked my butt. thought it might work if you had control over your schedule, like a journeyman printer could. but as a master who deals with folks on their own time, not so much. keeping things in place, papers and all, was tough too. never got the hang of it early on and leaned on my good memory to get by. but that lack of method was a pain. tried fixing it, but man, it was slow going and super frustrating. kept slipping back. almost threw in the towel and decided maybe i'd just be the guy with a messy ax.

there's this story of a guy wanting his ax shiny like the blade. he spun the wheel while the smith ground it, which was exhausting. eventually, he was like, "nah, speckled's fine." that's me with order. tried hard but maybe a few rough edges aren't so bad. thought being too perfect might make me look silly or even get folks jealous. maybe letting a few faults slide helps keep things real with friends.

nowadays, my memory's shot, and i feel the lack of order more. never hit that perfect mark i wanted, but aiming for it made me better off than if i'd done nothing. like trying to write perfectly by copying engraved examples—might not nail it, but my writing's better and still readable.

if you want the truth, my long run of good years came from a mix of habits. kept me steady, helped me get here. temperance kept me healthy, industry and frugality gave me a leg up financially and taught me how to be useful. sincerity and justice earned me trust and good jobs. the virtues, even half-baked, kept me balanced and pleasant to be around, even with younger folks. hope my descendants catch on to this.

now, my plan wasn’t all about religion, though it had shades of it. i dodged specifics from any sect because i knew my method was solid and could help anyone, regardless of belief. wanted to write about each virtue, show the upsides and the pitfalls of their vices. planned to call it "The Art of Virtue" — more than just a pep talk, it’d lay out the how-to, not just tell you to be good.

never got to finish that book. had notes, ideas ready to go, but life pulled me in other directions. private work, then public duties kept me busy. the whole thing needed full focus, which i couldn't give.

the main idea was this: bad actions hurt us not because they're banned, but because they're harmful by nature. being virtuous is in your best interest if you want to be happy here and now. there's always a need for honest people in high places, but they're rare. i wanted to show young folks that honesty and integrity are the real keys to success.

started with twelve virtues, then a Quaker buddy pointed out my pride. said it showed in convo, and i was pushy, maybe rude. he gave examples. so i figured i’d tackle it too, added Humility to the list, broad meaning.

didn't nail the essence, but got the look of it down. stopped outright contradicting folks and claiming my own opinions as gospel. swapped ‘certainly’ for ‘i think’ or ‘seems to me’. if i thought someone was wrong, i’d start by saying they might be right in certain situations. made chats smoother. opinions got better reception, less pushback. when wrong, less embarrassment; when right, folks more likely joined me.

over time, what felt forced became second nature. for fifty years, haven’t been dogmatic. think that habit, plus integrity, gave me sway in civic matters despite being a lousy speaker. pride’s tough. you can try to hide or crush it, but it sneaks back. maybe you’ll spot it here. even defeating it would make me proud of my humility.

now writing at home in 1788, lost many papers in the war. but found some. had a big idea, worth sharing. started in 1731.

from library notes: big world events—wars, revolutions—driven by parties. each party follows what they see as their interest. these different views cause chaos. even while pursuing a shared goal, individuals chase their own interests.

every time a group wins its big goal, folks in it start chasing their own stuff. they butt heads, and soon you got splits and chaos.

most people in politics don't just aim for the country's good, even if they say they do. their actions might help the country, but they're thinking their own and their country's interests line up. it's not really about being kind.

even fewer think about the good of all people.

right now, i'm seeing a big need for a United Party for Virtue. get all the good folks across nations into one group, follow some solid rules. wise folks might stick to those more than regular laws.

whoever does this right, with the right skills, will please God and succeed.

i've been mulling this over, planning to dive in once i've got the time. jotted thoughts on paper scraps, most lost now. found one draft creed, covering essentials of every faith without offending any. it says:

one God made everything. he runs the world with providence. deserves worship through adoration, prayer, thanks. best service is doing good to others. souls are immortal. God rewards virtue, punishes vice, here or later.

back then, i thought the group should start with young, single men. they'd agree to the creed, do the thirteen-week virtues drill. keep it secret until it's big enough to avoid bad recruits. members would find good young folks to slowly share the plan with. support each other in careers, call it The Society of the Free and Easy. free from vice by living virtues, free from debt by being industrious and frugal. debt’s a trap, like a slavery to creditors.

i can remember bits of the project now. told it to a couple young guys who got into it. but back then, money was tight and i had to keep my nose to the grindstone, so couldn't chase it further. got wrapped up in so many things, kept putting it off. now, im out of gas for it, but still think it could've done good—made solid citizens out of folks. never worried about the size of the job; always figured if you got a decent plan and cut out distractions, you can move mountains.

in 1732, kicked off my Almanack under Richard Saunders. ran it for about 25 years—people knew it as Poor Richard's Almanac. tried to make it fun and useful. got real popular, sold nearly ten thousand copies a year. saw it was read everywhere, so used it to teach folks—most didn't buy other books. squeezed in proverbs about working hard and saving money in the extra spaces. thought it’d help folks since it's tough to stay honest when you're broke. like they say, an empty sack can't stand upright.

those proverbs, packed with old wisdom, i pieced together in a speech for the 1757 Almanack. old wise man at an auction, preaching to the crowd. all those little bits focused together made a bigger impact. folks loved it, newspapers ran it everywhere, even got translated into French. clergy and gentry bought loads to give away. in Pennsylvania, some folks thought it helped the economy, cutting back on wasteful spending on foreign stuff.

my newspaper was another way to teach. reprinted stuff from the Spectator and moral writers, and sometimes my own pieces. wrote a Socratic dialogue proving a bad man can't be truly wise, and a piece on self-denial—virtue only sticks when it's a habit. you can find those in the papers around 1735.

running a newspaper, i made sure to keep out all the libel and personal attacks that have been staining our country lately. when folks tried to convince me to print that junk, arguing freedom of the press and comparing it to a stagecoach where anyone can have a ride if they pay, i'd tell them i'd print it separately if they wanted, and they could distribute it themselves. but i wasn't about to spread their trash. i promised my subscribers content that was useful or entertaining, not personal fights they had no stake in. it's unjust to them. a lot of printers now don't hesitate to fuel personal vendettas with false accusations against good people, even pushing folks to duels. even worse, they print nasty stuff about neighboring governments and our allies, which can have awful consequences. young printers should steer clear of these dirty practices. my approach shows it won't harm their success in the long run.

back in 1733, i sent one of my guys to Charleston, South Carolina, to fill a printer's spot. set him up with a press and type on a partnership deal. i'd get a third of the profits and cover a third of the expenses. he was smart and honest but clueless with numbers. he sent me money sometimes but never a proper account of our partnership while he was alive. after he passed, his widow, who was Dutch and knew her accounting, sent me a clear report of past dealings. she kept perfect records every quarter and ran the business well. she raised her kids respectably and ended up buying the printing house from me, setting her son up in it.

i share this to highlight how useful accounting knowledge is for young women. it's more practical than music or dancing, especially if widowed. it protects against deceit and helps them keep a profitable business going until a son can take over, benefiting the family long term.

around 1734, a young preacher named hemphill came over from ireland. he was presbyterian, good voice, and his sermons pulled in folks from all over, different beliefs and all. i got hooked too. his talks weren’t all fire and brimstone, more on living right and doing good. but some in our church didn’t like it, said he wasn’t sticking to the script. old clergy got involved, wanted him out. i backed him, wrote some pamphlets, even a piece in the gazette. tried to rally support, but it didn’t last. turns out one of his admired sermons wasn’t his own. someone caught on, found it in a british review. that hit hard, folks bailed on him, and we lost in the synod. even after, i stuck with him. i’d rather hear good borrowed sermons than bad originals. he admitted later none were his, but he could memorize them after one read. eventually, he left, and i stopped going to that church, though i kept up my support for its ministers.

in 1733, i got into studying languages. picked up french, moved to italian. had a buddy learning too, we played chess but it ate up study time. so we made it a game—winner sets a language task. we beat each other into italian that way. tackled spanish next, could read their books after a bit. i’d had one year of latin as a kid, then dropped it. once i got into french, italian, and spanish, i looked back at latin and found i understood more than i thought. gave it another go, and those languages made latin a lot easier.

been thinking about how we teach languages, and there's a weird inconsistency. they say start with Latin first 'cause it's supposed to make modern languages easier. but why not start with Greek if that's the logic? it's like saying you should jump to the top of the staircase to make it easier to go down, but starting from the bottom step seems smarter. maybe we ought to rethink this — like, what if we started with French and Italian instead? even if they never get to Latin, they'd at least pick up some modern languages we actually use.

came back to Boston after ten years, finally had the cash to do it. stopped by Newport to see my brother, he's got a printing shop there. we patched things up, real friendly. he wasn't doing well health-wise, asked me to look after his son if anything happened. did just that, put the kid through school, then brought him into printing. helped him with new types when he was ready since the old ones were shot. felt like i made it up to my brother for leaving early.

lost my son to smallpox in '36. he was only four. regret not getting him inoculated. hard lesson. for parents out there, don't skip it thinking you'll blame yourself if it goes wrong. trust me, the regret cuts just as deep if you don't. pick the safer path.

our club, the Junto, was pretty tight-knit, and we liked how it worked. some folks wanted to bring in their friends, but we capped it at twelve to keep things manageable. we kept it under wraps to dodge unwanted members, which mostly worked. i didn't want to expand, so i proposed an idea: each member starts a side club with similar rules without spilling the beans about the Junto. this way, we spread our influence and stayed connected to what's happening around us. it also helped us push our interests and gain more sway in public matters.

the plan got the green light, and everyone tried to set up their own groups. about five or six clubs got off the ground with names like the Vine, the Union, the Band. they were helpful and gave us insights and fun, and helped shape public opinions when needed. i'll share some stories about that later.

in 1736, i got a boost by being elected clerk of the General Assembly. no competition that year, but the next year, a new guy tried to block me. still got the job, which was sweet since it kept me in the loop and brought in work like printing votes and laws — good money there.

wasn't thrilled about the new guy's opposition. he was smart and well-off, likely to gain influence. didn't suck up to him, though. instead, i heard he had a rare book, so i asked to borrow it. he lent it, i returned it with thanks, and next thing, he's all friendly. we got along great after that. proves the point: someone who helps you once is more likely to help again than someone you've helped. better to smooth things over than hold a grudge.

so back in 1737, colonel spotswood, who used to be the governor of virginia and was then postmaster-general, wasn't too happy with his deputy in philly. guy was being sloppy with his accounts. spotswood yanks the commission and hands it to me. i jumped on it. salary wasn't much, but it made it easier to get my newspaper going. more readers, more ads, more money. my old rival’s paper? went downhill. he wouldn’t let my papers ride when he was in charge, so it was kinda fitting. when you're managing stuff for someone, keep your accounts clear and pay up on time. reputation for that? opens doors.

started thinking about public stuff, small steps at first. city watch was a mess. constables ran it, had housekeepers take turns or pay six shillings to skip. too much money, and constables made a profit. they'd hire lousy substitutes, and watch duty was more like a pub crawl. i wrote about it for the junto, highlighting how unfair the six-shilling tax was. poor widow paid the same as rich merchants. proposed hiring real watchmen and a tax based on property value. junto liked it, shared it around, planted the seed for change. law came later once we had more pull.

around then, i wrote about house fires, how they start and how to prevent them. folks said it was useful. led to starting a fire company for quick response and helping each other out. thirty of us joined up. everyone kept leather buckets, bags, and baskets ready. monthly meetings to talk fire safety and share ideas. good times, good purpose.

The utility of starting this fire company became clear real quick. More folks wanted in than we thought was good for one group, so we told them to start another. They did, and next thing you know, companies were popping up everywhere, mostly made of men with some property. Now, even fifty years later, the first one we formed, Union Fire Company, is still going strong, even though most of the original guys are gone except me and one other, who's a year older. Members paid small fines for missing monthly meetings, and that cash went into getting fire-engines, ladders, fire-hooks, and other gear. I doubt there's any city better prepped to stop fires early. Since we set this up, we've never lost more than one or two houses at a time, and often, the fire's out before the first house is half gone.

In 1739, Reverend Mr. Whitefield showed up from Ireland, known for preaching everywhere. He got to preach in some churches at first, but the clergy didn't like him and shut him out, so he took to the fields. Huge crowds from all backgrounds came to hear him, and it was wild to see his effect on them. Even though he called them half beasts and devils, they admired him. Suddenly, everyone seemed to get religious. Walking through town at night, you could hear psalms from different houses on every street.

Preaching outside had its problems, so when someone suggested building a meeting house, folks donated enough money to buy land and get it built fast. It was about the size of Westminster Hall. The place was for any preacher of any faith, not just one group, so even if the Mufti of Constantinople sent someone to teach Islam, they'd find a pulpit waiting.

Whitefield headed south, preaching all the way to Georgia. That province was just starting but was filled with folks from jails, not the hard workers needed for new settlements. Many couldn't handle it and died, leaving orphans. Whitefield saw this and wanted to build an Orphan House to help. Coming back north, he preached about this charity and raised a lot of money. His words could move people to open their hearts and wallets, me included.

didn't disapprove of the design, but Georgia was out of materials and workmen. The plan was to send them from Philly, which would've been pricey. Thought it'd be better to build the house here and bring the kids over. Suggested that, but he was set on his plan, ignored my advice, so I wouldn't chip in. Went to one of his sermons later. Could see he was aiming to collect, and I decided he wouldn't get a dime from me. Had some copper coins, silver dollars, and five gold pistoles. As he preached, I softened, figured I'd give the coppers. Then he hit me with another bit of oratory, felt guilty, switched to silver. He closed so well, I dumped my whole pocket into the dish, gold and all. One of our club was there too, thought the same about the Georgia build, and emptied his pockets before coming, suspecting a collection. But he got moved too, tried to borrow money from a neighbor, who was probably the only guy not moved by the sermon. Neighbor told him, "Any other time, Friend Hopkinson, I'd lend you, but not now. You seem out of your right senses."

some folks thought Whitefield might pocket the collections. i printed his sermons and journals, knew him well, never doubted his honesty. i’m sure he was straight-up honest, even if we didn't share religion. he'd pray for my conversion sometimes, but didn't think it worked. we were just civil friends, genuine, till he passed.

one time, he came from England to Boston, wrote me he'd be in Philly soon but needed a place to stay since his old host moved. told him, "you know my house; it's not fancy, but you're welcome." he said if i offered for Christ's sake, i'd get a reward. replied, "not for Christ's sake, but for yours." a mutual friend joked since saints usually shift the obligation heavenward, i'd pinned it right here on earth.

last time i saw Whitefield was in London, talked about his Orphan House and turning it into a college.

this guy's voice carried like nobody's business. you'd stand way back and still catch every word because folks listened like they were in a library. one night, he stood on the courthouse steps, middle of Market street, right at Second street crossing. crowd filled up both streets. i hung back to see how far his voice went, kept walking towards the river. still clear until Front street noise got in the way. figured if you made a semicircle from where I stopped, his voice hit over thirty thousand people easy. made those stories about him preaching to thousands feel real. generals rallying troops? maybe not so far-fetched.

heard him a lot, and started picking up on his patterns. fresh sermons had a different vibe compared to the ones he'd practiced a bunch. the repeats were smooth, like he was playing an instrument. didn't even matter if you cared about the topic, the delivery was just solid, like listening to good music. traveling preachers get that edge with all the practice they get, unlike the guys who stay put.

writing down his thoughts didn't do him any favors. folks could twist his words or call him out on mistakes, and it hurt his following. left unwritten, people might've just imagined the best about him, growing his legend way beyond anything he actually said.

meanwhile, my work kept picking up. newspaper was raking it in, almost the only one around for miles. figured out real quick that once you make the first bit of cash, the rest comes easier. money seems to make more of itself.

carolina gig went well, so i rolled out similar setups in other colonies. promoted some of my guys who did good, gave 'em their own print shops. six years later, they bought the gear off me and ran solo. most partnerships end ugly, but mine stayed smooth. clear contracts helped—spelled out everything, left no room for beef. good tip if you're teaming up; even buddies can end up at odds over who's pulling more weight.

being in pennsylvania worked out for me, though i had two regrets: no defense setup, no real education system—no militia, no college. in 1743, i drew up plans for an academy. thought mr. peters would fit as head, but he had better offers and passed. shelved it for a bit. in '44, got a philosophical society going instead. wrote a piece on it; you’ll find it in my collected works.

about defense, with spain and france ganging up on britain, we were in a tight spot. governor thomas tried hard to get a militia law through our quaker assembly—no luck. so i pushed for a voluntary group. wrote a pamphlet, plain truth, laying out how exposed we were, pushed for unity. it hit hard. folks wanted action, so i drafted an association paper. called a meeting, packed house, handed out copies, got signatures—over 1200 that night, eventually more than 10,000.

everyone geared up, formed units, picked officers, and trained weekly. women chipped in, made silk flags with designs and mottos i provided. whole community pulled together.

the officers of the philadelphia regiment picked me for colonel, but i didn't feel right for it. instead, i suggested lawrence, a solid guy with influence. he got the spot. then i set up a lottery to fund a battery below town. filled up quick, and we built it with logs and earth. got some old cannons from boston, but not enough, so we wrote to england, asking for more help, knowing we'd probably get nothing.

lawrence, allen, taylor, and i went to new york to ask governor clinton for more cannons. first, he straight-up refused. but after some madeira wine at dinner, he warmed up. from six cannons, he went to ten, then eighteen. they were solid eighteen-pounders. we moved and set them up on our battery, where we all took turns standing guard.

the governor and council liked my hustle and looped me in on plans. i suggested a fast day for reformation and luck, and they agreed. no precedent here, but my new england background helped. i drafted it, got it translated to german, and spread it around. clergy got involved, and it caught on, except the Quakers, but then peace hit.

some friends thought id upset the Quakers and lose my assembly spot. a young guy tipped me off, said i might be replaced, and suggested i resign. told him about the rule i heard: never ask, never refuse an office. added my twist: i won't resign either. if they want it, they'll have to take it. never heard more about it; got re-elected unanimously. maybe they wanted me gone quietly, but didn't dare over my support for the association.

had a hunch about the Quakers. they didn't mind the idea of defending the country as long as they didn't have to get involved directly. turns out, more of them were into the idea of defense than i thought, even published pamphlets supporting it. younger folks seemed convinced.

one time, at our fire company meeting, we talked about using our funds to buy lottery tickets to help build a battery. we needed a majority vote, but 22 out of 30 members were Quakers. only eight of us were non-Quakers, so we weren't sure about the outcome. mr. james morris, a Quaker, opposed it, worried it might split the group. we assured him we'd respect the vote, but he wanted to wait for more opponents to show.

then, while we were discussing, two Quaker members pulled me aside. they had eight of them ready to back us if needed but hoped it wouldn't come to that to avoid trouble with their community. knowing we had the numbers now, i agreed to delay the vote an hour. mr. morris thought that was fair, but none of his allies showed. we won the vote eight to one. out of 22 Quakers, eight were with us, and 13 didn't oppose by not showing up. figured only about one in 21 were truly against defense. these were all respected members who knew the plan.

logan was always one for defensive war, argued it hard. put sixty pounds in my hands for lottery tickets for the battery, said any winnings go back into it. shared a story about william penn. they were on a ship during war, captain readied for a fight, told the quakers to stay in the cabin. logan stayed on deck, ready at a gun. turned out the ship was friendly, no fight. penn wasn’t happy, scolded logan for going against quaker principles. logan snapped back, said if he wasn’t wanted on deck, why wasn’t he ordered below?

being in the assembly for years, with a lot of quakers, i saw the mess they got into because of their no-war stance. when the crown asked for military funds, they didn’t want to anger the government or go against their beliefs. so they got creative. gave money "for the king’s use," never asking where it went. but when it wasn’t a direct crown request, they had to be clever. like when new england wanted powder, they granted three thousand pounds for "bread, flour, wheat, or other grain." governor thomas caught on, said "other grain is gunpowder," bought it, no objections.

that’s why, when we worried about the lottery for our fire company, i told syng, "if we fail, let’s buy a fire-engine. quakers can’t argue with that. nominate each other, we’ll get a big gun. fire-engine, right?" syng laughed, said i’d learned from the assembly. our plan matched their "wheat or other grain" trick.

The Quakers got themselves in a bind with their no-war stance. Once you say something that loud, it's hard to backtrack. Reminds me of the Dunkers. I knew one of the founders, Michael Welfare. He griped about how other sects painted them in the worst light, accusing them of all sorts of nonsense. I told him, hey, that's the deal with new groups. Maybe lay out your beliefs in writing to clear things up? But he said they'd considered it and decided against it. They'd realized some things they thought were true weren't, and some errors were actually truths. They didn't want to lock themselves into anything, figuring they'd keep learning and evolving. Printing it might make them and those who come after too rigid, thinking the founders' words were gospel.

That's rare, a sect that knows it doesn't know everything. Most think they've got the truth locked down, everyone else is wrong. Like when you're walking in fog, you see folks ahead and behind wrapped in it, but right where you are seems clear. But you're just as fogged up. Quakers, to dodge these traps, have been stepping back from public roles, keeping their principles intact.

Now, back in 1742, I came up with an open stove design. Kept rooms warm, saved fuel. Gave the model to my buddy Robert Grace, who had an iron-furnace. It took off. I wrote a pamphlet explaining the stove's perks, knocked down any objections. Gov. Thomas liked it so much he offered me a patent, but I turned it down. I figure if we benefit from others' ideas, we should share ours too, without strings.

some guy in london snagged my pamphlet, tweaked the machine a bit (worse, actually), and got a patent. made a small fortune, they say. not the first time someone patented my stuff, but i didnt bother fighting it. i dont care for patents or the hassle. those fireplaces i made save folks a ton of wood around here.

once peace hit, i switched gears to start an academy. first move: rounded up some friends, mostly from the Junto. wrote up a pamphlet, "Proposals Relating to the Education of Youth in Pennsylvania," and handed it out for free. after folks had a read, started a subscription to fund the academy. five-year payments, thought it'd boost the amount, and it did — hit about five thousand pounds.

in that pamphlet, i made it seem like the idea came from some public-minded gents, not me. never liked putting my name front and center.

subscribers picked 24 trustees, got Mr. Francis and me to draft the academy's rules. found a spot, hired teachers, opened by 1749.

students came fast, so the place got cramped. we needed land to build, then luck hit. found a ready-made big house, just needed tweaks. it was built by Mr. Whitefield's crowd. 

contributors were from different sects, so we made trustees one from each group to keep things fair. no sect dominance. but the Moravian trustee didn’t gel with the others. when he passed, they didn’t want another. problem was how to avoid doubling up on another sect.

they tossed around names, but couldn’t agree. finally, someone said “what about him? honest guy, not tied to any sect,” and that sealed it. they picked me. the buzz from when the house was built had faded, and the trustees couldn’t scrape together enough to pay the rent or cover other debts. being on both trustee boards now, i had a chance to work it out. got them to a deal: building trustees handed the place over to the academy trustees. academy folks agreed to clear the debt, keep a big hall open for guest preachers like they originally planned, and run a free school for poor kids. we got the papers done, paid off the debts, and the academy took over. we split up the big hall into stories and rooms, added some land, and it all fit our needs. moved the students in. i handled the deals with workmen, bought materials, oversaw the work. didn’t clash with my business, since i’d teamed up with david hall, a solid partner who ran the print shop and paid me my cut. eighteen-year partnership, worked out well for both of us.

later, the academy trustees got a charter from the governor. boosted funds from britain and land grants, with the assembly chipping in too. that’s how the university of philadelphia started. been a trustee since day one, almost forty years now. watched a lot of students come through, do well, serve the public, make the country proud.

when i stepped back from private business, i thought i’d have time for study and fun. got dr. spence’s gear, dove into electrical experiments. but with my new free time, folks kept pulling me into public roles. governor made me a peace commissioner, city put me on the council, then alderman, and finally, a burgess in the assembly. liked that last role, tired of just listening to debates as a clerk. started doing magic squares to pass the time. joining as a member let me actually do some good. not gonna lie, all these positions boosted my ego, especially considering where i started. it felt good being recognized without asking for it.

tried my hand at being justice of the peace for a bit. Sat in on a few courts, got on the bench. But honestly, needed more common law knowledge than I had to do it right. So I backed off, said I had to focus on my legislative duties in the Assembly. They kept electing me each year for ten years. Never asked anyone for a vote. My son got the clerk gig when I joined the House.

Next year, there was a treaty at Carlisle with the Indians. Governor wanted us to send some members as commissioners. House picked the speaker, Mr. Norris, and me. So off we went to Carlisle to meet the Indians.

Indians tend to get drunk, and when they do, it's chaos. So we banned liquor sales to them. They complained, but we promised rum after the treaty if they stayed sober. They did, cause they had no choice, and the treaty went smooth. Afterward, they got their rum. Come evening, they were all drunk, fighting, yelling, around a big bonfire. Looked like hell on earth. We just left them to it. At midnight, they banged on our door for more rum. Ignored it.

Next day, they sent three old counselors to apologize. Orator blamed the rum, said the Great Spirit made rum for them to get drunk. Thought maybe rum's how Providence plans to replace them with farmers. It's wiped out coastal tribes already.

In 1751, my friend Dr. Thomas Bond came up with this hospital idea in Philadelphia. People think it was my idea, but it was his. Wanted to help sick folks, locals or strangers. He pushed hard for funding, but it was new for America, so he struggled at first.

this dude came up to me, saying no public project got anywhere unless i was involved. he said folks always asked if id weighed in before theyd commit. so i asked about his idea, liked what i heard, and jumped in. started by writing about it in the papers to get folks warmed up, which he shoulda done first.

subscriptions came in, but started slowing. figured wed need some help from the Assembly. proposed a petition. some country members thought it only helped the city and doubted wed raise money. i said wed get two thousand pounds easy, but they laughed that off.

so, i drafted a bill to incorporate contributors and add a condition. it said if we raised enough money for the hospital, the Assembly had to chip in two thousand pounds too. they liked that theyd look good without spending, so the bill passed. told folks their donations would be doubled by the law, and money flowed in.

we hit our goal, got the public funds, and built a nice hospital. it worked out great, and looking back, i dont mind using a bit of strategy to get it done.

So, Rev. Gilbert Tennent came to me needing help to drum up subscriptions for a new meeting-house. It was for his Presbyterian crowd, who originally followed Mr. Whitefield. I really didn’t want to bug folks too often for donations, so I flat-out said no. Then he asked me for names of generous folks, but I didn't think it was right to send them more beggars after they'd already helped out before, so that was a no too. Finally, he asked for advice. I said, "Sure, go ask those you know will give, then try those you aren’t sure about and show them who’s already contributed. And don’t skip the ones you think won’t give anything. You might be surprised." He laughed, thanked me, and followed my advice. He asked everyone and got more funds than expected, enough to build that big meeting-house on Arch Street.

Now, our city had nice, wide streets laid out in a neat grid, but they stayed unpaved way too long. When it rained, they turned into mud pits, tough to cross. Dry days meant dust everywhere. I lived near the Jersey Market and hated seeing folks slog through mud just to buy their groceries. Eventually, they bricked a path down the market’s middle, but getting there was still a mess. I talked and wrote about it until I got the street paved with stone between the market and the walkways. It made things better for a bit, but without paving the whole street, mud from carriages ended up on the pavement, turning it back into a mess since the city had no street cleaners yet.

After looking around, I found a hardworking guy willing to keep the pavement clean, sweeping twice a week, and hauling off dirt for sixpence a month per house. I wrote up a paper explaining how this little cost would keep our homes cleaner and bring more business to the shops, with no dust on goods, and sent it to each house. I then went around to get folks to sign up, and they all did. Everyone loved how clean the pavement around the market became, which got folks eager to pave all the streets and more willing to pay a tax for it.

drew up a plan for paving the city, just before heading to England in 1757. it passed after i left, with changes i didn’t love, but they added street lighting, which was a big win. all kicked off by Mr. John Clifton. he put a lamp outside his place that got folks thinking about lighting the whole city. folks credit me, but really, it’s his idea. i just tweaked the lamps. london sent us globe lamps that smoked up, got dirty, and broke easy. figured out a better design with flat panes and a funnel to keep smoke out. stayed bright till morning and easier to fix.

wondered why london didn’t copy the Vauxhall lamps with holes for airflow. those holes were for something else, to light the wick faster, so nobody thought about airflow. so, london streets stayed dim after a few hours.

had another idea in london, shared it with Dr. Fothergill, a man who loved useful projects. noticed streets weren’t swept when dry, dust piled up till rain turned it to mud, then they’d rake it into open carts, spilling slush everywhere. reason for not sweeping dry streets? dust in windows.

Found out something interesting about cleaning the street. One morning in Craven-street, saw a pale woman sweeping my pavement. Just out of sickness, she said she was sweeping for a bit of charity. Told her if she swept the whole street, I'd give her a shilling. It was 9 a.m. She came back at noon. Didn't think she could've done it, so sent my guy to check. Turns out, she'd swept it all clean, dust right down the middle gutter. Rain came, washed it away, street was spotless.

Realized if she could do it, a strong guy could do it even quicker. Noticed how having one gutter in the middle of a narrow street works better than two on the sides. Water gathers, washes everything down, instead of making mud that carts and horses just splash around.

Told the doc my idea: hire watchmen to keep streets clean. Give 'em brooms and tools, let them hire folks to help. In summer, sweep dust into heaps before shops open, then scavengers take it. For mud, use low carts with lattice bottoms and straw. Let water drain, makes mud lighter and easier to move. Simple plan, better streets.

lately been second-guessing the last part of my idea, mainly 'cause some streets are just too narrow and those draining-sleds would crowd things up. but i still think the first part's solid. getting the dust swept up and out before shops open is doable, especially in summer with longer days. walked through the Strand and Fleet-street at 7 AM and not a single shop was open. even though the sun's been up for hours, folks in London seem to prefer sleeping through daylight and burning candles, and then they gripe about candle taxes and tallow prices. 

some say this stuff's trivial, but think about it: a bit of dust in your eye or a shop might seem small, but multiply that by a busy city and it adds up. happiness doesn't come from rare windfalls, but from everyday wins. like teaching a young guy to shave himself. that can make him happier than a thousand guineas, 'cause money's gone quick but a good shave's a daily win. with that thought, i penned these pages, hoping they might help a city i love or maybe some towns back home in america.

worked as comptroller for the postmaster-general in america, then got the gig with Mr. William Hunter after the previous guy passed in 1753. our job? make the office pay. first few years, we were out 900 pounds, but eventually it paid off, turning more revenue than ireland's postoffice. then some minister's whim displaced me, and since then, they've gotten zilch from it.

the postoffice business took me to New England this year, where Cambridge College gave me a Master of Arts. yale did the same earlier. no college study, but got honors for my work in electricity.

back in 1754, with war against france looming, a bunch of us commissioners from the different colonies got together in albany. the lords of trade ordered it to chat with the chiefs of the six nations about defending our lands. governor hamilton told the house about it and asked them to get some gifts for the indians. he pointed at mr. norris and me to join mr. thomas penn and mr. secretary peters as pennsylvania's commissioners. the house was cool with it and got the presents, even if they weren’t thrilled about working outside the provinces. we met up in albany around mid-june. 

on the way, i sketched out a plan to unite the colonies under one government, just enough for defense and the important stuff. in new york, i showed it to mr. james alexander and mr. kennedy, who knew their stuff in public affairs, and they liked it. that gave me enough confidence to bring it to the congress. turns out, others had similar ideas. first question was if a union should happen, and everyone agreed. a committee with one member from each colony looked at the plans. mine got picked, with a few tweaks, and that’s what they went with.

the plan had a president-general, picked and backed by the crown, and a grand council chosen by the colony reps. lots of debate, but we finally agreed and sent it off to the board of trade and the colonies' assemblies. but the assemblies thought it gave too much power to the crown, while england thought it was too democratic. so, the board didn’t push it to the king. they came up with another plan where the governors and some council members would handle troops and forts, and charge it to britain, who’d tax america later to pay back.

that winter, in boston, i talked a lot with governor shirley about both plans. you can find some of those chats in my papers. the mixed reactions to my plan make me think it was actually the right middle ground. if we’d adopted it, the colonies could’ve defended themselves, no need for english troops, and the whole tax mess and the bloody fight that followed might've been avoided. but these mistakes aren’t new; history’s full of them. 

look around, how few know their own good or chase it. those in charge, swamped with tasks, don’t usually like new ideas until they have no choice. best plans often come from necessity, not foresight.

the governor of pennsylvania sent the plan to the assembly with high praise, said it was clear and well-judged. he wanted them to give it serious thought. but while i was away, a certain member got them to reject it without even looking at it. that was a blow.

on my way to boston, i ran into our new governor, mr. morris, in new york. we'd known each other before. he'd just replaced mr. hamilton, who quit because he'd had enough of the proprietary mess. morris asked if he should brace for the same headaches. i told him, "you'll do fine if you steer clear of fights with the assembly." he laughed, said he loved a good dispute but promised to try avoiding them. he was good at arguing, but i've seen folks like that win the argument but lose friends. we parted ways, him to philly, me to boston.

on my return, i caught wind that morris and the assembly were already at it. i got back and found myself knee-deep in drafting replies to his speeches. we exchanged sharp words, but no personal beef came from it. he'd still invite me over, even when things were heated.

one day, amid the turmoil, he pulled me aside in the street, invited me over, promising good company. over wine, he joked about sancho panza wanting a government of blacks so he could sell them if they disagreed. a friend nudged me, saying i should sell the quakers i sided with. i shot back, "the governor hasn't blackened them enough yet." he tried to paint the assembly in a bad light, but they always turned it back on him. eventually, like hamilton, he got fed up and stepped down.

these fights all came down to the proprietaries, the bosses who ran things by inheritance. they were real cheap, always telling their deputies not to pass any tax laws unless their huge estates got a free pass. they even had these deputies sign agreements to follow those orders. the Assemblies fought this for three years but eventually had to give in. then Captain Denny, who took over after Governor Morris, decided to ignore those orders. more on that later.

jumped ahead there. need to cover some stuff from Morris's time.

war was heating up with France, and Massachusetts Bay wanted to hit Crown Point. they sent Mr. Quincy to Pennsylvania and Mr. Pownall to New York to ask for help. since i was in the Assembly and knew the folks, Quincy asked me for help. i drafted his speech, and it went over well. they agreed to send ten thousand pounds for supplies. but the governor wouldn't sign off unless the proprietaries' land was tax-free. the Assembly wanted to help New England but was stuck.

i came up with a workaround. suggested using orders on the Loan Office trustees, which the Assembly could legally write. there wasn't much money in the office then, so i proposed the orders be payable in a year with five percent interest. figured we could buy the supplies this way. the Assembly went for it. orders got printed, and i signed and handled them. the plan worked because the orders were backed by more than enough funds from paper currency interest and excise revenue. folks trusted them and even invested their cash in these orders since they earned interest and could be used like cash. they got snapped up quick. Quincy thanked the Assembly in a nice note, went home happy, and we stayed good friends.

british government wasn't keen on letting the colonies get too chummy at Albany. didn't want them feeling all military and strong. so they sent General Braddock with some regiments to keep things in line. he landed in Alexandria, Virginia, then moved to Frederictown, Maryland, waiting for carriages. our Assembly thought he might have it in for them, so they sent me, under the guise of postmaster-general, to smooth things over.

found him in Frederictown, pacing around, waiting on wagons from Maryland and Virginia. hung out with him for a few days, cleared up some of his misconceptions about what the Assembly was doing to help. when it was time to leave, we got the wagon count—only twenty-five and some barely holding together. everyone was shocked, saying the expedition was doomed without at least one hundred and fifty.

mentioned offhand that Pennsylvania would've been a better spot since farmers there all have wagons. Braddock jumped on that, asked me to arrange some. wrote up terms for the wagon owners, they agreed, and I got a commission to sort it out. advertised in Lancaster—details in the ad show how quickly things moved after that.

need 150 wagons, each with four horses, and 1500 saddle or pack horses for his majesty's forces at Will's Creek. General Braddock gave me the go-ahead to contract for them. i'll be in Lancaster from now until Wednesday evening, then in York from Thursday morning till Friday evening. ready to make deals for wagons and teams or single horses. here's the deal:

1. fifteen shillings a day for a wagon with four horses and a driver. each horse with a pack-saddle or saddle gets two shillings a day, and eighteen pence for one without a saddle.
2. pay starts when they join the forces at Will's Creek by May 20th. reasonable travel pay for getting there and back.
3. wagons, teams, and horses valued by neutral folks picked by me and the owner. if there's a loss, we pay the agreed value.
4. i'll advance seven days' pay when contracting. the rest gets paid by Braddock or army paymaster when discharged.
5. drivers and horse handlers aren't soldiers; their job is just the carriages or horses.
6. extra oats or forage brought to camp is for the army, and we'll pay fairly.

note: my son William Franklin can make similar contracts in Cumberland county.

to the folks of Lancaster, York, and Cumberland,

i was at the camp in Frederic recently. general and officers are ticked off because they expected horses and carriages from this province, but politics here blocked the funds. they wanted to send troops to seize what they need and force folks to drive and care for them. i figured british soldiers marching through would cause a lot of trouble, so i thought fair contracts would be better. you guys have said you need more currency; here's a shot at a big sum. if this expedition runs for 120 days, hiring these wagons and horses could bring in over thirty thousand pounds, paid in silver and gold.

the essentials. 

service is straightforward. army's not gonna move past twelve miles a day. waggons and horses, carrying crucial stuff, gotta stay with the army, no faster. they’re always placed securely, whether we’re on the move or in camp.

if you're truly loyal to the king, you can do a good service here and make it easier on yourselves. don’t have enough for a full setup on your own? team up. one provides the waggon, another some horses, someone else the driver, and split the pay. but if you pass on this offer, your loyalty's gonna be questioned. king's business can't wait. troops are here for your defense, and they can’t be idle because of a lack of waggons. if you don't step up, the army will take what it needs, and you’ll have to figure out the rest yourself.

i’m not personally gaining from this, just trying to help. if this plan doesn't pan out, i’ve gotta inform the general in two weeks. then Sir John St. Clair and soldiers might step in, which i'd hate to see happen, because i’m genuinely looking out for you.

got about eight hundred pounds from the general for advance money for waggon owners. wasn’t enough, so i put in over two hundred pounds more. in two weeks, we had 150 waggons and 259 horses on the way to camp. promised owners they'd be paid for any losses. they didn’t know General Braddock, so they wanted my bond on it, which i gave.

while at camp, Colonel Dunbar mentioned the struggles of his subalterns. they couldn’t afford supplies in this expensive area. decided i’d help them out. wrote to the Assembly committee, suggesting they send supplies. my son, who knows camp life, made a list. committee approved, and my son got the supplies to camp with the waggons. twenty parcels in all, each packed with what was needed.

6 lbs loaf sugar, 6 lbs good Muscovado sugar, 1 lb good green tea, 1 lb good bohea tea, 6 lbs good ground coffee, 6 lbs chocolate, 1-2 cwt best white biscuit, 1-2 lb pepper, 1 quart best white wine vinegar, 1 Gloucester cheese, 1 keg with 20 lbs good butter, 2 dozen old Madeira wine, 2 gallons Jamaica spirits, 1 bottle flour of mustard, 2 well-cured hams, 1-2 dozen dried tongues, 6 lbs rice, 6 lbs raisins.

Packed those up on horses, one for each officer. They were grateful and the colonels sent letters back, really appreciative. The general was pleased too, especially with the wagons I got him, and he settled up my costs and thanked me. Asked me to keep supplying provisions. I did, using my own money, over a thousand pounds sterling. Got my account to him just before the battle. He paid me back a thousand pounds, but the rest... never saw it. More on that later.

This general, brave but too confident. Thought too highly of regular troops, underestimated Americans and Indians. George Croghan joined with a hundred Indians. Could have been good scouts and guides if treated right. But the general ignored them, and they left.

He told me his plan: take Fort Duquesne, then Niagara, maybe Frontenac if time allowed. Said Duquesne wouldn’t take more than a few days, no big obstacles to Niagara. I’d been thinking about the long narrow road they’d have to cut through the woods and remembered a past French defeat. So I said, "Sure, sir, with your strong troops and artillery, Duquesne shouldn't hold long. But watch for Indian ambushes. A narrow line, four miles long, could get split by surprise attacks, and the parts might not support each other in time."

he kinda laughed at me and said, "these 'savages' might be trouble for your raw american militia, but they can't touch the king's regular troops." i knew better than to argue with a military guy about his own field, so i kept quiet. turns out, the enemy didn't hit them while they were stretched out on the march like i thought they would. they let the troops get closer, about nine miles from the target. right after crossing a river and regrouping in the woods, the enemy hit the front guard with heavy fire from the trees. the general had no idea they were even there until bullets started flying.

the guard was in chaos, and the general rushed the rest of the troops forward to help, but it was a mess with the wagons and baggage everywhere. the enemy started firing on their flank. officers on horseback were easy targets and went down fast. soldiers clumped up, no orders, just standing there getting picked off till two-thirds were dead. then panic hit, and they all bolted.

wagon drivers ditched their teams and ran, setting off a chain reaction. left everything behind for the enemy: wagons, provisions, artillery. the general got out with difficulty, wounded. his secretary, mr. shirley, was killed right next to him. out of eighty-six officers, sixty-three were down, and seven hundred fourteen men out of eleven hundred were killed. these were supposed to be the best, picked from the whole army. colonel dunbar was supposed to follow with more supplies and men but got spooked when the survivors brought back panic to his camp. he had over a thousand men, and the enemy wasn't even four hundred strong, but instead of recovering, he destroyed all the supplies to run faster to safety.

he got pleas from the governors of virginia, maryland, and pennsylvania to help protect the frontiers, but he just kept running until philadelphia, where he felt safe with the city to shield him. this whole mess made us americans start doubting the supposed strength of british regulars.

and when they first landed, they looted and ruined some of the locals, even roughing them up if they complained. it made us lose faith in these defenders. compare that to our french allies in 1781, who marched through seven hundred miles of our country without so much as taking an apple.

captain orme, one of the general's aids, got hit pretty bad. stuck with him till he passed a few days later. orme said the general was quiet the first day, just muttered "who would have thought it?" at night. next day, silent again, then said, "we'll know better next time." died soon after.

the enemy got their hands on the secretary's papers, with all the orders and correspondence. they translated and published some stuff in french, showing britain's hostile plans before war was official. saw letters praising my service to the army. david hume, later on, mentioned he saw letters from braddock recommending me too. but the expedition flopped, so those letters didn’t really do anything for me.

asked braddock for one favor: stop enlisting our bought servants and discharge those already taken. he agreed and returned several. when dunbar took over, he wasn't as decent. in philly during his retreat, i asked him to release three servants from lancaster. he said if their masters met him in trenton, he'd hand them over. they made the trip, but he didn't keep his word.

once news spread about the lost wagons and horses, the owners came to me for the money i'd promised. told them the paymaster had the funds, but needed general shirley's orders first. wrote to shirley, but with him far away, replies took time. some folks started suing me. finally, shirley appointed commissioners to check claims and ordered payment. the total was about twenty thousand pounds. would've ruined me if i'd had to pay it alone.

before we got the bad news, these two Doctors Bond came by with a plan to raise cash for a big firework show. they figured we'd be celebrating after taking Fort Duquesne. i just looked at them and said, maybe let's wait till we actually have something to celebrate. they looked shocked. "you don't think we'll take it?" one asked. i told them anything can happen in war, explained my doubts, and they dropped the idea. Dr. Bond later mentioned he didn't like my warnings.

Governor Morris was pestering the Assembly non-stop before Braddock's defeat, trying to push them into raising defense money without taxing the big estate owners. rejected every bill that didn't include that. after the defeat, he pushed even harder, thinking he'd get somewhere with the fear factor. but the Assembly stood firm, not wanting to lose their rights. one bill for fifty thousand pounds, he wanted to change just one word. it said all estates get taxed, proprietary ones included. he wanted it to read "not included." small word, big change. when England heard about the defeat, folks there got mad at the proprietaries for their cheapness, saying they might lose their rights by blocking defense efforts. that pressure got them to tell their receiver-general to add five thousand pounds to the Assembly's fund.

the Assembly accepted that as their share of the tax, passed a new bill with the exemption. they made me one of the money commissioners, sixty thousand pounds in total. i had a hand in shaping that bill, got it through, and also drafted a militia bill. got it through the House without much trouble, left the Quakers alone in it. wrote a dialogue to tackle objections to the militia, printed it, seemed to do the trick.

while a bunch of companies were getting their act together, the governor asked me to handle our north-west frontier. the enemy was causing trouble, so he wanted me to raise troops and build a line of forts. i wasn’t exactly trained for military stuff, but i took the job. got a commission with full powers and some blank ones for officers i could choose. didn’t take long to get 560 men. my son, who’d been an officer before, was my right-hand man. the indians had burned down gnadenhut, a moravian village, which seemed like a good spot for a fort.

we gathered the companies at bethlehem. surprised to see them so ready for defense. the gnadenhut attack had them on edge. the main buildings had a stockade, they’d bought arms from new york, and even had stones ready for the women to drop on attackers. they had folks on watch, just like a real garrison. i talked with bishop spangenberg about it, since i thought they didn’t do military stuff. turns out, not a solid principle, just a belief some had when they got exempted from military duties by parliament. common sense and danger overruled any scruples.

we started on the fort-building in january. sent one group to the minisink area, another to the lower part, and i went to gnadenhut with the rest. moravians gave us five wagons for tools and supplies.

right before leaving bethlehem, eleven farmers, chased off by indians, asked for guns to get their cattle. gave them each a gun and ammo. we hadn’t gone far before the rain started. no shelter till nightfall at a german’s house, all soaked. lucky we weren’t attacked. our guns were basic and the rain messed with the gun locks. indians know tricks to keep theirs dry, which we didn’t. they met the farmers, killed ten. one escaped to say their guns wouldn’t fire, too wet.

next day was clear, so we kept moving and got to Gnadenhut. place was wrecked. found a saw-mill with piles of boards. made some huts quick since we had no tents and the weather was rough. first task was burying the dead better. locals had left 'em half-buried.

morning after, we planned the fort. 455 feet around, meant 455 palisades, each a foot thick. had 70 axes, so we got to chopping. guys were good at it. i timed two of 'em on a pine — down in six minutes, 14 inches across. each pine gave us three 18-foot palisades, pointed at one end. while those were getting ready, rest of the crew dug a 3-foot trench for planting the palisades. stripped down the wagons, split the wheels, and used 'em to haul the logs. carpenters built a 6-foot high stage inside for the men to shoot through loopholes. we had a swivel gun set up on an angle, fired it to make sure any nearby Indians knew we had it. fort was done in a week, despite rain every other day.

noticed the guys were happier when busy. working days, they were in good spirits, evenings were lively. idle days, they got cranky, complained about the food, bickered. reminded me of a sea captain i knew who never let his crew sit idle. when there was nothing left to do, he'd say, "make 'em scour the anchor."

this fort we put together, not much to look at, but it works against Indians since they don't got cannons. we felt safer knowing we had a place to fall back to, so we went out in groups to check the area. didn't run into any Indians, but saw where they had been watching us from the hills. their setup was clever. it's winter, so they needed fires. but a regular fire would give away their spot. so they dug holes, about three feet wide and deeper than that. they used charcoal from burnt logs for small fires in the holes. we could see where they'd laid around the holes, legs down to keep their feet warm. smart move — no light, no smoke, no sparks giving them away. they didn't have many folks, and saw we had too many to take on.

we had this chaplain, Mr. Beatty, who was upset not many men showed for his prayers. they were promised a gill of rum daily, and they sure didn't miss showing up for that. I suggested to Mr. Beatty, "might feel beneath you to handle the rum, but if you give it out right after prayers, they'll come." he tried it, got some help to measure it out, and suddenly prayers were packed. better than punishing them for skipping.

just got the fort stocked when the governor sent me a letter. wanted me at the Assembly meeting if things were calm enough here. friends from the Assembly wrote too, urging me to come. with the forts done and folks feeling safe, I planned to head back. Colonel Clapham, a New England guy who knows Indian warfare, was visiting. he agreed to take command. I gave him a commission, introduced him to the garrison, saying he's more fit to lead. then I headed out. got to Bethlehem, took a few days rest. first night in a real bed felt strange after sleeping on the hut floor with just a blanket.

went to Bethlehem, checked out the Moravians. some came with me, all real friendly. they work for a common pot, eat, and sleep together. noticed the dorms had these smart air holes up top. went to their church, music was solid — organ, violins, all that. sermons aren’t mixed, like here. they split by groups: married men, wives, young folks, kids. the one i caught was for kids, boys led by a young guy, girls by a young woman. sermon fit their level, kind of coaxing them to behave. kids were well-behaved but looked pale, maybe too much inside time, not enough running around.

asked about their marriages, heard they use lots sometimes. usually, a guy tells his elders he wants to marry, they check with the women’s elders. they know the young folks’ personalities, so they decide what works. if they’re stuck, they draw lots. i said if matches aren’t by choice, they might be unhappy. got told they can be unhappy choosing for themselves too, couldn’t argue that.

back in Philly, saw the association thriving, non-Quakers mostly joined, formed companies, picked their leaders. Dr. B swung by, proud of spreading support for the law, thought his efforts did it. i thought it was my Dialogue, but let him have his view. officers chose me as colonel, so i took it. can’t recall company count, but we had about 1200 sharp guys, artillery too, firing twelve shots a minute. first review, they followed me home, fired rounds in salute, broke some of my electrical gear. my new title didn’t last long — England repealed the law, broke all our commissions.

i was about to head out to Virginia when my regiment's officers decided they'd escort me to the Lower Ferry. i had no clue about this plan, or id have stopped it. im not into pomp. but there they were, thirty or forty of them, all uniformed, and i couldnt get out of it. they even drew their swords, which didnt help. someone wrote to the proprietor about it, and he wasnt happy. he'd never gotten such an escort, nor any of his governors. said it was only for royalty. all this just made him dislike me more, which was already a lot, since i always opposed his tax exemptions in the Assembly. he even tried to get me fired from my postmaster gig, but all he got was a mild warning from Sir Everard Fawkener.

despite the constant disputes with the governor, we kept things civil. we never had a personal beef. i think, as a lawyer, he saw us as just doing our jobs for our sides. sometimes he'd even ask my advice on tough issues. we worked together to supply Braddock's army, and after Braddock's defeat, he called me in for advice on stopping the back counties from deserting. i suggested writing to Dunbar to hold the frontiers till more troops came. when i got back from the frontier, he wanted me to lead an expedition against Fort Duquesne, offering to make me general. i wasnt sure about my military skills, and i think he just wanted to use my popularity to raise troops and money without taxing the proprietary estate. i wasnt eager, so the plan fell through. soon after, Captain Denny replaced him.

before gettin into my role with the new governor, gotta tell you how my name got around in the science circles. 

in 1746, i hit up boston and ran into a dr. spence from scotland. he was showing off some electric experiments. they weren't top-notch, but new to me, so i was hooked. soon as i got back to philly, our library got a glass tube from mr. p. collinson of the royal society, along with how to use it for experiments. i jumped at the chance to try what i saw in boston, and with practice, got real good at it. my place was packed with folks wanting to see these new tricks.

to ease the crowd, i had more tubes made at our glass-house. friends got their hands on them, and we had a crew doing experiments. mr. kinnersley, a smart neighbor out of work, was one of them. i helped him set up lectures for cash, arranged the experiments logically, so they built on each other. he got a slick setup made by instrument-makers, better than my rough versions. his lectures took off, he toured the colonies, and made some money. the west indies were tricky though, air's too damp.

since mr. collinson gave us the tube, i figured he should know how we were doing. wrote him letters about our experiments. the royal society didn't think much of them at first, laughed at the idea of electricity being like lightning. but dr. fothergill saw their worth and pushed for printing. collinson got them into a pamphlet, and it sold well, even expanding to a full volume. cave, who published it, made a good call profit-wise.

Took a while for folks in England to notice those papers. Count de Buffon got his hands on a copy, convinced M. Dalibard to translate them into French, and they got printed in Paris. This ticked off Abbé Nollet, a big name in Natural Philosophy, who’d already put out his own theory on electricity. He thought the papers were from his Paris enemies trying to mess up his system. Once he realized Franklin was real and in Philly, he fired off a bunch of letters defending his theory and trashing my experiments.

I thought about replying to the abbé, even started drafting an answer. But my experiments were laid out clearly enough for anyone to repeat and verify. If they couldn’t be verified, they weren’t defendable. Plus, with the whole language barrier, things could get lost in translation. One of his points was based on a translation error. I figured it was better to focus on new experiments than argue about old ones. Never replied to M. Nollet, and it worked out. M. le Roy from the Royal Academy of Sciences backed me up, and my work got translated into Italian, German, and Latin. Eventually, my ideas gained ground over the abbé’s, and he ended up isolated in his views.

What really kicked off my book’s fame was an experiment by Dalibard and De Lor at Marly pulling lightning from clouds. That caught everyone’s attention. M. de Lor, with his gear and lectures, redid the Philadelphia Experiments. After showing them to the king and court, all of Paris wanted to see. I won’t dive into the details of that big experiment or the thrill of my kite experiment in Philly. Those stories are in the electricity histories.

Dr. Wright from England wrote about how folks abroad were impressed with my experiments, wondering why England didn’t notice much. That got the Royal Society interested again. Dr. Watson put together a summary of my work and gave me some praise. They published it, and after Mr. Canton proved the lightning experiment with a rod, they finally took notice. Without me asking, they made me a member and waived the usual fees, worth twenty-five guineas. They’ve given me their Transactions for free since then and awarded me the Copley gold medal for 1753. Lord Macclesfield made a nice speech when they handed it over.

Governor Denny brought the medal from the Royal Society to me at a city event. He spoke well of me, saying he knew my character. After dinner, he pulled me aside, saying folks in England advised him to befriend me for smooth administration. He promised help and mentioned the owner's good will towards the province. He suggested dropping opposition for harmony, hinting at benefits for me. The drinkers sent Madeira wine, and with more wine, the governor got more generous with his offers.

I told him I was doing well enough and didn’t need favors. As an Assembly member, I couldn’t take any. No personal issue with the owner, but I'd support any public measures that were genuinely for the people’s good. My past opposition was because the measures seemed to favor the owner's interest over the people's. I thanked Denny for his regard and assured him I'd help his administration where I could, hoping he didn’t have the same restrictive instructions as his predecessor.

he didnt explain himself at first, but when he dealt with the Assembly, his issues came back up. disputes flared again, and i was right there in the opposition. wrote up the request for those instructions, then the remarks on them. you can find those in the votes and in the Historical Review i published later. personally, we stayed cool. no bad blood. we hung out a lot; dude had stories and could talk, told me my old buddy Jas. Ralph was alive and kicking in England as a top political writer. Ralph had a pension going, though Pope trashed his poetry. his prose was solid.

the Assembly hit a wall with the proprietary, who wouldn't budge on tying up their deputies with bad instructions. so they decided to petition the king about it, named me as their agent to take it to England. they'd sent a bill to the governor for sixty thousand pounds for the king, but he wouldn't pass it because of his orders.

i'd booked passage with Captain Morris from New York, had my gear on board, when Lord Loudoun showed up in Philadelphia. said he wanted to fix things between the governor and Assembly so the king's work wouldn't stall. he got us together to talk it out. i made the case for the Assembly, all my arguments are in the public papers and minutes. governor stuck to his orders, said he'd be ruined if he didn't, but hinted he'd bend if Loudoun backed him. Loudoun didn't go for it, though i almost had him. he leaned on the Assembly to comply instead, asked me to push them that way, saying no troops would defend our borders if we didn't handle it ourselves.

told the House what went down, gave them resolutions i wrote up, saying we kept our rights but had to pause them under pressure, protested that. they finally agreed to drop the bill and make one that fit the instructions, which the governor passed. then i was clear to sail. but the paquet left with my supplies, so i lost out there. only got thanks from his lordship, who took all the credit.

he got to new york before me. he had control over when the packet boats would leave. he said one was leaving soon, so i asked for the exact time to make sure i didnt miss it. he told me, "officially she's set for saturday, but between us, if you're there by monday morning, you're good. but dont push it past that." i hit a snag at a ferry and only got in by monday noon, worried she mightve left with the fair wind. but no, she was still in the harbor, leaving the next day. thought i was about to head to europe, but i didnt know lordship's knack for indecision yet. this was april, and we didnt sail till june. two packet boats were stuck in port waiting for his letters, always supposed to be ready "tomorrow". another boat came, then another, all delayed. ours was first up since it had been waiting longest. passengers were restless, merchants worried about their letters and war-time insurance orders. didnt matter though, lordship's letters werent ready. always found him at his desk, pen in hand, seemed like he'd write plenty.

one morning i went to see him, found innis, a messenger from philly, in the antechamber. he had a packet from governor denny for the general, handed me some letters from friends. i asked when he'd head back so i could send replies. he said he'd call at nine the next day for the general's response, then leave right after. gave him my letters. two weeks later, ran into him again, same place. "back already, innis?" "back? i havent left yet." "why?" "been calling every morning for two weeks for lordship's letter, still not ready." "but he's always writing, right?" "yeah," innis said, "like st. george on signs, always on horseback, never moving." turns out innis was right, heard later mr. pitt replaced this general with amherst and wolfe because he never got updates, couldnt tell what he was up to.

every day we expected to sail. all three paquets headed down to Sandy Hook, ready to join the fleet. passengers figured best to be on board, just in case the ships got sudden orders to leave. we ended up hanging there about six weeks, burned through our sea-stores, so had to get more. finally, we sailed. the General and his army were bound for Louisburg, planning to siege it. all the paquet-boats tagged along, waiting for the General's dispatches. five days out, we got the letter to leave, so our ship broke from the fleet and headed for England. the other two paquets got dragged to Halifax, where they played soldier games and then, out of the blue, decided to skip Louisburg. back they went to New York, troops and all, with the paquets and passengers in tow. meanwhile, French and savages took Fort George, massacring the garrison after surrender.

later in London, i met Captain Bonnell, who commanded one of those paquets. he said he told his lordship his ship was fouled up, slowing her down, which is bad for a paquet-boat. asked for three days to clean her up. the general said, "do it in a day, or not at all. you're sailing tomorrow." never got the leave, stuck there for three months.

met one of Bonnell's passengers too. guy was furious at his lordship for all the deceit and delays, getting dragged to Halifax and back. swore he'd sue for damages, but i never heard if he did. he claimed the delay wrecked his business.

seeing all this, i was amazed such a man got trusted with leading a big army. but after seeing more of how the world works, i'm less surprised. General Shirley, who took over after Braddock died, would've done better than Loudoun in '57. Shirley wasn't a soldier, but he was smart, listened to good advice, made solid plans, and executed them quick. Loudoun, though, left the colonies wide open while he played around in Halifax. Fort George fell. he messed up trade with pointless embargoes, supposedly to block enemy supplies but really to cut prices for contractors he maybe had a stake in. and when he finally lifted the embargo, he forgot to tell Charlestown. Carolina fleet stuck three months, ships rotting, many sunk on the way home.

shirley seemed pretty relieved to not have to run an army anymore. i was at this big event in new york for lord loudoun taking command. shirley was there too, even though he was getting replaced. tons of officers, citizens, and visitors. they borrowed some chairs, and shirley got stuck with a really low one. sitting next to him, i said, "they've given you too low a seat, sir." he just laughed it off, "mr. franklin, a low seat's the easiest."

while stuck in new york, i gathered up all my accounts for the provisions i got for braddock. some of the receipts took forever to come in. gave them to lord loudoun, wanting my balance. he had them checked, everything matched, and said he'd get me an order on the paymaster. but kept delaying. finally, right before i left, he said he wasn’t gonna mix his accounts with his predecessors. told me to show my accounts in england for payment. 

i tried explaining the unexpected costs from being stuck so long, and how i didn't charge any commission. he said, "don't try convincing us you didn't gain anything; we know everyone profits from supplying the army." i told him i didn't pocket a cent, but he didn't buy it. turns out, people do make big money in those roles. still haven't seen that balance.

our packet captain bragged about his fast ship, but it was the slowest out of ninety-six. near another slow ship that was still faster, he had everyone move to the back. about forty of us. speed picked up, leaving the other ship behind. turns out, the ship was too heavy at the front. moved the water casks back, and she sailed like a champ again.

captain said she hit thirteen knots once. thats thirteen miles per hour. captain kennedy, navy guy on board, didn't buy it. said no ship sails that fast. thought maybe the log-line was off or they messed up heaving the log. they made a bet to settle it when the wind picked up. kennedy checked the log-line himself, all good. later, with a strong wind, captain lutwidge claimed thirteen knots again. kennedy threw the log himself, had to admit he lost the bet.

now, here's the thing. ship-building's got a flaw. you can't tell if a new ship's fast till you test her. even if you copy a good ship, it might turn out slow. i figure it's partly cause sailors have different ideas on loading, rigging, and sailing. each captain does his own thing. same ship might sail better under one captain than another. plus, the guy who builds the hull isn't the one who rigs or sails her. no one's got the full picture to make the best call.

even at sea, officers disagree on sail trim. same wind, different calls. they don't have a set rule. but i think we could experiment. first, find the best hull shape for speed. then, best mast placement. figure out sail form and position based on the wind. finally, sort out the lading. we're in an age of experiments. done right, it'd be useful. bet someone smart will take it on soon, hope they do well.

we got chased a few times but always outran them. hit soundings in thirty days. clear night, captain figured we were near falmouth. wanted to run in the night, dodge enemy privateers near the channel entrance. loaded all sails, wind was strong and fair, we flew. captain aimed to pass wide of the scilly isles, but there's a sneaky indraught in st. george's channel. thats what got sir cloudesley shovel's squadron. probably what caught us too.

we had a lookout up front. they'd call out often, "watch ahead," and he'd reply, "ay ay." but probably half-asleep, might’ve missed a light right in front. the studdingsails blocked it from the helm and the rest of the crew, but a random yaw from the ship revealed it, causing a big scare. that light looked massive, like a cart-wheel. midnight, captain was out cold, but captain kennedy jumped on deck, saw the mess, and ordered a quick wear around with sails up. risky for the masts, but it saved us. we were headed straight for rocks under the lighthouse. made me see how crucial lighthouses are. decided right then to push for more in america if i made it back.

come morning, soundings showed we were close to port, but a heavy fog hid the land. around nine, the fog lifted like a theater curtain, revealing falmouth, the harbor, and fields. after endless ocean, it was a sight for sore eyes, especially being finally out of war's grip.

took off for london with my son, paused briefly to check out stonehenge and lord pembroke’s place at wilton. got to london july 27, 1757.

this wraps up the autobiography as temple franklin published it. what comes next was written in franklin's final year, first printed in bigelow's 1868 edition.

got settled in the place Mr. Charles arranged, then headed to see Dr. Fothergill. supposed to get his take on how to proceed. he said skip the government complaint for now. better to talk to the proprietaries first. maybe some private persuasion could sort things out. caught up with Mr. Peter Collinson next. he mentioned John Hanbury, big name in Virginia trade, wanted to know when id arrive. Hanbury planned to take me to Lord Granville, head of the council. we set that up for the next morning. Hanbury picked me up, took me to see Granville. got a polite welcome. he asked about American affairs. then he hit me with this: "you Americans misunderstand your constitution. you think the king's instructions arent laws. but they are. crafted by legal experts, debated, signed by the king. theyre the law for you, because the king is your legislator." told him that was news to me. our charters said laws come from our Assemblies, then to the king for assent. once given, cant be changed. he disagreed. didnt change my mind, but it got me worried about the court's views. wrote it all down back at the lodgings. remembered a bill from 20 years back that tried to make the king's instructions colonial law. commons shut it down. we saw them as liberty's friends, but by '65, their actions showed they just wanted that power for themselves.

few days later, Dr. Fothergill got the proprietaries to meet me at Mr. T. Penn's house, Spring Garden. we started off with both sides saying they wanted to be reasonable, but everyone had their own idea of what "reasonable" meant. i laid out our complaints, and they defended their actions as much as they could. we were miles apart in our views, didn't seem like we'd agree. they wanted me to write down our complaints, promising to consider them. i did, but they just handed it to their solicitor, Ferdinand John Paris. this guy handled all their legal stuff, including a long fight with Lord Baltimore over Maryland. paris was proud and angry, probably didn't like how i'd criticized his weak arguments before. i refused to discuss with him, only wanted to deal directly with the proprietaries.

they took paris's advice to get the Attorney and Solicitor-General's opinion, and my paper just sat there for almost a year. kept asking for an answer, but they always said they were waiting. never found out what opinion they got, but they sent a long-winded message to the Assembly, drafted by paris, complaining my paper lacked formality and called it rude. they justified their actions and said they'd negotiate if the Assembly sent someone more "candid," hinting that wasn't me.

guess they meant i didn't use their fancy titles, "True and Absolute Proprietaries of the Province of Pennsylvania," in my paper. didn't think it was needed since i'd already said all this in person. but while they stalled, the Assembly got Governor Denny to pass a tax on the proprietary estate like everyone else's, which was the main issue, so they didn't bother answering.

when the act came over, the proprietaries, with advice from paris, decided to block it from getting royal assent. they petitioned the king in council, got a hearing, and hired two lawyers to argue against it, while i had two lawyers supporting it. they claimed the act unfairly targeted the proprietary estate to lighten the load on the people's estates. said if the act stayed in force, the unpopular proprietaries would be at the mercy of the people and get ruined. we argued the act wasn't meant to do that and wouldn't. assessors were honest, took an oath to be fair, and any small benefit they'd get from shifting taxes to the proprietaries wasn't worth perjury. we pushed hard on the damage a repeal would cause – the £100,000 already printed for the king's use was out there, and repealing would devastate many and prevent future grants. the proprietors’ fear of high taxes was baseless and selfish.

lord mansfield, one of the counsel, pulled me aside during the hearing. asked if i truly believed the proprietary estate wouldn't be harmed by the act. i said sure. he said if that's true, would i agree to ensure it. i said no problem. he called in paris, we talked, and both sides agreed to his proposal. the clerk of the council wrote it up, i signed with mr. charles, who was also an agent for the province. lord mansfield went back to the council chamber, and the law passed. some changes were suggested, and we agreed to make them later, but the assembly didn't think they were needed. the first year's tax was already collected before the council's order reached us. they put a committee on it, including friends of the proprietaries, and after a full check, they reported the tax was assessed fairly.

the assembly saw my agreement as crucial for keeping the paper money's value stable across the country. they formally thanked me when i got back. but the proprietaries were furious with governor denny for passing the act and ousted him, threatening to sue for ignoring instructions he'd bonded to follow. still, denny had done it for the general and the king's service, and he had strong court connections, so he shrugged off the threats, which never happened.

chief events in franklin's life:

ending with 1757, the autobiography skips some key facts. so here's a detailed list of franklin's life events from the start:

1706 born in boston. baptized at Old South Church.  
1714 starts grammar school at eight.  
1716 helps dad in the tallow-chandlery.  
1718 apprenticed to brother james, a printer.  
1721 writes ballads, sells them on the street. anonymous contributor and temp editor for "New England Courant." becomes a free-thinker, vegetarian.  
1723 breaks indenture, moves to philly. works at Keimer's print shop. drops vegetarianism.  
1724 Governor Keith convinces him to go solo. heads to london for type. works and publishes "Dissertation on Liberty and Necessity, Pleasure and Pain."  
1726 back in philly. works in a dry-goods store, then manages Keimer's print shop.  
1727 starts the Junto, aka "Leathern Apron" Club.  
1728 opens a print shop with Hugh Meredith.  
1729 becomes owner/editor of "Pennsylvania Gazette." anonymously prints "Nature and Necessity of a Paper Currency." opens a stationer's shop.  
1730 marries Deborah Read.  
1731 starts the Philadelphia Library.  
1732 launches "Poor Richard's Almanac" as "Richard Saunders." Almanac runs 25 years, shapes American character.  
1733 starts learning French, Italian, Spanish, Latin.  
1736 becomes clerk of the General Assembly. forms Union Fire Company of Philly.  
1737 elected to the Assembly, Deputy Postmaster-General. plans city police.  
1742 invents the "Franklin" stove.  
1743 suggests an Academy, becomes University of Pennsylvania by 1749.  
1744 starts the American Philosophical Society.  
1746 publishes "Plain Truth" on disciplined defense. forms a military company. starts electrical experiments.  
1748 sells printing biz. joins Commission of the Peace, Common Council, Assembly.  
1749 becomes Commissioner for Indian trade.  
1751 helps start a hospital.  
1752 kite experiment proves lightning is electrical.  
1753 gets Copley medal, joins Royal Society, M.A. from Yale and Harvard. becomes joint Postmaster-General.  
1754 Commissioner from Pennsylvania to Albany Congress. proposes colonies' union.  
1756 pledges property for Braddock's army supplies. gets Assembly grant for Crown Point. establishes volunteer militia. becomes Colonel.  
1757 proposes street paving in Assembly. publishes "Way to Wealth." goes to England to represent Assembly vs. Proprietaries. stays as Pennsylvania agent. mingles with scientific, literary folks.  
1760 secures compromise from Privy Council on Proprietary estates' taxes.  
1762 gets LL.D. from Oxford and Edinburgh. returns to America.  
1763 tours northern colonies for post-office inspection.  
1764 loses Assembly reelection to Penn faction. sent to England as Pennsylvania agent.  
1765 tries stopping Stamp Act.  
1766 testifies to House of Commons on Stamp Act. becomes agent for Massachusetts, New Jersey, Georgia. visits Göttingen University.  
1767 travels France, presented at court.  
1769 gets a telescope for Harvard.  
1772 elected Associé Etranger of French Academy.  
1774 removed as Postmaster-General. influences Thomas Paine to move to America.  
1775 back in America. delegate to Second Continental Congress. on secret correspondence committee. commissioner to secure Canadian cooperation.  
1776 on Declaration of Independence drafting committee. president of Pennsylvania's Constitutional Committee. sent to France as colonies' agent.  
1778 signs treaties of alliance and commerce. received at court.  
1779 appointed Minister Plenipotentiary to France.  
1780 appoints Paul Jones commander of "Alliance."  
1782 signs preliminary peace articles.  
1783 signs definitive peace treaty.  
1785 returns to America. elected President of Pennsylvania, reelected 1786.  
1787 reelected President. delegate to Federal Constitution convention.  
1788 retires from public life.  
1790 April 17, dies. buried at Fifth and Arch, Philly.

Franklin's autobiography? It's surprisingly grounded. Doesn't come off as conceited. Just a straightforward guy taking stock of his life and career. 

Beard and Beard say it's not just a classic but a great self-education story too.

Becker thinks Franklin's and Adams' writings are key to understanding 18th-century America. Franklin's autobiography is right up there.

Parker talks about Franklin's massive influence on America—shaping ideas, institutions, the whole deal. He made practical sense for daily life.

Labaree and team say Franklin's life story makes the Declaration feel less revolutionary. It's about hard work, effective writing, planning, and public service with a focus on people's needs.

Perry notes that if you only know Franklin from his autobiography, you're missing out on his full range and vitality. He picked a clear, simple style that suited his versatile mind.
