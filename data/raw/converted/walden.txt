ON THE DUTY OF CIVIL DISOBEDIENCE

_"I dont plan on writing some sad ode, but to crow like a morning rooster, just to stir the neighbors."_

WALDEN

Economy

When I put these words down, I was living alone in the woods, a mile from anyone, in a house I built on Walden Pond, Concord, Massachusetts. I made my living with my hands. Spent two years and two months like that. Now Im back in the bustle.

Wouldnt talk so much about my life if folks in town hadnt asked. They want to know what I ate, if I got lonely or scared. Some curious about how much I gave away, or how many kids I supported. So I'm answering those here. Most books cut out the "I", but here it stays. It's always the first person speaking, really. I write about myself cause I'm what I know best. Wish I'd broader experience, but here we are. Every writer ought to share their life straight up, not just stories they heard. Maybe these pages are for the broke students out there. The rest can take what fits. If the coat fits, it might do some good.

let's talk straight about folks here in new england. not the chinese or sandwich islanders. i mean the condition you're living in. seriously, is it necessary to be as rough as it is? can't it be better? i've walked around concord a lot, seen folks in stores, offices, fields. they seem like they're doing penance in every odd way imaginable. those brahmins sitting in fire or hanging upside down? looks like a day at the park compared to what i see here. hercules had twelve labors, but my neighbors? endless tasks, no monsters slain, no finish line. 

young guys here, stuck with farms and barns and cattle they didn't ask for. would’ve been better off born in the wild, raised by wolves. at least then they might see life for what it is, not just land to labor on. who made them slaves to dirt? why start digging their graves the moment they're born? they slog through life, pushing barns and fields ahead of them. met too many souls near crushed by this load. 

we're working under a wrong idea. the best part of us ends up buried in the ground. we call it necessity, but it’s just piling up treasures that rot and get stolen. it's a fool’s life, and they'll see it, if not now, then later. remember deucalion and pyrrha, making men by tossing stones behind them? from hard stones, hard people, enduring pain. that's the result of blindly following bad advice, not looking where things land.

most folks, even here where we're supposed to be free, get so tangled up in pointless work and fake responsibilities that they miss out on life's finer stuff. they're working so hard their hands shake too much to hold onto the good things. the working guy doesn't have the luxury to live with real integrity every day. market doesn't pay for that. he's got no time to be anything but a machine. how can he remember his own ignorance — which he needs to grow — when he's always using what he already knows? sometimes we ought to just give him a break, offer some help, before judging him. the best parts of us need careful handling, but we're rough on ourselves and each other.

some of you are struggling, barely making ends meet, maybe borrowing time to read this. it's clear a lot of you are living on the edge, trying to get by, stuck in the old cycle of debt. you're always promising to pay later and never quite making it. you bend over backwards to keep business coming in, doing everything short of breaking the law, just to make a living. you save for a rainy day, hiding what little you have wherever you can, no matter how much or how little it is.

i wonder sometimes why we focus so much on slavery far away when we're caught in subtler forms of it ourselves. having a boss is one thing, but being your own worst taskmaster is another. look at the guy hauling goods along the road, just a cog in the machine, more worried about his job than his own future. does any divine spark shine in him? he's a slave to his own opinion, more than public opinion ever could be. what a man thinks of himself shapes his fate, more than anything else. where's the wilberforce to free our own minds? and think about the women, busying themselves with trivial things, as if killing time doesn't hurt the bigger picture.

Most folks just drift through life, keeping their worries quiet. They call it resignation, but it's just desperation dressed up. Whether you're in the city or out in the sticks, you're still running from one desperate place to another. We say we unwind with games and fun, but there's no real play in it — just distraction after work. True wisdom? It's knowing not to act out of desperation.

Think about it: what do we really need in life? Seems like people choose the usual way of living because they think there's no other option. But the sharp ones, they know the sun still rises clear every day. It's never too late to ditch old prejudices. Just because something's been done forever doesn't mean it's right. What people think is truth today might just be smoke tomorrow, misleading like a cloud that promised rain but never delivered. Try what they say you can't, and you'll probably find you can. Old ways for old folks, new ways for the new. 

Age doesn't make you a better teacher; it's lost more than it's gained. Doubt if the wisest learned anything truly valuable by just living. The older generation, they've got little to offer—their lives haven't been the roaring successes they pretend, and they might still cling to beliefs that don't match their experiences. I've been around thirty years and still waiting for some real, earnest advice from my elders. So far, they've told me nothing useful. Life's an experiment I'm still figuring out, and their attempts don't help me much. Any insights I have, my so-called mentors never mentioned.

A farmer once told me, “You can't live on just veggies; they don't build bones.” Yet there he is, feeding his oxen nothing but plants, and those oxen pull him and his plow just fine. What's essential in one circle is a luxury or unknown in another.

life and what we think we know — it’s like a well-trodden path, isn’t it? Some folks figure everything’s been sorted out by those before us. Solomon’s got rules about tree spacing, even Romans had acorn laws. Hippocrates had nail-cutting instructions — even with your fingers, not too short, not too long. But here's the thing: humans ain’t been fully figured out. We barely scratched the surface of what we can do. Even if you’ve messed up before, don’t stress. No one can say what you’re yet to do.

we have all these tests, like how the same sun lights up both my beans and other worlds. If I’d thought of that, might’ve saved some mistakes. Didn’t see it that way when I was hoeing. Stars form triangles out there, reaching across to who knows what kind of beings. Nature’s as varied as we are. Who knows what life looks like to someone else? Imagine seeing through another’s eyes for just a moment. History, poetry, mythology — reading experiences like that would be wild.

most “good” stuff neighbors rave about, I reckon is bad. If I regret anything, it's my good behavior. Why’d I behave so well? Even the wisest old soul can’t quiet that pull to break away. Generations ditch past ventures like stranded ships.

we could trust more than we do. Care for ourselves like we care for others. Nature fits our strengths and weaknesses. Stress is like a disease. We overrate our work’s importance. Imagine if we got sick? We stay vigilant, avoid living by faith. At night, grudgingly pray, handling uncertainties. We live sincerely, cling to life’s patterns, deny change. We say there’s one way, but there’re countless paths. Change is constant, a miracle. Confucius nailed it: knowing what we know and don’t know is true knowledge. When one sees imagination as understanding, everyone will build on that.

look, most of the stress and worry we deal with boils down to figuring out what's really essential in life. living simply, like in frontier days, might teach us what we truly need and how we got it. even flipping through old merchant logs could show us what folks used to buy and stash away. despite all our modern advancements, the basics haven't changed much—our bones look like our ancestors'.

when i say "necessaries of life," i mean stuff so crucial that nobody tries to do without it, whether they're struggling or just plain stubborn. for many animals, it's just food. a bison needs some grass and a sip of water. they don't ask for more. for people, especially around here, it's food, shelter, clothing, and fuel. cover those, and then we can tackle life's bigger questions.

we didn't just stop at houses—cooked food, clothes, and the warmth of fire came next. fire probably started as a luxury, but now we can't imagine being without it. even pets get used to it. the right shelter and clothing keep our heat in, but too much can start the cooking process, literally. darwin noticed folks in tierra del fuego sweating like crazy even with little clothes, while his well-dressed crew stayed chilly. some cultures handle the cold better, like new hollanders, while europeans shiver fully dressed. combining the toughness of these folks with our modern smarts—now there's a thought.

liebig says our body is like a stove, with food as the fuel. we eat more when it's cold, less when it's warm. animal heat comes from slow burning inside us. if it burns too fast, we get sick. no fuel or a poor draft, and the fire goes out. think of animal life as animal heat—food fuels the fire inside, and fuel preps the food or warms us up. shelter and clothing keep all that heat in.

keeping warm, man, that's the big deal for our bodies. we do all sorts of things for it—food, clothes, shelter, even our beds which are like night clothes. we go robbing birds’ nests just to get comfy. folks say it’s a cold world, and yeah, a lot of our troubles come from cold, both physical and social. summer makes life easier in some places, no need for much fuel, the sun cooks your food. life gets simpler—less clothes, less shelter. here, in this country, i've found you don’t need much—some tools, a lamp, a few books. some folks head off to far places, trade for years, just to keep warm and then come back to die here. the rich? they’re not just warm, they’re cooked.

most luxuries and comforts aren’t just unnecessary, they’re obstacles. the wisest people lived simply, poorer in stuff, richer inside. ancient philosophers didn’t have much, but they had wisdom. we know more about them than you'd expect. modern thinkers, too, lived simply. real understanding of life needs a simple way of living. luxury breeds luxury, whether it’s farming or art. there’s professors, but few real philosophers. a philosopher lives by wisdom—simple, independent, grand, trusting. they solve life's problems practically, not just in theory. scholars often succeed by fitting in, not leading to a better future. why do we slip into decline? why do families fade? luxury weakens nations. is there some of that in us too? a philosopher stays ahead, lives differently, keeps warm in better ways.

when a guy's got all the basics covered — food, shelter, clothes, a warm fire — what's next? not just piling on more of the same. once he's got what he needs to live, maybe it's time to really live, to explore. like a seed in the right soil, rooted deep, ready to grow upward. why dig in so hard if not to reach for the sky? the best plants reach for the light, not just thicken their roots.

i'm not here to preach to those who already know their path, who thrive whether in heaven or hell, building big and living large without running dry. if you're happy where you are, that's your call. but for those grumbling about life's tough breaks, maybe it's time to change the game. some folks moan about duty like it's a badge. others, rich in stuff but poor in spirit, trapped by their own gold chains.

if you asked how i wanted to live, my past dreams might shock you. i've got tales to tell, and some might raise an eyebrow. i've always been keen to make the most of every moment, right where past meets future — the now. forgive the mysteries; my trade's full of them, not hidden on purpose, just part of the deal. i'd share it all if i could, no "keep out" signs here.

i lost a hound, a bay horse, and a turtle-dove once. still tracking them down. talked to lots of folks about it, describing tracks and calls. a few have heard the hound, felt the horse's hoofbeats, or seen the dove vanish into the clouds. they were as eager to find them as if they'd been theirs.

anticipating not just the sunrise but nature itself. mornings, summer and winter, while everyone else slept, i was already on it. seen my neighbors heading out, farmers off to boston, woodchoppers getting to it. didn't help the sun rise, but being there mattered.

autumn, winter days, out of town, listening to the wind, trying to catch something. almost lost myself in it, running headfirst into the wind. if it was political, would've hit the papers. other times up on a cliff or tree, waiting to catch something new, usually didn't, and when i did, it'd vanish with the sun.

spent years reporting for a journal no one read, editor never printed my stuff, like most writers, got nothing but the work itself. but that was enough.

self-appointed inspector of storms, kept paths open, bridges passable, even where no one bothered. watched over the town's wild stock, they loved jumping fences. checked those forgotten corners of the farm, didn't care who worked where. watered plants that would've died in dry spells.

did this a long time, not boasting, just did my job. but the town never put me on the list, never gave me a title or pay. my records, kept true, never audited, never paid. but i didn't do it for that.

not long ago, an indian came by a lawyer's house in my area wanting to sell baskets. "want to buy some baskets?" he asked. "no, we don't need any," said the lawyer. "what!" said the indian, leaving the gate, "you trying to starve us?" he'd seen how his white neighbors made it — that lawyer just wove arguments and somehow got money and status. so he thought, i'll start a business too; i'll weave baskets, that's something i can do. he figured once the baskets were made, he'd done his part, and the white folks would just buy them. he didn't realize he had to make them worth buying, or at least make others think so. i had my own kind of basket too, something delicate, but i hadn't made anyone want to buy it. still, i thought it was worth weaving them, and instead of figuring out how to sell, i figured out how not to need to sell. the life everyone praises as successful is just one version. why make it bigger than the others?

realizing my fellow citizens weren't about to give me a spot in the courthouse or any kind of job, i figured i had to fend for myself. i turned more to the woods, where i was better known. decided to jump right into business with whatever little i had. heading to walden pond wasn't about living cheap or expensive, but handling some personal business without a lot of hassle. missing out on that cause of a lack of common sense or business skill seemed more foolish than sad.

strict business habits are a must. if you're trading with the Celestial Empire, you don't need much more than a little office on the coast, maybe in a place like Salem. you'll ship out what's local—ice, pine timber, a bit of granite—all on local vessels. these are solid ventures. you gotta handle everything yourself: be the pilot, captain, owner, and underwriter. buy, sell, keep the books, read every letter in and out. oversee unloading imports day and night. be in multiple places at once. sometimes the best cargo lands on a Jersey shore. basically, you become your own communication system, always scanning the horizon, checking in with passing ships. keep a steady flow of goods to a far-off market. stay on top of market conditions, war and peace prospects, and trade trends. use new routes, adopt navigation improvements. study charts, learn about reefs, lights, buoys. correct those logarithmic tables—one mistake and you're on the rocks instead of at the dock. think La Perouse. keep up with global science, study explorers and merchants from Hanno onward. check your stock regularly to see where you stand. it's a challenge that demands a lot from a person: profit, loss, interest, tare, tret, gauging—it's a test of universal knowledge.

walden pond seems like a smart spot for business, not just for the railroad and ice trade. it has perks you might want to keep under wraps. it's a solid port with a strong foundation. no marshes to fill like the Neva, though you still gotta drive your own piles everywhere. folks say a flood tide with a westerly wind and Neva ice could wipe St. Petersburg off the map.

starting a business without the usual cash is tricky. you gotta figure out where to get what you need. about clothing, let's cut to the chase. a lot of us go for new threads more because we want to look good rather than for true practicality. if you're working, remember clothes are to keep you warm and cover up. think about how much you can get done without buying more clothes. kings and queens have fancy suits they wear once but they miss out on the comfort of a suit that fits well. they're like mannequins for clothes. our clothes start to reflect who we are. it's hard to give them up, like we need a whole ceremony. i don't think less of someone with a patch on their clothes, yet everyone stresses over being fashionable or at least clean. but if you don't fix that tear, it shows maybe you didn't plan well. i've tested friends by asking who could wear a patch or an extra seam. most act like their future depends on spotless pants. they'd rather limp with a broken leg than a torn pant. if a guy's leg breaks, they fix it. if his pants rip, he thinks it's over. people focus on what's respected, not respectability itself. we see a few men but lots of coats and pants. dress a scarecrow in your clothes and people might greet it before you. saw a scarecrow the other day and it looked like the farmer. even dogs know the drill. they'll bark at strangers with clothes, not a naked thief. makes you wonder how status would hold up if we ditched clothes. could you tell who's who without them? madam pfeiffer, traveling the world, felt she had to dress up in russia cause "people are judged by their clothes." even here in new england, wealth and fancy dress get you respect. but that's just being a bit heathen, needing a missionary. plus, clothes brought sewing, a job that never ends. especially women's dresses — never done.

if a guy's really found his thing, he doesn't need fancy clothes to do it. old stuff from the attic works just fine. shoes too, a hero keeps 'em running longer than a valet would. bare feet been around longer than any shoe, and they get the job done. folks who need new suits are hitting soirées or fancy halls. my old jacket, pants, hat, shoes, they're good enough if you're worshipping or working. who really wears out their clothes 'til they can't be passed on to someone with even less? i say, watch out for stuff that needs new clothes over a new mindset. if you're not changing, clothes won't help. try your next big thing in what you already got. people need purpose, not just tools. don't buy a new suit just 'cause the old one's ragged or dirty. wait 'til your actions make you feel new in the old. like birds or snakes shedding skin, it's a life moment. clothes are just our outer layer. without a real change, you're just playing dress-up and fooling no one.

we layer up like plants growing outwards, but clothes don't make us. the fancy stuff's just surface, easy to shed. thicker clothes, always there, more like a second skin. shirts are the core, can't take 'em off without losing yourself. everyone’s got their version of a shirt. dress so you can find yourself in the dark. live so simple and ready that if trouble hits, you can walk away without a worry. one good coat beats three thin ones. cheap clothes are out there, and a thick coat for five bucks will last years, pants for two, boots for a buck fifty, summer hat for a quarter, winter cap for sixty-two cents. make better ones at home for next to nothing. who can't stand tall in a suit like that, earned by their own work? wise folks will respect 'em.

asked for a specific style from my tailor, and she just says, “They don’t make them like that now,” like it’s some law of nature. Makes it tough to get what i want because she thinks i’m just talking crazy. Every time i hear that, i have to pause and think about who "They" even are and why they have a say in my clothes. Sometimes i feel like responding with my own mystery, like, “Sure, they didn’t make them recently, but they do now.” What's the point of measuring me if she’s just checking my shoulder width and not my style?

Fashion rules, not the Graces or Fates. Paris sets a trend, and we all follow. Makes me wonder if getting anything honest done is possible. People would need a good press to squeeze out old ideas, but then someone with a wild notion messes it up. Still, remember, some ancient wheat came to us from a mummy.

Overall, dressing hasn't become an art. Men wear what they find, like shipwreck survivors grabbing what washes up. We mock old fashions but chase new ones like gospel. We laugh at Henry VIII’s outfits like they’re from a far-off land. Clothes without the person are just silly. Only the serious look and sincere life within them make any outfit respectable. Harlequin with a stomach ache needs his costume to fit that mood too. When a soldier is hit, rags work as well as royal robes.

Our taste for new patterns has everyone peering through kaleidoscopes for today’s look. Manufacturers know it’s just whimsy. Two patterns almost the same, and one sells out, while the other gathers dust—until next season, when it’s suddenly in demand. Compared to this, tattooing doesn’t seem so barbaric. At least it’s consistent.

cant believe we think our factory system is the best way to get clothing. everyday the workers' conditions edge closer to the poor English ones. makes sense though, seems like the goal's not to dress folks well but to line the pockets of corporations. people tend to hit what they aim for. so even if they miss now, better to aim high.

shelter's necessary now, no doubt. but folks have gone without it in colder places. take Samuel Laing's Laplanders, sleeping on snow in a skin dress. not tougher than others, just adapted. but somewhere along the line, folks figured houses were convenient. in warmer climates, a house's more about winter or rain. in summer here, it was just a night cover. Native Americans marked their camps with wigwams on trees. man started out bare, but rain, winter, and hot sun made him build a house for cover. Adam and Eve had a bower before clothes. warmth and comfort start with a home.

imagine the first guy who found a rock hollow for shelter. every kid relives this, loves playing outside, making pretend houses. who doesn't remember checking out caves or rocks as a kid? it's the ancient part of us. caves to palm roofs to tiles. now we're indoors all the time, and life feels domestic. maybe we should spend more time outside. poets and saints might see things differently without a roof. birds and doves don't hide in caves.

if you're building a house, use some good sense or you might end up with something more like a maze or a museum. think about what you really need. ive seen Penobscot Indians here living in thin cotton tents in a foot of snow, probably wishing for more snow just to block the wind better. back when i was more worried about making an honest living and still having time for my own pursuits, i saw a big tool box by the railroad. it was six by three feet, and i thought if someone was really pressed, they could buy one for a dollar, drill a few holes for air, and use it for shelter. close the lid at night, stay dry, and be free from landlords. it's not the worst option. some folks struggle to pay rent on bigger, fancier places they dont even need. economy isn't a joke, though it might seem funny. long ago, folks built warm homes from what nature offered, like tree bark. Gookin, who supervised Indians for the Massachusetts Colony, wrote in 1674 about their houses being tightly covered with tree bark or mats. he said they were as warm as English houses. they had mats for floors and walls, and managed the wind with a mat over the roof hole. these lodges went up in a day or two and came down just as fast. every family had one or a part of one.

alright, let's break this down. back in the day, every family had a basic place to call home. even if it was simple, it was theirs. now, birds got nests, foxes got holes, and old-time folks had wigwams. but today, only about half the families own their homes. in cities, it's even less. most folks are renting, paying what feels like a tax, which could’ve bought a whole village of wigwams, keeping them stuck in a cycle of poverty. owning a place is tough because it costs a lot, while renting doesn't get any cheaper either. sure, folks say renting gets you a better place with modern comforts, but why's the guy in the nice apartment still poor while the guy with the wigwam isn’t?

they say civilization improves life, but it should mean better homes without skyrocketing costs. the real cost's the time and effort you swap for it. around here, a house might go for 800 bucks, taking a worker 10–15 years to save up, assuming no family to support. most of his life’s gone before he owns it. renting? just another tough choice. would the old-time folks trade their wigwam for a palace with these costs?

some might think having extra property is a safety net for later, like covering funeral costs. but maybe you don’t need to bury yourself in debt. this shows the difference between civilized folks and the old-timers. they say it's for our benefit, making life about society over the individual, to advance the race. but at what cost? maybe we can have the benefits without the drawbacks. why accept poverty as a given or blame past actions for present woes?

the soul's what matters. each one’s valuable, and each choice counts.

looking at my neighbors, the farmers in Concord, they're about as well off as anyone else, but most of them have been grinding for decades just to really own their farms. usually inherited with debt or bought with borrowed cash. a third of that effort's just to cover their houses, but more often than not, they're still paying. sometimes the debt's more than the farm's worth, turning it into a burden. yet, someone always seems ready to take it on, knowing it well, they say. ask the assessors, and they can't name a dozen who own outright. check the banks for the real story—those farms are tied up in mortgages. a farmer who’s paid off his land with his own labor is so rare, everyone knows him by name. i doubt more than three exist here.

people say most merchants fail, but a lot of those failures aren't about money, more about not keeping promises when it’s a hassle. that’s a moral collapse, worse than honest failure. bankruptcy's a launchpad for our civilization, but a hungry man stands on nothing. yet, the cattle show here dazzles every year like the farming machine's running smooth.

farmers complicate survival with convoluted plans. for shoelaces, they gamble on cattle. skillfully set traps for comfort and freedom, then trap themselves. that's why they're poor, and in a way, we all are—surrounded by luxury but missing basic comforts. when a farmer finally has his house, he might be poorer, trapped by it rather than sheltered. our houses aren't mobile, so we’re stuck with bad neighbors—often ourselves. some families have longed to move closer to town but can't, and only death will free them.

even if most end up owning or renting modern homes, civilization's improved houses more than the people living in them. it's built palaces, but not the nobility to match. if our lives are just about getting by like the savages, why should our homes be any better?

how do the poor minority fare? some rise above the savage, others fall below. luxury for one class means poverty for another. palace on one side, almshouse on the other. the folks who built the pyramids were fed on garlic and maybe didn’t even get a decent burial. mason finishes a palace, goes home to a hut worse than a wigwam. don’t be fooled — even with civilization around, people can be as bad off as savages. look at the shanties by the railroads, supposed sign of progress. people living with open doors in winter, no wood pile, bodies shrunk from cold and misery. they build what defines our age. same story in england, the world’s workhouse, and ireland, marked as enlightened yet struggling. compare the irish to native americans or south sea islanders before they met civilization. their rulers are no less wise than ours, but their state shows how squalid civilization can be. and don’t forget the southern laborers making our exports. even those in moderate circumstances aren’t spared.

most folks never think about what a house really is. they end up poor trying to keep up with neighbors. like wearing any coat a tailor makes or wanting a crown when a cap does the job. we could design a fancier house, sure, but who can afford it? why chase more stuff when we could be happy with less? respectable folks push this idea of needing more, teaching young people they need extra shoes and rooms for guests who never come. why not keep it simple, like the arabs or indians? the real heroes, the ones we look up to, didn't haul around fancy furniture. if our stuff's more complex than theirs, it's not because we're better. our homes are cluttered, and a good sweep would clear it out. i had some limestone on my desk but tossed it out because it needed dusting, while my mind stayed messy. how could i have a furnished house? better to sit outside where dust doesn't gather unless man messes it up.

luxury chasers set trends, and the rest follow. travelers stopping at fancy places see this. they assume you're living large, and if you go with it, you lose yourself. in trains, we spend on luxury over safety, turning them into moving drawing rooms. i’d rather sit on a pumpkin alone than be squished on a velvet seat. give me an ox cart with fresh air over a plush train with bad air.

life used to be simple. folks lived like travelers, just passing through. wake up, eat, move on. but now? we've chained ourselves to our own inventions. that guy who used to grab an apple off a tree when hungry is now a farmer. the one who found shade under a tree is a homeowner. we're not camping for a night anymore; we've planted ourselves firmly, forgetting the bigger picture. we embraced religion like a better farming method. built homes for this life, tombs for the next. art, meant to elevate us, is just decoration now, keeping us comfy in a low place. no room for true art here. no nails for a picture, no shelf for a hero's bust. our houses, how they're built, not paid for, should collapse under the weight of our nonsense. rich life seems like a leap, but it's hollow. real leap? some arab nomads jumping 25 feet on flat ground. that's genuine. without support, we all hit the ground again. i want to ask the owners of these grand messes: who props you up? are you one of the successful few or the many who fail? answer that, then maybe i'll see the point in your trinkets. putting the cart before the horse ain't pretty or useful. before we decorate, we need to strip down to basics. real beauty's found outside, no house, no keeper.

old johnson wrote about the first settlers, saying they dug into hillsides for shelter, tossing dirt over timber and making smoky fires for warmth. they didn’t build houses till the land grew enough food, which meant thin bread for a while. in 1650, a dutch secretary described how folks in new england, lacking funds, dug pits and lined them with wood and bark to make temporary homes. they raised a roof of spars and covered it with bark or sod. even the wealthy started like this, to save time and encourage immigrants. after a few years, they built big houses.

this strategy was smart, tackling immediate needs first. but, are our needs met now? when i think about getting a fancy house, i hesitate, because the world’s not ready for true human growth. we still cut our spiritual bread thin. houses should be lined with beauty where they touch our lives, not just covered in it. i’ve been in a few, and i know what they’re really lined with.

we could live in caves or wigwams, but modern materials are easier and cheaper. i know this both in theory and in practice. with some cleverness, we can use these resources to become richer than the richest, making civilization a genuine blessing. a civilized man is just a wiser savage. now, onto my own experiment.

late march, 1845, i borrowed an axe and headed to the woods by walden pond. needed timber for a house plan. started chopping down some young white pines. always gotta borrow something to get going, right? the guy lending me the axe said it was his prized possession, but i returned it sharper. nice spot to work, hillside with pines and a view of the pond. ice on the pond was still hanging on, but starting to open up. felt the spring creeping in as i worked — snow flurries now and then, but mostly sun on the rails, birds coming back.

one day, axe head slipped off. cut some green hickory for a wedge, drove it with a stone. soaked it to swell the wood. saw a striped snake in the pond. it just lay there, seemed fine underwater. maybe still waking up from winter. got me thinking, maybe people are like that too, stuck in their state till something stirs them. seen snakes half-frozen in the mornings, waiting for sun.

april 1st, it rained, ice melted. heard a lost goose over the pond, like it was the spirit of the fog.

kept on cutting and shaping timber. no deep thoughts, just sang to myself:

men say they know many things;
     but lo! they have taken wings,—
     the arts and sciences,
     and a thousand appliances;
     the wind that blows
     is all that any body knows.

i shaped the main beams six inches square, just trimmed the studs on two sides, and the rafters and floor on one. left some bark on for strength. borrowed tools helped with the mortise and tenon work. times in the woods weren’t long, but id bring bread and butter, read the newspaper it was wrapped in, and sit among the pine boughs id cut. hands sticky with pitch gave the bread a piney taste. cutting down trees, i got friendly with them, not just a foe. sometimes folks heard my axe and stopped to chat.

by mid-april, the frame was set for raising. bought James Collins' shanty for boards—said to be real nice. when i checked it out, he wasnt home. small place with a peaked roof, dirt piled up like a compost heap. roof was the best part, though warped. no door-sill, hens had their way in. Mrs. C. showed me inside, warned about a cellar that was more a dust hole. boards overhead, boards around, a window that the cat had used lately. stove, bed, a place to sit, and a few odds and ends around. James came back, deal made fast. four dollars and twenty-five cents tonight, he’d be out by five in the morning, leaving me to move in at six. he mentioned some vague claims on rent and fuel, but nothing serious. by six, i passed them on the road, all their stuff bundled up. the cat took off to the woods, ended up in a trap like a wild one.

took down the place early that morning. pulled the nails out and moved it all down to the pond in small loads. spread the boards out on the grass to let the sun do its thing. while driving through the woods, a thrush gave me a little tune. young Patrick, sneaky kid, tipped me off about neighbor Seeley. Seeley was pocketing the decent nails and spikes when I wasn't looking. he'd act innocent, chatting me up with springtime thoughts, saying work was hard to come by. he was there to watch, turning a small task into something epic.

dug out my cellar on a hill facing south, where a woodchuck once had his spot. cut through sumach and blackberry roots, six feet by seven feet deep, down to fine sand. perfect for keeping potatoes from freezing in winter. left the sides sloped, no stones. the sand never saw the sun, so it stayed put. only took two hours. loved this kind of work. people dig into the earth everywhere for steady temps. under any grand city house, there's still a cellar. after the building's gone, the ground keeps the memory. houses are like porches to a burrow.

by May, with some friends, started putting up the house frame. used the chance to be neighborly, not because I needed help. never felt more honored by the folks there. hope they'll build grander things one day. moved in on July 4th once it was boarded and roofed. boards were lapped tight, no rain could get in. before that, laid a chimney foundation with stones I lugged up from the pond. built the chimney after fall hoeing, cooked outside till then. kind of liked it. when rain came before the bread was done, rigged up some boards over the fire, sat there watching the loaf. found it peaceful. hands were busy those days, didn't read much. even scraps of paper lying around kept me entertained, did the job like the Iliad.

if i had to do it again, id build more thoughtfully. think about why a door's there, why we need a window, what a cellar or attic really means to us. maybe don't build anything till you've got a reason beyond just needing it right now. building your own place is like a bird making its own nest. imagine if we all made our own homes and grew our own food—maybe we'd all find a bit of that creative spark. but we act like cowbirds, using nests we didn't make, not singing our own songs. why give up building to the carpenter alone? most folks never get to know the simple joy of putting up their own house. we're all just part of this community machine. it's not just the tailor who's only a fraction of a man; it's everyone—the preacher, the merchant, the farmer. where's this dividing of labor leading us? and is it really worth it? sure, someone else can think for me, but doesn't mean they should, not at the cost of me thinking for myself.

some folks call themselves architects around here, but only a few seem to get that real beauty in architecture comes from necessity, not just decoration. there's one guy who thinks putting truth into architectural ornaments is some big revelation, but he’s just scratching at the surface. he's like a reformer starting at the cornice instead of the foundation. thinks every sugar plum needs an almond inside, but sometimes almonds are just better without the sugar, you know? real architecture grows from the needs and character of the people living in it, not from how it looks on the outside. the best homes are the simple ones—the log huts and cottages that just fit the life of the people inside. you can't fake that with fancy decorations.

a lot of architectural ornaments are hollow, and a strong wind can peel them off without damaging what’s underneath. if architects spent as much time on the substance as they do on the style, we might get somewhere. it’s like building your own coffin when the life’s gone out of the place. better to paint your house your own color—let it show your life, not just cover it up.

so last winter, i put up a chimney and shingled my house with some not-so-great shingles. had to straighten the edges with a plane, but it kept the rain out, and that’s what mattered.

got a tight little setup here. house is 10x15 feet with 8-foot posts, garret, closet, big windows on each side, two trap doors, door at the end, and a brick fireplace. did all the work myself, but materials cost me:

- boards: $8.03½ (mostly shanty boards)
- refuse shingles: $4.00
- laths: $1.25
- two second-hand windows with glass: $2.43
- thousand old brick: $4.00
- two casks of lime: $2.40 (that was high)
- hair: $0.31 (more than i needed)
- mantle-tree iron: $0.15
- nails: $3.90
- hinges and screws: $0.14
- latch: $0.10
- chalk: $0.01
- transportation: $1.40 (carried a lot of it myself)

total: $28.12½

claimed timber, stones, and sand by squatter’s right. got a small wood-shed too, built from leftovers. thinking about building something fancier one day, but only if it costs the same and pleases me more.

so heres the deal. a student looking for a place to stay can actually own a spot for life at the same cost as what they pay for yearly rent now. maybe i sound like im bragging, but it's not about me—it's about a bigger picture. yeah, theres a lot of nonsense out there, but lets cut to the chase. a room at cambridge? just for rent, you're down thirty bucks a year, and thats with all the noise and cramped living. imagine if we got smart about this. less formal schooling, more real-life learning, and the cost of education would drop. the stuff that costs the most isnt even what students need. sure, tuition's on the bill, but the real learning happens when they're with their peers.

starting a college usually means raising cash, then handing it off to a contractor, and the cycle continues. what if students actually laid down those foundations themselves? working with their hands wouldn't take away from their thinking; it'd make their free time worth something. dont just play life or study it on someone else's dime—live it. want to know about arts and sciences? dont just hang around professors; dive into real life. learn how to make a knife or build something from scratch. by the end of it, who'd be better? the kid who crafted his own knife or the one who sat through a lecture?

when i finished college, they said i studied navigation. but if id just taken a boat out once, id know more. even the broke student only learns about political economy, but not the real economy of living. so while hes reading the classic texts, hes unknowingly sinking his folks into debt.

modern improvements are like those shiny new tools that look good but dont always get the job done better. sometimes they distract us more than help us. railroads and telegraphs connect places fast, but what's the point if there's nothing worth saying between them? it's like rushing to tell someone something only to realize you didnt have anything important to say in the first place. 

someone once asked why i dont save up money to travel by train. they said i could see the countryside by taking the cars to fitchburg. but ive found walking gets me there just as well, and maybe quicker. the distance is about thirty miles and the fare is nearly a day's wages. back when workers earned sixty cents a day building these roads, i figured out i could walk it and get there by nightfall. while youre working to earn your fare, id already be on my way, seeing things youd miss from a train window. if the railroad circled the globe, id still bet on foot to keep me ahead. seeing and experiencing the world means moving at your own pace, not rushing to keep up with the train.

you cant outsmart the railroad's rules. it’s like leveling the whole planet. folks think if they keep digging and investing, everyone will travel freely and fast. but when the dust settles, a few ride, most get run over. they call it an accident. sure, some who earn can ride, but by then, they might not even want to anymore. reminds me of that guy who went to india for money to live like a poet later. he should've just started in the attic. look, i get it, the railroad's a big deal. but digging in dirt, wish you could've spent your time better.

before i wrapped up my house, needed to make a bit of cash, like ten or twelve bucks. so i planted a couple acres near it, mostly beans, some potatoes, corn, peas, turnips. the land's mostly pines and hickories, sold for about eight bucks an acre. one farmer said it was only good for squirrels. didn’t use manure, just squatting, didn’t expect to farm much again. got a bunch of stumps out, gave me fuel for a while. hired a guy and a team for plowing, but i held the plow myself. spent $14.72½ on tools, seeds, work. seed corn was free. ended up with twelve bushels of beans, eighteen of potatoes, some peas and sweet corn. corn and turnips didn’t make it.

made $23.44, spent $14.72½, so left with $8.71½. that’s not counting the $4.50 in produce i had left. given everything, even with the short time, i did better than most farmers in concord that year.

next year, i got smarter. spaded up about a third of an acre, learned from past years. didn’t bother with the big names in farming, like arthur young. figured out if you live simple, eat what you grow, don't swap it for fancy stuff, you only need a small patch of land. cheaper to spade it than use oxen, and easier to just move to a fresh spot than deal with manure. all the farm work can fit into spare summer hours, not tied down to animals. i’m not caught up in how things are usually done, more free than any farmer in concord. didn’t have a house or farm keeping me stuck, followed my crooked path. if my house burned or crops failed, wouldn’t change much for me.

men think they control animals, but it's the other way around. the herds got more freedom. we trade work with oxen, but they come out on top with more land and less hassle. haying is no joke, takes a lot out of you. no wise society would use animals for labor, but then again, there's never been a nation of philosophers. i wouldn't break a horse or a bull for work, cause then i'd just be a horse-man or herds-man. society thinks it wins using animals, but does it? does the stable-boy feel like a winner too? sure, some public works need oxen and horses, but maybe without them, we'd build things more worthy of us. when luxury and idle work take priority, a few end up doing all the work with the animals, becoming slaves to the strongest. it's not just about the beast within us, but the beast we work for outside.

in towns, barns dwarf houses. biggest houses for animals, but few places for free speech or worship. nations want to memorialize themselves with stone and architecture, but the real legacy is in thought and manners. a piece of good sense beats a moon-high monument. i prefer stones where they belong. the grandeur of Thebes was all show. better a simple stone wall for an honest man than a hundred-gated Thebes off course from life's true purpose. barbaric civilizations build grand temples, but true faith doesn't need that. most stonework's just a tomb. pyramids? more shocking that so many wasted lives building a tomb for some fool. i'd rather they drowned him and fed him to dogs. it's the same story everywhere, whether it's an Egyptian temple or a U.S. bank—vanity and bread. architects draw plans, stonecutters get to work, and when ancient eyes look down, people look up. high towers and monuments? a guy here tried digging to China, heard pots and kettles. i won't go admire his hole. folks focus on who built monuments, but i'm more interested in who didn't waste their time on them. now, back to my stats.

so i’ve been juggling a bunch of trades here—surveying, carpentry, odd jobs—pulled in $13.34. now, food costs over eight months, from july 4 to march 1, came down to this (excluding what i grew like potatoes and peas):

rice: $1.73½  
molasses: $1.73 (cheapest sweet stuff)  
rye meal: $1.04¾  
indian meal: $0.99¾ (more budget-friendly than rye)  
pork: $0.22  

failed experiments:  
flour: $0.88 (pricier than indian meal, both in cash and hassle)  
sugar: $0.80  
lard: $0.65  
apples: $0.25  
dried apple: $0.22  
sweet potatoes: $0.10  
one pumpkin: $0.06  
one watermelon: $0.02  
salt: $0.03  

so yeah, i ate $8.74 worth of food. but let's be real, most of you are in the same boat, and your shopping lists wouldn’t look any better printed out. i also caught fish and even cooked a woodchuck once, just to try it—let’s say it didn’t make it to the regular menu.

clothing and other expenses in the same period hit $8.40¾. oil and household stuff added $2.00.

total outgoes were:  
house: $28.12½  
farm for a year: $14.72½  
food eight months: $8.74  
clothing, etc., eight months: $8.40¾  
oil, &c., eight months: $2.00  

that’s $61.99¾ out.  

for income, farm produce brought in $23.44, and day-labor $13.34. that’s $36.78 in. so i’m down $25.21¾, which is about what i started with, and covered a comfy house and some independence.

now these numbers might seem random, but they show something. my food cost was about 27 cents a week. mostly rye, indian meal, rice, a bit of pork, molasses, salt, and water. fit for someone into indian philosophy, right? yeah, i dined out sometimes, but that’s a constant, so it doesn’t mess with the math.

two years in, i figured out you dont need much to get by food-wise. even here, you can eat simple like the critters and stay healthy. made a solid dinner just from some purslane (Portulaca oleracea) out of the cornfield, boiled and salted. threw in the Latin cause the name's kinda bland. really, what more do you need on a calm day than some boiled sweet-corn with salt? the little extras i added were just because i felt like it, not cause i needed it. funny how folks often go hungry not cause they lack essentials but cause they miss the fancy stuff. i know a woman who thinks her son died from just drinking water. 

so yeah, looking at this from a budget angle, not diet advice. dont try my simple eating unless you've got plenty stocked up.

started with pure cornmeal and salt. made hoe-cakes over an outdoor fire on a shingle or a stick. they tasted a bit smoky, piney even. tried flour too, but mixing rye with the cornmeal turned out best. cold days, id bake small loaves, tending them like they were precious. felt like growing fruit, wrapping them in cloth to keep the scent. dove into bread-making history, from unleavened times to the first sour dough, all the way to "good, sweet, wholesome bread." leaven’s crucial, some say, like the soul of bread. but i scalded my yeast once, and guess what? still made bread. dropped yeast after that. folks said id suffer without it, but here i am, still kicking. simpler without carrying a bottle around. no soda or acids either. just basic kneading like Cato described ages ago. sometimes though, couldn’t afford bread for a month. life adjusts.

new england's got the land for growing your own breadstuff, like rye and corn, instead of counting on up-and-down markets. but here in concord, you barely find fresh meal in the shops. folks feed the grain to their cattle and buy pricier flour that's not even better. i figured i could grow my own rye and corn, use a hand-mill, skip the rice and pork. for sweeteners, i experimented with pumpkins or beets. even thought about setting up some maples for syrup, but till then, there's plenty of other options. 

salt? might take a trip to the seashore for that, or just do without and drink less water. indians didn't seem to chase after it. 

by sidestepping trade for food and having a shelter already, just need clothes and fuel. my pants came from a farmer's family, which is a relief. i see a big drop from farmer to factory worker. fuel’s not hard to find in a new country. and if squatting wasn't an option, i could buy an acre for the same price they sold the land i worked on. but squatting actually adds value to the land. 

some skeptics question if i can live on just veggies. i tell them i could live on board nails. if they don't get that, they won't get much else. i'm all for these experiments, like living on hard, raw corn. squirrels do it, why not us? some folks might fret, but the human race learns from these trials.

my furniture list is simple. made some of it myself, rest didn’t cost a dime. here it is: bed, table, desk, three chairs, tiny mirror, tongs and andirons, kettle, skillet, frying-pan, dipper, wash-bowl, two knives and forks, three plates, one cup, one spoon, oil jug, molasses jug, and a lamp. nobody’s too poor to avoid sitting on a pumpkin—that’s just lazy. plenty of good chairs lying around for free if you look. furniture! i’m just glad i can sit and stand without needing a furniture store. it’s a joke seeing folks’ stuff packed up and carted around. doesn’t matter if the owner’s rich or poor, they always look broke with that load of junk. more you got, poorer you are. moving is just an excuse to ditch stuff, right? like getting rid of our old shells. dragging all that around is a pain. lucky fox that can leave his tail behind. muskrats will chew off a leg to escape. no surprise we’re stuck, always clinging to the junk we pretend we don’t. meet a guy, and you see all the stuff he owns trailing behind him. some folks talk about their “furniture” like it’s life or death. that’s when you know they’re trapped. even those without much still got some stashed away. england’s that old guy dragging too much baggage. ditch the big stuff, keep moving. when i see someone struggling with their whole life in a bundle, i pity them—not for having little, but for having to carry it all. if i gotta drag a trap, make it light, and don’t let it bite. maybe best not to step in one at all.

curtains don't cost me a dime cause i dont have any gazers to block out except the sun and the moon, and im fine with them peeking in. the moon doesn't spoil my milk or mess with my meat, and the sun wont ruin my furniture or fade my carpet. if it gets too hot, nature's got enough shade for me to duck under without adding to my housekeeping list. a lady once offered me a mat, but i had no room inside for it, nor time to shake it out, so i just wipe my feet on the grass outside. best to avoid starting trouble.

recently, i went to a deacon's auction. his life's work wasn't wasted: "the evil that men do lives after them." a lot of junk there, mostly from his dad's time. even found a dried tapeworm. instead of burning the lot, they auctioned it off. neighbors grabbed it all and stuffed it in their attics to gather dust till it's their turn to move on. when a man dies, he kicks the dust.

some old customs might be worth imitating. some tribes would shed their old stuff every year. bartram talks about the Mucclasse Indians' "busk" or "feast of first fruits." they’d gather all their worn-out stuff, clean their homes and town, and burn it all. after fasting and taking medicine for three days, they'd put out all the fires. then the high priest starts a new fire in the public square. everyone gets new flames, and they feast and celebrate with neighbors.

the mexicans did a similar thing every fifty-two years, thinking the world might end. i've rarely seen a truer sacrament than this, a real sign of inward change. even without a biblical record, it's like they got the word straight from heaven.

for over five years, i got by just working with my hands. six weeks of work a year covered all my living costs. rest of the time, especially in winter, i had for study. tried teaching, but the costs didn’t match up to the income. had to dress and act a certain way, didn’t like it, and it wasted my time. teaching for money, not passion? yeah, didn’t work. tried trade too, but figured i'd spend ten years just getting started and probably end up in a bad spot. was worried i’d end up running a “good business” and hating it.

when i was figuring out how to make a living, had some rough lessons trying to please others. thought about picking huckleberries. it seemed simple enough, with small profits. i didn’t need much, so it seemed doable. while others jumped into trade or careers, i thought of wandering hills, picking berries, and selling them. even thought about gathering wild herbs or evergreens for townsfolk who missed the woods. but learned the hard way that trade taints everything it touches, even if it’s something pure.

i valued freedom and didn’t want to chase after fancy stuff like rich furniture or gourmet meals. if others can balance those without losing themselves, more power to them. some folks love work just to stay busy or out of trouble, but i don’t have advice for them. for me, day-labor seemed the most free. work 30-40 days a year and you’re good. the sun sets, work ends, and you’re free to do what you love. employers, though, they’re stuck in the grind year-round.

bottom line, keeping yourself on this earth ain’t a struggle if you live simply and smart. living shouldn’t just be about working hard unless sweating comes easy to you.

i knew a guy, had some land, said he'd live like me if he had the means. but i wouldnt want anyone copying my way. by the time they figure it out, i might be onto something else. everybody needs to find their own path, not just follow what their parents or neighbors did. build, plant, sail, whatever they want, but dont stop them from doing it. like a sailor with the polestar, you might not get to your destination quick, but you'll stay on course.

and whats true for one is truer for many. a big house isnt that much more expensive than a small one, since you share things like the roof and walls. i liked living alone though. usually cheaper to build for yourself than to convince someone else of shared benefits. and shared walls can lead to bad neighbors or maintenance issues. true cooperation is rare. if you have faith in what you're doing, you can cooperate anywhere. without it, you just live like everyone else, no matter who youre with.

to really cooperate is to earn a living together. i heard about two guys planning to travel the world, one working his way, the other with money in his pocket. they'd split at the first tough spot. if you travel alone, you can leave today. with someone else, you wait till they're ready, and that might take forever.

i've heard folks say i'm selfish, not into the whole helping others scene. truth is, i haven't really jumped into those projects. did my duty here and there, sure, but when it comes to supporting a family in town, i've offered, and they always prefer to keep their situation. seems like you need a knack for charity like anything else. tried it, didn't suit me. wouldn't drop my work to do what society thinks i should, saving the universe and all. if someone's got it in them to do that, i'd say keep at it, even if folks call it wrong.

i bet i'm not alone in this. others probably feel the same. if i'm doing something, can't promise my neighbors will call it good, but i figure i'd be a good hire for something. that's for my boss to figure out. any good i do is mostly accidental. folks say start where you are, aim to help. i'd say, focus on being good yourself. like the sun, don't stop at a little light. keep getting brighter, do your thing, and the world'll get the good just by orbiting you. remember Phaeton? tried to show off, got the sun’s chariot for a day, and burned everything. lesson there, stick to your path.

goodness gone bad stinks worse than anything. if i knew someone was coming to do me good as a mission, id run like hell. it's like the simoom, that desert wind that chokes you with dust. i'd rather face natural evil. a man isnt good to me just cause he'll feed me if im starving or pull me out of a ditch. a dog can do that. real help is more than just being nice. you hear about philanthropists like howard, but what do they do for us when we really need help? i never heard of a charity meeting that aimed to help someone like me.

the jesuits tried their tricks on the indians, but some of those folks were tough. they'd come up with new ways to take the pain, and the missionaries' comforts fell flat. they didn't care about being treated well or badly, and they had their own way of forgiving enemies. pretty close to just letting it all go.

Look, if you want to help the poor, give them what actually helps. Might just be setting an example instead of handing out cash. If you do give money, be involved. Don’t just throw it at them and walk away. We screw up on this a lot. Sometimes it's not about being cold and hungry, it's about being dirty and ragged because that's partly their choice, not just bad luck. Give them money, and they might just buy more rags.

I used to feel sorry for those Irish guys cutting ice, all ragged while I was freezing in my own clothes. One time, one of them fell in the pond and came over to dry off. Turned out he had three pairs of pants on. Dirty, yeah, but plenty warm. He didn't even need the extra clothes I tried to give him. Made me realize I could use a flannel shirt more than he needed more rags.

A lot of people attack problems at the wrong end. They spend a ton on charity but live in a way that makes the problem worse. It's like a slave owner giving Sundays off and thinking that's enough. Some folks think they help by hiring the poor to work in their kitchens. Maybe they should try working there themselves. Bragging about giving a tenth of your income to charity? Maybe rethink how you use the other nine-tenths first. Society only gets back a little of what you have. Is that generosity or just the system being lazy?

Philanthropy gets too much credit. It’s our selfishness that makes us overrate it. I talked to a poor guy here in Concord who praised someone for being "kind" to him. Those who throw money around get more love than the ones who actually try to make a difference. I heard this lecturer go on about England's heroes, listing Shakespeare and Newton, then putting some Christian do-gooders above them. Felt like a load of nonsense. Those folks might’ve been nice, but they weren’t England’s best. Just its best philanthropists, maybe.

i won't take anything from philanthropy, but let's give credit to everyone making a real difference. a man's goodness isn't just his uprightness and benevolence, which are like leaves and stems. those plants, for making herb tea for the sick, serve a minor role and are mostly for quacks. i want the flower and fruit of a person; some fragrance and flavor in our interactions. his goodness can't be just a temporary act; it should be constant and effortless, like charity that covers a multitude of sins. too often, philanthropists surround us with their past griefs and call it sympathy. we should share our courage, not despair, our health, not disease, and make sure it doesn't spread like a virus. 

where's the crying voice coming from? which place has the people we want to enlighten? who's that reckless man we aim to redeem? if something's wrong with someone, if he's got a bellyache—since that's where sympathy sits—he starts trying to fix the world. being a microcosm, he finds, rightly so, that the world’s been eating green apples; to him, the globe’s a huge green apple, with the risk that people nibble it before it's ripe. so his drastic philanthropy reaches everywhere, even though it's mostly for himself, the world gets a bit riper, life gets sweeter, less raw. i never dreamed of a greater mistake than ive made. i don't know a worse man than myself.

it's not the reformer's sympathy that gets him down, but his own issues. fix that, let spring come, and he'll leave his generous group without looking back. i don't lecture against tobacco because i never chewed it; former users have to do that. but there are other things ive chewed, which i could speak against. if you get caught in philanthropy, don't let your left hand know what your right hand's doing, because it's not worth it. save the drowning and tie your shoes. take your time and start some honest work.

our manners have slipped, talking like we're saints. hymn-books sound like we're cursing God in a sweet tune, barely grateful for life’s gift. prophets and redeemers? they seem to soothe our fears more than boost our hopes. you won't find a simple joy in being alive, or a true praise of God anywhere. all health and success feels good, even if it's distant. disease and failure? they drag me down, no matter the sympathy shared.

if we want to truly heal mankind in a natural way, we gotta start by being as simple and healthy as nature itself. clear the clouds from our heads, soak up some life. don't just oversee the poor; aim to be one of the world's greats.

in the Gulistan by Sheik Sadi, a wise man said the cypress is called azad, or free, because it doesn't fruit, always thriving. unlike fruit trees that cycle with seasons. be like the cypress, not tied to the temporary. if you have plenty, share like the date tree. if not, be free like the cypress.

---

poverty’s got its pretensions: thinking it deserves a place among the stars just because it fosters some lazy virtue in humble settings. tearing away the passions that let virtues thrive, turning folks to stone. we don't need forced temperance or joyless existence. we value virtues that go beyond limits—bold acts, grand generosity, wisdom without bounds. aim for the heroic, like the legends of old. leave your cell, and learn from the greats.

where i lived, and what i lived for

at some point, you start looking at every spot as a potential place for a house. ive done this all around my area, checking out farms, tasting wild apples, talking shop with the farmers. i'd imagine buying each one, pricing it out in my head, even imagining the mortgage. i got pretty good at this imaginary real estate game. my friends thought i was some kind of broker. any place i sat, i could picture living there, with the landscape spread out all around me.

i'd find spots perfect for a house, maybe too far from the village for some, but to me, the village was too far from them. id live there in my head for a bit, thinking how id deal with the seasons, watch spring come in. future folks who build here should know, ive already imagined it all. in an afternoon, id plan out orchards, woodlots, pastures, deciding which trees to keep or clear, then id let it be. richer for what i could leave alone.

evenings out here, you catch the weirdest sounds. first time i heard a distant cow, i thought it was some folks with guitars wandering around, but no, just a cow doing its thing. kinda funny how natural sounds blend with human music. gets you thinking about how it all meshes together.

each summer night around seven-thirty, right after the train rumbles past, the whippoorwills start up. like clockwork, serious. they park themselves on a stump or the roof and just go at it for half an hour. sometimes you catch a few of them out of sync, so close you hear every little cluck and buzz. had one circle me like it was tied to me, probably guarding its eggs. they keep at it all night and hit their stride again by dawn.

when the other birds quiet down, the screech owls kick in. their cries are like something out of a ben jonson play—proper eerie. not your usual owl sounds, these are deep and sorrowful, like the ghosts of old loves haunting the woods. they remind me of music's darker side, the kind that aches. they seem like restless spirits, stuck in the woods paying for past misdeeds. one owl starts with its mournful call on one side of the pond, and another echoes from the other side, like they're in some sad conversation.

then there's this hooting owl. up close, it's the saddest sound, like nature's trying to capture the last cries of something losing hope. has this gurgling tone that makes you think of someone who's lost their way. reminds me of ghouls, but from a distance, it's almost musical. day or night, summer or winter, it's got its own kind of charm.

i like that owls got their thing going on. they do the crazy hoots for us, fit for swamps and dusk woods untouched by daylight. they’re like the twilight thoughts we all have but ignore. sun shines all day on some wild swamp, spruce draped in lichens, hawks above, chickadees in the evergreens, partridges and rabbits hiding below. then night hits, and a new crew wakes up, showing what nature means in a different way.

late at night, hear wagons rumbling over bridges—carries far in the dark—dogs barking, maybe a lonely cow in a far-off barnyard. by the water, bullfrogs are belting out like old drunkards, trying to hold a party in their murky lake. they croak loud and serious, mocking the fun they remember. wine's gone flat, just fills their bellies now. biggest frog, chin on a heart-leaf napkin, gulps water with a loud _tr-r-r-oonk, tr-r-r-oonk, tr-r-r-oonk!_ and from across the lake, the echo comes back, each frog taking his turn. when it’s made the round, the lead frog croaks his satisfaction, and they all follow, down to the smallest, flabbiest one. then it starts over until morning breaks, and only the old patriarch’s left, bellowing _troonk_ here and there, waiting for an answer.

never really heard a rooster from my clearing but thought about keeping one just for the noise. they got a sound like nothing else, those wild Indian pheasants. if they could live out there without us taming them, they’d outshine all the other noises in the woods, like geese or owls. imagine hens filling the quiet spots between their calls. no surprise folks added them to their flocks, eggs and drumsticks aside. walking in winter woods where they roam, hearing them crow clear and sharp over everything else, it’d get people up early. who wouldn’t try to rise before them every day, chasing health and wisdom? poets everywhere sing about this foreign bird’s call. chanticleer fits in anywhere. always healthy, strong lungs, never down. even sailors hear him on the seas, but he never woke me. no pets here—no dogs, cats, cows, pigs, or hens. just nature’s own sounds. an old-timer might go mad from the quiet. no rats either. only squirrels, a whippoorwill, a blue-jay, wild geese, a loon, a fox at night. no larks or orioles. no yard or cocks crowing, just nature right up to your door. young forest creeping in, sumachs and blackberries in the cellar, pines rubbing shingles. no gale blowing shutters away, just a pine snapped or uprooted for firewood. no front yard, no path, just raw land. solitude.

sometimes you just walk outside and everything clicks. like walking along the pond, wind biting a bit, but it feels right. bullfrogs start their night song, whippoorwills in the mix. even with the wind stirring things up, my calm's still there. like surface ripples that don't mess with the lake's peace. it's dark now but life's still buzzing, foxes and skunks on the move—nature's own night crew.

back home, i find signs of visitors—flowers left behind, names scratched on leaves. folks who drop by take a piece of the woods with them, leave something behind—like a willow ring on my table. i can tell who's been here just by small traces: bent grass, shoe prints, or a cigar's scent. sometimes even catch a whiff of a passing traveler from way out there.

out here, space isn't an issue. i've got my own stretch of woods, neighbors far off. the woods hug the horizon, train tracks peek by the pond, fences line the road. it's like having my own piece of the world, quiet as a prairie. feels like any place—asia, africa, new england. nights are mine alone unless someone's fishing at the pond, but they dont stay long. darkness has its own kind of solitude people still shy away from, even with all the lights and beliefs we carry.

sometimes you find the best company in things you wouldn't expect. nature's got this way of keeping you grounded, even when you're feeling low. out here, storms sound more like music if you're listening right. there's no reason to sink into gloom if you're living with nature and paying attention. 

like today, the rain's keeping me inside, but it's doing good for my beans, so i can't complain. sure, i can't hoe them right now, but the rain's worth more than that work. if it keeps on and ruins some seeds, it'll still help the grass on the hills, and that's a win.

sometimes i feel like i've got a special deal with the universe, like i'm being looked after in ways others might not be. i've never felt truly alone, except once when i first got to the woods. thought maybe i needed people around, felt a bit off. but then the rain came, and i felt this connection with everything around me. made human company seem less important. 

since then, every little detail in nature feels like it's reaching out to me, like we're on the same wavelength. even the wild places feel like home now.

rainy days, man. they're something else. stuck inside, listening to the storm outside, gives you time to think. reminds me of one time, lightning hit a big old pine across the pond. left this perfect spiral groove, like nature was whittling a walking stick. saw it again the other day, and it's still there, like a reminder of that crazy storm from eight years back. people ask if i get lonely out here, especially when it's raining or snowing. but honestly, why would i? we're just a speck in space, right? what really separates people? it's not distance. it's something else. i think about where we choose to set our roots. it's not always near people or places. it's about being close to what gives us life, like a willow by the water. once met a guy on the road who couldn't understand why i'd give up the comforts of life. told him i liked it just fine. and i wasn't joking. we let distractions run our lives. but the real stuff, the power behind it all, that's always close. it's not about who's next to us; it's about what we are part of. how deep is the influence of those powers we can't even see or hear? they're there, part of everything.

people get all dressed up, hearts cleaned out, ready to make offerings to their ancestors. it's like a sea of intelligence out there, surrounding us, above, left, right, everywhere.

we're in an experiment, kinda interesting. can we skip the gossip crowd and just think our own thoughts for a bit? confucius nailed it: "virtue needs neighbors."

thinking deeply, we can step back from actions, see the rush of life pass like a torrent. we're not just part of nature; we can be like driftwood or up in the sky watching it all. i might be moved by a play, or not by something real. i know myself as a collection of thoughts, feeling a split where part of me stands back, just watching. when life's show ends, that part leaves. it’s all a fiction to it. this split can make us lousy neighbors sometimes.

i find being alone most of the time healthy. even the best company tires me out. i love solitude, never found a companion as good as being alone. we're lonelier out in the world than in our rooms. a thinking or working man is always alone. solitude isn't about distance from others. a student in a crowded college is as solitary as a desert dervish. a farmer works alone all day fine, but at night needs people around. he wonders how a student sits alone without getting the blues, not seeing the student working mentally like he does physically, each seeking rest in their own way.

we spend too much time crammed together in little spaces, meeting day after day without anything new to bring to the table. etiquette and politeness are just band-aids to keep us from going at each other's throats. running into folks at the post office or around the fire every night gets old, and we end up tripping over each other, losing respect. less might be more when it comes to real conversations. think about factory workers, always surrounded, even in their dreams. maybe it'd be better if folks were more spread out, like how i live out here. a person's worth isn't in the touch.

heard of a guy lost in the woods, starving, but his mind gave him company with crazy visions. on the flip side, when we're healthy, we can find company in the everyday, realizing we're never truly alone.

i've got plenty of company at home, especially in the morning when no one drops by. i'm like the loon on the pond, not lonely at all. walden pond's got its own company in its blue waters. the sun's alone, unless clouds mess with it. god's alone too, but the devil? he's got a crowd. i'm as lonely as a dandelion or a bumblebee, or a january thaw, or the north star.

sometimes on winter nights, i get visits from an old settler who supposedly made walden pond himself. we share stories and laughs, no need for apples or cider. and there's an old lady nearby, hidden to most, with an herb garden i love visiting. she tells tales older than time, knows the roots of every fable. she's tough, weathered, might even outlive her kids.

nature's got this way of making you feel small and huge at the same time. sun, wind, rain doing their thing, summer or winter, always there cheering us on. it's like they know us, feel what we feel. imagine if the whole world would mourn with you when you've got a reason. am i not part of this earth? aren't we all a bit of leaves and dirt?

what's the secret to staying well and content? not some old remedy from grandpa's time, but nature's own medicine. nature's been around, outliving everything with her own way of staying young. instead of some fake elixir from a dusty cart, give me a gulp of that fresh morning air. if folks won't take it straight from sunrise, maybe we should bottle it for those who've lost their morning pass. but it won't last long, even in the coolest spot, chasing the dawn westward. i'm not about old remedies but more like keeping things fresh, like hebes springtime touch.

visitors

i like folks as much as anybody, hanging on like a leech when a lively one comes by. no hermit here, id outlast the bar if i needed to.

got three chairs at home: one for me, two for company, three for a crowd. when more show up, they squeeze in or stand. you'd be surprised how many people can fit in a small place. ive had twenty-five, thirty folks packed in, and we still leave feeling like strangers. houses today, with their endless rooms and grand halls, feel too big for the people in them. like mice in a mansion, they come out with a little squeak.

sometimes a small house feels even smaller when you try to have deep conversations. you need space for big thoughts to stretch their legs. it's like throwing a rock in a pond, hoping the ripples dont crash into each other. when talking gets real, you find yourself pushing your chair back till you're practically in opposite corners. not much room for grand ideas in a tight space.

my real "best" room was the pine woods out back. that's where we went when the conversation needed room to breathe. no fancy setups, just nature keeping things tidy.

if just one guest showed, they'd join me for a simple meal. no fuss, just stirring pudding or baking bread on the side. but if twenty showed up, dinner wasnt the focus. we didnt need to eat to have a good time. somehow, the energy stayed up. could host a thousand like that. if anyone left hungry, trust me, i felt for them.

funny how easy it is to change traditions. no need to impress with dinners. nothing kept me from a man's house like a big deal about feeding me. felt like a polite way to say, "dont come back." those visits? not missing them. a visitor once left me a line from Spenser on a walnut leaf. would make a fine motto for my place.

little place they found, not much for comfort but they weren't looking for any. rest was the meal and having what they needed. noblest folks find their peace inside, not outside.

Winslow, who later ran Plymouth, took a walk with a buddy to see Massasoit. long trek through the woods. tired and hungry when they got there. king welcomed them warmly, but no dinner was mentioned. come night, they all piled onto one bed. Winslow and his friend at one end, king and queen at the other. just some planks and a thin mat. two more squeezed in, made sleeping rougher than the walk. by one in the afternoon next day, Massasoit brought a couple fish, about three times a bream's size. boiled up, forty folks shared. most got a taste. only meal in two days and nights. if not for a partridge one of them bought, they'd have left with empty stomachs. worried about going faint from hunger and the singing that kept them up, they hit the road. now, the sleeping arrangements might have been tough, but it was meant as an honor. and for food, well, the locals didn't have much themselves. no point in empty apologies. they tightened their belts and kept quiet. next time Winslow visited, times were good, and food was plenty.

people aren't hard to find if you look. had more visitors in the woods than ever. met folks in better ways than i could have elsewhere. fewer came for nonsense. distance weeded them out. deep in the solitude sea, far from society's rivers, only the best settled around me. signs of untapped lands on the other side drifted in too.

who should show up today but a real old-world type, name fit like poetry. Canadian, woodchopper, post-maker, fifty posts a day. last meal? woodchuck his dog caught. knows of Homer, says without books, rainy days would be lost. hasn't read one cover to cover in a while though. priest taught him Greek in the old country. now i'm translating Achilles’ words for him. "why crying like a girl, Patroclus?"

“or did you hear from home?
     they say Menoetius, Actor’s son, still lives,
     and Peleus, Æacus' son, with the Myrmidons,
     if either died, we’d be grieving."

he carries a big bundle of white-oak bark, says it's for a sick man. "no harm in that today," he figures. doesn’t know much about Homer, but thinks he’s great. simple guy, untouched by vice or disease. left Canada at sixteen, wants a farm someday. built like a tank, moves easy though. sunburnt neck, bushy hair, sleepy blue eyes that sometimes spark up. wears a gray cap, rough coat, cowhide boots. loves his meat, hauls lunch past my place in a tin pail—cold meats, sometimes woodchucks, coffee in a stone bottle. he’ll offer me a sip sometimes. strolls through my bean-field, no rush, no Yankee urgency. he’s in no hurry to break a sweat for just room and board. dog catches a woodchuck, he’ll backtrack to dress it, maybe spend half an hour wondering if he can stash it in the pond till night. comes by in the morning saying, “pigeons are thick! if working wasn’t my trade, i’d hunt enough meat for a week in a day.”

he’s a skilled chopper, adds his own flair. cuts trees low so sprouts grow strong and sleds slide over stumps. pares trees down to a splinter you could snap by hand, just for the challenge.

met this guy who was quiet and kept to himself but was always happy. kinda like a well of good humor. id see him in the woods, chopping trees, and he'd laugh and say something in Canadian French, even though he spoke English fine. he'd stop work, lie on a pine trunk, peel bark, roll it up, chew it, and just laugh and chat. he’d laugh so hard sometimes he'd end up rolling on the ground. he'd say, "by George! chopping's all the fun i need." he'd fire off his pocket pistol for kicks, just saluting himself. in winter, he'd warm his coffee over a fire, sit eating lunch with chickadees landing on him. he'd say he liked having the "little fellers" around.

he was all about the physical stuff, tough like the trees and rocks. asked him once if he ever got tired, he said, "gorrappit, i never was tired in my life." but emotionally and spiritually, he was like a kid. learned from Catholic priests in a way that kept him trusting and reverent without waking him up. nature gave him a strong body and satisfaction, so he could live a long life like a child. genuine guy, no introductions needed—like meeting a woodchuck. folks paid him for work, but he kept his opinions to himself. humility wasn't even a thing with him; it was just how he was. thought of wiser men as demigods. if you said someone important was coming, he'd just figure they'd handle everything. never heard praise. thought writers and preachers did miracles. when i told him i wrote, he thought i meant handwriting, which he was pretty good at. sometimes id see his parish name written nicely in the snow and know he'd been there. asked if he ever wanted to write his thoughts. he said he'd read and written letters for others, but writing thoughts? nah, couldn't figure out what to put first. worried about spelling too!

heard a story once about a wise guy — a reformer type — who asked this fellow if he wanted to change the world. guy just chuckled, Canadian accent, like he'd never even thought about it, and said, "No, I like it well enough." funny thing, to anyone who didn't know him, seemed like he didn't know much about anything. but sometimes, i caught a glimpse of something different in him. couldn't tell if he was as wise as Shakespeare or as clueless as a kid. one person said seeing him strolling around, whistling with his cap on, was like spotting a prince in disguise.

his library was an almanac and arithmetic book. he was pretty good with numbers. thought the almanac was a mini encyclopedia, and honestly, it kinda is. loved asking him about all the reforms going on. he'd always look at things plain and practical. could he live without factories? wore Vermont gray once, it worked fine for him. tea and coffee? hemlock leaves soaked in water was his thing. could he do without money? he'd explain how money's handy, like if he owned an ox and needed needles and thread, trading bits of the ox each time'd be a mess.

he got institutions right because he spoke from how they mattered to him. once heard about Plato's man being a featherless biped and thought the knees bending wrong was a big deal. loved to talk, said he could yap all day. asked him once if he picked up a new idea. "Good Lord," he said, "a man working like me, if he remembers his old ideas, he's doing alright. gotta focus on the weeds if you're hoeing with a racer."

asked him if he was ever satisfied with himself. "Satisfied!" he'd say, "some folks are happy with one thing, others with another. some guy might just want to sit all day by the fire with a full belly." never could steer him towards anything spiritual. for him, it was all about simple practicality, like most folks. mention any lifestyle change, and he'd just say it was too late but always believed in honesty and virtues like that.

had this guy. bit of originality to him, like he was thinking for himself. rare enough id go miles to see it. he'd hesitate, maybe not always clear, but there was a solid thought behind his words. his thinking was more raw, tied to his animal instincts. more promising than some book-smart types, but rarely hit that reportable mark. he figured there might be real genius in the most humble folks, those who see their own way or dont bother to pretend. deep like walden pond, though maybe dark and muddy.

people would trek out to see me, use a glass of water as their excuse. told em i drank from the pond. pointed it out, offered a dipper. even far out there, i got the spring visitors. some curious ones. half-witted folks from the almshouse would stop by. id get them talking, make wit the subject, and it paid off. found some wiser than the so-called town overseers.

one day, a simple pauper, used to being treated as nothing, came by. wanted to live like i did. told me straight, thought he was “deficient in intellect.” said the Lord made him so but cared for him the same. “always been like this,” he said. “weak in the head. the Lord’s will, i guess.” he was a puzzle. genuine and true, in humbling himself he seemed almost exalted. at first, thought it might be some clever tactic. but with his truth and frankness, felt like our talks could go beyond what the wise manage.

got some visitors who aren't the town's poor but might as well be. they don't just want your help, they expect it. they say they're never gonna help themselves. i figure, if you're not starving, you're welcome, but you better have a good appetite. charity cases aren't guests. some folks don't know when to leave, even when i'm busy and getting distant. they come in all types, especially during migrating season. some have more wits than they know what to do with; runaway slaves with manners like they're always listening for hounds, asking silently if i'll turn them in. helped one real runaway head north once.

then there's men with one idea, like a hen with a duckling, and others with too many ideas, all chasing one bug and getting lost. one guy wanted a guestbook like at tourist spots, but i remember faces just fine.

noticed my visitors have quirks. kids and young women like the woods, peering in ponds and picking flowers. businessmen and farmers talk solitude but don’t really mean it. they're too busy with life. ministers act like they own God, can't handle other opinions. doctors, lawyers, nosy folks checking my place when i'm out—how’d mrs. —— know my sheets weren't up to her standards? young men who'd stopped being young, sticking to safe careers, said i couldn't do much good out here. but that's the point. the old, timid folks see danger everywhere; life's full of risk, but it’s only dangerous if you think it is. they treat the village like a fortress, probably wouldn't go berry-picking without a first aid kit. truth is, if you're alive, there's always a risk of dying—less risk if you're half-dead to start. you sit on as many risks as you run.

and the reformers, the real pests, think i’m always bragging about my house and life. they don’t realize i've got a third line for them: they're the ones bugging the man in the house i built.

i didn't worry about hen-harriers since i had no chickens to lose, but those men-harriers, that's another story.

more visitors this time. kids picking berries, railroad guys in clean shirts taking a sunday stroll, fishermen, hunters, poets, philosophers. honest folks looking for a break from the village grind. i'd greet them all, "welcome, englishmen!" had some good chats with their kind before.

my beans though, they needed attention. got seven miles of rows planted. earlier plants were shooting up while i was still getting the last ones in the ground. didn't know why i took to this labor — small but mighty. loved those beans though, felt grounded like antaeus. why grow them? only the heavens know. spent all summer turning the land from wild growth to bean fields. what would the beans teach me? no idea, but i tended them daily. broad leaves, good to see. dews, rains, and some soil fertility were my allies. enemies? worms, chilly days, woodchucks. woodchucks chewed up a quarter-acre. but soon enough, the beans would get too tough for them.

i remember, at four, moving from boston through these woods to the pond. oldest memory i got. now, playing my flute here, the echoes bounce back. pines stand older than me. cooked my supper with their fallen stumps. new growth's rising, making a fresh view for new eyes. same johnswort's back in the pasture. i've even added to this landscape of my childhood dreams. now, bean leaves, corn blades, and potato vines mark my presence.

planted two and a half acres of upland. land was cleared fifteen years back. i pulled up a few cords of stumps myself. didn't bother with manure. found arrowheads while hoeing — turns out an old nation planted here before us, wore out the soil a bit for beans and corn.

early mornings, before any critters darted across the road, and before the sun climbed over the shrubs, i got to work in the bean-field while the dew was still fresh. farmers told me to work then, and i agree—dew helps. barefoot, like sculpting in the wet sand, i started pulling up those arrogant weeds. later, the sun would scorch my feet. my routine was slow, stepping back and forth across the gravelly patch, between green rows of beans. one end was shaded by some oaks, the other led to a blackberry patch that deepened its colors as i made rounds.

my work was simple: clear weeds, pile fresh soil around bean stalks, and make sure the earth spoke beans, not grass. without horses, hired hands, or fancy gear, i worked slower, got to know my beans better. even when it felt like drudgery, hand labor never feels like wasted time. it’s got its own lesson, maybe even a classic one, for those who care to see it. i was the local farmer, plodding along, while folks in carriages passed by, critiquing my fields, “Beans so late! Peas so late!”—i kept planting when others were already hoeing. some travelers asked outright if i actually lived there, seeing no manure in the furrows, suggesting i use chip dirt or ashes. their comments let me know how my field ranked among others. 

my plot was a kind of middle ground, neither wild nor fully tamed. like some places being half-civilized, my field was half-cultivated, with beans nodding back to their wild roots. they were happy to be halfway wild, and i was happy to be there with them.

up in the birch, a brown-thrasher's singing away. makes you glad for the company while you're planting. he’s got his own little tune, like he's telling you what to do with the seeds: "drop it, cover it, pull it up." but don't worry, this ain't corn, so he's not a threat. funny how his music works with your planting, better than any old leached ashes or plaster. cheap top dressing, but i trust it.

as i dig fresh soil with the hoe, i come across stuff from folks who lived here long before. old tools, bits of pottery, mixed with stones that saw either indian fires or just the sun. when the hoe hits the stones, it rings out through the woods. it’s like playing music while i work. it's not just beans i'm tending to; it's a connection to everything around. makes me think of city folks at their fancy concerts, but this right here beats that.

the night-hawk's up there in the afternoons, swooping around like it’s part of the sky. does its thing, dropping fast with a sound like the sky's tearing, but it’s all still whole. they lay eggs on bare ground where few find them. nature’s got this harmony, like the hawk and the wave are kin. sometimes i watch hen-hawks up high, moving like my own thoughts. wild pigeons pass with that soft sound, and the hoe might turn up a spotted salamander, like a touch of ancient Egypt in my own backyard. all this, right here in the field, is the best show i could ask for.

on gala days, the town lets off its big guns. sounds like popguns out here in the woods. sometimes a bit of music drifts over. when im out in the bean-field, those big guns sound like a puffball popping. if there's a military parade i dont know about, i'll feel this itch in the horizon, like somethings about to erupt. then a breeze comes by and clues me in on the "trainers." sounds like bees swarming and the neighbors trying to coax them back with kitchen clatter. once it quiets down, i know they've herded them all back to the hive and they're thinking about the honey.

felt good knowing massachusetts' liberties were safe, let me get back to hoeing with some peace of mind, trusting the future.

when there were more bands, it was like the village turned into a giant bellows, buildings breathing in and out. sometimes the music was really something, like a trumpet calling out, and i felt like i could take on a mexican—why settle for less?—looked around for a woodchuck or skunk to test my mettle. those tunes felt as distant as crusaders in the horizon, with the elm trees shaking a bit over the village. it was a great day, though from my clearing, the sky looked the same as always.

working with beans was a strange relationship—planting, hoeing, harvesting, threshing, sorting, and selling them. selling was the hardest. i even ate some. i was set on knowing beans. when they grew, i hoed from five in the morning till noon, then moved on to other tasks. you get to know weeds well—there's a lot of repetition in it, tearing up their delicate roots, showing no mercy with the hoe. that's roman wormwood, pigweed, sorrel, piper-grass—chop them up, turn their roots to the sun, dont leave a shadow, or they'll be green again in two days. a long battle with weeds, those trojans with sun and rain on their side. daily, the beans watched me, hoe in hand, thinning out their foes, filling the trenches with the fallen. many a proud weed, towering above the rest, met my hoe and bit the dust.

those summer days, while some folks were off with the arts in boston or rome, others meditating in india, and others trading in london or new york, i was out in the fields with the new england farmers, working the land. not because i needed beans for eating—i'm more a rice guy, to be honest—but because there's something in the work itself. it's like a parable waiting to happen. i didn't give those beans any manure, and i definitely didn't hoe them all the time, but when i did, i did it right, and it paid off. evelyn says there's nothing like turning the soil with a spade, and i felt that. earth's got this pull, a magnetism, that makes it all worth the effort. twelve bushels of beans came out of it.

lesson learned: plant common small white bush beans around early june, rows three feet by eighteen inches apart. fresh, round, unmixed seeds. watch for worms, replant where needed. keep an eye out for woodchucks, they'll eat the tender leaves and buds like nobody's business. and harvest early to dodge frost and get a good crop. saves a lot of loss.

learned something new again. thought maybe id stop planting beans and corn like a madman next summer. instead, id plant seeds of sincerity, truth, simplicity, faith, innocence. see if they grow with less effort. figured this soil aint exhausted for those. but another summer rolled by, and another. gotta tell you, Reader, those seeds, if they were virtues, mustve been duds. didnt sprout. folks usually just brave or timid like their dads. each year, they plant corn and beans just like ages ago. saw an old guy doing it for the 70th time, holes in the ground, not for himself to lie in. why not try something new? why so focused on crops and not on raising a new kind of people? meeting someone with qualities like truth or justice would be real food for the soul. ambassadors ought to bring home seeds of worth and friendliness, spread them wide. we shouldnt stand on ceremony with sincerity. most folks, i dont meet at all, too busy with their beans. theyre like swallows on the ground, wings twitching like they might fly. bread might not always fill us, but generosity and simple joy sure make us feel alive.

old myths say farming was once a sacred art, but now it's rushed and careless. we chase big farms and big crops, but forget the sacredness of it. no real ceremonies left, just cattle-shows and thanksgivings, driven by profit. we don't honor the earth like the old romans, who saw farming as a noble life. now it's all greed and treating land as just property. farming's degraded, and farmers miss the bigger picture.

we forget the sun shines on fields and forests the same. they all reflect his light like a big garden. we should trust that light and heat with open hearts. sure, i harvest beans, but this field looks beyond me to something better that nourishes it. beans grow for woodchucks too. wheat isn't the only hope; its grain isn't the only thing it bears. our harvest can't really fail. let's celebrate weeds that feed birds just like squirrels don't worry about chestnuts. real farmers let go of anxiety, finishing each day's work and letting go of their claim to the land's produce.

mornings were busy, either working or getting lost in a book or two. when i'd finally clean up, id head to the pond for a swim. washed off the day. afternoons were mine to spend how i wanted. every now and then, id wander into the village, catch up on gossip. it's funny how the chatter there can be as refreshing as a walk in the woods. different sounds, but the same feeling.

the village felt like an open-air newsroom. there's always a group hanging out, catching the latest news, like they're soaking it in. id see them perched like birds on a wire, leaning here and there, eyes darting around. they're the first to hear what's new. grocery stores, bars, post office, and the bank—those are the village's heartbeat. the setup's like a gauntlet for travelers, everyone gets a piece of the action. prime spots for gossip go for a premium, and the folks on the edges just pick up the scraps.

shops lure you in with everything from food to trinkets. you gotta navigate it all, like dodging sirens with a song. sometimes id make a quick exit, other times id drop in on a familiar place, hear the latest, and slip out the back. then id be back in the woods, away from it all.

staying late in town, there's something about launching into the night. especially if it's dark and stormy. you leave a bright spot like a village parlor or lecture room with a bag of rye or Indian meal on your shoulder, heading for your quiet spot in the woods. all secured, you dive into your thoughts, leaving the body to steer itself. never lost or worried, even in rough storms. woods are darker than you'd think, even on regular nights. sometimes i'd look up to see the path between trees or feel my way with my feet, navigating by touch alone, passing between two pines just 18 inches apart, even on the blackest nights. coming home on such nights, path underfoot but unseen, absent-minded till the latch lifts. sometimes i couldn't recall the walk at all. thought maybe the body would find its way home on its own, like a hand to the mouth. when folks stayed late and it got dark, i'd guide them to the cart-path, tell them to feel their way more than see. one pitch-black night, sent two fishermen off through the woods. they knew the way, a mile out, but ended up wandering half the night, soaked by morning showers. heard of folks getting lost in town too, darkness so thick you could slice it. some out in wagons had to stay overnight; others, on a simple call, wandered off track. being lost in the woods is a surprising, valuable experience. even in daytime snowstorms, a familiar road can seem foreign. by night, that confusion just gets worse. in our simplest walks, we navigate like pilots with known landmarks, and when truly lost, we see the vastness of nature. getting turned around once is enough to feel lost. it's only when lost, when we lose the world, that we start finding ourselves, seeing where we really are and how deep our connections go.

one afternoon, late summer, went to the village for a cobbler fix. got tossed in jail 'cause i didn't pay tax or bow to a state trading humans like cattle. i was in the woods for other reasons, but society loves to corner you with its messy rules. could've fought back but nah, let them run wild. next day, they let me go. got my shoe, had dinner on Fair-Haven Hill. no lock on my door, just a desk for papers. left it open all the time, even when i wandered to Maine for two weeks. house was respected more than if it had guards. folks stopped by, read some books, saw my leftovers. never lost anything but one book, some Homer, maybe a soldier has it now. live simple and crime fades. problems only start where some hoard while others starve.

“Nor wars did men molest,
     When only beechen bowls were in request.”

you who lead, why punish? love virtue, people follow. superior man's virtues are like wind; common man's like grass, bending when wind blows.

The Ponds

sometimes, tired of people and gossip, i wandered further west, past usual haunts, into quieter spots. picked huckleberries on Fair Haven Hill at sunset, stocked up for days. true taste of fruit isn't for buyers or sellers. only one way to know it, but few do. if you want the taste, ask a cow-boy or partridge. thinking you've tasted huckleberries without picking them is a mistake. they never make it to Boston intact. the best part is lost in transit, turning them into feed. as long as justice rules, no pure huckleberry reaches there from the hills.

sometimes after i'd finished my work for the day, i'd join a buddy down at the pond. he'd been there since morning, sitting quiet and still, like a leaf on the water. by the time i showed up, he'd usually decided he was part of some old monastic order. there was this one older guy, knew his way around fishing and the woods, who liked to think of my place as a fisherman's lodge. i didn't mind when he'd sit by my door, sorting his lines. every now and then, we'd go out on the pond, him at one end of the boat, me at the other. he was hard of hearing by then, so we didn't talk much. he'd hum a psalm sometimes, which fit alright with my own thoughts. it was a kind of quiet harmony between us, better than words. when i was alone, i'd use a paddle to make echoes bounce around the woods, stirring things up like a zookeeper with his animals.

on warm nights, i'd sit in the boat playing my flute. the perch seemed to like it, they'd gather 'round. the moon would shine on the water, lighting up the forest debris below. used to come here with a friend on dark summer nights, make a fire by the water, thinking it drew in the fish. we'd catch them with worms on a string, and when we were done, we'd toss the burning sticks in the air, watch them hiss out in the pond, leaving us in the dark. then we'd whistle our way back to town. now, my home's by the shore.

sometimes, after hanging out in town till everyone went to bed, i'd head back to the woods. maybe do some midnight fishing for the next day's meal, with owls and foxes as my audience. out there, anchored deep, with perch and shiners everywhere, i'd feel the line connect me to something unknown lurking below. you'd pull up a horned pout now and then, squeaking its way to the surface. odd feeling, especially at night when your mind's off in space, to have that little tug bring you back to nature. felt like i was casting into the sky as much as the water. caught two worlds with one line.

walden's scenery isn't grand, but it's got this deep, pure pond that's worth talking about. it's a clear green well, half a mile long, almost two miles around, about sixty-one acres. fed by a spring, no visible streams, just clouds and evaporation. hills rise up around it, mostly woods, hitting about forty to eighty feet, some spots a hundred to a hundred fifty feet high. concord waters change colors — distance shows one thing, up close another. light's tricky. in summer, they seem blue from afar, but stormy days turn them slate gray. curious thing: walden can be blue or green from the same spot. reflects sky from a hilltop, yellowish near the shore, then light green, deepening to dark green. vivid green against sand, even before leaves come out. ice melts first in spring, sun's heat through the earth. disturbed waters mix more light, make them darker blue than the sky at times. it's like looking at a changing silk, a sword blade's blue. surprisingly, a glass of this water is as clear as air. glassmakers say a big sheet of glass turns green cause of its "body," but a small piece stays clear. never tested how much walden water you need for green reflection. river water's dark, gives swimmers a yellow tint, but walden's so clear, it makes them look like alabaster, limbs magnified, like michael angelo's study.

water's clear enough you can see the bottom 25-30 feet down. paddling over, you spot perch and shiners way below. perch are easy to spot with those stripes. once, in winter ages ago, i was cutting ice holes for pickerel fishing. tossed my axe on the ice when heading back, and it slid right into a hole, 25 feet deep. curious, i peered in and saw the axe standing on its head, handle swaying with the pond's pulse. would've stayed there, rotting away, if i hadn't fished it out. made another hole with an ice chisel, cut a long birch, fashioned a slip-noose on the end, and snagged the axe handle. dragged it up and out.

shore's lined with smooth white stones, like a pavement, except a few sandy spots. steep enough that one leap puts you in deep water. it's so clear, you see the bottom across. some folks say it's bottomless. no mud, barely any weeds. except for some small heart-leaves, potamogetons, maybe a water-target—clean and bright like the water. stones go a rod or two out, then it's all sand, until the deep parts with a bit of sediment from fallen leaves. even in winter, there's bright green weed clinging to anchors.

we've got another pond like this one, White Pond, over in Nine Acre Corner, about two and a half miles west. i know most of the ponds within a dozen miles, but a third one like this? not really. different folks have probably drunk from it, admired it, tried to measure it, and then moved on, but the water stays green and clear. no stopping the flow. maybe when Adam and Eve got the boot from Eden, Walden Pond was already here, getting rained on, wrapped in mist, and full of ducks and geese who hadn't heard about any fall. even then, it started to rise and fall, clearing up its water and getting that color it's known for now, like it had a deal with heaven to be the only Walden Pond in the world. who knows how many old stories call this the Castalian Fountain? or what myths wrapped around it back in the Golden Age? Concord's crown jewel.

maybe the first folks to find this pond left a mark. surprised me to notice this narrow path around the pond even where fresh woods got cleared on the shore. it rides up and down the hillside, in and out from the water’s edge, probably as old as folks around here. made by native hunters' feet, still walked on by people today without even knowing. you can see it clearly from the middle of the pond in winter after a light snow. a clear white line, no weeds or twigs, standing out from a quarter-mile away, where in summer you'd hardly notice it up close. snow imprints it like it wants you to see. maybe when they build fancy homes here, they'll keep some trace of this path.

pond does its own thing. goes up, goes down. no one really knows the pattern, though folks act like they do. usually higher in winter, lower in summer. not always about rain. i remember times it was way lower, then times it was five feet higher. used to cook chowder on a sand-bar back in 1824, can't do that now. friends didn't believe me when i said i fished from a boat where there's meadow now. but pond's been rising, and now in summer '52, it's back up five feet, like 30 years ago. fishing in the meadow again. level shifts about six or seven feet. not much runoff from hills, so it's the deep springs at work. this summer, it's dropping again. takes years for these changes. seen one rise, parts of two falls. guessing it'll drop in a dozen years or so. flint's pond, white pond, all act like this too. 

walden's rise every so often clears out shrubs and trees, makes walking hard but leaves clear shore when it drops. pitch pines near my house got killed, tipped over. shows how long since last big rise. pond claims its shore this way. on high water, alders, willows, maples grow roots to hang on. high-blueberry bushes bear fruit when normally they dont. pond trims its own shores.

So here's how it went down. Folks around here like to talk about how the shore got all neatly paved. The older folks say when they were kids, they heard this story about the Indians having a pow-wow on a hill that was as high as the pond's deep now. Lots of cursing, which supposedly wasn't really their thing, and then boom, the hill shook, dropped, and left only one old squaw named Walden. That's how the pond got its name. Rocks supposedly rolled down when the hill shook, making the shore.

Now, some argue the stones can't just be from wave action, but I see the hills around are packed with the same rocks. They've even piled them up along the railroad. More stones where the shore's steep too. So, mystery solved. If Walden wasn't from some English name like Saffron Walden, maybe it was _Walled-in_ Pond from the start.

As for the pond itself, that's my ready-made well. Four months out of the year, the water's as cold as it is pure. Beats any other in town, I’d say. Winter's got all exposed water colder than the protected springs and wells. Checked the temp in March 1846, and it was 42°, colder than the coldest well in town. Boiling Spring was 45°, warmest I tested, yet coldest come summer. Walden doesn't warm up like other shallow waters because of its depth. I’d put a pail in the cellar at night to keep it cool or use a nearby spring. Water stayed fresh for a week, no pump taste. Go camp by the pond in summer, bury a pail a few feet deep, and you won't even need ice.

caught some big ones in Walden. Pickerel mostly. One hit seven pounds, heard of another taking off with a reel, guessed at eight. Perch and pouts too, some over two pounds. Got shiners, chivins, maybe a few breams, and a couple of eels, one four pounds. i'm going into the details cause weight's the big brag for fish, and these are the only eels i've heard about here. faint memory of a small five-inch fish, silvery sides, greenish back, kinda dace-like. just throwing that in to mix fact with fable. not a lot of fish here though. pickerel are the stars, even if they're not plenty. 

seen three kinds of pickerel on the ice: long and steel-colored like river ones, a bright golden type with greenish shimmer, deep-bodied — that's the usual here. and another golden, similar shape but spotted with dark and a few red, looks almost like a trout. should call it _guttatus_, not _reticulatus_. they're solid fish, heavier than they look. shiners, pouts, perch are clean and firm too, better than river fish, cause the water's purer. ichthyologists might find new varieties here. clean frogs and turtles live here too, few mussels, muskrats and minks leave signs, sometimes a mud-turtle drops by. ducks and geese in spring and fall, swallows skimming, peetweets along the shores. sometimes a fishhawk over a pine, but no gulls, unlike Fair Haven. maybe one loon a year. that's about it for wildlife.

in calm weather, by the sandy eastern shore, you might see circular heaps of small stones, about a foot high, six feet across, in eight to ten feet deep water. all around is bare sand, so you wonder if maybe Indians made them on ice and they sunk when it melted. too regular and fresh-looking though. like river ones, but without suckers or lampreys here, it's a mystery. maybe chivin nests? adds a nice mystery to the pond's bottom.

the shore's got this irregular vibe that keeps it from being boring. i picture the west with deep bays, the north a bit bolder, and the south all scalloped with capes overlapping, hiding those secret coves. forests never look better than when you're on a small lake with hills right up to the edge. that water reflects everything perfectly, making a natural border without the mess of chopped trees or fields butting up against it. trees stretch out their branches towards the water, like nature's own trim. your eyes move from low shrubs to tall trees, no trace of people messing with it. the water's touched the shore the same way for a thousand years.

a lake's like the landscape's best feature. it's like looking into earth's eye, and in that reflection, you kind of see yourself. the trees by the shore are like eyelashes, and the hills and cliffs around are its brows.

standing out here on the sandy beach at the pond's east end, september's doing its thing with a slight haze blurring the far shore. that "glassy surface" thing? yeah, i get it. flip your head upside down, and it looks like a thread of silk across the valley, gleaming against the pine woods. it almost feels like you could walk under it dry to the hills, with swallows maybe perching on it. they sometimes dive below, like they're fooled too.

looking west, both sun and reflection hit you hard, so you gotta shield your eyes. the surface is smooth like glass, except for the skater bugs leaving sparkles or a duck doing its thing. sometimes a fish jumps, making a silvery arc, and there's a flash when it hits the water. thistle-down might float by, with fish darting at it, dimpling the surface again. it's like molten glass, with motes that are pure and perfect like imperfections in glass.

from a hilltop, you can spot fish jumping almost anywhere. any little disturbance shows up. even a water-bug a quarter-mile off makes a ripple, though skaters glide without much fuss. when the surface gets choppy, the bugs are gone, but on calm days they venture out, covering the pond. it's calming to sit high up, watching ripples form and fade on this mirror-like water. any disturbance is smoothed away, like a vase's tremble settling.

every leap or fall on the pond is noted in dimples and lines, like the lake's breathing. joy and pain's indistinguishable here. peaceful, with everything sparkling like a spring morning. every oar stroke or insect creates a light flash; even an oar's splash echoes sweetly.

on days like this in september or october, walden's a mirror of the forest. stones around it look like gems. nothing matches a lake like this, so clear and big. sky water, doesn't need a fence. nations come and go, but it stays untouched. no stone can crack it, nature's always fixing its shine; no storm or dust dulls it. sun sweeps it clean, no breath sticks, just floats up as clouds and reflects back down.

water shows the spirit of the air, always getting new life from above. it's between land and sky. on land, just grass and trees move, but wind ripples water. you can see where the breeze hits, light streaks show it. funny we can look down on it. maybe one day we'll look down on air like that, see a finer spirit move over it.

Late October rolls in, and the skaters and water-bugs vanish with the frost. By November, the pond sits still, no ripples. One afternoon, after a long rain, I found the pond smooth as glass. Couldn't tell water from sky — all muted colors now, no more October bright. As I drifted, the boat's wake made the surface ribbed, like a pattern. Spotted something shimmering ahead. Thought it might be late skaters or maybe a spring below. 

Paddled over, found myself in a swarm of perch, bronze in green water. They'd rise, dimpled the water, left bubbles. Felt like I was floating through air, not water. They moved like a flock, fins like sails. Schools everywhere, taking advantage of the last days before winter froze them in. Sometimes the surface looked like a breeze or rain hit it. If I spooked them, they'd splash and dive deep, tails kicking up a fuss.

Then the wind picked up, mist thickened, waves came, and the perch started leaping, black spikes breaking the surface. Even into December, I'd see dimples. One December fifth, mist everywhere, thought rain was coming, so I rowed home fast. But the dimples stopped — perch scared off by my oars. Rain never came. Stayed dry after all.

met an old guy who used to hit this pond about sixty years back when it was all forested and dark. he talked about ducks everywhere back then and eagles too. he'd fish using this log canoe made from two white-pine logs. pretty clumsy but lasted for years till it got waterlogged. nobody knew who owned it—it just belonged to the pond. for anchoring, he tied hickory bark strips together. another old-timer, a potter from Revolutionary days, claimed there's an iron chest at the bottom. says it sometimes floated up but would sink back if you tried to get close. kinda liked hearing about that canoe—it replaced an Indian one, probably from the same tree on the bank, floating there for generations. remember seeing old trunks at the bottom when i first looked in, left from when wood was cheaper, but they're mostly gone now.

when i first took a boat on Walden, pines and oaks surrounded it thick. grapevines made bowers over some coves—could pass a boat under. the hills were steep, woods high, looked like a natural amphitheater from the west. spent hours drifting, lying back across the seats, dreaming till the boat hit sand and i’d see where i ended up. preferred those hours over the grind—rich in sunny time, not money. no regrets not spending more in workshops or classrooms. but since then, woodchoppers have cleared more trees, no more rambling through those woods. don't expect my Muse to sing much now—their groves are gone.

trees used to line the bottom, old log canoe there too, with dark woods all around. now they're gone. villagers, barely know where it is anymore. they're talking about piping the water to town, washing dishes with it! that iron horse, loud as hell, messed up the boiling spring, chewed through the woods on walden shore. who’s gonna step up and stop it?

but you know, walden’s still got it. stays pure. people might compare themselves to it, but most don’t measure up. woodcutters, railroads, ice-men—they’ve all taken their piece, but walden stays the same. it’s me that’s changed. still the same water from when i was a kid. no permanent mark on it, still young. saw a swallow dip in tonight, same as always. feels like it’s never changed. where trees fell, new ones grow. same thoughts bubbling up from the water, same happiness. who made this lake did it right. he shaped it with his hand, left it for concord. when i look at it, it’s like seeing an old friend.

trains don’t stop for it, but i bet the folks on board feel better seeing it. engineers, firemen, passengers—they carry a piece of its peace. one look, and it scrubs away the city grime. should call it “god’s drop.”

walden doesn’t have a stream in or out, but it’s quietly linked to flint’s pond uphill and concord river downhill. maybe it flowed there once. dig a bit, and it could again. but who’d want to spoil it by mixing in flint’s pond water or losing it to the ocean?

flint's pond, or sandy pond, over in lincoln, is our big lake, about a mile east of walden. it's bigger, around 197 acres, and has more fish, but it's shallow and not the clearest. walking through the woods to get there was my go-to for a break. feeling the wind and seeing the waves always brought thoughts of sailors. in the fall, i'd go chestnutting there on windy days. nuts would drop into the water and drift to me. once, walking along the shore with spray in my face, i found a wreck of a boat. sides gone, just the flat bottom shape left in the rushes. it was like a decayed pad, veins and all. quite the scene, like a beach wreck. now it's all just pond shore with rushes growing through it. i'd admire the ripple marks on the sandy bottom at the north end, firm underfoot, with rushes growing in lines like soldiers. also found these strange spherical balls, made of fine grass or roots, maybe pipewort, from half an inch to four inches across. they'd roll in the shallow water, sometimes ending up on the shore. all grass or a bit of sand inside. look like they're wave-formed but they're not, smallest ones are made of coarse stuff. only show up in one season. waves might not make them, more like they just wear them down. they keep their shape when dry for a long time.

flint's pond. yeah, the name's a joke. some clueless farmer's got his name on it, stripped the place bare. more interested in coins than the water. he sees ducks as intruders. never swam in it, never loved it. better to name it after the fish, the birds, the flowers, or anyone who truly connects with it. not some guy who sees only dollar signs. his presence sours the shore. he’d drain it if he could make a buck. no respect for the land, everything's a transaction. give me a poor farmer who knows true wealth. those "model farms" are a mess, a jumble of man and beast, stinking of manure and greed. like planting potatoes in a graveyard.

if we're naming places after people, make it count. name them for the ones who deserve it. nearby, there's goose pond, fair-haven, white pond. this is my lake country. these waters are my grindstones, always working, always there.

since we've all messed with walden and the railroad cut through, white pond stands out. not the fanciest name, but it's got that clear water, kinda like walden's little sibling. same rocky shore, same water color. on those hot days, looking down through the trees, you get that bluish-green tint. years back, i used to haul sand from there for sandpaper. one guy calls it virid lake, but it could also be yellow-pine lake. about fifteen years ago, you could see a pitch-pine top sticking out in deep water. some folks thought the pond sank and it was an old forest remnant. way back in 1792, someone wrote about a tree in white pond seen when water was low. talked to a guy in '49 who pulled it out. stood 12-15 rods from shore, water about 30-40 feet deep. he sawed a path in the ice, hauled it out with oxen. turned out it was upside down, branches down, stuck in the sandy bottom. wanted a saw-log, but it was too rotten. still had pieces in his shed. axe marks and woodpecker holes on it. maybe a dead tree blown into the pond, top waterlogged, sank wrong end up. his dad couldn't remember a time without it. more logs still down there, look like giant water snakes.

this pond's mostly left alone by boats, nothing much here for fishermen. not like white lilies that need mud, but got the blue flag iris growing thin around the edges, coming up from the stony bottom. hummingbirds come around in june, and the colors, man, they just fit with that glaucous water.

white pond and walden, they're like earth's crystals, lakes of light. if they were frozen solid and small enough, folks might snatch them up like jewels for kings. but they're liquid, vast, and here for us forever, so we overlook them, chasing after diamonds. too pure to sell, no muck in them. way more beautiful than our lives, more clear than our characters. never learned any meanness from them. cleaner than the farm pond with its swimming ducks. wild ducks, they're drawn here. nature, she’s got no human who gets her. birds and flowers in tune, but what young folks really connect with her wild beauty? she thrives best alone, away from towns. heaven? earth gets disgraced.

baker farm

sometimes i wandered to pine groves, like temples or ships at sea, soft, green, with light rippling through. druids might’ve left their oaks for these. or i’d head to the cedar wood past flint’s pond, trees reaching up, covered in berries, fit for valhalla. juniper creeps around, full of fruit. went to swamps too, usnea lichen hanging like curtains, toadstools like swamp gods’ tables. fungi all over, like butterflies or shells. swamp-pink and dogwood, red alder-berries glowing like imp eyes, waxwork twisting wood, wild-holly berries so beautiful they make you forget home. dazzled by wild fruits too fair to eat. didn’t visit scholars, but made my rounds to rare trees—black-birch, yellow birch with its golden bark, beeches neat and lichen-painted. just one small grove left, maybe pigeons planted them with beech nuts long ago. worth seeing the silver grain in split wood. bass, hornbeam, false elm—only one grown here. tall pines, shingle trees, perfect hemlocks like pagodas in the woods. these were my shrines, summer and winter.

one time i ended up right at the base of a rainbow. everything around me was lit up like a stained glass window. it felt like swimming in a lake of color. if it lasted longer, i might've started seeing the world different. walking on the railroad, my shadow had this ring of light, almost made me think i was special. a visitor once said only locals had that halo, not the irish guys. benvenuto cellini talked about seeing a light over his shadow too, after some wild dream. it shows up mostly in the morning but can be any time, even at night. not many notice it, but someone with a wild imagination might think it's magical.

one afternoon, went to fish at fair-haven. passed through pleasant meadow, part of baker farm. a poet wrote about it:

“entry's a pleasant field,
     mossy fruit trees yield
     to a ruddy brook,
     musquash glide,
     trout darting.”

almost lived there before walden. snagged apples, jumped the brook, spooked musquash and trout. felt like one of those endless afternoons where anything could happen, even though half the day was gone when i started. got caught in a shower, hid under a pine with boughs and a handkerchief for cover. finally cast my line, up to my waist in water, when a cloud shadowed me. thunder crashed so loud i just listened. thought the gods must be showing off with those lightning bolts. hurried to an old, empty hut nearby for shelter:

“And here a poet builded,
         In the completed years,
     For behold a trivial cabin
         That to destruction steers.”

john field, irish guy, living in a leaky roof with his family. kids are all over, from the boy helping dad in the bog to a baby on his knee. we sat where it leaked least, rain hammering outside. john's honest but can't seem to get ahead. his wife, cooking non-stop, hoping for better but never seeing it. even chickens in the house like family, too human to eat.

john tells me about bogging, ten bucks an acre, land use for a year. his boy works beside him, not knowing it's a bad deal. i share my own way—tight, simple house, no tea, coffee, meat. saves me work and money. told him he could build his own place in no time if he cut the extras. america's freedom isn't in getting more stuff but in needing less.

i talked like he's a thinker, maybe he wanted that. said i'd rather see wild meadows than wasted lives. told him his hard work needed thick boots, but i wore light shoes, cost less. i could fish for fun or make a week's living in a couple of hours, while they could pick berries for joy.

been thinking a lot about fishing. every time i go, i feel like ive lost a little respect for myself. i mean, ive got the skills, that old instinct kicks in sometimes, but after its done, i question why i even bothered. feels like theres a part of me that’s tied to something more primitive, like the animal side, but each year, i care less about catching fish. im not more humane or wise, just not a fisherman anymore. if i lived out in the wild, maybe id go back to it, but right now? nah.

one day i went out to my wood-pile, more like a pile of stumps, and saw two big ants, one red, one black, going at it. they were locked in, rolling around on the chips without letting go. then i noticed the whole pile was covered in ants fighting, red against black, sometimes two reds on one black. it was a full-on war. i watched a red ant gripping onto a black one, gnawing at its feelers, while the black tried shaking it off. no backing down. they fought quietly, but just as fiercely as soldiers. 

a single red ant, maybe an achilles, came rushing in to join the fight, picking his moment to jump in. now it was three tangled up, like they'd invented a new way to lock together. i almost expected to hear them playing battle tunes. got me thinking, if you squint, not much different from human battles—same grit and resolve. this ant fight, in its own way, was as intense as any battle i've heard of, even if it was just ants. every ant fighting for something, not just avoiding a tea tax. might be their bunker hill when it comes down to it.

brought the chip inside with the three ants fighting, set it under a glass on the windowsill to watch. using a microscope, saw the red ant going for the black ant's fore-leg, but his own chest was all torn open. black ant's armor was too tough for him. eyes of the red ant were wild with that kind of war rage. they kept going for another half hour. when i checked back, the black ant had decapitated his enemies, and their heads were hanging like trophies, still biting him. black ant struggled, missing feelers and most of a leg, took him another half hour to shake them off. lifted the glass, and he hobbled away. no idea if he made it, but doubt he was much use after that. didn't find out who won overall or why they fought. left me feeling like i'd watched a human war right on my doorstep.

kirby and spence say ant battles have been noted for ages, though huber’s the only modern guy to see them. they mention æneas sylvius talking about a fight between big and small ants on a pear tree, back when eugenius the fourth was pope. nicholas pistoriensis, some lawyer, recorded the whole thing. olaus magnus also wrote about a similar fight where the small ants won and buried their dead, leaving the big ones for the birds. that was before christiern the second got kicked out of sweden. the battle i saw happened during polk’s presidency, five years before webster’s fugitive-slave bill.

sometimes i think about a house, big and solid, without all the frills, just one massive room. imagine a hall with no ceiling or plaster, just bare wood beams holding up the sky, keeping the rain and snow out. it's a place where posts stand like kings, and you nod to the old gods when you step inside. you might need a torch to see the roof, and folks could hang out by the fire, near a window, or even up in the rafters with the spiders if they want.

this house, you walk in and you're there, no fuss. weary travelers wash up, eat, chat, and sleep without going further. it's the kind of spot you'd want in a storm, with all the basics, nothing extra. you see everything in one sweep—kitchen, pantry, living room, bedroom, storage, attic. the essentials are right there, like a barrel or ladder, and you hear the pot boiling, respect the fire cooking your meal, and the oven baking your bread.

you might have to move for the cook to hit the cellar, checking if the ground's solid or not. inside's as open as a bird's nest, and you can't go in the front and out the back without seeing everyone. being a guest means you're really part of the place, not shoved into a small corner like you're in solitary. these days, hosts keep you far from their hearth, building you a spot down the alley, like hospitality means distance. cooking's secret like they're trying to poison you. i've been on many properties, but not really inside many homes.

i could visit a king and queen living simple in such a house, but getting out of a fancy palace, that's all id want to learn if i ever end up in one.

parlor talk gets weak, all fluff, since we're so far from what it really represents. dinner's just pretending to be dinner. seems only the wild ones live close enough to nature to use it right. how can a scholar, stuck out somewhere remote, know what's happening in the kitchen?

most folks wouldn’t stick around long enough to try my hasty-pudding. they’d bolt like the house might collapse. but it held up through plenty of those meals.

waited till it was cold to plaster. got some clean sand from across the pond. took a boat, which was tempting to use for a longer trip if needed. shingles came down to the ground. liked how i could drive each nail with one hammer swing. aimed to get the plaster up quick and neat. thought about this guy, all dressed up, who tried to show off to workers. he loaded a trowel, went to plaster, and the whole lot ended up on his fancy clothes. plastering’s neat — keeps out the cold and looks good. learned how thirsty bricks are, soaking up moisture before i could smooth it out. used loads of water to set a new hearth. last winter, burned some river shells for lime as an experiment. knew my materials well. could’ve found good limestone nearby and done it myself if i wanted.

the pond started skimming over in the shady, shallow spots before the rest froze up. that first ice is something else—hard, dark, see-through. perfect for checking out the bottom. you can lay on it, just an inch thick, like some water bug, and see everything only a couple inches away. there's tracks in the sand where critters doubled back, and wrecks of cadis worm cases made of tiny quartz grains. maybe they made the furrows, but some tracks are too deep for that. now, the ice is the real deal. gotta check it right after it freezes. morning's best. those bubbles you see? they're mostly stuck underneath, rising up, while the ice is still solid and dark, showing water through it. bubbles are clear, from tiny to about an eighth inch wide, and you can see your face in them. lots of them, thirty to forty per square inch. and inside the ice, vertical bubbles like cones or strings of beads. not as many as the ones underneath, though. used to toss stones to test the ice strength. when they broke through, they'd trap air, leaving big white bubbles. once, two days after a warm spell, the ice was thicker but not stronger. bubbles expanded, lost shape, overlapping like coins. no more clear ice to see the bottom. broke a piece out, flipped it. big bubbles were between two ice layers. surprised me how regular the melted ice under the bubble was, like an upside-down saucer. small bubbles burst downward, some spots probably had no ice. figured those tiny bubbles froze in too, each melting the ice below like a lens. these bubbles make the ice crack and pop.

winter hit hard just as i wrapped up plastering. wind howled like it finally got the go-ahead. every night, geese came clattering in through the dark, wings whistling, even with snow covering the ground. some landed at Walden, some flew low toward Fair Haven, heading for Mexico. late nights back from the village, i’d hear geese or ducks rustling dry leaves by the pond behind my place, their leader honking or quacking as they took off. in '45, Walden froze over completely on Dec 22nd. Flint's and other ponds had been frozen for over ten days by then. dates shifted a bit each year. snow was on the ground since November 25th, surrounding me with full-on winter. i retreated inward, keeping the fire bright both in the house and in me. outside work meant gathering dead wood from the forest, carrying or dragging it back. an old fence past its prime became fuel. stealing wood in the snow makes a meal taste better. plenty of waste wood in town forests for fires, but it sits unused, some say stunting young growth. found a raft of pitch-pine logs over summer, left by the railroad builders. hauled them to shore. after soaking two years and drying six months, they were waterlogged but sound. one winter day, i slid them across the pond, skating behind with the log balanced on my shoulder. tied logs with birch, dragged them with a hooked alder. waterlogged almost like lead, they burned long and hot. soaking kept the pitch burning longer, like a lamp.

Gilpin talked about how folks building on forest borders in England were seen as a nuisance, and those structures got punished under old laws for scaring game and hurting the forest. But for me, the venison and the greenery mattered more than the hunters or lumberjacks. Even if I accidentally set part of it on fire, I'd feel worse than the landowners themselves. I wished our farmers would feel a bit of that old Roman reverence when cutting down a forest, treating it as something sacred.

It's interesting how much wood is still valued, even more than gold sometimes. No matter our advancements, a stack of wood still catches the eye. Michaux noted decades ago that wood prices in New York and Philly nearly matched Paris, despite Paris needing tons of it and being surrounded by plains. Here, wood prices just keep climbing. People, even those not in the wood business, show up at auctions and pay good money to pick what’s left after the chopping. From all corners of the world and walks of life, everyone needs wood for warmth and cooking. I’m no different.

There’s something about a woodpile that just feels right. I like mine visible from the window, chips and all, reminding me of the work. Had this old axe, unclaimed, that I used on winter days out by the stumps I cleared from my bean-field. Got told to have it sharpened by the blacksmith, but I fixed it up myself with a hickory handle. Maybe it wasn’t the sharpest, but it was solid.

had some fat pine, real treasure out there. crazy to think about all that hidden firewood still under the ground. used to go out on those bare hills where old pitch-pine grew, digging up fat pine roots. those things last forever. found stumps from decades ago still solid inside even when the outer layers turned to dirt. axe and shovel, you dig up this yellow gold, like striking a rich vein. mostly used dry leaves I stashed before snow hit. green hickory split fine was my go-to when I could get it. villagers lighting fires, and I'd join with a smoke signal from my chimney.

smoke rising like a bird, silent like dawn's messenger, circling above, like a dream fading into night or shadow. by night hiding stars, by day blocking sun. rise from my hearth, ask the gods to forgive this clear flame.

but that green wood, hard and fresh-cut, served me well. left a fire burning while I wandered those winter afternoons, and it stayed alive, warm. felt like leaving a friend behind. it was me and Fire living there. mostly reliable. checked once while splitting wood, just in case. saw a spark on my bed, put it out, just a hand-sized burn. but the house was set up right, sunny spot, low roof. could let the fire die most winter days and be fine.

moles took over the cellar, munching on every third potato. they made a cozy nest from leftover plaster hair and brown paper. even wild critters crave a warm spot. some folks thought i'd freeze on purpose heading to the woods. animals just make a bed and warm it with their bodies, but man, with fire, heats up a whole room, creating a sort of summer in winter. you can walk around without all those layers, let in light, and extend the day with a lamp. after facing harsh winds, stepping into a warm house wakes up the senses. but even in a plush setup, humans aren't invincible. a sharper north wind could end us all. we talk about Cold Fridays and Great Snows, but a bit more would finish us off.

next winter, i switched to a small cooking-stove for economy. it didn’t hold fire like the open fireplace. cooking became a chemic task, losing its poetry. with stoves, we forget roasting potatoes in the ashes like the Indians. the stove took space, scented the house, and hid the fire. it felt like losing a friend. staring into a fire clears the mind after a day's grind. but with the stove, that was gone. a poet's words hit me hard: the fire’s bright flame was a companion, its glow a conversation, now replaced by a safe and strong pile that warms but doesn't inspire.

i hung in through some wild snow storms, kept warm by the fire while everything outside went silent. weeks went by without seeing anyone except the odd woodcutter hauling to the village. nature helped me carve paths through the deep snow. once i broke through, the wind filled my tracks with oak leaves. they'd soak up the sun, melt the snow, and make a dry path that even glowed in the dark. for company, i imagined the folks who used to live here. back then, the road near my place was lively with chatter, and the woods were dotted with gardens and homes. pines crowded the way, brushing both sides of a carriage, and folks, especially women and kids, walked to Lincoln with a mix of fear and hurry. that road entertained travelers with its twists and turns, unlike now. open fields stretch where it once wound through a maple swamp, resting on a bed of logs that still lie beneath the modern road, from the old Stratton Farm to Brister’s Hill.

east of my bean-field, across the road, was Cato Ingraham’s place. he was a slave of Duncan Ingraham, who built Cato a house in Walden Woods. people say Cato was a Guinea Negro. his walnut patch was meant for his old age, but a younger, whiter man got it first. Cato's cellar hole is still there, hidden by pines and filled with sumach and golden-rod.

closer to town, Zilpha had her house and spun linen, filling the woods with her singing. during the war of 1812, her home was burned by English soldiers. her animals didn't make it. she lived tough, sometimes harsh. a passerby once heard her muttering over a pot, “Ye are all bones, bones!” bricks still peek through the oaks now.

down the road on the right, up on brister’s hill, you’d find brister freeman, once a slave of squire cummings. “handy negro” they called him. still see the apple trees he planted, big old things now but the apples are still wild and sour. read his epitaph in the old lincoln graveyard, near some british grenadiers’ unmarked graves from the concord retreat. they called him “sippio brister” there—scipio africanus, a man of color like he was different. it said when he died, which is just a roundabout way of saying he lived. his wife fenda lived with him, real friendly, told fortunes, big and darker than any night, a sight that never rose on concord again.

further down on the left, through the woods, there’s traces of the stratton family’s old place. their orchard spread over brister’s hill once, but the pines took over. just a few stumps left, still feeding the village trees.

closer to town, across the way, there’s breed’s spot, right by the woods. place known for antics of a demon not named in myths, but big in new england life. comes as a friend or helper then wrecks everything—new england rum. no need for history to spill the tragedies here yet; let time blur them a bit. rumor says there was a tavern here once; the well’s still there, kept the travelers and horses refreshed. folks met, shared news, and moved on.

breed's hut was still there about a dozen years back, though empty for a while. same size as mine. got set on fire by some kids on election night, if i remember right. i was living on the village edge then, lost in davenant's gondibert that winter, fighting off a lethargy. might be a family thing — got an uncle who dozes off shaving and sprouts potatoes in a cellar to stay awake on sundays. more likely from trying to read chalmers’ poetry collection without skipping. really knocked me out. just as i was nodding off, the fire bells went crazy. engines rolled out, men and boys trailing behind, me up front 'cause i jumped the brook. thought it was south over the woods, could've been barn, shop, or house. “it's baker's barn,” someone yelled. “no, codman place,” said another. sparks flew up like the roof caved in, and we all shouted “concord to the rescue!” wagons zipped past with heavy loads, maybe even the insurance guy. engine bell tinkled steadily behind, slow but sure. rumor had it the fire starters were tagging along at the back. we kept running, ignoring what we saw, until we hit a bend and felt the heat. realized then we were right there. fire's closeness pretty much cooled our rush. thought about dumping a frog-pond on it but figured we'd let it burn—too far gone, too worthless. stood around the engine, bumped into each other, yelled through trumpets, and swapped stories of great fires, like bascom’s shop. secretly reckoned if we had our tub and a frog-pond handy, we could’ve drowned even the biggest blaze. eventually, we left without doing much harm, went back to sleep and gondibert. but gondibert's worth it for the preface bit on wit being the soul’s powder—“but most of mankind are strangers to wit, as indians are to powder.”

walked past there again the next night, about the same time. heard this low moaning noise and got curious, so i went closer. found the last family member i knew, the one who inherited it all, good and bad. he was lying on his stomach, staring over the cellar wall at the smoldering remains, mumbling to himself like he does. he’d been working out in the river meadows all day and finally had a moment to come back to his old home. looked into the cellar from every angle, like he thought there was some hidden treasure there, but really it was just bricks and ashes. with the house gone, he focused on what was left. seemed to find some comfort in me just being there. showed me where the well was, covered up but still there, thank god. felt around for the well-sweep his dad had made, looking for the iron hook. i felt it too and still notice it on my walks, tied to the family’s history.

further out, past where Nutting and Le Grosse lived by the wall with lilac bushes, you hit the woods. near the pond, Wyman the potter set up shop. made earthenware for the town. they weren’t well-off, just got by while they lived there. sheriff came by often trying to collect taxes, couldn’t find much to take except a token chip. midsummer day, i was out hoeing and a guy hauling pottery stopped by. asked about Wyman’s son. he’d bought a potter’s wheel from him ages ago and wondered what happened to him. always read about potter’s clay and wheels in scripture, but never thought about where our pots come from. nice to know that kind of craft was happening nearby.

the woods had an irishman before me, hugh quoiol or something like that, living in wyman’s place. they called him col. quoiol. rumor had it he fought at waterloo. if he was still around, i'd make him tell those stories again. he was a ditch digger here. napoleon went to st. helena; quoiol came to walden woods. it’s a sad story. he had some manners, seen the world, talked more polite than needed. wore a big coat in summer, trembled from delirium, face red like paint. died on the road near brister's hill not long after i showed up. never really knew him as a neighbor. before they tore his house down, folks said it was cursed. i went by once. his clothes were still there, looking like he just stepped out. broken pipe on the hearth. never saw brister’s spring, he told me. cards scattered all over. one black chicken still roamed around, quiet. his garden was just a trace, weeds everywhere, never hoed. woodchuck skin stretched on the wall, his last big hunt. but no more need for mittens.

now, just a dent in the earth remains, buried stones, wild strawberries and sumachs growing. maybe a pine or oak stands where the chimney was. sometimes you see the well dent, dry now. covering wells feels like shutting off tears. these cellar dents, like old fox holes, are all that's left of busy lives. talk about fate and free will once went on here, but all that’s left of that wisdom is “cato and brister pulled wool.” not much different from bigger philosophies.

the lilac keeps growing, long after door and lintel gone. blossoms every spring, for travelers to pick. once planted by kids in front yards, now by old walls, replaced by new forests. last of its kind, outlasting those kids, telling their story lightly to anyone who passes by. gentle lilac colors still as bright as ever.

this little village, had potential, but it didn't last. concord stood its ground. no water advantages? walden pond and brister's spring were right there. they could've turned this place into something, with basket-making or pottery. but they didn't. soil was tough but could've kept the land strong. memory of those folks doesn't add much to the landscape's beauty. maybe nature's trying again with me. my place might just be the oldest here.

nobody built where i am now. keep me away from cities built on ruins, where gardens are just graveyards. that soil's cursed. earth might end before that's needed. thinking about all this, i filled the woods with people in my mind and drifted to sleep.

during winter, visitors were rare. snow piled up, and no one came by for weeks. but i lived cozy, like a meadow mouse or animals surviving in snow drifts. like that settler's family in sutton buried by the snow of 1717. an indian saved them, found them by chimney smoke. no one had to save me; i was home. the Great Snow! it's a cheerful tale. farmers couldn't reach woods, had to cut shade trees by their homes. snow crust so hard, they cut swamp trees ten feet up.

deep snow out here, path from the highway to my house is more like a dotted line now. half-mile of this routine, stepping in my own tracks, back and forth, like some mechanical dividers. winter's got us on repeat, but sometimes the snow's got this blue glow that feels like magic. no weather kept me from heading out though. i'd trudge eight or ten miles through the deepest snow to meet an old pine or birch friend. pines got their limbs drooped with ice, looking more like firs. snow nearly two feet deep, i'd wade up hills, snowstorm falling on my head each step, or crawl on hands and knees when the hunters hunkered down for winter.

one afternoon, a barred owl caught my eye. perched on a white-pine limb, he was close enough, could hear the snow crunch under my feet but couldn't see me. every loud step, he'd stretch out, eyes wide, but soon droop again, nodding off. half an hour of watching him, felt sleepy myself. like he was in a dream, just a slit of eye to keep me in sight. any louder sound, he'd shuffle impatiently, then take off through the pines, wings wide, silent. he wasn't seeing his way, more like feeling it, finding a new perch to wait for his night.

walking the railroad causeway through the meadows, winds had their way with me, frost biting one cheek, i'd just turn the other. not much better on the road from Brister’s Hill. came into town, fields piled up along Walden road, tracks gone in half an hour. new drifts when i returned, snow rounded the bends, no rabbit or mouse tracks to be seen. still, even in mid-winter, i'd find a warm swamp, grass and skunk-cabbage green, some bird toughing it out, waiting for spring.

sometimes, despite the snow, id come back from a walk and see deep tracks from a woodchopper heading to my door. he'd leave a pile of wood shavings by the hearth, and the house smelled like his pipe. on a sunday, if i was around, id hear the crunch of snow from a farmer making his way through the woods to my place. he'd come by for a chat, one of those rare folks who's just as comfortable on his farm as in a church debate, wearing a frock instead of a fancy robe. we'd talk about the old days, sitting by big fires when it was cold, cracking nuts left by wise squirrels, though the ones with the hardest shells were usually empty.

the furthest traveler to my place, through snow and storms, was a poet. a farmer or soldier might hesitate, but a poet? driven by pure love, he'll show up anytime. we filled that small house with laughter and serious talk, bringing life back to walden's quiet. broadway was nothing compared to that. our laughs came in bursts, tied to the last or next joke. over thin gruel, we spun out fresh theories of life, balancing fun with the clear thinking philosophy needs.

can't forget another visitor from my last winter at the pond. he came all the way through town, braving snow and rain, drawn by the light in my window. a philosopher from connecticut, he once sold wares, now his thoughts. he still trades those thoughts, challenging God and man, his faith unshaken. his words hint at a better world than most can imagine, never disappointed as time rolls on. he's not focused on the present, but when his time arrives, his wisdom will guide others. 

how blind are those who can't see peace.

a real friend, not just to people but to progress too. like some kind of immortal reminder, always patiently showing the truth in folks, like monuments worn down over time. his mind's wide open, taking in everyone from kids to scholars, adding a touch of grace to their thoughts. he should run a halfway house for the world's thinkers, with a sign that says, “come in if you've got time and peace of mind, looking for the right path.” he's the steadiest guy i know, no weird quirks. we used to wander and chat, leaving the world behind, no ties holding him down, free as they come. wherever we went, the world felt more alive, like he made it all better. a guy in blue, with the sky itself as his roof, calm like the sky. can't imagine him ever gone; nature needs him too much.

we'd sit with our own ideas, carving them down, testing our thoughts, admiring how clear they were. we'd move so gently in our minds that ideas came freely, like clouds drifting. we worked on myths, tweaking stories, building dreams with no real ground to stand on. talking with him was like a night of deep thought in new england. those talks, with him and the old settler, stretched my mind so much it felt like my little house would burst, but i had plenty of patchwork for that later.

there was another friend, solid times spent at his place in the village, but not much more company there.

always waiting for that guest who never showed. like the Vishnu Purana says, wait as long as it takes to milk a cow for a guest. i waited long enough to milk a whole herd, but never saw anyone coming from town.

when the ponds froze solid, they opened up shortcuts everywhere and gave me new angles on the same old views. crossing Flint's Pond after a snow, even though id paddled and skated over it plenty of times, felt like hitting Baffin's Bay. the Lincoln hills seemed to rise from this huge snowy flat id never stood on before, and the fishermen way out there with their dogs looked like they belonged to some other world. on misty days, they couldve been giants or pygmies for all i knew. took that route when i had a lecture in Lincoln, no roads, no houses between my cabin and the hall. Goose Pond had its muskrat colony with their huts sticking up above the ice, though they stayed hidden when i passed. Walden, usually snow-free or just spotted with drifts, was my playground when snow elsewhere was knee-deep, and everyone else stuck to the streets. out there, far from the village and the sleigh bells, i skated around, surrounded by oak and pine, weighed down with snow or sparkling with icicles.

in winter, id hear the lonely call of an owl, like the earth itself was singing back. never saw it, but that _hoo hoo hoo, hoorer, hoo_ became a part of the woods' language to me. many nights id step outside and hear it. once, before the pond froze over, a goose honked loud around nine, and as i looked out, its wings sounded like a storm passing over. the owl, not to be outdone, answered back, its voice huge and harsh, like it was challenging the goose to a vocal showdown. _boo-hoo, boo-hoo, boo-hoo!_ man, that was a wild chorus, but if you listened close, you could catch a strange sort of harmony you wouldn't expect.

the ice on the pond, my big restless roommate out there, would groan and shift like it was trying to turn over in its sleep. sometimes, the frost would crack the ground, sounded like something hit my door, and id find a long, thin crack in the morning, just a reminder of how alive everything was out there.

sometimes i heard the foxes skitter over the snow crust on moonlit nights, hunting for partridge or whatever they could find. their barks were rough, like forest dogs, full of some kind of anxiety. felt like they were trying to be something more, maybe even like us, roaming the streets. makes you wonder if animals have their own kind of civilization going on. they seemed like early versions of us, still guarded, waiting to evolve. sometimes one would get close to my window, maybe drawn by the light, bark a fox's curse at me, then disappear.

most mornings, the red squirrel would wake me up at dawn, like it was on a mission from the woods. in winter, i tossed half a bushel of sweet corn ears that didn't ripen onto the snow by my door. watching the critters come for it was my entertainment. rabbits showed up at twilight, munching away. all day, red squirrels darted around. they'd sneak up through the shrub-oaks, like wind-blown leaves, darting this way and that, never straightforward. they'd pause, look around like they were on stage, then zip to a pine top, chattering away for no reason i could see.

eventually, one would reach the corn, pick an ear, and zig-zag to my wood-pile. he'd sit there, nibbling voraciously at first, tossing half-naked cobs around. then he'd get picky, eating just the kernel insides. sometimes he'd drop the ear, look at it like it might crawl away, deciding whether to fetch it or get a new one. he'd waste a bunch of ears like that, until he found a big one, almost his size. he'd drag it off, zig-zagging and tumbling, determined to get it to his stash. i'd find cobs scattered all over the woods later. quirky little guy, but he got the job done.

jays showed up first. you could hear them squawking from way off, sneaking closer tree by tree. they’d grab the nuts the squirrels dropped, try to gulp them whole, choke, then spit them out. spent forever trying to crack them open. never liked jays much, seemed like thieves. squirrels, on the other hand, took what was theirs, nothing more.

then came the chickadees, always in flocks. they'd nab crumbs left by squirrels, hop up to a twig, and hammer away till the crumbs were small enough to swallow. they’d visit daily, picking off my wood-pile or crumbs by the door. their calls were like icicles tinkling in grass. one got so bold it landed on my armful of wood, pecking away like i wasn't even there. made me think of a sparrow that once landed on my shoulder while i was hoeing. felt more special than any badge i could wear. squirrels got comfy too, sometimes stepping on my shoe just to get across.

once the snow thinned, partridges came out mornings and evenings to feed. they’d burst into flight, knocking snow off branches, making it fall like golden dust in the sun. not easily scared by winter. sometimes they dive into snow drifts, stay hidden for days. found them in open fields too, coming out at sunset to nibble wild apple buds. they had their favorite trees, and hunters knew it. orchards near woods took a hit. but i'm glad the partridge gets its fill. it’s nature’s bird, living off buds and whatnot.

dark winter mornings, sometimes you hear this pack of hounds out in the woods. they're just going wild with the chase, the sound of a hunting horn here and there, tells you a man’s back there somewhere. but you don’t see a fox breaking out onto the pond, and there’s no pack right behind it. maybe in the evening, you spot hunters coming back with a single brush hanging off their sleigh, like a trophy. they say if a fox just stayed buried in the frozen ground, safe. or if it took off in a straight line, no hound could catch it. but it slows down, listens, circles back to where the hunters wait. sometimes it’ll run along a wall and then leap off, knows water won’t hold its scent. heard one story where a fox hit the ice on walden, ran across puddles, then looped back. hounds showed up, lost the trail. sometimes hounds, on their own, circle my place in a frenzy, like they’re mad, till they pick up a fresh fox trail. wise hound drops everything else for that. one day, a guy from lexington came to my hut, looking for his hound. big track, been hunting solo for a week. but he didn’t get much wiser from our chat, kept cutting me off with, “what do you do here?” lost a dog, found a man.

an old hunter used to swing by for a dip in walden when the water warmed up. once he told me about a time he grabbed his gun and took a walk through walden wood. as he was on the wayland road, he heard hounds barking. suddenly, a fox jumped across the road, dodged his bullet like it was nothing, and vanished. behind it, an old hound and her pups were chasing hard, but they disappeared into the woods again.

later, while resting in the thick woods south of walden, he heard those hounds again, still chasing the fox toward fair haven, their cries getting closer. then the fox showed up again, moving quiet and fast, leaving the hounds in the dust. it leapt onto a rock right in front of the hunter, sat there listening. for a split second, he felt sorry for the fox, but instincts kicked in. he aimed, fired, and the fox dropped.

the hounds arrived, the old one first, puzzled by the dead fox. she circled it, silent, then her pups joined, equally confused. the hunter stepped up, skinned the fox, while they watched. later, a squire from weston came looking for his hounds. the hunter told him the story, offered the skin, but the squire wasn’t interested. he didn’t find his hounds that night, but next day, heard they’d crashed at a farm, well-fed and gone by morning.

the hunter told me about this guy, sam nutting, who hunted bears on fair haven ledges. he'd trade their skins for rum in concord village. nutting had this fox-hound named burgoyne, but he said it like bugine. the hunter used to borrow him. found an entry in an old trader's book, who was also a captain and town clerk. jan 18th, 1742–3, “john melven cr. by 1 grey fox 0—2—3;” you dont find them here now. feb 7th, 1743, hezekiah stratton got credit “by ½ a catt skin 0—1—4½;” wild-cat, not a house cat. stratton fought in the old french war, hunted big game. deer skins got sold all the time. still, one guy's got the horns from the last deer killed here. another guy told me about the hunt his uncle was in. hunters used to be a big, happy group around here. remember a tall guy who could play a tune on a leaf better than any hunting horn.

sometimes at midnight, with the moon up, i'd run into hounds sneaking through the woods. they'd hide from me, stand quiet in the bushes till i passed.

squirrels and wild mice fought over my nuts. pitch-pines near my house, one to four inches thick, got gnawed by mice last winter. snow was deep, mice had to eat pine bark too. trees looked fine by mid-summer, grew a foot even if girdled, but after another winter, all dead. odd a mouse gets a whole tree to itself, but maybe it's to thin out the dense growth.

hares (_lepus americanus_) were bold. one wintered under my house, only the floor between us. every morning, she'd dash off when i moved, banging into the floor timbers. they'd come by in the evening for potato peels i tossed out, blending into the ground. at twilight, i'd lose and find one sitting still under my window. open the door, and they'd dash off squeaking. up close, they looked pitiful. one night, one sat two steps away, trembling but not moving. scrawny, ragged ears, sharp nose, skinny tail, thin paws. looked like nature's last stand. its eyes were big, sickly. i took a step, and off it went, springing over the snow crust, stretching out, disappearing into the woods. wild and free, nature still in charge. its slenderness made sense, that's its nature. Mirabeau tried highway robbery just to see what it takes to stand against society's laws. said it takes more courage than being a soldier. honor and religion didn't stop him. might seem brave, but really pointless. anyone with sense finds themselves up against society's rules just by following their own truth. the real challenge is sticking to who you are without having to fight everything. follow your nature, and you'll never clash with a fair government.

left the woods for the same reason i went. felt like i had more lives to live, couldn't waste it all there. funny how quick we settle into routines. a week in and i'd already worn a path to the pond. still there years later, maybe others walked it too. earth’s soft, paths are easy to mark, just like our minds. world’s highways are worn and dusty, deep with tradition and conformity. didn't want the easy passage, wanted to be out in the open, seeing the moonlight from the deck. not going below now.

i figured this out by trying it myself: push toward your dreams, live the life you picture, you'll find success sneaks up on you. you leave some stuff behind, cross a line you can't see; suddenly things around you shift, old limits stretch, feel like the universe is cutting you some slack. life gets simpler, and the universe seems less tangled. solitude, poverty, weakness? they won't feel the same. built castles in the air? good. now lay the groundwork under them.

it's wild how England and America demand you speak clearly for them. like you have to fit their mold. nature doesn't just support one kind of understanding. it's like saying only birds or only four-legged creatures are valid. i worry my words aren't bold enough, don't stretch far enough beyond the everyday. being bold depends on your limits. a buffalo seeking new pastures isn't reckless like a cow jumping the fence. i want to speak without bounds, like someone fully awake talking to others waking up. can't exaggerate enough to express truth. when you hear music, you don't fear you'll overstate again. let's live with blurry edges, open to what's possible, like shadows stretching toward the sun. words should hint at more than what they literally say. the truth of our words shifts, leaving behind only the literal shell. words of faith and piety aren't fixed, but they're meaningful, like incense to higher beings.

why settle for the dullest perception and call that common sense? the sleep of men is the common sense they snore out. sometimes we lump the once-and-a-half-witted with the half-witted, missing most of their wit. some would critique the morning-red if they woke up for it. "they say," as i hear, "Kabir's verses have four meanings: illusion, spirit, intellect, and Vedic doctrine"; but here, if a man’s words have more than one meaning, it's a complaint. England's fixing potato-rot; why not fix the brain-rot that's everywhere, worse?

i don't think i've reached obscurity, but i'd be okay if the worst criticism of my writing was like the Walden ice. southern folks didn't like its blue, a sign of purity, preferring the white Cambridge ice that tastes of weeds. purity people love is like earth's mist, not the clear blue sky beyond.

people keep saying we're small-minded compared to the ancients or even the Elizabethans. but so what? a living dog beats a dead lion. no need to mope about being a pygmy, just be the best pygmy you can. focus on your own stuff and be what you're meant to be.

why rush into big risky ventures just to succeed fast? if someone doesn't keep up, maybe they're hearing a different beat. let them follow it, even if it's far off. not everyone needs to grow like an apple tree or oak. why force spring into summer? if what we're meant for isn't here yet, why settle for a fake reality? let's not get wrecked on something empty. no point building a fake sky when the real one is right there.

there was this artist in Kouroo, aiming for perfection. decided to make a staff. thought time spoils imperfection but doesn't touch perfection. he went to get the right wood, rejecting pieces. friends left him, they aged and died, but he stayed young. time couldn't touch him. by the time he found the perfect stick, Kouroo was ruins. while shaping it, dynasties ended. polished it, stars shifted. but why list all this? when it was done, the staff was a masterpiece, like a world of its own. old things gone, new better ones appeared. he realized time was an illusion for him. his materials and craft were pure; the result couldn't be anything but amazing.

truth's the best tool we got. most of the time, we're not really where we think we are. we imagine stuff, place ourselves in it, and then we're stuck in two different spots at once. when we're clear-headed, only the facts matter. speak your truth, not what you think you should say. a real truth beats any lie. tom hyde, up on the gallows, told them to remind tailors about tying a knot in their thread before stitching. his buddy's prayer? forgotten.

no matter how rough your life looks, face it. don't run or badmouth it. it's not as bad as you think. you see it worst when you're doing best. even in a poor-house, you can find bright, thrilling moments. the sun hits the alms-house windows just like the rich man's place. snow melts there at the same time too. a calm mind can be happy anywhere, palace or not. the town's poor have some of the freest lives. maybe they're just big enough to accept help without shame. most folks think they're too good for charity but will stoop low to get by. embrace poverty like a garden herb, like sage. don't fuss over getting new stuff—clothes or friends. return to the old. things don't change; we do. sell your clothes, keep your thoughts. you won't lack company. even stuck in a garret corner, the world's huge if you've got your mind. a philosopher said: take away a general, and an army falls apart, but a man's thoughts can't be taken. don't chase development or let yourself be influenced too much—it scatters you. humility shows us the real lights. shadows of poverty close in, and suddenly the world's bigger. even with endless wealth, our goals and means don't change much. if poverty limits you, it forces you to focus on what really counts. life close to the bone is the sweetest. it keeps you from being a dabbler. no one loses by being generous on a high level. extra wealth buys only extras. money can't buy what the soul truly needs.

i live in a corner of this heavy world, where the sounds from outside drift in like a jumbled bell ringing. my neighbors talk about who they met, all those big names at dinners, but it’s like reading the Daily Times—same old stuff. they chat about clothes and manners, but a goose is still a goose, no matter the dress. they mention places like California, Texas, England, but it's all just passing stuff. makes me want to break free like a Mameluke bey.

i prefer to find my own ground—walk with the universe, not in some parade. this century is frantic, but i'd rather stand still and watch it pass. everyone’s expecting speeches, as if God’s just a figurehead for the day and Webster’s his speaker. i like to find what pulls me the strongest and go there, not pretending but facing what’s real. no point in starting something without a solid base. let’s not play games. there’s always solid ground somewhere.

like the traveler asking about the swamp’s bottom, only to find it deep. society’s the same—full of hidden traps, but you learn with time. only rare truths matter. i won’t nail into flimsy walls; i need to trust my work. every nail should be secure, so i can sleep easy, knowing it’s good work. every piece should fit the universe perfectly.

truth over love, money, fame. i’d leave a fancy table if there’s no sincerity. riches can be cold. they talked of old wine, but i thought of a purer one they can't buy. style and entertainment don’t impress me. even kings can lack true hospitality. a neighbor lived in a hollow tree, but he had a king’s manners. i should’ve visited him instead.

how long are we gonna sit around, pretending to be virtuous while doing nothing? like starting the day with patience, then hiring someone to do our work, and later pretending to be all charitable with intent. think about the pride and complacency we see everywhere. people patting themselves on the back, thinking they're the peak of civilization. in places like boston, london, paris, rome, they boast about their progress in art, science, literature. they've got their records and eulogies of 'great men'. it's like adam admiring his own virtue. "we've done great deeds, sung amazing songs"—as long as we remember them, that is. where are the great minds of assyria now? we're all still young in the big picture. our time here might just be the spring of human existence. if we've had our small troubles, we haven't seen the big ones yet. we barely know the surface of this world. most haven't dug six feet down or jumped six feet high. we're asleep half our lives. yet we think we're wise, with our little order on the surface. we think we're deep thinkers, ambitious. looking at an insect hiding in the pines, i wonder why it hides, not realizing i could help it. then i think, maybe there's a greater intelligence watching over me, the human insect.

new things keep coming into the world, but we put up with so much dullness. look at the sermons people still listen to in the most 'enlightened' places. words like joy and sorrow are just part of a song sung flat, while we believe in the ordinary. we think we can only change our clothes. they say the british empire is big and respectable, the u.s. is a major power. but there's a tide behind every man that could lift the empire like a chip if he realized it. who knows what the next big change will be? the world i live in wasn't planned over wine like britain's was.

life's like a river. some years it spills over, floods places we thought were dry. maybe this is the year we lose all our muskrats. long ago, the water reached places we now walk on. you can see where the river used to run, before anyone tracked it. reminds me of that old story from New England, about a bug coming out of a table. table was apple wood, sat in a kitchen for sixty years, first in Connecticut, then Massachusetts. bug came from an egg laid in the tree even longer ago. took weeks for it to chew its way out, maybe helped by the warmth of a nearby urn. hearing that, you gotta wonder about resurrection, about what’s buried in society’s layers, waiting to break free. maybe there’s something under all this, waiting to live.

not saying John or Jonathan will see it. time alone won’t make it happen. we only see the day we're awake for. there's more to come. sun's just the morning star.

accept the motto: “best government governs least.” want to see it happen faster. take it further: “best government governs not at all.” when people are ready, that’s what they'll choose. government’s a tool, often misused. standing armies, standing governments — both can be abused. look at the Mexican war. a few people drove it, using the government as their tool. people wouldn’t have agreed at first.

this american government, it's like a tradition. not that old, but trying to pass itself on without falling apart, though it's losing pieces every moment. it doesn't have the power of one determined person; a person can twist it around. feels like a wooden gun to us; if we ever use it seriously against each other, it'll split. but it's still needed; people need some kind of complex machinery, some noise, to feel like they have a government. shows how good we are at fooling ourselves for our own benefit. it's a fine system, sure, but the government never pushed anything forward by itself, only by getting out of its own way. it doesn't keep us free, settle the west, or educate us. it's the american people who did all that, and probably would've done more if the government hadn't blocked the path sometimes. government's just a tool so folks can leave each other be. when it's working best, it leaves us alone. trade and commerce, they're like india rubber, bouncing over the hurdles lawmakers put up. if we judged these lawmakers just by what they do, not what they mean, they might be seen like those who mess with railroad tracks.

but, practically speaking, as a citizen, i'm not saying no government. i want a better government. let everyone say what kind of government they'd respect, and that's a step toward getting it.

when people hold the power, majorities rule not cause they're right, but cause they're stronger. a gov where majorities call the shots ain't just. it should be about conscience, not just numbers. why should you ever hand your conscience to a legislator? be a man first, subject second. respect what's right, not just what's written. laws don't make folks just, they just make 'em follow orders. like soldiers marching to war, knowing it's wrong but doing it anyway. they're not men, just tools for some guy in power. most serve as machines, no judgment or moral sense. they're as good as wood or stone. a few serve with conscience and resist, often seen as enemies. a wise man won't be just clay for the state. too high-born for that, too.

give yourself to people fully, and they might call you useless. hold back a bit, and suddenly you're a hero.

how should a man deal with the american government today? simple: he cant, not without shame. i wont ever see a system as mine if it’s also a slave’s government.

everyone knows they can rise up against a government when it's too tyrannical or useless. most say we're not there now. they look back to '75 and say that was the time. but if someone tells me it's bad just because it taxes foreign goods, i might let it slide. all systems have some friction and maybe the good outweighs the bad there. but when the friction becomes the system itself, when oppression and theft are organized, that's when enough is enough. when a country claiming to be liberty's refuge has a sixth of its people in chains, and another land is unfairly invaded and ruled by military force, it's time for honest folks to revolt. it's even more urgent when we're the invaders, not the invaded.

paley, a go-to for many on moral stuff, says obey the government as long as it benefits society, and only resist if it doesn't. it's all about weighing danger against the chance and cost of fixing it. but paley missed something: sometimes, justice must be served no matter the cost. if i snatch a plank from a drowning man, i should give it back even if it means drowning myself. paley would call that inconvenient, but sometimes doing right is more important than surviving. this nation has to stop holding slaves and waging war on mexico, even if it risks everything.

nations follow paley's logic, but really, is massachusetts doing what's right now?

"a drab of state, a cloth-o’-silver slut,
    to have her train borne up, and her soul trail in the dirt."

let's break it down. the real challenge in massachusetts ain't some distant southern politicians. it's the local merchants and farmers who care more about their business than about people. they're not ready to take a stand on slavery or mexico if it costs them. it's easy to blame the world, but the real issue's right here at home. folks talk about change being slow because we're all waiting on others to wise up. but it's not about everyone being perfect. it's about having a few truly good folks to make a difference. 

there's a bunch of people who say they're against slavery and war, but they just sit around. they call themselves the heirs of washington and franklin, but really, they're just reading the news and doing nothing. they talk about free trade and then nap over the papers. what's an honest man's worth these days? they hesitate, maybe regret a bit, sometimes even sign a petition. but that's it. they wait around, hoping someone else fixes things so they don't have to feel bad. maybe they vote, give a weak nod to the right cause, but that's not action. 

voting's like a game, a mix of morals and chance. you might vote for what's right, but it's still just a gamble. you're not risking anything personal. a vote alone isn't enough. it's just a weak wish for change. real change doesn't come from leaving things to chance or hoping the majority gets it right. when the masses finally vote to end slavery, it'll be because either they don't care or there's nothing left to fix. they become the real slaves. only those who vote with true freedom can push for real change.

there's a convention in baltimore or somewhere, picking a presidential candidate. mostly editors and career politicians. but really, why should any smart, independent guy care about their choice? can’t we rely on some independent votes still? aren’t there folks out there who skip these conventions? but nope, seems like the so-called respectable man just caves in, loses faith in the country when really, the country's losing faith in him. he jumps on the bandwagon, picking one of their candidates as the only option, showing he's just available to any political game. his vote, no better than a bought one, foreign or local. we need a real stand-up guy, like my neighbor says, someone with a backbone you can’t bend. our numbers are off: way fewer real men per thousand miles here than we think. does america not have enough to offer real men anymore? the american's become just another odd fellow, more interested in groupthink than intellect or self-reliance, worried more about alms-houses than living with integrity. 

it’s not automatically a man's job to fight every big wrong out there, but he should at least not support it. if i'm off doing my own thing, i gotta make sure i'm not doing it at another man's expense. i can't be sitting on his shoulders while i think. inconsistency's everywhere. some guys say they'd refuse to help put down a slave uprising or march to mexico, but they’re still backing the very system that does it, directly or with their money. we praise soldiers who refuse unjust wars but ignore the fact that we’re still supporting the government behind those wars. it's like we're paying someone to punish us for our own wrongs without stopping the wrongs themselves. under the guise of order, we end up supporting our own mediocrity. first, we sin, then we become indifferent, and eventually, it’s like it doesn't even matter — it's just part of the life we've built.

biggest error needs the most honest folks to keep it going. patriotism gets a bad rep, and those with noble intentions often fall for it. folks who don't like what the government does but still support it are the ones making it hard to change things. some are asking the State to break away from the Union, ignore what the President demands. why don't they just break their own ties with the State and stop funding it? they’re tied to the State like the State is to the Union. same reasons holding the State back are holding them back.

how can you just hold an opinion and think that’s enough? if you think you’ve been wronged, is there any joy in just knowing it? if a neighbor cheats you out of a dollar, you don’t just sit there saying you’ve been cheated; you take action to get what’s yours and make sure it doesn’t happen again. acting on principle changes things; it's revolutionary and doesn’t fit with what's been. it divides more than states and churches; it cuts through families and even the self, separating the bad from the good inside.

unjust laws exist: do we just follow them, try to change them and follow them until we succeed, or break them right away? most folks think we should wait for the majority to agree to change. they fear resisting would make things worse. but it's the government’s fault if the fix is worse than the problem. why doesn’t it foresee and plan for change? why doesn’t it value its smart minority? why does it fight before it's even hurt? why doesn’t it encourage folks to speak up and do better? why does it always punish the brave thinkers and call them rebels?

seems like the government never expects someone to flat-out deny its authority; otherwise, why hasn’t it set a clear penalty for it? if a person refuses to earn for the State, they're jailed indefinitely, but if they steal from the State, they get freedom soon enough.

if the injustice is just part of the system's grind, let it pass; the machine will wear out. but if the injustice is a core part, then maybe think twice before acting. but if it forces you to harm another, break the law. let your life counteract the machine. i just need to make sure i’m not part of the wrong i call out.

i dont know much about the state's ways of fixing things, but they're slow. life's too short for that. i got other things to handle. im here to live in this world, good or bad, not fix it. can't do everything, but that doesn't mean doing something wrong. not my job to beg the governor or legislature, same as they dont beg me. if they ignore me, then what? the state's got no fix. its constitution is the problem. might sound tough, but it's the only way to deal with it right. change ain't easy, like being born or dying.

i'll say it: abolitionists should just stop supporting the massachusetts government, with their bodies and wallets, instead of waiting to be a majority. if they got God, they dont need more. being more right than others makes you a majority of one.

once a year, the state shows up as the tax man. it's the only time i really deal with them. it says, "recognize me," and the best way to show im not happy is to say no. my civil neighbor, the tax collector, is who i deal with. it's men, not paper, i argue with. he's chosen to be the state's agent. how will he know his role unless he decides if he'll treat me as a neighbor or a problem? can he get past this without getting rough? i know if just ten honest men stop supporting slavery, even if jailed, it'd end slavery in america. once something's done right, it's done forever. but we prefer talking. reform's got papers but no people. if the state's man, working on human rights, was jailed here instead of threatened in carolina, massachusetts wouldn't ignore it next winter.

if a government starts throwing people in prison for standing up for what's right, then the real place for a just person is in there too. Massachusetts has a spot for folks who think freely and don't just go with the flow—right in their prisons. That's where the real rebels, the fugitive slaves, the Mexican parolees, and the Indians speaking out should be. It's a weird kind of honor, but it's the only place left for truly free men in a slave state.

some might think they'd lose their voice in there, but they don't get how truth's way stronger than lies. your whole vote's not just a piece of paper. it's your whole influence. a minority's got no power if it just follows the majority. but when it stands firm, it's got the weight to change things. if keeping all the good folks behind bars is the only way to keep war and slavery going, the State will have to think twice about that choice.

picture this: if a thousand people refused to pay taxes this year, it wouldn't be violent. it'd be stopping the State from using that money for violence. that's a peaceful revolution if there's ever one. if a tax collector asked me what to do, I'd say, "quit your job." once the people stop obeying and the officials step down, that's when change happens.

and if blood does flow—well, isn't a wounded conscience a kind of bloodshed too? that's when a person's real self drains away. I see that happening now.

been thinking about how locking someone up hits harder than just taking their stuff. folks with the clearest rights, the real threats to a messed-up system, usually don't have much to lose in terms of property. they don't get much from the State anyway, and even a small tax feels huge if they have to work extra hard to pay it. if someone lived without money, the State might not even bother asking for it. but the rich, they're tied to the systems that keep them wealthy. more money usually means less virtue, cause cash gets in the way of what you really want. it answers easy questions but leaves you with the big one — how to spend it. your moral foundation gets shaky as your means go up. the best a rich man can do is chase the dreams he had when he was poor. like when christ told the Herodians to give Cæsar whats his and leave gods things to god. didn't clear up much for them cause they weren't really looking for answers.

talking with my neighbors, even the freest, shows me they can't let go of the government's safety net. fear of losing property and safety keeps them in line. me, i don't like leaning on the State's protection. but if i say no to their tax, they'll just take my stuff and make life hell for me and my kids. that's tough. makes living honest and comfy at the same time impossible. no point in hoarding wealth; it's gonna vanish anyway. gotta live light, be ready to move, not get tangled in too many affairs. you can get rich anywhere if you play by the rules, like Confucius said. if a State's fair, being poor is a shame. if not, being rich is. until i need Massachusetts to save me or help me build an empire, i'll pass on allegiance. cheaper to face the State's wrath than to comply. otherwise, i'd feel like i'd lost something essential.

years back, the State came after me about the church, telling me to chip in for a preacher my dad listened to, but not me. "Pay up," they said, "or see jail time." i said no. someone else paid it. didn't make sense to me why the schoolmaster should fund the priest, but not vice versa; i wasn't the State's schoolmaster, i survived on voluntary support. why not have the lyceum send a tax bill too, backed by the State? anyhow, at the selectmen's request, i wrote down: "Know all men by these presents, that i, Henry Thoreau, do not wish to be regarded as a member of any incorporated society which i have not joined." handed that to the town-clerk; it's with him now. since then, the State hasn't asked me again, though it stood by its first presumption back then. if i knew how, i would've signed off from all societies i never signed onto, but i didn't know where to find a full list.

haven't paid poll-tax for six years. got jailed once for one night over it. standing there, looking at the thick stone walls, solid wood and iron doors, and iron grating filtering the light, i just marveled at this foolish setup treating me like just flesh and bones. i wondered why they thought this was the best use of me and never bothered to use my abilities. if there was a stone wall between me and my townsfolk, there was a tougher one blocking their freedom more than mine. didn't feel confined, walls were just wasted stone and mortar. felt like i was the only one who truly paid my tax. they couldn't handle me right, acting like amateurs. every threat and compliment was off; they thought i wanted to be on the other side of that wall. i smiled watching them lock the door on my thoughts, which slipped right past them. they couldn't touch me, so they punished my body, like kids who can't touch someone, so they go after his dog. saw the State was dim-witted, scared like a woman guarding her spoons, not knowing friends from foes, and i lost the last of my respect for it, felt sorry for it.

state doesn't go for your mind or morals, just your body. not about being smarter or more honest, just stronger. nobody born to be forced. i'll breathe my own way, thanks. let's see who's stronger. only those who follow a higher law can really force me, make me like them. i don't hear about folks getting pushed around by crowds. what kind of life is that? when a government demands "your money or your life," why rush to pay? maybe it's in trouble, but that's not my problem. it should handle itself, like i do. no point whining about it. i'm not here to make society's machine run right. not my job. when an acorn and chestnut fall, neither waits for the other. they grow as they can, till one overshadows or destroys the other. if a plant can't live its nature, it dies. same for a man.

prison night was new and kinda interesting. prisoners in shirt-sleeves chatting at the door when i got there. jailer says, "time to lock up," and they head back to their cells. my cellmate, jailer called him "first-rate and clever," showed me the ropes. room whitewashed monthly, neatest place in town probably. he asked why i was there; i did the same. "barn burning," he said, but claimed innocence. likely he slept drunk in a barn, smoked, and it burned. smart guy, been waiting on trial three months, content with free board and decent treatment.

he had a window, i had the other. if you stay long, mostly you watch out the window. read all the tracts soon enough, checked where others broke out, heard stories of past prisoners. even here, there's history and gossip that stays inside. probably the only place in town where verses get written but not published. saw a list of verses by guys who tried to escape, getting back by singing.

drained my cellmate of stories, not sure if i'd see him again. finally, he pointed out my bed and left me to blow out the lamp.

spent the night somewhere that felt like stepping into another world. didn't expect to see my own hometown like that, like it was out of the Middle Ages, windows open, hearing every sound like it was the first time. concord turned into some medieval scene with knights and castles. felt like i was inside the town for the first time, seeing things i never saw. it's a shire town, and i finally got what that meant.

breakfast came through a slot in the door. tin pans with chocolate and brown bread, an iron spoon. made the rookie mistake of giving back the leftover bread, but my buddy stopped me. he took off to work in a field, said we might not see each other again. 

someone paid my tax, got me out of jail. nothing outside changed much, but i saw things different. realized the people around me weren't exactly the friends i thought. fair weather folks, stuck in their ways, not risking much for others. figured out they hoped to save themselves with rituals and prayers, like they were on some straight path that didn't lead anywhere. maybe i'm judging too hard, but i bet most don't even remember there's a jail here.

used to be when someone got out of jail, the neighbors would make a joke, crossing their fingers like jail bars, asking "how do ye do?" nobody did that for me. just stared, like i'd been gone forever. got locked up on my way to the shoemaker's, and when i got out, i finished what i started. put on my fixed shoe, joined a huckleberry picking group, and headed out. in no time, i was in a field two miles away, and the state felt far behind. 

that's the tale of "My Prisons."

never skipped out on paying my highway tax. i want to be a good neighbor but not just another cog in the wheel. i know my part in supporting schools, educating the folks around me. it's not about a line item on the tax bill. it's about stepping back from the state. not interested in tracking my dollar all the way to a bullet. but i do want to see what my allegiance does. i'm at odds with the state in my own way, though i'll take what i can get from it.

if others pay the tax out of state sympathy, they’re just deepening their own ties. if they do it to help me avoid trouble, they’re letting personal feelings skew the bigger picture.

that's where i stand now. gotta be careful not to let stubbornness or others’ opinions sway me. focus on what's mine to do, right now.

sometimes i think folks mean well, just don't know better. why put them through the hassle of dealing with me like this? but then, why should i follow the crowd and let others bear even bigger burdens? it's a tricky balance. when millions demand a few bucks without room for change, why fight it? i don't resist nature like that. but when i see it as a human force, i know there’s room for appeal. you don’t argue with fire, but with people, there's a chance.

if i could be content with people as they are, i'd just accept things and call it fate. but unlike nature, this can be resisted with some hope of change. can't expect to change rocks and trees, but people? there's a shot.

i dont want to argue with anyone or any nation. not trying to split hairs or act like im better than my neighbors. honestly just looking for a reason to stick to the laws. i find myself too willing to go along with them. every year when the tax man comes, i end up thinking about what the government is doing and what the people are like, trying to find a reason to conform.

we should treat our country like our parents. if we ever separate our love of work from honoring it, we should respect the consequences and teach ourselves the conscience and religion of it, not just rule or benefit.

most folks think different from me, but even the experts in these fields don't quite satisfy. statesmen and legislators, stuck inside the system, can't see it clearly. they talk about moving society but have no base outside it. sure, they've built some clever and useful systems, thanks for that, but they're stuck within small boundaries. they forget the world ain't run by policy alone. webster, for example, never looks beyond government, so he can't speak with real authority. his words work for those who don't seek deep reform, but thinkers aiming for timeless change? he misses the mark. i know some who'd quickly show the limits of his thinking. compared to the usual reformers and politicians, his words are wise and valuable, and we ought to be thankful for him. he's strong, original, practical, but his strength is prudence, not wisdom. a lawyer's truth is about consistency, not the deeper truth. truth aligns with itself, not justifying wrongs. webster's called the defender of the constitution, and he's defensive, not a leader. his leaders are the men of '87. he won't try to change the original union setup. even when considering slavery's place in the constitution, he says, "let it stand." despite his skill, he can't see beyond politics to what truly needs doing about slavery here and now. he offers answers, but they're limited, like saying the states where slavery exists must handle it themselves, untouched by outside associations, and with no encouragement from him.

those who haven't found purer truth stick to the bible and constitution, drinking from them respectfully. but those who see where truth trickles in, move forward, seeking its source.

no genius for legislation in america yet. we've got plenty of talkers, politicians, folks who sound good. but no one who can settle the big questions. we love the sound of a good speech more than the truth it might hold. our legislators don't get the real value of things like free-trade vs freedom, union vs integrity. they're not skilled in the nitty-gritty of taxes, finance, commerce, and agriculture. if we only listened to congress without the people's pushback, america wouldn't last long as a leading nation. the new testament's been around for eighteen hundred years, but still no legislator wise enough to use its light on making laws.

government's authority, even the kind i'm okay with following, isn't pure. real justice needs the governed to agree. it has no right over me that i don't give it. moving from monarchy to democracy is about respecting the individual more. even the old chinese thinkers got that the individual is the empire's foundation. is democracy the best we can do? can we go further in recognizing individual rights? a truly free state will see the individual as the true source of its power. i imagine a state that respects each person, even if some choose to live apart, fulfilling their duties without interference. a state like that would pave the way for something even better, a vision i've got but haven't witnessed yet.
