PREFACE.

Walt Whitman talks about "loving by allowance" vs. "loving with personal love." Applies to books too. Authors we personally love stir up more debate about their best work. With Austen, everyone has their pick. _Northanger Abbey_ for humor, _Persuasion_ for delicacy, _Mansfield Park_ for its characters despite a theatrical ending, and _Sense and Sensibility_ with fewer hardcore fans. But most votes split between _Emma_ and _Pride and Prejudice_. _Emma_ is more varied and popular, but I'm sticking with _Pride and Prejudice_—quintessential Austen. I'll show you why.

jane austen's "pride and prejudice" started early, around 1796, when she was just 21. she revisited it at chawton 15 years later, but it didn't hit the shelves until 1813. so you've got this mix of youthful energy and a mature, critical eye. the plot's tight—every bit matters. lydia and wickham's elopement isn't just drama for drama's sake; it ties right into the story and wraps things up nicely. all the smaller stories like jane and bingley's romance, mr. collins dropping in, the hunsford visit, and the derbyshire tour—those all fit perfectly together without shouting for attention. no wild plot twists like in "emma" with frank churchill and jane fairfax, though those were fun in their way.

austen liked a bit of misunderstanding, and here it's darcy's bad rap from wickham and elizabeth's evolving feelings that drive the story naturally. now, i don't know if "pride and prejudice" ever got the stage treatment, but if it did, it might not be flashy enough for the theater crowd. still, the structure's solid, not like some novels where plot holes get patched up on stage.

but here's the thing—structure isn't everything. it's great for showcasing other talents, but a novel with fantastic characters or killer dialogue, even if the plot's a mess, is better than a perfectly plotted snooze-fest. "pride and prejudice" stands out because of austen's humor and characters. they're the real stars, even if some other characters from her works might come close.

miss austen's humor, it's subtle, real subtle. hard to pin down with words, but you feel it. kind of like addison's work, more than anyone else. different times, different rules, but they both had this knack for gently roasting fools. not loud, not flashy, just sharp and precise.

people often say addison was nice while swift was savage, austen mild and fielding wild. but both addison and austen had this controlled, almost polite way of tearing into someone. sure, austen's time and being a woman meant she couldn't go as far as addison sometimes did. but her sharpness, it's there.

when folks call austen cynical, they're missing the point. cynicism, in the way they mean it, isn't just about being harsh. it's about seeing the truth beneath the surface, knowing people aren't always what they seem. if that's cynicism, then sure, austen was a cynic. like mr. bennet, she enjoyed picking apart her characters, and it made her stories better. she's not worse for it; she's better. an artist through and through.

mr. goldwin smith once said metaphor's been worn out trying to capture her art's perfection and narrow field. her work's like a miniature painter's, but not in the limited sense. think more like memling or meissonier, not cosway. calling her world "narrow" might be off. it's small, but it's got the whole cosmos in it. she painted what she felt called to paint, but maybe she could've painted more if she'd wanted. in two short writing spurts, she cranked out six major works, no flops. her romantic side might've been lacking, but she was an english girl in a time when society had some weird restrictions on women. she backed off when the frost hit her shoes, but I bet she could've walked through a blizzard.

she might not have known everything, but she nailed two things: humanity and art. her characters feel real, men and women both. she didn't mess with idealism, but her realism's so strong it makes today's look fake. compare her to any french writer, minus maybe maupassant, and you'll see them piling on details hoping to make a point. doesn't work. with her, every small brushstroke adds up perfectly, like magic. nothing's fake, nothing's wasted. when mr. collins switched from jane to elizabeth "while mrs. bennet was stirring the fire," you know exactly what that moment's like. or when mr. darcy brought back his coffee cup himself. those little touches are like swift. even thackeray had to respect that. she’s as close to swift in some ways as she is to addison in others.

mr. collins in this novel is like no other. he's huge, like something out of swift or fielding. some say he's too much, but i think he could’ve been a real guy in the 18th century. look at john dashwood, kinda like a rough draft of collins. dashwood’s got the formality and pettiness, but collins? collins is alive, every bit of him. he shows off the small-mindedness and materialism of his time perfectly.

he's even better with mr. bennet and lady catherine de bourgh around him. lady catherine's seen as exaggerated too, but not by much. you could still find people like her today, especially back then, daughters of earls in small parishes. mr. bennet? folks are tough on him, but what's he supposed to do with a wife like that? his irony is the only way to deal. his humor when talking to her or putting collins in his place is top-notch. like when he tells his wife not to worry about the entail, hoping he outlives her, or when he asks collins if his compliments to lady catherine were spontaneous or practiced. those are the moments that make readers feel what they feel with swift or fielding.

minor characters in *Pride and Prejudice* bring a lot to the table, even if it's tough to dive deep into them here. mrs. bennet, for one, is a mix—hilariously real but also painfully so. kitty and lydia? same deal. austen nailed how different kinds of foolishness play out at different ages. then there's mary. austen didn't do her any favors, even marrying her off to some clerk in the background notes. feels like mary got the short end of the stick, a scapegoat for her time's faults. she deserves a nod, especially when she reflects on lydia's mess. kind of wish austen paired her with mr. collins, might've been a win-win and eased mrs. bennet's stress.

bingleys, gardiners, lucases, and the rest—no time to break them all down. charlotte lucas? a masterpiece in subtlety. wickham's tricky but avoids the usual pitfalls. only austen could pull that off.

as for darcy, he's tops among austen’s heroes. some folks say his pride’s unrealistic, but i don’t buy it. his pampered pride makes sense, and elizabeth's rejection wakes him up. that change when they meet at pemberley? spot on. if he were a jerk, he’d hold a grudge or suspect elizabeth was after him for his money. but he’s neither, and that fits a guy who's spoiled but genuinely good and in love. elizabeth nails why he fell for her—it's her charm that sealed the deal.

elizabeth bennet's got that kind of charm you can't really pin down, just feel. jane austen doesn't tell us much about her looks—just some fine eyes and a bright complexion now and then—but man, she stands out. she's clever, sharp-tongued, but not mean. she can dish it out and take it, but never starts trouble. in her big scenes with darcy or lady catherine, she's spot on. 

she's real, doesn't hide her feelings when darcy's rude, and lets her anger about jane's treatment simmer just right. but what really sets her apart is her fearless nature. where most would be in awe of darcy or swoon over wickham, elizabeth's got a modern woman's confidence without being over the top. no fear of being bullied or swept away. she's strong, sensible, and true to herself. 

sure, she might not show passion outright as austen's time linked it with qualities she didn't give elizabeth, but i bet she'd have said yes to darcy even without his estate. those final chapters? read between the lines—they're not as cold as they seem.

seeking the reason behind charm? it's just there, plain and simple. figuring out why it’s missing, like in some books, might be more useful. tons of novels in the past century have ladies you’d enjoy falling for. but five stand out: Elizabeth Bennet, Diana Vernon, Argemone Lavington, Beatrix Esmond, and Barbara Grant. personally, Beatrix and Argemone would've been my top picks. for hanging out, Diana and Barbara seem better. but if you’re talking living with and marrying, Elizabeth's got no rivals.

Chapter I.]

everyone knows a rich single man’s gotta be looking for a wife. doesn’t matter what he thinks. the neighbors already got him matched up with one of their daughters.

"Mr. Bennet," his wife said, "did you hear Netherfield Park finally got rented?"

he hadn't.

"Well, it is," she said. "Mrs. Long just told me."

no response from him.

"Don't you wanna know who rented it?" she pressed.

"You’re dying to tell me, so go ahead."

that was enough for her.

"Mrs. Long says it's a young man with a big fortune from up north. Came down Monday, loved it, made a deal with Mr. Morris right away. Moving in before Michaelmas, and some staff by end of next week."

"His name?"

"Bingley."

"Married or single?"

"Single, of course! Big fortune; four or five thousand a year. Perfect for our girls!"

"How’s that?"

"Oh, Mr. Bennet," she said, "he might marry one of them."

"Is that why he’s moving here?"

"Design? No, but he might fall for one. You need to visit him when he arrives."

"Don't see the need. You and the girls go. Or just the girls. You might outshine them."

"You’re kind to say that. But with five grown daughters, I’m past that."

"Usually not much left to think about beauty-wise."

"But you really should meet Mr. Bingley."

"I’m not promising."

"Think of the girls. What a match that’d be. Sir William and Lady Lucas are going just for that. You must, or we can't visit him."

"You’re being too careful. Mr. Bingley will be glad to see you. I’ll send a note giving him my blessing to pick any daughter he likes—though Lizzy’s my favorite."

"Don’t you dare. Lizzy’s not better than the others. She’s not as pretty as Jane or as cheerful as Lydia. But you always favor her."

"They’re all silly, like other girls, but Lizzy’s sharper."

mr. bennet, why do you torment your own kids like that? you just enjoy winding me up. no sympathy for my poor nerves.

you're mistaken, dear. i've got great respect for your nerves. they're like old friends at this point. you've talked about them for twenty years now.

ah, you don't understand my suffering.

but i hope you'll get past it and see many young guys with four thousand a year move nearby.

won't matter if twenty show up. you won't visit them.

trust me, dear, when there are twenty, i'll visit them all.

mr. bennet's mix of quick wit, sarcasm, and unpredictability kept his wife guessing even after twenty-three years. her mind was less complex. she lacked understanding and had a temper. when discontented, she thought she was nervous. her life's mission was marrying off her daughters; her hobby was visiting and gossip.

mr. bennet was one of the first to visit mr. bingley. always planned to, even while telling his wife he wouldn't. she found out the evening after he went. saw lizzy working on a hat and said,—

“hope mr. bingley likes it, lizzy.”

“we don’t know what he likes,” her mom snapped, “since we won’t visit.”

“but you forget, mom,” lizzy said, “we’ll meet him at the assemblies, and mrs. long promised introductions.”

“i doubt mrs. long will. she’s got her own nieces. selfish woman.”

“same here,” mr. bennet said. “good you aren’t depending on her.”

mrs. bennet stayed silent, but started scolding one daughter.

“stop coughing, kitty! think of my nerves. you wreck them.”

“kitty’s coughs have bad timing,” her dad added.

“i’m not coughing for fun,” kitty grumbled. “lizzy, when’s the next ball?”

“two weeks from tomorrow.”

“right,” her mom said, “and mrs. long’s not back till the day before; she can’t introduce him.”

“then you can introduce mr. bingley to her,” mr. bennet suggested.

“impossible, mr. bennet! how can you tease me when i don’t know him?”

“i appreciate your caution. two weeks is short. but if we don’t act, someone else will. mrs. long and her nieces will have to take their chances. if you pass, i’ll handle it.”

the girls were surprised. mrs. bennet just said, “nonsense!”

“what’s that about?” he asked. “do you think introductions are nonsense? don’t agree there. mary, what do you say? you’re wise, read big books, make notes.”

mary wanted to say something smart but couldn’t.

while mary's sorting out her thoughts, let's get back to bingley.

"im tired of bingley," his wife complained.

"wish you'd told me sooner. wouldn't have bothered visiting if id known. too late now."

mrs. bennet was thrilled, which was exactly what he expected. once her excitement eased off, she started saying she'd seen it coming all along.

"youre so thoughtful, mr. bennet! i knew id convince you. you love your daughters too much to miss such a match. im delighted! and it's hilarious you went and didn't even mention it till now."

"kitty, feel free to cough all you want," mr. bennet said, leaving the room, worn out by his wife's excitement.

"what a great dad you have, girls," she said after he left. "not sure how you'll repay him, or me for that matter. meeting new people isn't exactly a thrill at our age, but we'd do anything for you. lydia, even if you're the youngest, i bet mr. bingley will dance with you at the next ball."

"im not worried," lydia said confidently. "even if im the youngest, im the tallest."

the rest of the night was all about guessing when he'd return mr. bennet's call and planning when to invite him for dinner.

mrs. bennet and her daughters tried all sorts of tactics to get mr. bennet to spill details about bingley, but he dodged them all. they had to rely on lady lucas for info. she had only good things to say. sir william thought bingley was young, handsome, and charming. plus, he'd be at the next assembly with a big group. perfect! liking to dance was a step toward love, and there were high hopes for bingley's interest.

"if one daughter settles at netherfield," mrs. bennet told her husband, "and the rest marry well, ill be satisfied."

soon enough, bingley visited mr. bennet, spent ten minutes in his library, hoping to meet the daughters he'd heard so much about, but he only saw mr. bennet. the ladies did manage to spot his blue coat and black horse from an upstairs window.

mrs. bennet sent out a dinner invite, had all these plans for a big meal, but got shut down quick. bingley had to head to town the next day, so he couldn’t make it. she was pretty upset, couldn’t figure why he had business in town right after showing up in hertfordshire. she worried he’d keep bouncing around and never settle at netherfield. lady lucas tried to calm her down, saying maybe he was just in london to gather folks for the ball. soon enough, there was talk that bingley was bringing twelve ladies and seven guys. the girls were bummed about so many ladies, but then heard it was only six, including his sisters and a cousin. when they showed up, it was just bingley, two sisters, his brother-in-law, and another guy.

bingley was good-looking, easy-going, pleasant. his sisters had style. mr. hurst, the brother-in-law, looked the part of a gentleman, but it was darcy who caught the room’s attention. tall, handsome, and the word spread fast about his ten thousand a year. everyone admired him, but that went south when they found out he was proud and too good for them. not even his big derbyshire estate could save him from being seen as disagreeable compared to bingley.

bingley got to know everyone fast, danced every dance, and wished the night didn’t end so soon. he was talking about hosting his own ball at netherfield. quite the contrast to darcy, who danced only twice, didn’t want to meet other ladies, and just strolled around. his reputation was set: proud and unpleasant, and folks hoped he wouldn’t come back. mrs. bennet was especially ticked off since he slighted one of her daughters.

elizabeth bennet found herself sitting out two dances due to the lack of gentlemen. darcy stood nearby, and she overheard him talking to bingley, who tried to get him to dance. “come on, darcy,” bingley said, “i hate seeing you just standing there. better to dance.” darcy replied, “no way. i hate dancing unless i know my partner. here, it’d be unbearable. your sisters are busy, and no other lady here would be tolerable to dance with.”

bingley was all like, “man, i wouldn’t be as picky as you are for anything. tonight’s been full of pleasant girls, and some are real pretty.”

darcy, eyeing jane bennet, said, “you’re dancing with the only good-looking girl here.”

bingley’s like, “she’s gorgeous! but look, her sister behind you is pretty too. let me introduce you.”

darcy glanced over at elizabeth, caught her eye, then looked away, saying, “she’s okay, but not enough to interest me. i’m not in the mood to entertain girls ignored by others. go back to your partner, you’re wasting your time here.”

bingley took his advice. darcy walked off. elizabeth wasn’t too happy with him, but she told the story to her friends with a playful spin. 

the evening was good for the bennet clan. mrs. bennet saw jane getting attention from the netherfield crowd. bingley danced with her twice, and his sisters noticed her too. jane was pleased, quietly. elizabeth shared in her happiness. mary heard she was the most accomplished girl around. catherine and lydia had partners all night, which is all they cared about at a ball. they went back to longbourn in good spirits, where mr. bennet, lost in his book, was curious about the night. he hoped his wife’s hopes would be dashed, but that wasn’t the case.

mrs. bennet came in, all excited, “mr. bennet, it was a fantastic night, so much admiration for jane. bingley danced with her twice! just imagine. first with miss lucas, but he didn’t like her much. he was taken by jane and danced with her again. then with miss king, maria lucas, and back to jane and then lizzy.”

her husband, tired of hearing about partners, said, “if he had any mercy on me, he’d have danced less. wish he’d sprained his ankle early on.”

mrs. bennet went on, “he’s so handsome, his sisters are charming, and their dresses! the lace on mrs. hurst’s gown…”

she got cut off again. mr. bennet wasn't having any of that fancy talk. so she had to switch gears and vent about mr. darcy's rudeness with a bit too much drama.

“but trust me,” she said, “lizzy's not missing much by not catching his eye; he's a disagreeable, awful man, not worth impressing. so full of himself, you can't stand it! struts around like he's something special! thinks he's too good to dance with anyone! wish you'd been there, would've given him a piece of your mind. can't stand the guy.”

when jane and elizabeth were on their own, jane, usually careful with her words about mr. bingley, told her sister how much she admired him.

“he's everything a young man should be,” she said, “smart, cheerful, lively; never seen manners that happy! so easygoing, and such good breeding!”

“and he's good-looking too,” elizabeth replied, “which a young man should be if he can help it. makes him the whole package.”

“i was really flattered he asked me to dance again. didn't see that coming.”

“didn't you? i did for you. that's where we're different. compliments surprise you, not me. what could be more natural than him asking again? you're about five times prettier than anyone else there. not his gallantry. well, he's agreeable, you can like him. you've liked dumber people.”

“oh lizzy!”

“you're too quick to like people. you never see flaws. everyone’s good and agreeable to you. never heard you badmouth anyone.”

“i don’t want to judge too quickly; but i say what i think.”

“i know: that's the surprise. with your smarts, to be so blind to others' nonsense! lots pretend to be honest; you see it all the time. but to be truly candid, to see the good and make it better, and ignore the bad, that's you. so you like this guy's sisters too? their manners aren't up to his.”

“not at first; but they're nice when you talk to them. miss bingley's gonna live with her brother and keep house; i'm betting we'll find her a charming neighbor.”

elizabeth stayed quiet, not buying it: they weren't great at the party; she's quicker to judge and less flexible than her sister, with no personal bias, she wasn't ready to give them a pass. they were fancy enough; good-humored when pleased, agreeable when they chose; but proud and stuck-up. kinda good-looking; educated at top private schools; had a twenty thousand pound fortune; spent too much, hung with the high rank; so, fit to think well of themselves and less of others. came from a respectable northern family; remembered that more than how their brother’s and their own money came from trade.

mr. bingley got nearly a hundred thousand pounds from his dad. dad wanted an estate, but didn't make it happen. bingley had the same idea, picked a county sometimes, but with a good place at netherfield and a manor, folks weren't sure he'd move. his sisters pushed for his own estate, but even as a tenant, miss bingley loved hosting, and mrs. hurst treated the place like home when it fit her. bingley wasn't even 21 when a chance led him to netherfield house. he checked it out for half an hour, liked the spot and rooms, and took it on the spot.

bingley and darcy had a solid friendship despite being opposites. bingley's easygoing nature didn't clash with darcy's serious one. bingley trusted darcy's judgment completely. darcy was the sharper one, no doubt, haughty and reserved. bingley was liked everywhere; darcy, not so much.

at the meryton assembly, bingley found the folks and girls pleasant, no stiffness, felt at home quick. miss bennet was like an angel to him. darcy saw it differently—no beauty, no fashion, no interest. he admitted miss bennet was pretty but thought she smiled too much.

mrs. hurst and her sister liked miss bennet too, called her sweet. bingley felt he could think of her however he wanted with that kind of approval.

not far from longbourn lived sir william lucas and family, close with the bennets. sir william made a decent fortune and got knighted after addressing the king. he left his business and the small town life, moved a mile out to lucas lodge. he enjoyed his importance, was friendly and courteous, always civil to everyone. rank didn't make him snobby; it made him even more attentive.

lady lucas was a solid neighbor for mrs. bennet. not too clever, which made her a good friend in that neighborhood, yknow? her eldest, charlotte, was tight with elizabeth.

after the big ball, the lucases came over to talk it all out. 

mrs. bennet started, “charlotte, you kicked off the night well. mr. bingley's first choice, huh?”

charlotte shrugged, “yeah, but he seemed to like his second better.”

mrs. bennet nodded, “you mean jane? danced with her twice, right? word is he admired her. something about a chat with mr. robinson.”

charlotte added, “i caught that. robinson asked bingley about the ladies, and bingley said jane was the prettiest, no contest.”

“wow, that's pretty clear,” mrs. bennet said. “but who knows how it'll go.”

charlotte joked, “my overhearing was more useful than yours, eliza. mr. darcy's not worth listening to, huh? poor eliza, just 'tolerable'."

elizabeth's mom jumped in, “don't let it bug you. he's so disagreeable, it's probably better he doesn't like you. mrs. long said he sat silent by her for ages.”

jane was skeptical, “are you sure? i saw him speak to her.”

“he answered when asked about netherfield,” mrs. bennet explained, “but looked mad about it.”

jane countered, “miss bingley says he's great with friends.”

mrs. bennet waved it off, “if he was so friendly, he'd have spoken to mrs. long. probably too proud, hearing she doesn’t keep a carriage.”

miss lucas said, “i don't mind him ignoring mrs. long, but wish he'd danced with eliza.”

mrs. bennet advised, “next time, lizzy, don't bother dancing with him.”

lizzy promised, “no chance of that, ma’am.”

miss lucas excused darcy’s pride, “he's got reasons. family, fortune. he’s got a right to it.”

elizabeth sighed, “true, i’d forgive his pride if he hadn’t hurt mine.”

pride's a funny thing, mary says, like she's cracked the code. it's everywhere. read enough, and you see it. we all think we're hot stuff for something, real or not. pride's what we think of ourselves; vanity's what we want others to think.

"if i had darcy's money," young lucas jumps in, "i'd be as proud as i want. foxhounds, wine every day."

mrs. bennet's not having it. "you'd drink too much, and i'd take that bottle right away."
